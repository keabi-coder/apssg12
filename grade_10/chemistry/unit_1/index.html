UNIT 1: ATOMIC STRUCTURE AND PERIODIC PROPERTIES OF THE ELEMENTS

1. Historical Development of Atomic Structure

Early Philosophical Ideas

  • Democritus (460–370 BC): Suggested that matter is made of tiny, indestructible particles called atomos, meaning "indivisible."
    • His ideas were based on philosophical speculation, not experimental evidence.
  • Plato and Aristotle: Believed that matter is continuous and infinitely divisible.
    • Their view was dominant for nearly 2000 years.

John Dalton’s Atomic Theory (1808)

Dalton developed the first experimental-based atomic theory.

The Five Postulates of Dalton’s Atomic Theory

  1. All matter is composed of extremely small, indivisible particles called atoms.
  2. All atoms of a given element are identical in mass and properties.
  3. Atoms of different elements have different masses and properties.
  4. Atoms combine in simple whole-number ratios to form compounds.
  5. In chemical reactions, atoms are rearranged, combined, or separated. They are never created, destroyed, or changed into atoms of another element.

The Three Fundamental Laws of Chemistry (Basis for Dalton's Theory)

  • Law of Conservation of Mass: Mass is neither created nor destroyed in a chemical reaction.
  • Law of Definite Proportions (Law of Constant Composition): A given compound always contains exactly the same proportion of elements by mass.
    • Example: Water is always 11.2% hydrogen and 88.8% oxygen by mass.
  • Law of Multiple Proportions: When two elements form more than one compound, the masses of one element that combine with a fixed mass of the other are in ratios of small whole numbers.
    • Example: Nitrogen and oxygen form compounds where the masses of nitrogen combining with 1 g oxygen are 1.750 g, 0.8750 g, 0.4375 g. The ratios are 4:2:1.

Evaluation of Dalton’s Atomic Theory

  • What continues to have significance (retained in modern theory):
    • Atoms are the basic building blocks of elements.
    • Atoms combine in simple whole-number ratios.
    • Chemical reactions involve the rearrangement of atoms.
  • Limitations (inconsistent with later observations):
    • Atoms are not indivisible. They consist of subatomic particles (electrons, protons, neutrons).
    • Atoms of the same element can have different masses (isotopes).
    • Atoms can be changed into atoms of other elements (nuclear reactions).

Modern Atomic Theory (Postulates)

The modern atomic theory emerged after J.J. Thomson discovered the electron in 1897. Its main postulates are:

  1. All matter is made up of atoms.
  2. Atoms of an element are not necessarily identical. Isotopes exist.
  3. Atoms are divisible. They consist of subatomic particles.
  4. Atoms combine in simple whole-number ratios to form compounds.
  5. In ordinary chemical reactions, atoms are rearranged. Nuclear reactions, however, can change atoms of one element into another.

How the three fundamental laws are explained: Atoms are rearranged and combined in fixed, whole-number ratios. They are not created or destroyed in ordinary reactions.

2. Early Experiments to Characterize the Atom

Discovery of the Electron: Cathode Ray Experiments (J.J. Thomson, 1897)

  • Apparatus: A glass tube with evacuated air, containing a cathode (negative electrode) and an anode (positive electrode).
  • When a high voltage was applied, a greenish glow appeared on the glass. The invisible rays causing the glow were called cathode rays.

Properties of Cathode Rays

  • They travel from the cathode to the anode.
  • They are invisible, but their path can be seen when they strike certain materials.
  • They are deflected by electric fields (bent toward a positively charged plate) and by magnetic fields.
  • Their properties are independent of the material of the cathode.

Conclusion: Cathode rays consist of a beam of negatively charged particles, later called electrons. Electrons are constituents of all matter.

  • Thomson measured the charge-to-mass ratio of the electron:
    eme=−5.686×10−12 kg C−1\frac{e}{m_e} = -5.686 \times 10^{-12} \, \text{kg C}^{-1}me​e​=−5.686×10−12kg C−1
    He could not determine the charge or mass separately.

Millikan’s Oil Drop Experiment (1909)

  • Robert A. Millikan measured the charge of an electron by balancing charged oil drops in an electric field.
  • He found the electron charge: e=−1.602×10−19 Ce = -1.602 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{C}e=−1.602×10−19C.
  • Using Thomson’s ratio, he calculated the electron mass:
    me=5.686×10−12 kg C−11.602×10−19 C=9.109×10−31 kgm_e = \frac{5.686 \times 10^{-12} \, \text{kg C}^{-1}}{1.602 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{C}} = 9.109 \times 10^{-31} \, \text{kg}me​=1.602×10−19C5.686×10−12kg C−1​=9.109×10−31kg.

Radioactivity

  • Radioactivity (radioactive decay): The spontaneous emission of particles and/or radiation from the unstable nuclei of certain atoms, such as uranium and radium.

Three Types of Radioactive Emissions

  • Alpha (α) particles: Positively charged particles with a mass about four times that of a hydrogen atom and a charge twice the magnitude of an electron. They are identical to helium nuclei. They are deflected by a positively charged plate.
  • Beta (β) particles: Electrons coming from inside the nucleus. They are negatively charged and deflected by a negatively charged plate.
  • Gamma (γ) rays: High-energy electromagnetic radiation. They have no charge and are not affected by an external electric or magnetic field.

Discovery of the Nucleus: Rutherford’s Gold Foil Experiment (1911)

  • Before Rutherford: J.J. Thomson proposed the "plum-pudding" model. In this model, electrons and protons were thought to be randomly distributed in a positively charged cloud.
  • Rutherford’s experiment: A beam of positively charged alpha particles was aimed at a thin sheet of gold foil.

Observations

  • Most alpha particles passed straight through the foil with no deflection.
  • A small fraction were deflected at small angles.
  • Very few (about 1 in 20,000) were deflected back at large angles or even bounced back.

Inferences and Conclusions

  • Most of the atom is empty space (because most alpha particles passed through undeflected).
  • The positive charge and almost all the mass of the atom are concentrated in a tiny, dense central region, which Rutherford called the nucleus.
  • The nucleus is positively charged. This positive charge causes alpha particles to be repelled and deflected. Large deflections occur when an alpha particle approaches the nucleus closely.

Result: The nuclear model of the atom — a small, dense, positively charged nucleus surrounded by electrons.

Discovery of the Proton (1919)

  • Rutherford bombarded nitrogen with alpha particles and produced hydrogen nuclei. These positively charged particles were identified as protons.
  • The proton has a mass mp=1.67262×10−27 kgm_p = 1.67262 \times 10^{-27} \, \text{kg}mp​=1.67262×10−27kg (about 1840 times the electron mass) and a charge equal in magnitude to that of the electron, but positive.

Discovery of the Neutron (1932)

  • James Chadwick bombarded beryllium metal with alpha rays and obtained a highly penetrating radiation.
  • This radiation consisted of neutral particles, called neutrons.
  • The neutron has a mass mn=1.67493×10−27 kgm_n = 1.67493 \times 10^{-27} \, \text{kg}mn​=1.67493×10−27kg (almost identical to the proton) and zero electric charge.

4. Electromagnetic Radiation (EMR) and Atomic Spectra

Characteristics of Electromagnetic Radiation

  • EMR: The emission and transmission of energy in the form of electromagnetic waves (James Clerk Maxwell, 1873).
  • An electromagnetic wave consists of oscillating electric and magnetic field components that are perpendicular to each other.
  • Three primary characteristics:
    • Wavelength (λ): The distance between two successive peaks. Expressed in meters (m), nanometers (nm), picometers (pm), or angstroms (Å).
    • Frequency (ν): The number of wave cycles per second. Expressed in hertz (Hz) or s⁻¹.
    • Speed (c): All EMR travels at the speed of light in a vacuum, c=3.0×108 m/sc = 3.0 \times 10^8 \, \text{m/s}c=3.0×108m/s.
  • The wave relationship: c=λνc = \lambda \nuc=λν

The Electromagnetic Spectrum

  • EMR covers a broad continuous range from gamma rays (shortest wavelength, highest energy) to radio waves (longest wavelength, lowest energy).
    • The full spectrum, in order of increasing wavelength: gamma rays, X-rays, ultraviolet, visible, infrared, microwaves, radio waves.
  • Visible light occupies a narrow band from about 380 nm (violet) to 750 nm (red). A rainbow is an example of a continuous spectrum.

Quantum Theory (Max Planck, 1900)

  • Energy, like matter, is discontinuous. Atoms and molecules can emit or absorb energy only in discrete quantities called quanta (singular: quantum).
  • The energy of a single quantum (a photon) is directly proportional to its frequency:
    E=hνE = h\nuE=hν
    Since ν=c/λ\nu = c/\lambdaν=c/λ, we also have:
    E=hcλE = \frac{hc}{\lambda}E=λhc​
    where h=6.63×10−34 J\cdotpsh = 6.63 \times 10^{-34} \, \text{J·s}h=6.63×10−34J\cdotps is Planck’s constant.
  • Energy is always emitted or absorbed in whole-number multiples of hνh\nuhν (e.g., hν,2hν,3hν,…h\nu, 2h\nu, 3h\nu, \dotshν,2hν,3hν,…).

The Photoelectric Effect (Albert Einstein, 1905)

  • Photoelectric effect: The phenomenon in which electrons are ejected from the surface of certain metals when light of a sufficiently high frequency (the threshold frequency, ν₀) strikes the metal.
  • Einstein explained this by assuming that EMR is quantized (consists of photons).
  • The minimum energy required to remove an electron from the metal surface is:
    E0=hν0E_0 = h\nu_0E0​=hν0​
  • If the incident photon has energy hνh\nuhν greater than hν0h\nu_0hν0​, the excess energy becomes the kinetic energy (KE) of the ejected electron:
    KEe=12mv2=hν−hν0KE_e = \frac{1}{2} m v^2 = h\nu - h\nu_0KEe​=21​mv2=hν−hν0​
  • If ν<ν0\nu < \nu_0ν<ν0​, no electrons are ejected, no matter how intense the light.
  • Intensity of light: A measure of the number of photons. Greater intensity means more photons are available to release electrons, provided ν>ν0\nu > \nu_0ν>ν0​.

The Dual Nature of Light and Matter

  • Light exhibits both wave-like and particle-like properties. This is called the wave-particle duality of light.
  • Einstein derived the mass–energy equivalence: E=mc2E = mc^2E=mc2.
    The apparent mass of a photon is therefore: m=Ec2=hcλm = \frac{E}{c^2} = \frac{h}{c\lambda}m=c2E​=cλh​.

De Broglie’s Hypothesis (1924)

  • Louis de Broglie proposed that if energy can behave like a particle, then particles of matter should also exhibit wave properties.
  • The de Broglie wavelength of any particle of mass mmm moving at speed vvv is:
    λ=hmv\lambda = \frac{h}{mv}λ=mvh​
  • This wave-particle duality applies to all matter but is only significant for subatomic particles.

Atomic (Line) Spectra

  • When an electric discharge (spark) passes through a gas such as hydrogen, the atoms absorb energy. Electrons are excited to higher energy states. As the electrons return to lower energy states, they emit the absorbed energy in the form of light.
  • When this emitted light is passed through a prism, it does not produce a continuous rainbow. Instead, only a few discrete colours are seen, appearing as a series of individual lines. This is called the element’s atomic spectrum or emission spectrum.
  • The wavelengths of these spectral lines are characteristic of the element and can be used for identification.
  • Each spectral line corresponds to a specific energy transition between two discrete energy levels. The energy of the emitted photon is given by:
    ΔE=hν=hcλ\Delta E = h\nu = \frac{hc}{\lambda}ΔE=hν=λhc​.

The Bohr Model of the Hydrogen Atom (Niels Bohr, 1913)

Bohr combined Rutherford’s nuclear model with Planck’s quantum theory to explain the hydrogen line spectrum.

Bohr’s Postulates

  1. The electron in a hydrogen atom travels around the nucleus in a circular orbit.
  2. The energy of the electron is proportional to its distance from the nucleus. The farther the electron, the more energy it has.
  3. Only a limited number of orbits with certain discrete energies are allowed. The orbits are quantized.
  4. The only allowed orbits are those for which the angular momentum of the electron is an integral multiple of h/2πh/2\pih/2π:
    mvr=nh2πm v r = n \frac{h}{2\pi}mvr=n2πh​ (quantum condition)
    where n=1,2,3,…n = 1, 2, 3, \dotsn=1,2,3,… is the principal quantum number.
  5. As long as an electron stays in a given allowed orbit, it neither gains nor loses energy. The atom does not change its energy while the electron moves within that orbit.
  6. The electron moves to a higher energy orbit only by absorbing a photon of light. It emits a photon of light when it falls to a lower energy orbit. The photon’s energy equals the energy difference between the two orbits:
    ΔE=Efinal−Einitial=hν\Delta E = E_{\text{final}} - E_{\text{initial}} = h\nuΔE=Efinal​−Einitial​=hν.

  • Bohr derived that the radius of the nth orbit is:
    r=n2a0r = n^2 a_0r=n2a0​
    where a0=0.53 A˚a_0 = 0.53 \, \text{Å}a0​=0.53A˚ is the Bohr radius (radius of the first orbit, n = 1).
    So, r=(0.53 A˚)n2r = (0.53 \, \text{Å}) n^2r=(0.53A˚)n2.

  • The allowed energies are:
    En=−RHn2E_n = -\frac{R_H}{n^2}En​=−n2RH​​

  • Ground state: n=1n = 1n=1, the lowest and most stable energy state.
  • Excited states: n=2,3,4,…n = 2, 3, 4, \dotsn=2,3,4,…, higher energy states. An electron with n>1n > 1n>1 is said to be in an excited state.

Limitations of the Bohr Model

  • It works well only for atoms or ions that have a single electron (H, He⁺, Li²⁺, etc.).
  • It cannot explain the atomic spectra of atoms with more than one electron, not even helium.
  • It cannot explain the further splitting of spectral lines when a magnetic field is applied (Zeeman effect).
  • It assumes that an electron has both a known radius and a known orbit simultaneously, which contradicts the Heisenberg uncertainty principle.

5. The Quantum Mechanical Model of the Atom

Heisenberg’s Uncertainty Principle (1927)

  • Werner Heisenberg proposed that it is impossible to know both the exact position and the exact momentum (where momentum p=mvp = mvp=mv) of a particle at the same time.
  • The principle is expressed mathematically as:
    (Δx)(Δp)≥h4π(\Delta x)(\Delta p) \geq \frac{h}{4\pi}(Δx)(Δp)≥4πh​
    where Δx\Delta xΔx is the uncertainty in position and Δp\Delta pΔp is the uncertainty in momentum.
  • In simple terms: if we know precisely where a particle is, we cannot know precisely how it is moving. If we know precisely how it is moving, we cannot know precisely where it is.
  • This principle has no practical consequence for macroscopic objects but is fundamental for atomic and subatomic particles.

The Wave Function and Orbitals (Erwin Schrödinger, 1927)

  • Schrödinger developed a mathematical equation (the wave equation) that describes the behaviour of an electron as a matter-wave.
  • The solution of this equation is a wave function, represented by the Greek letter ψ\psiψ.
  • The square of the wave function, ψ2\psi^2ψ2, gives the probability density — the probability of finding the electron at a particular point in space.
  • The electron is not considered to be in a fixed orbit, but rather in a region of space where the probability of finding it is high. This region is called an orbital.
  • An orbital is a three-dimensional region around the nucleus that has a high probability (about 90%) of containing an electron. It is often visualized as an “electron cloud.”

The Four Quantum Numbers

Each electron in an atom is described by a unique set of four quantum numbers:

  1. Principal Quantum Number (n):

    • Describes the main energy level, or shell, an electron occupies.
    • Allowed values: positive integers n=1,2,3,4,…n = 1, 2, 3, 4, \dotsn=1,2,3,4,…
    • Determines the size and energy of the orbital. The larger the value of n, the larger the orbital and the higher its energy.

  2. Angular Momentum Quantum Number (ℓ):

    • Describes the shape of the orbital. It is also called the azimuthal or subsidiary quantum number.
    • For a given n, ℓ can have integer values from 0 to n−1n-1n−1.
    • Orbitals with the same n but different ℓ belong to different sublevels (subshells).
    • The letters designating each ℓ value are:
      ℓ = 0 → s
      ℓ = 1 → p
      ℓ = 2 → d
      ℓ = 3 → f
    • For a given principal energy level n, there are n2n^2n2 total orbitals.

  3. Magnetic Quantum Number (mℓ):

    • Describes the orientation of the orbital in space relative to the other orbitals.
    • For a given ℓ, mℓ takes all integer values from −ℓ-\ell−ℓ to +ℓ+\ell+ℓ, including 0.
    • The number of possible mℓ values (and thus the number of orbitals) for a given ℓ is 2ℓ+12\ell + 12ℓ+1.
      • s subshell (ℓ=0): 1 orbital (mℓ = 0)
      • p subshell (ℓ=1): 3 orbitals (mℓ = –1, 0, +1)
      • d subshell (ℓ=2): 5 orbitals (mℓ = –2, –1, 0, +1, +2)
      • f subshell (ℓ=3): 7 orbitals (mℓ = –3, –2, –1, 0, +1, +2, +3)

  4. Electron Spin Quantum Number (mₛ):

    • Refers to the spin of the electron, which generates a magnetic field.
    • For any orbital, there are only two allowed values: +12+\frac{1}{2}+21​ and −12-\frac{1}{2}−21​.
    • Each atomic orbital can accommodate a maximum of two electrons. These two electrons must have opposite spins.

Shapes of Atomic Orbitals

  • s orbitals (ℓ = 0): Spherically symmetrical. All s orbitals are spherical, regardless of n. The 1s is the smallest, 2s is larger, etc. The probability of finding an s electron is greatest at the nucleus and decreases with distance.
  • p orbitals (ℓ = 1): Dumbbell-shaped, with two lobes on opposite sides of the nucleus. There are three p orbitals in a subshell. They are oriented along mutually perpendicular axes and are designated as pxp_xpx​, pyp_ypy​, and pzp_zpz​.
  • d orbitals (ℓ = 2): Five orbitals with more complex shapes. Four of them have a cloverleaf shape with four lobes, and one ( dz2d_{z^2}dz2​ ) has two large lobes and a ring.
  • f orbitals (ℓ = 3): Seven orbitals with even more complex shapes. They are found in principal energy levels n ≥ 4.

6. Electronic Configurations and Orbital Diagrams

The Three Principles for Filling Orbitals

  1. Aufbau (Building-up) Principle:

    • Electrons occupy the lowest-energy atomic orbitals available before filling higher-energy ones.
    • The order of filling subshells is:
      1s → 2s → 2p → 3s → 3p → 4s → 3d → 4p → 5s → 4d → 5p → 6s → 4f → 5d → 6p → 7s → 5f → 6d → 7p...
    • Note that the 4s orbital has a slightly lower energy than the 3d orbital, so 4s fills before 3d.

  2. Pauli’s Exclusion Principle:

    • No two electrons in the same atom can have the same set of four quantum numbers (n, ℓ, mℓ, mₛ).
    • As a consequence, an atomic orbital can hold a maximum of two electrons, and those two electrons must have opposite spins (one with mₛ = +½, the other with mₛ = –½).

  3. Hund’s Rule:

    • When electrons fill a set of degenerate (equal-energy) orbitals, the lowest energy arrangement is the one that maximizes the number of unpaired electrons with parallel spins.
    • In other words, every orbital in a subshell receives one electron before any orbital receives a second electron. The unpaired electrons all have the same spin direction.

Writing Electronic Configurations

Two common notations are used:

  • Subshell (sublevel) notation: The principal quantum number is followed by the letter of the subshell and a superscript indicating the number of electrons.
    Example: The electron configuration of a nitrogen atom (Z=7) is 1s22s22p31s^2 2s^2 2p^31s22s22p3.

  • A shorthand notation uses the preceding noble gas symbol in square brackets to represent the inner core electrons.
    Example: Sodium (Z=11) is [Ne]3s1[\text{Ne}] 3s^1[Ne]3s1.

  • Orbital diagram: Each orbital is represented by a box (or a circle or a line). An electron is shown as an arrow, with the direction indicating its spin (↑ for mₛ = +½, ↓ for mₛ = –½).
    For nitrogen:
    1s: [↑↓]
    2s: [↑↓]
    2p: [↑ ][↑ ][↑ ] (three unpaired electrons, each in a separate 2p orbital)

Valence Electrons and Core Electrons

  • Valence electrons: The electrons in the outermost principal quantum level (highest n). They are the most important electrons because they are involved in chemical bonding.
  • Core electrons: The inner electrons that are not in the valence shell.

Exceptions to the Aufbau Principle

For some atoms, the observed ground-state electron configuration differs slightly from the one predicted by the Aufbau order. This occurs because half-filled and completely filled d subshells impart extra stability.

  • Chromium (Cr, Z=24):
    Expected: [Ar] 4s23d44s^2 3d^44s23d4
    Observed: [Ar] 4s13d54s^1 3d^54s13d5
    Reason: The half-filled 3d subshell (3d53d^53d5) and half-filled 4s subshell (4s14s^14s1) are more stable.
  • Copper (Cu, Z=29):
    Expected: [Ar] 4s23d94s^2 3d^94s23d9
    Observed: [Ar] 4s13d104s^1 3d^{10}4s13d10
    Reason: The completely filled 3d subshell (3d103d^{10}3d10) is exceptionally stable.

These exceptions occur because the energy difference between the 4s and 3d orbitals is very small.

7. Electronic Configurations and the Periodic Table of the Elements

The Modern Periodic Table

  • Elements are arranged in order of increasing atomic number.
  • Periodic Law: When elements are arranged by increasing atomic number, certain sets of physical and chemical properties repeat at regular intervals (periodically).
  • Period: A horizontal row in the periodic table. The period number is the same as the highest principal quantum number (n) for the elements in that row.
  • Group: A vertical column. Elements in the same group have similar valence-shell electron configurations and therefore similar chemical properties. There are 18 groups in the modern table.

Classification of Elements Based on Electron Configuration

  • Representative (main-group) elements: The s or p subshells of the outermost shell are being filled. These are found in Groups 1, 2, and 13–18.
  • Transition elements: The d subshell is being filled. These are located in Groups 3–12.
  • Inner transition elements: The f subshell is being filled. These are the lanthanides and actinides, usually placed below the main body of the table.
  • Metalloids: Elements with properties intermediate between metals and non-metals. Examples include boron (B), silicon (Si), and germanium (Ge).

Periodic Trends in Atomic Properties

1. Atomic Size (Atomic Radius)

  • The atomic radius is half the distance between the nuclei of two identical atoms bonded together.
  • Trend down a group: Atomic radius increases.
    Reason: As you go down a group, electrons occupy higher principal energy levels (n increases), which are farther from the nucleus. The outer electrons are also shielded from the full nuclear charge by inner electrons.
  • Trend across a period (left to right): Atomic radius decreases.
    Reason: As you move across a period, the principal quantum number n stays the same, but the nuclear charge (number of protons) increases. The increased nuclear charge exerts a stronger pull on the electrons. Inner electrons do not perfectly shield the outer electrons, so the effective nuclear charge (Zeff) increases. The electron cloud is drawn closer to the nucleus, resulting in a smaller atomic radius.
  • For transition metals, size changes are less regular. After the first few transition elements, atomic radii remain relatively constant because the shielding effect of inner d electrons cancels the effect of the increasing nuclear charge.

2. Ionization Energy (IE)

  • Ionization energy is the minimum energy required to completely remove one mole of electrons from one mole of gaseous atoms or ions.
  • First ionization energy (IE₁): Atom(g)→Ion+(g)+e−\text{Atom(g)} \rightarrow \text{Ion}^+(\text{g}) + e^-Atom(g)→Ion+(g)+e−
  • Second ionization energy (IE₂): Ion+(g)→Ion2+(g)+e−\text{Ion}^+(\text{g}) \rightarrow \text{Ion}^{2+}(\text{g}) + e^-Ion+(g)→Ion2+(g)+e−
    IE₂ is always larger than IE₁ because it is more difficult to remove an electron from a positively charged ion.
  • Trend across a period: IE₁ generally increases from left to right.
    Reason: As Zeff increases and atomic radius decreases, the outer electrons are held more tightly by the nucleus.
    • Small deviations occur: A Group 3A element (ns² np¹) has a lower IE₁ than the preceding Group 2A element (ns²) because the np electron is easier to remove than an ns electron. A Group 6A element (ns² np⁴) has a lower IE₁ than the preceding Group 5A element (ns² np³) because electron–electron repulsion in the doubly occupied p orbital makes it easier to remove an electron.
  • Trend down a group: IE₁ generally decreases.
    Reason: As n increases, the atomic size increases. The valence electrons are farther from the nucleus and experience more shielding, so they are easier to remove.
  • Alkali metals (Group 1) have the lowest ionization energies. Noble gases (Group 18) have the highest.
  • A very large jump in ionization energy indicates that a core (inner-shell) electron is being removed after all valence electrons have been stripped away.

3. Electron Affinity (EA)

  • Electron affinity is the energy change (usually negative) when an electron is added to a gaseous atom to form a gaseous negative ion:
    Atom(g)+e−→Ion−(g)\text{Atom(g)} + e^- \rightarrow \text{Ion}^-(\text{g})Atom(g)+e−→Ion−(g)
  • The first electron affinity (EA₁) is usually negative, meaning energy is released. The second electron affinity (EA₂) is always positive because energy must be absorbed to overcome electrostatic repulsion and add an electron to a negative ion.
  • Trend across a period: EA generally becomes more negative (more energy released) from left to right. Halogens (Group 7A) have the most negative electron affinities.
    • Exceptions: Group 2A and Group 5A elements have less negative or even positive EA values. For Group 2A, an added electron must enter a higher-energy p orbital. For Group 5A, an added electron would pair up in an already half-filled p orbital, causing repulsion.
  • Trend down a group: No simple uniform trend.

4. Electronegativity

  • Electronegativity is a measure of the ability of an atom in a molecule to attract the shared electrons in a chemical bond (Pauling scale).
  • Fluorine is the most electronegative element, assigned a value of 4.0.
  • Trend across a period: Electronegativity increases from left to right.
  • Trend down a group: Electronegativity decreases.
  • Reason: The trends follow ionization energy. As atomic radius decreases across a period, the attraction between the nucleus and bonding electrons increases, so electronegativity increases. Down a group, larger atomic radius means weaker attraction, so electronegativity decreases.

5. Metallic Character

  • Metallic character refers to the ease with which an element loses electrons. Metals are typically lustrous, malleable, ductile, and good conductors of heat and electricity.
  • Trend across a period: Metallic character decreases from left to right. Elements on the left are metals; those on the right are non-metals.
  • Trend down a group: Metallic character increases. As atomic size increases and ionization energy decreases, atoms lose electrons more readily.

Advantages of the Periodic Classification of Elements

  1. Classification is based on atomic number, a fundamental property.
  2. All isotopes of an element are placed in the same position because atomic number is the same.
  3. The table explains the periodicity of properties and directly relates them to electronic configurations.
  4. Anomalous pairs based on mass number (like argon and potassium) are correctly ordered by atomic number.
  5. The lanthanides and actinides are placed separately, keeping the table compact and organised.
  6. The table is a simple, systematic, and easy way to remember and predict the properties of elements.

8. Comprehensive Exam-Focused Summary

Key Definitions to Know

  • Atom: The smallest particle of an element that retains the chemical identity of that element.
  • Atomic Number (Z): The number of protons in the nucleus of an atom.
  • Mass Number (A): The total number of protons and neutrons in the nucleus.
  • Isotopes: Atoms of the same element that have different numbers of neutrons and therefore different mass numbers.
  • Atomic Mass: The weighted average mass of all naturally occurring isotopes of an element.
  • Cathode Rays: Streams of electrons emitted from the cathode in a discharge tube.
  • Radioactivity: The spontaneous emission of particles and/or radiation from the unstable nuclei of certain atoms.
  • Alpha Particle: A particle identical to a helium nucleus, with 2 protons and 2 neutrons, carrying a +2 charge.
  • Beta Particle: A high-speed electron emitted from the nucleus, carrying a –1 charge.
  • Gamma Ray: High-energy electromagnetic radiation with no mass and no charge.
  • Photon: A quantum (packet) of electromagnetic energy.
  • Photoelectric Effect: The ejection of electrons from a metal surface when light of a sufficiently high frequency strikes it.
  • Threshold Frequency (ν₀): The minimum frequency of light required to eject electrons from a given metal surface in the photoelectric effect.
  • Line Spectrum (Emission Spectrum): A spectrum consisting of only discrete wavelengths, characteristic of the emitting element.
  • Ground State: The lowest energy state of an atom or electron.
  • Excited State: Any energy state higher than the ground state.
  • Orbital: A three-dimensional region around the nucleus where there is a high probability of finding an electron.
  • Quantum Numbers: A set of four numbers (n, ℓ, mℓ, mₛ) that describe the energy, shape, orientation, and spin of an electron.
  • Aufbau Principle: The rule that electrons fill atomic orbitals starting from the lowest available energy level.
  • Pauli Exclusion Principle: No two electrons in an atom can have the same four quantum numbers.
  • Hund’s Rule: Electrons fill degenerate orbitals singly with parallel spins before pairing up.
  • Electron Configuration: The distribution of electrons among the various atomic orbitals.
  • Valence Electrons: Electrons in the outermost shell of an atom that are involved in chemical bonding.
  • Core Electrons: Electrons in the inner, completely filled shells of an atom.
  • Periodic Law: The statement that the properties of elements are periodic functions of their atomic numbers.
  • Effective Nuclear Charge (Zeff): The net positive charge experienced by an electron after taking into account the shielding effect of inner electrons.
  • Ionization Energy (IE): The energy required to remove an electron from a gaseous atom or ion.
  • Electron Affinity (EA): The energy change when an electron is added to a gaseous atom.
  • Electronegativity: A relative measure of an atom’s ability to attract shared electrons in a chemical bond.
  • Metallic Character: The tendency of an element to lose electrons and exhibit properties typical of metals.

Comparison of Atomic Models

Important Differences and Similarities

  • Dalton’s Atomic Theory vs. Modern Atomic Theory: Both agree that atoms are the basic units of elements. They agree that atoms combine in fixed, whole-number ratios and are rearranged in chemical reactions. However, Dalton’s theory considered atoms indivisible and identical for a given element. The modern theory accepts that atoms are divisible, that isotopes exist, and that nuclear reactions can change one element into another.
  • Alpha (α), Beta (β), and Gamma (γ) Radiation: Alpha is a helium nucleus (heavy, +2 charge). Beta is a fast electron (light, –1 charge). Gamma is a high-energy photon (no mass, no charge). Alpha and beta are deflected by electric and magnetic fields. Gamma is not.
  • Continuous Spectrum vs. Line Spectrum: A continuous spectrum contains all wavelengths of light (like a rainbow). A line spectrum contains only a few discrete wavelengths (coloured lines on a dark background). A line spectrum is unique to each element and is produced when excited electrons fall to lower energy levels.
  • Bohr Model vs. Quantum Mechanical Model: Both models have quantized energy levels. However, Bohr’s model uses fixed circular orbits with exact radii. The quantum mechanical model replaces orbits with orbitals — probability regions where electrons are most likely to be found. Bohr’s model fails for atoms with more than one electron. The quantum mechanical model works for all atoms.
  • Orbit vs. Orbital: An orbit is a well-defined circular path of an electron (Bohr model). An orbital is a fuzzy, three-dimensional region of high electron probability (quantum mechanical model).
  • Electron Affinity vs. Electronegativity: Electron affinity is the energy change when an isolated gaseous atom gains an electron. Electronegativity is the ability of an atom in a molecule to attract the shared electrons in a chemical bond. Both measure electron-attracting power, but EA is a measurable energy change for a free atom. Electronegativity is a relative scale for atoms in molecules.

Step-by-Step Processes

  • Determining electron configuration:
    1. Note the atomic number (Z), which gives the number of electrons.
    2. Follow the Aufbau filling order: 1s, 2s, 2p, 3s, 3p, 4s, 3d, 4p, 5s, 4d, 5p, 6s, 4f, 5d, 6p...
    3. Place electrons in each orbital, respecting the Pauli principle (maximum 2 electrons per orbital, opposite spins).
    4. When filling a subshell with degenerate orbitals (p, d, f), apply Hund’s rule: place one electron in each orbital with parallel spins before pairing any.
    5. Recognise the two common exceptions: Cr and Cu (and similar) where half-filled or fully filled d subshells are more stable.
  • Calculating average atomic mass from isotopic data:
    1. Multiply the mass of each isotope by its fractional abundance (the percentage divided by 100).
    2. Add the products together.
  • Calculating the wavelength or energy of a photon emitted during an electron transition in hydrogen:
    1. Identify the initial energy level (n_i) and the final level (n_f), where n_i > n_f.
    2. Calculate the energy difference: ΔE=2.18×10−18 J(1ni2−1nf2)\Delta E = 2.18 \times 10^{-18} \, \text{J} \left( \frac{1}{n_i^2} - \frac{1}{n_f^2} \right)ΔE=2.18×10−18J(ni2​1​−nf2​1​).
    3. Then determine the wavelength: λ=hcΔE\lambda = \frac{hc}{\Delta E}λ=ΔEhc​, or frequency: ν=ΔEh\nu = \frac{\Delta E}{h}ν=hΔE​.

Pros and Cons of Key Concepts

  • Dalton’s Atomic Theory:
    • Pros: Explained the laws of conservation of mass, definite proportions, and multiple proportions. Provided the foundation of modern chemistry.
    • Cons: Postulated that atoms are indivisible, which was later disproved. Did not account for the existence of isotopes or subatomic particles.
  • Bohr Model:
    • Pros: First model to introduce quantized energy levels. Successfully explained the line spectrum of the hydrogen atom.
    • Cons: Failed to explain the spectra of atoms with more than one electron. Violates the Heisenberg uncertainty principle by assigning a definite orbit and momentum to the electron.
  • Quantum Mechanical Model:
    • Pros: Successfully describes the electronic structure of all atoms and accounts for fine spectral details. Forms the basis for modern understanding of chemical bonding.
    • Cons: Abstract and mathematically complex. Describes electrons only in terms of probabilities, not exact paths, which can be conceptually challenging.

Final Exam Reminders

  • The three fundamental laws of chemistry are: conservation of mass, definite proportions, and multiple proportions.
  • Cathode rays are beams of electrons. This was proved by J.J. Thomson.
  • Rutherford’s gold foil experiment established the existence of the atomic nucleus.
  • The energy, wavelength, and frequency of light are related by E=hν=hc/λE = h\nu = hc/\lambdaE=hν=hc/λ.
  • In the photoelectric effect, only light with a frequency above the threshold frequency can eject electrons. The kinetic energy of ejected electrons depends on the light frequency, not intensity.
  • Bohr’s hydrogen atom has quantized energies given by En=−RH/n2E_n = -R_H / n^2En​=−RH​/n2. The ground state is n=1. Spectral lines arise from electron transitions between these levels.
  • The four quantum numbers define an electron’s state: n (shell/energy), ℓ (subshell/shape), mℓ (orbital orientation), mₛ (spin).
  • The Aufbau principle, Pauli exclusion principle, and Hund’s rule govern the ground-state electron configuration of atoms.
  • Chromium and copper are exceptions to the Aufbau principle because half-filled (d⁵) and completely filled (d¹⁰) subshells confer extra stability.
  • Periodic trends to remember: Atomic radius decreases across a period and increases down a group. Ionization energy and electronegativity increase across a period and decrease down a group. Metallic character follows the opposite trend. These trends are explained by changes in effective nuclear charge and the principal quantum number.

2.1 Introduction to Chemical Bonding

Definition of a Chemical Bond

  • A chemical bond is the attractive force that holds atoms, ions or molecules together.
  • These forces are intramolecular forces. They affect both the chemical properties and the physical properties of a substance.

Why Atoms Form Chemical Bonds

  • Atoms combine to achieve a more stable electron configuration.
  • The noble gases (except helium) have an outer electron configuration of ns² np⁶. This configuration gives them very high stability, high ionization energies and low electron affinities.
  • Many atoms, when reacting, try to attain eight valence electrons, like the noble gases. This is known as the octet rule.

The Octet Rule

  • The octet rule states that atoms tend to gain, lose or share electrons until they are surrounded by an octet (eight) of valence electrons.
  • Example: Helium is an exception. It has a stable duplet (two electrons).
  • Some compounds have central atoms that do not obey the octet rule. These exceptions are discussed later.

Types of Chemical Bonding

  • The bonding type depends on the metallic or non-metallic character of the combining atoms.
  • Three main types:
    • Metal with non-metal: ionic bond.
    • Non-metal with non-metal: covalent bond.
    • Metal with metal: metallic bond.

2.2 Ionic Bonds

Definition and Formation

  • An ionic bond is the electrostatic attraction between positive ions (cations) and negative ions (anions).

  • It forms when one or more electrons are transferred from the valence shell of one atom (usually a metal) to the valence shell of another atom (usually a non-metal).

  • The atom that loses electrons becomes a cation. The atom that gains electrons becomes an anion.

  • Example: Formation of NaCl

    • Na → Na⁺ + e⁻ (Na loses one electron, becomes [Ne])
    • Cl + e⁻ → Cl⁻ (Cl gains one electron, becomes [Ar])
    • Na⁺ + Cl⁻ → NaCl (electrostatic attraction forms the ionic bond)

  • In an ionic solid, large numbers of cations and anions pack together in a regular crystalline structure to maximize attractions.

Lewis Electron Dot Symbols

  • A Lewis symbol shows the chemical symbol of an atom, with dots placed around it to represent valence electrons.

  • The inner electrons and nucleus are represented by the chemical symbol.

  • Example: Chlorine (Cl) with 7 valence electrons is drawn with 7 dots around Cl.

  • A Lewis structure combines Lewis symbols to show electron transfer or sharing.

  • For ionic bonding, the transfer is shown by moving an electron dot from the metal to the non-metal and enclosing the resulting ions in brackets with charges.

  • Example: Na + Cl → [Na]⁺ [ :Cl: ]⁻.

Lattice Energy

  • The lattice energy (U) is the enthalpy change when one mole of an ionic solid separates into its gaseous ions.
  • It represents the strength of ionic interactions. The larger the lattice energy, the stronger the ionic bonds.
  • Ionic solids exist because the large release of energy when gaseous ions coalesce into a crystal (the lattice energy) overcomes the endothermic steps of forming the ions.
  • For LiF: the electron-transfer steps (ionization of Li and electron gain by F) absorb net energy. However, when Li⁺(g) and F⁻(g) form LiF(s), a large amount of energy is released (lattice energy = –1050 kJ/mol). This makes the overall formation exothermic.

Born–Haber Cycle

  • The Born–Haber cycle is an application of Hess’s law to indirectly calculate lattice energy.
  • Hess’s law: The enthalpy change for an overall reaction is the sum of the enthalpy changes for the individual steps.
  • For NaCl, the direct formation from Na(s) and ½ Cl₂(g) has ΔH°_f = –411 kJ/mol.
  • The indirect route consists of five steps:
    1. Na(s) → Na(g) ΔH°_step1 = +108 kJ/mol (sublimation)
    2. ½ Cl₂(g) → Cl(g) ΔH°_step2 = +120 kJ/mol (½ bond energy of Cl₂)
    3. Na(g) → Na⁺(g) + e⁻ ΔH°_step3 = IE₁ = +496 kJ/mol
    4. Cl(g) + e⁻ → Cl⁻(g) ΔH°_step4 = EA = –349 kJ/mol
    5. Na⁺(g) + Cl⁻(g) → NaCl(s) ΔH°_step5 = U (lattice energy, unknown)
  • By Hess’s law: ΔH°_f = ΔH°_step1 + ΔH°_step2 + ΔH°_step3 + ΔH°_step4 + U.
  • Solving for U (NaCl): U = –411 – (108 + 120 + 496 – 349) = –786 kJ/mol.

Factors Affecting the Formation of Ionic Bonds

  • Ionization energy (IE): Low IE of a metal atom favours cation formation. Alkali and alkaline earth metals form cations easily.
  • Electron affinity (EA): High (very negative) EA of a non-metal atom favours anion formation. Halogens have high electron affinities.
  • Lattice energy (U): For the ionic solid to be stable, the energy released in forming the crystal must be large enough to overcome the energy needed to form the ions.
    • Lattice energy is directly proportional to the product of the ionic charges (q₁ × q₂) and inversely proportional to the distance between them (r).
    • U ∝ (q₁ × q₂) / r
    • Small ions with higher charges produce larger lattice energies, thus stronger ionic bonds.
    • Example: MgO has a higher lattice energy than LiF because Mg²⁺ and O²⁻ have greater charges than Li⁺ and F⁻, even though ion sizes are similar.

Exceptions to the Octet Rule in Ionic Bonding

  • Less than octet (electron-deficient ions): Ions of elements near helium (H, Li, Be, B) tend to achieve a duplet of electrons (like He) rather than an octet.
    • H⁻, Li⁺, Be²⁺ and B³⁺ are isoelectronic with He.
  • More than octet (18-electron rule): Ions of many transition and post-transition elements do not follow the octet rule. Their d orbitals allow them to have more than eight electrons in their valence shell.
    • Example: Fe²⁺ electron configuration [Ar]3d⁶, Fe³⁺ [Ar]3d⁵.
    • Example: Zn²⁺ [Ar]3d¹⁰, Ga³⁺ [Ar]3d¹⁰. Their valence shell can be described as ns² np⁶ nd¹⁰.
    • Note: When forming positive ions, electrons are lost first from the orbital with the highest principal quantum number (n). Thus, for transition metals, electrons are removed from 4s before 3d.

Properties of Ionic Compounds

  • Crystalline solids at room temperature.
  • Hard and brittle.
  • High melting and boiling points due to strong electrostatic forces (high lattice energy).
  • Do not conduct electricity in the solid state because ions are fixed in place.
  • Conduct electricity when molten or dissolved in water because ions become mobile.
  • Soluble in polar solvents (like water) but generally insoluble in non-polar solvents (like petroleum ether, cyclohexane).

2.3 Covalent Bonds and Molecular Geometry

Formation of a Covalent Bond

  • A covalent bond is formed when a pair of electrons is shared between two atoms.
  • In the H₂ molecule, as two hydrogen atoms approach, attractive forces (nucleus–electron) overcome repulsive forces (nucleus–nucleus, electron–electron), and a bond forms.
  • The most stable arrangement occurs at the bond length, where the total energy is minimum. For H₂, bond length is 74 pm.
  • The shared electron pair is concentrated in the region between the nuclei, holding the atoms together.

Drawing Lewis Structures (Electron Dot Formulas) for Covalent Compounds

Steps to write a Lewis formula:

  1. Determine the total number of valence electrons (sum of valence electrons of all atoms). For a polyatomic anion, add one electron per negative charge. For a cation, subtract one electron per positive charge.
  2. Write the skeletal structure. Usually the least electronegative atom (except hydrogen) is placed in the centre. Hydrogen is always a terminal atom.
  3. Connect atoms with single bonds (one electron pair) and distribute remaining electrons as lone pairs on terminal atoms first, completing octets (except hydrogen, which needs only two electrons).
  4. If the central atom has fewer than eight electrons, form multiple bonds by moving one or more lone pairs from a terminal atom to the bonding region between the central atom and that terminal atom.

Examples:

  • NF₃: N is central, bonded to three F atoms. Total valence electrons = 5 + (3 × 7) = 26. Single bonds use 6 electrons. 20 electrons remain, placed as lone pairs on F atoms (6 each), and one lone pair on N. All atoms have octets.
  • CO₃²⁻: Total electrons = 4 + (3 × 6) + 2 = 24. C is central. Single bonds and octets on O atoms leave C with only 6 electrons. One lone pair from an O is moved to form a double bond, giving C an octet and resonance structures.

Coordinate Covalent Bond (Dative Bond)

  • In most covalent bonds, each atom contributes one electron to the shared pair.
  • A coordinate covalent bond is formed when one atom donates both electrons of the bond (a lone pair) to an acceptor atom that has an empty orbital.
  • Example: The ammonium ion, NH₄⁺, is formed when the lone pair on nitrogen in NH₃ bonds to H⁺ (which has no electrons).
  • Other examples: H₃O⁺, AlCl₄⁻.

Resonance Structures

  • When more than one valid Lewis structure can be drawn for a molecule or ion by changing the positions of bonding and lone pairs (but keeping atom positions fixed), these are called resonance structures.
  • The true structure is a resonance hybrid—a blend of the resonance forms. The bonding is an average.
  • Resonance is indicated by a double-headed arrow (↔) between the structures.
  • Example: Ozone (O₃) has two resonance forms. Both O–O bonds are identical with a bond length of 1.278 Å, intermediate between a single and a double bond.
  • Example: Nitrite ion, NO₂⁻, has two equivalent resonance structures. Both N–O bonds are equivalent, part-way between single and double.

Exceptions to the Octet Rule in Covalent Bonding

Three categories:

  1. Less than an octet (electron-deficient molecules): Central atom has fewer than eight electrons. Common for elements of groups IIA and IIIA.
    • BeCl₂: only 4 electrons around Be.
    • BF₃: only 6 electrons around B.
  2. More than an octet (expanded valence): Central atom can accommodate 10, 12 or more electrons. Occurs for elements of period 3 and beyond because they have accessible d orbitals.
    • PF₅: 10 electrons around P.
    • SF₆: 12 electrons around S.
    • XeF₄: 12 electrons around Xe.
  3. Odd number of electrons (free radicals): Molecules with an unpaired electron.
    • Examples: NO (11 valence electrons), NO₂ (17 valence electrons), ClO₂ (19 valence electrons). Lewis structures for these show an unpaired electron.

Polar and Non-Polar Covalent Molecules

  • Non-polar covalent bond: Bonding electrons are shared equally between two identical atoms. Example: H–H, Cl–Cl.
  • Polar covalent bond: When atoms of different electronegativity share electrons unequally. The electrons spend more time near the more electronegative atom, creating partial charges (δ⁺ and δ⁻). Example: Hδ⁺–Fδ⁻.
  • Bond polarity is shown by a dipole arrow pointing toward the negative end, or by δ+/δ− symbols.
  • Dipole moment (μ): A measure of the polarity of a bond or molecule. μ = δ × d, where δ is the magnitude of partial charge and d is the distance of separation.
  • Unit: coulomb-metre (C·m). Often expressed in debye (D); 1 D = 3.33564 × 10⁻³⁰ C·m.
  • For a diatomic molecule, the bond moment is the dipole moment.
  • For polyatomic molecules, the overall dipole moment is the vector sum of the individual bond moments.
    • If bond dipoles cancel due to symmetry, the molecule is non-polar (μ = 0). Example: CO₂ is linear, bond dipoles oppose and cancel, so μ = 0.
    • If bond dipoles do not cancel, the molecule is polar. Example: H₂O is bent, bond dipoles add to give a net dipole moment (μ = 1.87 D).

Properties of Covalent Compounds

  • Covalent compounds exist as separate molecules. The intermolecular forces are weak compared to ionic bonds.
  • They are often gases, liquids, or low-melting solids at room temperature.
  • Low melting and boiling points (compared to ionic compounds).
  • Generally insoluble in water but soluble in non-polar solvents.
  • Non-polar covalent compounds are non-electrolytes: they do not conduct electricity in solid, molten or solution states, because they do not contain ions.
  • Polar covalent compounds may dissolve in water but do not form ions in the process unless they react.

Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion (VSEPR) Theory

  • The VSEPR model predicts the geometry of a molecule based on the idea that valence-shell electron pairs around a central atom repel each other and arrange as far apart as possible to minimize repulsion.
  • Electron groups include both bonding pairs (shared between atoms) and lone pairs (non-bonding pairs) on the central atom.
  • Repulsion strength order: lone pair–lone pair > lone pair–bonding pair > bonding pair–bonding pair.

Guidelines for applying VSEPR:

  1. Draw the Lewis structure and identify the electron pairs around the central atom.
  2. Count the total number of electron groups (bonding pairs + lone pairs). Treat multiple bonds as one group.
  3. The electron pair arrangement is determined by the total number of groups: 2 → linear, 3 → trigonal planar, 4 → tetrahedral, 5 → trigonal bipyramidal, 6 → octahedral.
  4. The molecular shape is named by the positions of atoms (nuclei) only. Lone pairs influence the shape but are not part of the shape name.

Types of molecules:

  • ABₓ with no lone pairs on A: molecular shape = electron pair arrangement.
    • AB₂: linear (BeCl₂)
    • AB₃: trigonal planar (BF₃)
    • AB₄: tetrahedral (CH₄)
    • AB₅: trigonal bipyramidal (PCl₅)
    • AB₆: octahedral (SF₆)
  • ABₓEy with one or more lone pairs on A: molecular shape is derived from electron arrangement but is named differently.
    • AB₂E: bent (SO₂) – 3 electron groups, shape is bent, ideal angle < 120°.
    • AB₃E: trigonal pyramidal (NH₃) – 4 groups, shape pyramidal, angle ~107°.
    • AB₂E₂: bent (H₂O) – 4 groups, shape bent, angle ~104.5°.
    • AB₄E: seesaw (SF₄) – 5 groups, lone pair occupies equatorial position to minimize repulsions.
    • AB₃E₂: T-shaped (ClF₃)
    • AB₅E: square pyramidal (BrF₅)
    • AB₄E₂: square planar (XeF₄)

Bond angles: Lone pairs compress bond angles because they exert greater repulsion. Example: in NH₃, the H–N–H angle is 107° (less than tetrahedral 109.5°). In H₂O, the H–O–H angle is 104.5° (two lone pairs compress it more).

Molecular Shape and Molecular Polarity

  • A molecule is polar if it has a net dipole moment (μ ≠ 0). This occurs when bond dipoles do not cancel.
  • Shape is crucial: CO₂ has polar C=O bonds, but the linear shape cancels the dipoles, making it non-polar. H₂O has polar O–H bonds and a bent shape, so it is polar.
  • Symmetry determines polarity. In tetrahedral CCl₄, the four polar C–Cl bonds are symmetrically arranged, dipoles cancel, μ = 0. In CHCl₃, the substitution of one Cl by H breaks the symmetry, giving a net dipole moment.
  • Cis–trans isomers: cis-1,2-dichloroethene is polar (μ = 1.89 D), trans-1,2-dichloroethene is non-polar (μ = 0 D) because the C–Cl dipoles cancel in the trans isomer.

Intermolecular Forces

  • Intermolecular forces are attractive forces between molecules. They are much weaker than intramolecular chemical bonds but determine physical properties like melting and boiling points.
  • Three main types exist between neutral molecules: London dispersion forces, dipole–dipole forces, and hydrogen bonding. Van der Waals forces is a general term that includes dipole–dipole and London forces.

1. Dipole–Dipole Forces

  • Occur between polar molecules that have permanent dipoles. The partially positive end of one molecule attracts the partially negative end of another.
  • These are stronger than London forces but weaker than ion–ion forces.
  • Example: HCl molecules attract each other through dipole–dipole forces.

2. Hydrogen Bonding

  • A particularly strong type of dipole–dipole attraction. It occurs when a hydrogen atom is covalently bonded to a highly electronegative atom—specifically nitrogen (N), oxygen (O), or fluorine (F)—and this hydrogen interacts with a lone pair on an N, O, or F atom of another molecule.
  • Represented by a dotted line.
  • Hydrogen bonds are the strongest intermolecular forces between neutral molecules.
  • They strongly affect properties: e.g., water’s high boiling point compared to similar molecules, the structure of ice, the shape of proteins and DNA.
  • Example: H₂O, NH₃, HF exhibit hydrogen bonding.

3. London Dispersion Forces (London Forces)

  • Present in all molecules (polar and non-polar). They arise from instantaneous, temporary dipoles that occur due to the random movement of electrons.
  • An instantaneous dipole in one molecule can induce a dipole in a neighboring molecule, causing a weak attraction.
  • Dispersion forces increase with molecular size (molar mass) because larger electron clouds are more polarizable.
  • For molecules of similar molar mass, shape matters: molecules with greater surface area have stronger dispersion forces. For example, n-pentane (linear) has a higher boiling point than its branched isomer neopentane (2,2-dimethylpropane).

Summary of Covalent Compounds: Intermolecular Forces and Properties

  • The physical state (solid, liquid, gas) depends on the strength of intermolecular forces.
  • Substances with only London forces (e.g., noble gases, non-polar molecules) generally have low melting and boiling points.
  • Polar molecules have higher boiling points than non-polar molecules of comparable molar mass because of additional dipole–dipole forces.
  • Hydrogen-bonded substances have unusually high melting and boiling points for their molar mass.

2.4 Metallic Bonding

Electron-Sea Model

  • Metallic bonding is the sharing of delocalized electrons among a lattice of positively charged metal ions.
  • The model pictures a metal as an array of metal cations immersed in a “sea” of valence electrons.
  • The valence electrons are not bound to any specific atom but are free to move throughout the entire solid.
  • This model explains many properties of metals.

Properties of Metals and Their Explanation

  • Electrical conductivity: The delocalized electrons can move freely, carrying an electric current. This occurs even in the solid state.
  • Thermal conductivity: Mobile electrons transfer kinetic energy (heat) rapidly.
  • Malleability and ductility: Metals can be hammered into sheets (malleable) and drawn into wires (ductile). When a stress is applied, the layers of metal ions can slide past each other without breaking the metallic bonds because the electron sea readjusts.
  • Lustre (shiny): The free electrons absorb and re-emit light of many wavelengths.
  • Melting and boiling points: Metals generally have moderate to high melting points. The attraction between cations and the electron sea is strong. Boiling points are very high because atoms and their electrons must separate completely.
    • Example: Gallium melts at 29.8 °C but boils at 2403 °C.
    • Group IIA metals have higher melting points than Group IA metals because of greater nuclear charge (+2 vs +1) and more delocalized electrons per atom, leading to stronger bonds.

How Metallic Bonding Differs from Ionic and Covalent Bonding

  • In ionic bonding, electrons are transferred and ions are held by electrostatic forces. In covalent bonding, electron pairs are localized between two atoms.
  • In metallic bonding, electrons are delocalized over many atoms. The bond is non-directional and the strength depends on the number of valence electrons and the packing arrangement.

2.5 Chemical Bonding Theories

Valence Bond (VB) Theory

  • The basic principle: a covalent bond forms when atomic orbitals of two atoms overlap. The overlap region, between the nuclei, is occupied by a pair of electrons with opposite spins.
  • The greater the overlap, the stronger (more stable) the bond.
  • Overlap of two s orbitals or an s and a p orbital occurs end-to-end (head-on), forming a sigma (σ) bond. In a σ bond, the electron density is concentrated along the internuclear axis.
  • p orbitals can also overlap sideways (laterally) to form a pi (π) bond. In a π bond, the electron density is concentrated above and below the internuclear axis.
  • A single bond is always a σ bond.
  • A double bond consists of one σ bond and one π bond.
  • A triple bond consists of one σ bond and two π bonds.
  • Sigma bonds are stronger than pi bonds, because head-on overlap is more extensive than side-on overlap.

Hybridization of Atomic Orbitals

  • Hybridization is the mixing of atomic orbitals on the same atom to form new hybrid orbitals of equal energy and identical shape.
  • The number of hybrid orbitals equals the number of atomic orbitals mixed.
  • Hybrid orbitals have specific orientations that correspond to the molecular shapes predicted by VSEPR.

Key hybridization schemes:

  1. sp hybridization: One s and one p orbital mix → two sp hybrid orbitals.
    • Orientation: linear, 180° angle.
    • Example: BeCl₂ (linear shape). Central Be atom undergoes sp hybridization.
  2. sp² hybridization: One s and two p orbitals mix → three sp² hybrid orbitals.
    • Orientation: trigonal planar, 120° angles.
    • Example: BCl₃. Boron uses sp² hybrids.
  3. sp³ hybridization: One s and three p orbitals mix → four sp³ hybrid orbitals.
    • Orientation: tetrahedral, 109.5° angles.
    • Examples: CH₄, NH₃, H₂O. The central atom uses sp³ hybrids. The lone pairs in NH₃ and H₂O occupy hybrid orbitals, leading to pyramidal and bent shapes respectively.
  4. sp³d hybridization: One s, three p and one d orbital mix → five sp³d hybrid orbitals.
    • Orientation: trigonal bipyramidal.
    • Example: PCl₅. Phosphorus uses sp³d hybrids. Note that axial bonds are slightly longer and weaker than equatorial bonds.
  5. sp³d² hybridization: One s, three p and two d orbitals mix → six sp³d² hybrid orbitals.
    • Orientation: octahedral.
    • Example: SF₆. Sulfur uses sp³d² hybrids.

Hybridization in Molecules with Multiple Bonds:

  • In ethene (C₂H₄), each carbon is sp² hybridized. The three sp² hybrids form σ bonds (two C–H and one C–C σ bond). The remaining unhybridized p orbital on each carbon overlaps sideways to form a π bond, creating the double bond.
  • In acetylene (C₂H₂), each carbon is sp hybridized. The two sp hybrids form σ bonds (one C–H and one C–C σ bond). The two unhybridized p orbitals on each carbon form two π bonds, giving a triple bond.
  • Rotation around a double or triple bond is restricted because it would break the π bond overlap.

Molecular Orbital (MO) Theory

  • In MO theory, atomic orbitals of the bonded atoms combine to form new orbitals called molecular orbitals (MOs). These MOs belong to the whole molecule.
  • The number of MOs formed equals the number of atomic orbitals combined.
  • Two types of MOs:
    • Bonding molecular orbital: Lower in energy than the original atomic orbitals. Electron density is concentrated between the nuclei, stabilizing the molecule.
    • *Antibonding molecular orbital (denoted with ): Higher in energy. There is a node (zero electron density) between the nuclei, which leads to repulsion.
  • For H₂: two 1s atomic orbitals combine to form one σ₁s (bonding) and one σ*₁s (antibonding) MO. Both electrons occupy the lower-energy σ₁s orbital. The bond order = ½ (2 – 0) = 1, indicating a single bond.

Electron Configurations of Diatomic Molecules:

  • Electrons fill MOs following the Aufbau principle, Pauli exclusion principle, and Hund’s rule.
  • The order of MO energy levels for homonuclear diatomic molecules (total electrons ≤ 14) is:
    σ₁s < σ₁s < σ₂s < σ₂s < π₂py = π₂pz < σ₂px < π₂py = π₂pz < σ*₂px
  • For molecules with more than 14 electrons (e.g., O₂, F₂, Ne₂), the order changes: σ₂px is lower than π₂py and π₂pz. So the sequence is:
    σ₁s < σ₁s < σ₂s < σ₂s < σ₂px < π₂py = π₂pz < π₂py = π₂pz < σ*₂px.
  • Example: O₂ (16 electrons). The configuration is:
    (σ₁s)² (σ₁s)² (σ₂s)² (σ₂s)² (σ₂px)² (π₂py)² (π₂pz)² (π₂py)¹ (π₂pz)¹.
    Bond order = ½ (8 bonding electrons – 4 antibonding electrons) = 2, consistent with a double bond. The two unpaired electrons in the π* orbitals explain oxygen’s paramagnetism.

Bond Order:
Bond order = ½ (number of electrons in bonding MOs – number of electrons in antibonding MOs).

  • A bond order > 0 indicates a stable molecule; bond order 0 means the molecule is unstable (e.g., He₂).
  • Higher bond order generally means a stronger and shorter bond.

Magnetic Properties:

  • If a molecule has unpaired electrons, it is paramagnetic (attracted to a magnetic field).
  • If all electrons are paired, it is diamagnetic (weakly repelled by a magnetic field).
  • MO theory correctly predicts that O₂ is paramagnetic (two unpaired electrons), whereas VB theory’s O=O would suggest all electrons paired.

2.6 Types of Crystals

Solids can be crystalline (ordered structure) or amorphous (disordered, e.g., glass). Crystalline solids are classified into four types based on the particles present and the attractive forces.

1. Ionic Crystals

  • Structural units: cations and anions held together by strong ionic bonds.
  • High melting points, hard and brittle.
  • Do not conduct electricity as solids. Conduct when molten or dissolved, as ions become mobile.
  • Examples: NaCl, CsCl, MgO.

2. Molecular Crystals

  • Structural units: molecules (or monatomic atoms) held together by intermolecular forces (dispersion, dipole–dipole, hydrogen bonding).
  • Low melting points, soft, brittle, often volatile.
  • Non-conductors of electricity in any state.
  • Solubility varies depending on polarity.
  • Examples: ice (H₂O), naphthalene, sucrose, iodine, solid CO₂.

3. Covalent Network Crystals

  • Structural units: atoms linked by strong covalent bonds in an extended three-dimensional network. There are no separate molecules; the whole crystal is a single giant molecule.
  • Very high melting points, extremely hard.
  • Usually non-conductors (except graphite, which conducts electricity due to delocalized electrons within layers).
  • Examples: diamond (C), silicon dioxide (SiO₂, quartz), silicon carbide (SiC). Graphite is also a network covalent solid with layered structure.

4. Metallic Crystals

  • Structural units: metal cations immersed in a “sea” of delocalized valence electrons. Bonding is metallic.
  • Wide range of melting points (generally high) and hardness.
  • Good conductors of heat and electricity in all states.
  • Malleable, ductile, lustrous.
  • Examples: Cu, Al, Fe, Na, Ag.

Unit 2 Key Terms and Definitions

  • Anion: A negatively charged ion.
  • Atomic orbital: A region of space around the nucleus where an electron is likely to be found.
  • Covalent bond: A chemical bond formed by sharing a pair of electrons between two atoms.
  • Crystal: A solid with a regular, repeating three-dimensional arrangement of its particles.
  • Delocalization of electrons: The spreading of electron density over several atoms rather than between two specific atoms.
  • Dipole: A separation of positive and negative charges in a molecule.
  • Dipole moment (μ): A quantitative measure of molecular polarity (μ = δ × d).
  • Double bond: A covalent bond consisting of one sigma and one pi bond (two shared pairs of electrons).
  • Electric charge: A property of matter responsible for electrical interactions.
  • Ionic bond: Electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions.
  • Ionization: The process of removing an electron from an atom or ion.
  • Kinetic energy: The energy of motion.
  • Lattice energy (U): The enthalpy change when one mole of an ionic solid separates into its gaseous ions.
  • London dispersion forces: Very weak intermolecular attractions arising from temporary, induced dipoles.
  • Molecule: A group of atoms held together by covalent bonds.
  • Molecular orbital (MO): An allowed energy state for an electron in a molecule, formed by the combination of atomic orbitals.
  • Noble gases: Group 18 elements, characterized by complete valence shells and chemical inertness.
  • Potential energy: Stored energy due to position or composition.
  • Single bond: A covalent bond consisting of one shared electron pair (a sigma bond).
  • Substance: A form of matter with a definite composition and properties.
  • Triple bond: A covalent bond consisting of one sigma and two pi bonds (three shared pairs of electrons).
  • Valence bond theory: A theory that explains covalent bonding as the overlap of atomic orbitals to form a bond.
  • Valence electron: An electron in the outermost shell of an atom, involved in bonding.
  • Van der Waals forces: A general term for intermolecular forces that include dipole–dipole interactions and London dispersion forces.

Comprehensive Exam Summary (Medium-Length Bulleted)

  • A chemical bond is an attractive force holding particles together. The octet rule states that atoms tend to attain eight valence electrons.
  • Ionic bonds form by electron transfer from a metal to a non-metal, creating cations and anions held by electrostatic forces. Lattice energy measures the strength of ionic bonds.
  • The Born–Haber cycle uses Hess’s law to calculate lattice energy from other thermochemical data.
  • Ionic bonds are favoured by low ionization energy of the metal, high electron affinity of the non-metal, and high lattice energy.
  • Exceptions to the octet rule in ionic bonding include small ions with duplets (Li⁺, Be²⁺) and transition metal ions with 18 electrons in the valence shell.
  • Ionic compounds are hard, brittle, high-melting solids that conduct electricity only when molten or in solution.
  • Covalent bonds form by sharing electron pairs. Lewis structures show the arrangement of valence electrons, with dots representing electrons.
  • Coordinate covalent bonds occur when one atom donates both electrons.
  • Resonance structures represent delocalized bonding in molecules like O₃ and CO₃²⁻.
  • Exceptions to the octet rule in covalent molecules include electron-deficient species (BF₃), expanded octets (PCl₅, SF₆), and odd-electron molecules (NO, NO₂).
  • Bond polarity arises from differences in electronegativity. Molecular polarity depends on both bond polarity and molecular shape (dipole moment).
  • Covalent compounds generally have low melting/boiling points, are soft, and do not conduct electricity.
  • VSEPR theory predicts molecular shape by minimizing electron pair repulsions. Number of electron groups gives the electron pair arrangement, and lone pairs modify the molecular shape.
  • Intermolecular forces (London, dipole–dipole, hydrogen bonding) determine physical properties of molecular substances. Hydrogen bonding is especially strong and affects water, DNA, etc.
  • Metallic bonding is the delocalization of valence electrons in a lattice of cations, explaining conductivity, malleability, and lustre.
  • Valence bond theory describes bond formation by orbital overlap (σ and π bonds). Hybridization (sp, sp², sp³, sp³d, sp³d²) explains molecular shapes.
  • Molecular orbital theory combines atomic orbitals to form bonding and antibonding MOs. Bond order indicates bond strength and stability. The theory explains the paramagnetism of O₂.
  • Crystalline solids are classified as ionic, molecular, covalent network, or metallic, based on the type of particle and bonding. This classification explains their macroscopic properties.

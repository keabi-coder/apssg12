COMPLETE DEFINITIONS, CLASSIFICATIONS & KEY POINTS – UNITS 1–6

UNIT 1: BIOLOGY AND TECHNOLOGY

Definitions

  • Imitation – the design and adaptation of technologies by copying natural working systems.
  • Biology – is called the study of life; the structures, functions, growth, origins, evolution and distribution of living organisms.
  • Technology – is called the application of scientific knowledge, skills, methods and processes for the production of devices and tools for scientific investigations.
  • Biotechnology – is called the blend (coupling) of biology and technology; a technology that utilises biological systems to develop useful products.
  • Ethical issues in biology – are called concerns about the rightness or wrongness of using biological discoveries for human health and wellbeing.
  • Ethical treatment of plants and animals – is called the process of subjecting animals and plants to experiments and rigorous tests while considering their moral status.

Benefits of biology to technology (key points)

  1. Medicinal plants – biochemical substances used to develop artificial medicines.
  2. Gene engineering and biosynthetic materials – imitating genes to cure genetic conditions and repair tissues.
  3. Building design – imitating termite mound architecture for temperature‑controlled buildings.
  4. Bullet train design – nose shape copied from kingfisher beak.

Uses of technology in biology (biological study areas)

  • Biochemical studies, biomedical studies, biophysical studies, environmental studies, bioinformatics, biogeographical information.
  • Technological devices: digital thermometer, pregnancy urine test (detects HCG), diabetic blood test, HIV test, microscope, CT scan, PET scan, GPS, handheld body fat calculator.

Impacts of biology and technology

  • Impacts – are called factors that pose positive or negative effects on society and the natural world.
  • Positive impacts of biology: food security, improved health, clean energy/water, antibiotics, bio‑based devices.
  • Negative impacts: biological weapons, sex‑chromosome selection.
  • Negative impacts of technology: pollution (air, water), depletion of natural resources, toxicity from pesticides/fertilisers.

Ethical treatment – key points

  • Unethical plant treatments: destructive testing, deforestation, burning forests, restructuring trees for aesthetics.
  • Unethical animal treatments: experiments causing injury or pain.
  • Three Rs principle:
    • Reduction – minimise number of animals used.
    • Refinement – reduce suffering through better techniques and care.
    • Replacement – use cell cultures, biosynthesis, computer models instead of live animals.

UNIT 2: ANIMALS

Definitions

  • Animals – are called eukaryotic, multicellular, heterotrophic organisms that reproduce, protect themselves, move, respire, excrete, grow, and have body symmetry.
  • Vertebrates – animals with a backbone/vertebral column.
  • Invertebrates – animals without a backbone.
  • Homeothermic (warm‑blooded) animals – animals that maintain a constant internal body temperature regardless of the environment (e.g., mammals, birds).
  • Poikilothermic (cold‑blooded) animals – animals whose body temperature changes with the environmental temperature (e.g., fish, reptiles, amphibians).
  • Reproduction – is called the process by which living organisms duplicate themselves to ensure species continuity.
  • Asexual reproduction – reproduction involving a single parent; no fusion of gametes.
  • Sexual reproduction – reproduction involving two parents and fusion of gametes.
  • Fertilisation – the fusion of sperm and egg to form a diploid zygote.
  • Metamorphosis – the series of major body‑structure changes some animals undergo after hatching.
  • Animal behaviour – all the ways animals interact with other organisms and the physical environment.
  • Homeostasis – the self‑regulatory process by which animals maintain stable internal conditions despite external changes.
  • Thermoregulation – the process of maintaining constant internal body temperature.
  • Osmoregulation – the process that regulates osmotic pressure of body fluids and electrolyte balance.
  • Feedback mechanism – a physiological regulation system that returns the body to its normal internal state.

Classifications and Types

Invertebrate groups: porifera, platyhelminthes, cnidaria, arthropoda, annelida, echinodermata, mollusca.
Vertebrate groups: fish, amphibians, reptiles, birds, mammals.

Reproduction types

  • Asexual reproduction forms: budding, fragmentation, parthenogenesis.
  • Types of fertilisation: external fertilisation (e.g., frogs), internal fertilisation (e.g., crocodiles, birds, rats).
  • Metamorphosis types:
    • Complete metamorphosis – 4 stages: egg → larva → pupa → adult (e.g., honeybee, butterfly).
    • Incomplete metamorphosis – 3 stages: egg → nymph → adult (e.g., grasshopper).

Animal behaviour types

  • Innate (inherent) behaviour – inborn, genetically determined, independent of experience.
    • Instinct – e.g., web making in spiders, nest building, honeybee dance.
    • Reflex – sudden, involuntary response (e.g., pulling hand from heat, blinking).
    • Orientation:
      • Taxis – directed movement toward/away from a stimulus; positive taxis (toward), negative taxis (away). Types: phototaxis, chemotaxis, thigmotaxis, geotaxis.
      • Kinesis – undirected, random movement in response to stimulus intensity.
  • Learned (acquired) behaviour – not inherited, acquired through experience.
    • Habituation – stopping response to a repeated harmless stimulus.
    • Classical conditioning – associative learning discovered by Pavlov; stages: before, during, after conditioning.
    • Operant conditioning – learning by rewards/punishments (Skinner); operants: neutral, reinforcers, punishers.
    • Insight learning – solving problems using past experience and reasoning.
    • Sensitisation – amplified response after repeated exposure to a stimulus.

Behavioural patterns:

  • Behavioural cycles – seasonal migration, circadian rhythms (24‑hour biological clock).
  • Reproductive behaviour – courtship, mate selection, parental care.
  • Social behaviour – group living, division of labour (ants, bees, elephants).
  • Competition – intraspecific (same species), interspecific (different species).
  • Territoriality – defending a space through display behaviour, pheromones.
  • Communication – via sight, sound, touch, chemicals (pheromones).

Homeostasis components: stimulus, receptor, control centre (hypothalamus), effector.

Thermoregulation strategies:

  • Poikilotherms – use behavioural methods (basking, burrowing, group activity).
  • Homeotherms – use morphological, physiological, behavioural methods:
    • Cold: insulation (fur, fat, feathers), vasoconstriction, shivering.
    • Hot: vasodilation, sweating, behavioural shade‑seeking.

Osmoregulation types:

  • Osmoconformers – match body osmolarity to environment (starfish, jellyfish).
  • Osmoregulators – actively regulate osmotic pressure independent of environment (vertebrates).

Blood sugar regulation: insulin (lowers glucose, promotes glycogen storage), glucagon (raises glucose, breaks down glycogen).

Feedback mechanisms:

  • Negative feedback – reverses a change, returns to set point (e.g., temperature, glucose regulation).
  • Positive feedback – amplifies the change (e.g., childbirth, oxytocin; also menstrual cycle ovulation surge).

Economic Importance of Insects

Beneficial aspects

  • Agriculture: pollination (35% crop production), pest regulation (natural biological control – ladybirds, mantis, blister beetles).
  • Food: edible insects as sustainable protein source.
  • Industry: honey/beeswax, silk, shellac, cochineal pigment, tannic acid.
  • Health/Medicine: honey for wounds, bee/ant venom for joint pain, cantharidan for viral infections/cancer.

Harmful aspects

  • Plant pests (locusts, aphids), stored grain pests (weevils, borers), disease vectors (Anopheles – malaria, Culex – filariasis, tsetse fly – sleeping sickness, housefly – food/water diseases).

UNIT 3: ENZYMES

Definitions

  • Enzyme – is called a protein biochemical catalyst that speeds up chemical reactions without being consumed or affecting the nature of products.
  • Denaturation – the process of breaking non‑covalent bonds that distort the enzyme’s shape and active site (caused by high heat, extreme pH, heavy metals).
  • Biocatalyst property – very small amount of enzyme converts a large quantity of substrate and remains unchanged.
  • Turnover number – the number of substrate molecules converted by one enzyme molecule per second when the active site is saturated.
  • Enzyme specificity – the property describing how restrictive an enzyme is in its choice of substrate.
  • Protein structure – the three‑dimensional arrangement of atoms in an amino acid chain (polymer of amino acids joined by peptide bonds).
  • Enzyme‑substrate models – models describing how the active site and substrate interact.
  • Enzyme regulation – a control system that turns enzymes “on” or “off” according to the organism’s needs.
  • Effectors – small molecules (inhibitor or activator) that modulate enzyme activity by binding to the allosteric site.
  • Enzyme inhibition – a decrease in enzyme activity caused by inhibitor molecules.
  • Enzyme kinetics – the study of the rates of enzyme‑catalysed reactions and binding affinities of substrates/inhibitors.
  • Malting – the process of steeping, germinating, and drying grain to convert it into malt.
  • Biochemist – a scientist who studies chemical processes and transformations in living organisms.

Properties of Enzymes

Physical properties: denaturation, solubility, colloidal nature (large size, no dialysis), biocatalyst, precipitation, high molecular weight, enzymatic activity (depends on temperature, pH, concentration).
Chemical properties: heat/pH sensitivity, regulation, catalysis (10³–10⁸ times faster), reversibility (both anabolic and catabolic directions), specificity.

Types of enzyme specificity:

  • Bond specificity – specific for a bond type.
  • Group specificity – specific for a chemical group.
  • Substrate specificity – acts only on a particular substrate.
  • Optical specificity – recognises optical/stereochemical configuration.
  • Co‑factor specificity – requires specific co‑factors.

Protein Structures

  1. Primary structure – linear sequence of amino acids linked by peptide bonds.
  2. Secondary structure – local folding: α‑helix (right‑handed coil, H‑bonds every 4 residues) and β‑pleated sheet (side‑by‑side strands, inter‑strand H‑bonds).
  3. Tertiary structure – overall 3‑D shape of a single polypeptide, stabilised by side‑chain interactions.
  4. Quaternary structure – assembly of multiple polypeptide subunits (e.g., haemoglobin).

Enzyme‑Substrate Models

  • Lock and key model – the active site is a rigid shape perfectly complementary to the substrate.
  • Induced fit model – the active site changes shape upon substrate binding, moulding around the substrate; may strain bonds and lower activation energy.

Transition state sequence: E + S ⇄ ES ⇄ ES* ⇄ EP ⇄ E + P (ES* = activated complex).

Enzyme Regulation

  • Allosteric enzymes – have an active site (catalytic) and an allosteric site (regulatory); effectors bind to allosteric site and cause conformational change.
  • Covalent modification – addition or removal of chemical groups (e.g., phosphorylation) switches enzymes between active/inactive forms.
  • Enzyme inhibition types:
    • Irreversible inhibitor – permanently blocks the enzyme.
    • Reversible inhibitor – non‑covalent, reversible binding.
      • Competitive inhibitor – competes for the active site; can be overcome by excess substrate.
      • Non‑competitive inhibitor – binds elsewhere, changing active site shape; not overcome by excess substrate.
      • Uncompetitive inhibitor – binds only to the enzyme‑substrate complex; slows reaction.

Types of Enzymes

Structural classification:

  • Simple protein (active enzyme).
  • Conjugated protein (holoenzyme) = apoenzyme (protein part, inactive) + cofactor (non‑protein part).
    • Cofactor can be:
      • Prosthetic group – firmly attached metal ion.
      • Coenzyme – loosely attached, often vitamin B‑derived.

Functional classification (6 classes, suffix “‑ase”):

  1. Oxidoreductases – catalyse oxidation‑reduction (electron transfer).
  2. Transferases – transfer functional groups (e.g., methyl).
  3. Hydrolases – catalyse hydrolysis of bonds.
  4. Lyases – cleave bonds by means other than hydrolysis/oxidation.
  5. Isomerases – convert molecules between isomers.
  6. Ligases – join two molecules, coupled with ATP hydrolysis.

Factors Affecting Enzyme Activity

  • Temperature (optimum ~37 °C in humans; high heat denatures irreversibly)
  • pH (optimum; extremes denature)
  • Substrate concentration (saturation at Vmax)
  • Enzyme concentration
  • Inhibitors (decrease activity)
  • Activators (increase activity)
  • Radiation, water, end‑product (feedback) inhibition (end product binds allosteric site, blocking enzyme)

Applications of Enzymes

  • Biological detergents (protease, amylase, lipase, mannanase)
  • Food processing (papain, rennin, pectinase, trypsin)
  • Brewing (β‑glucanase)
  • Biofuel (cellulase, ligninase)
  • Molecular biology (nuclease, DNA ligase, polymerase)
  • Pharmaceuticals, animal feed, environmental cleaning.

Malting Steps

  1. Steeping – soaking grain in water to activate enzymes (24–48 h).
  2. Germinating – growth and modification; enzymes (α‑amylase, β‑amylase) break down starch and protein (4–6 days).
  3. Kilning – heating to dry the grain, stop germination, develop colour/flavour.
  • Purpose: produce enzymes to convert starch into fermentable sugars for brewing (traditional Ethiopian drinks: Tella, Booka, Cheka, etc.).

UNIT 4: GENETICS

Definitions

  • Genetics – is called the branch of biology that studies genetic materials and how traits are passed from one generation to the next through genes.
  • Genetic material – material that carries genetic information and passes it to the next generation (DNA in most organisms; RNA in some viruses).
  • DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid) – is called the hereditary material; a double‑helix polymer of nucleotides.
  • RNA (ribonucleic acid) – a single‑stranded nucleic acid involved in protein synthesis; genetic material in some viruses.
  • Gene – is called a section of DNA that contains instructions for a characteristic; the basic unit of inheritance.
  • Chromosome – a thread‑like structure made of DNA and histone proteins; genes are located on chromosomes.
  • Locus – the specific position of a gene on a chromosome.
  • DNA replication – is called the process by which DNA makes a copy of itself; it is semi‑conservative.
  • Central dogma – the flow of genetic information: DNA → RNA → Protein.
  • Transcription – the synthesis of mRNA from a DNA template.
  • Translation – the synthesis of protein from mRNA, with the help of tRNA and ribosomes.
  • Codon – a sequence of three nucleotides on mRNA that specifies a particular amino acid.
  • Anticodon – a three‑nucleotide sequence on tRNA complementary to an mRNA codon.
  • Mendelian inheritance – patterns of inheritance controlled by a single gene with two alternative alleles.
  • Allele – an alternative form of a gene.
  • Dominant allele – expressed when present; masks the recessive allele.
  • Recessive allele – expressed only when two copies are present.
  • Genotype – the genetic makeup of an organism.
  • Phenotype – the physical expression of the genotype.
  • Homozygous – having two identical alleles for a trait (TT or tt).
  • Heterozygous – having two different alleles for a trait (Tt).
  • Test cross – crossing an unknown dominant phenotype with a homozygous recessive to determine the unknown genotype.
  • Law of segregation – states that two alleles for a trait separate during gamete formation; each gamete receives only one allele.
  • Law of independent assortment – states that genes for different traits are inherited independently of each other.
  • Co‑dominance – both alleles are expressed equally in the heterozygote.
  • Incomplete dominance – the heterozygote shows an intermediate (blended) phenotype.
  • Multiple alleles – a gene that exists in more than two allelic forms (e.g., ABO blood group: Iᴬ, Iᴮ, i).
  • Sex‑linked inheritance – traits controlled by genes located on sex chromosomes, especially the X chromosome.
  • Phenocopy – an environmentally induced phenotype that mimics a genetically determined trait.
  • Pedigree – a family tree diagram showing the inheritance pattern of a trait.
  • Genetic disorder – a disease caused by a change or mutation in an individual’s DNA.
  • Genetic testing – examines DNA to detect genetic disorders.
  • Genetic counselling – process of evaluating family history and genetic tests to help understand risks and make informed decisions.
  • Gene therapy – the technique of introducing genes into a patient’s cells to treat or prevent disease.
  • Breeding – the application of genetic principles in animal husbandry and agriculture to improve desirable traits.
  • Selective breeding (artificial selection) – choosing parents with desired traits to produce the next generation.
  • Bioinformatics – an interdisciplinary field combining biology, computer science, and IT to store, organise, and analyse biological data.

DNA Structure and Replication

  • Nucleotide: sugar (deoxyribose), phosphate, nitrogenous base.
  • Purines: Adenine (A), Guanine (G). Pyrimidines: Thymine (T), Cytosine (C). Base pairing: A‑T, C‑G.
  • Replication enzymes: helicase (unwinds), DNA polymerase (adds nucleotides), primase (RNA primers), ligase (joins fragments), topoisomerase (prevents supercoiling).
  • Leading strand (continuous, 5′→3′), lagging strand (discontinuous, Okazaki fragments).

RNA Types and Protein Synthesis

  • mRNA – carries genetic code; tRNA – brings amino acids (anticodon); rRNA – ribosomal component.
  • Start codon: AUG; Stop codons: UAG, UAA, UGA.
  • Transcription: in nucleus; translation: in cytoplasm on ribosomes.

Mendelian Crosses

  • Monohybrid cross: F2 phenotypic ratio 3:1, genotypic 1:2:1.
  • Dihybrid cross: F2 phenotypic ratio 9:3:3:1.
  • Test cross: cross with homozygous recessive; if all offspring dominant → unknown is homozygous; if 1:1 → heterozygous.

Sex Determination

  • Humans: XX female, XY male. Male determines sex of offspring.

Non‑Mendelian Inheritance

  • Co‑dominance example: roan cattle (RW).
  • Incomplete dominance example: pink snapdragon flowers.
  • ABO blood types: A (IᴬIᴬ or Iᴬi), B (IᴮIᴮ or Iᴮi), AB (IᴬIᴮ), O (ii). Antigens and antibodies.
  • Rh factor: Rh⁺ (has D antigen), Rh⁻ (no antigen); Rh sensitisation in pregnancy.

Genetic Disorders Categories

  • Single‑gene disorders:
    • Autosomal dominant (Huntington’s disease)
    • Autosomal recessive (cystic fibrosis, albinism)
    • X‑linked (haemophilia)
  • Chromosomal disorders: aneuploidy (Down syndrome – trisomy 21), deletion, inversion, translocation.
  • Multifactorial disorders: diabetes, cancer.

Gene Therapy Types

  • Somatic gene therapy – affects body cells; not inherited.
  • Germline gene therapy – affects sex cells; inherited.

Breeding Methods

  • Inbreeding – mating genetically similar individuals; increases homozygosity.
  • Crossbreeding – mating individuals from different breeds; hybrid vigour.
  • Plant breeding: mass selection, pure line selection, bulk method.
  • Indigenous Ethiopian knowledge: farmers select for growth, disease resistance, adaptation.

UNIT 5: HUMAN BODY SYSTEMS

Definitions

  • Musculoskeletal system – is called the organ system that provides shape, support, protection, and movement; composed of bones, muscles, cartilage, tendons, ligaments, joints.
  • Cardiac muscle – involuntary striated muscle of the heart wall (myocardium).
  • Skeletal muscle – voluntary striated muscle attached to bones; enables body movement.
  • Smooth muscle – involuntary non‑striated muscle found in walls of hollow organs.
  • Tendon – fibrous connective tissue connecting muscle to bone.
  • Ligament – fibrous connective tissue attaching bone to bone.
  • Joint – the part of the body where two or more bones meet.
  • Actin – thin filament protein in muscle; contains myosin‑binding sites.
  • Myosin – thick filament motor protein that converts ATP energy into mechanical force.
  • Troponin – regulatory protein complex that binds calcium, enabling contraction.
  • Tropomyosin – protein that blocks myosin‑binding sites on actin in a relaxed muscle.
  • Gametogenesis – the process of formation and development of gametes (sperm/egg) from germ cells.
  • Spermatogenesis – the process of sperm formation in seminiferous tubules of the testis.
  • Oogenesis – the development of female egg cells in the ovaries.
  • Menstrual cycle – the monthly process of discharge of blood and tissue through the vagina from puberty to menopause (except during pregnancy).
  • Fertilisation – union of sperm and egg (23 chromosomes each) in the fallopian tube to form a diploid zygote.
  • Pregnancy (gestation) – the period of foetal development inside the uterus; lasts about 40 weeks.
  • Contraception – the intentional prevention of pregnancy.
  • Infertility – the inability of a person to reproduce naturally.
  • Sexually transmitted infections (STIs) – communicable diseases primarily transmitted through sexual contact.
  • Epidemiology – the study of the transmission, causation, outbreak, surveillance, and monitoring of a disease.
  • Family planning – the ability of individuals/couples to anticipate and attain their desired number of children and timing/spacing of births.
  • Harmful traditional practices – actions against the rights of people that affect physical and mental health, such as early marriage, FGM.

Types of Muscles

  1. Cardiac muscle – involuntary, striated, heart only; intercalated discs.
  2. Skeletal muscle – voluntary, striated, attached to bones; motor unit: single motor axon + fibres innervated.
  3. Smooth muscle – involuntary, non‑striated; hollow organs, blood vessels, eyes.

Muscle Contraction Types

  • Isometric – tension without length change.
  • Concentric – muscle shortens.
  • Eccentric – muscle lengthens.

Skeleton Divisions

  • Axial skeleton (80 bones): skull, ossicles, hyoid, thoracic cage, vertebral column.
  • Appendicular skeleton (126 bones): shoulder girdles, pelvis, arms, legs, hands, feet.

Joint Types

  • Ball‑and‑socket (shoulder, hip), hinge (elbow, knee), condyloid (fingers, jaw), pivot (radius‑ulna, neck), gliding (wrist, ankle), saddle (thumb base).

Reproductive System Structures

  • Male: testes (sperm, testosterone), epididymis, vas deferens, seminal vesicles (fructose), prostate gland (alkaline buffer), Cowper’s glands, urethra, penis.
  • Female: ovaries (eggs, oestrogen, progesterone), fallopian tubes, fimbriae, uterus, cervix, vagina, vulva.

Gametogenesis

  • Spermatogenesis: diploid spermatogonia → primary spermatocyte → meiosis I → secondary spermatocytes → meiosis II → spermatids → spermatozoa.
  • Oogenesis: oogonium → primary oocyte (arrested in prophase I) → secondary oocyte + first polar body → ovum + second polar body after fertilisation.

Menstrual Cycle Phases

  • Menstrual (days 1–4), Follicular (5–13), Ovulation (14), Luteal (15–28).
  • Hormones: FSH, LH, oestrogen, progesterone.
  • Positive feedback (oestrogen surge → LH surge → ovulation); negative feedback (progesterone inhibits FSH/LH; drop causes menstruation).

Contraceptive Methods

  • Barrier: male/female condom, diaphragm, cervical cap.
  • Non‑barrier: spermicides, oral contraceptive pills (COC, POP), injectables (Depo‑Provera), implants, IUD (copper/hormonal), vaginal ring, post‑pill (emergency), sterilisation (tubal ligation, vasectomy).

Infertility Causes

  • Female: ovulatory disorders, thyroid problems, tubal blockage, fibroids, endometriosis, PID, drugs/alcohol.
  • Male: low sperm count, abnormal sperm, blockages, varicocele, hormonal imbalances, lifestyle factors.

STI Classification

  • Bacterial: bacterial vaginosis, chlamydia, syphilis, gonorrhoea, chancroid.
  • Viral: genital herpes (HSV), genital warts (HPV), molluscum contagiosum, HIV/AIDS, hepatitis B.
  • Parasitic: pubic lice (crabs), trichomoniasis, scabies.
  • HIV stages: acute (flu‑like), chronic (asymptomatic latency), AIDS (CD4 <200 cells/mm³).

Harmful Traditional Practices

  • Early marriage, abduction, gender‑based violence, Female Genital Mutilation (FGM).

Effects of Substances

  • Alcohol: brain damage, liver cirrhosis, risky sex, STI/HIV transmission.
  • Khat (cathinone): hypertension, heart attack, gastritis, impotence, oral cancer.
  • Drugs (marijuana, cocaine, heroin, inhalants): cardiovascular, respiratory, neurological damage; increased risky behaviours.

UNIT 6: POPULATION AND NATURAL RESOURCES

Definitions

  • Ecology – the study of the interaction between organisms and the physical environment.
  • Population – a group of interacting individuals of the same species living and interbreeding within a given area.
  • Population ecology – the study of how populations interact with the environment and the processes that affect their distribution and abundance.
  • Population size (N) – the total number of individuals in a particular habitat.
  • Population density – the number of individuals per unit area or volume.
  • Population dispersal – how individuals in a population are distributed in space at a given time.
  • Exponential growth model – a model describing population increase without environmental limits (J‑shaped curve).
  • Logistic growth model – a model describing population increase that levels off due to limited resources (S‑shaped curve).
  • Carrying capacity (K) – the maximum population size that the environment’s resources can sustain.
  • Demography – the study of population changes over time using parameters like size, birth rate, death rate, migration.
  • Population pyramid – a graphical representation of the age and sex composition of a population.
  • Survivorship curve – a graph of the number of individuals surviving at each age interval versus time.
  • Natural resources – any natural substances or materials available in the environment that are used by humans.
  • Conservation – the care, protection, and wise use of natural resources so they are available for future generations.
  • Environmental pollution – the contamination of environmental components that adversely affect normal processes.
  • Climate change – a change in the usual patterns of weather (rainfall, temperature, etc.).
  • Global warming – the average rapid rise in Earth’s temperature due to greenhouse gases.
  • Ozone depletion – erosion of the ozone layer in the stratosphere, caused mainly by CFCs.
  • Acid rain – rain with more acidic content (lower pH) than natural rain, caused by SO₂ and NOₓ dissolving in water.
  • Biodiversity – the variety of life on Earth in terms of genes, species, and biological communities.
  • Bioaccumulation – an increase in the concentration of chemicals in living organisms’ body tissues over time.
  • Resource depletion – when the consumption of natural resources becomes faster than they can recover.
  • Indigenous knowledge – a body of knowledge built over generations by local people living in a particular environment.

Population Sampling Methods

  • Quadrat method: for immobile organisms; estimate population size: N = (A/a) × n (A = total area, a = quadrat area, n = number counted).
  • Mark‑recapture method: N = (M × n)/X (M = initially marked, n = total in second capture, X = recaptured marked).
  • Human census: De Facto (fixed date), De Jure (enumeration period, permanent residents).

Dispersal Patterns

  • Uniform – evenly spaced.
  • Random – no predictable pattern.
  • Clumped – clustered in groups.

Population Growth Rate Formulas

  • Exponential: dN/dt = rN (r = birth rate − death rate).
  • Logistic: dN/dt = rN[(K−N)/K].

Demographic Parameters

  • Crude birth rate, crude death rate, infant mortality rate, life expectancy, fertility rate, mortality rate, migration (immigration/emigration).

Population Pyramid Types

  • Expansive – broad base, narrow top; young, growing population (e.g., Kenya, Ethiopia).
  • Constrictive – smaller younger population; older, shrinking (e.g., US, Italy).
  • Stationary – equal proportions; stable (e.g., some developed nations).

Survivorship Curve Types

  • Type I – high survival until old age (humans, mammals).
  • Type II – constant mortality rate (some birds).
  • Type III – high early mortality (plants, invertebrates, fish).

Population Regulation Factors

  • Density‑dependent – predation, competition, disease, waste accumulation (effect increases with density).
  • Density‑independent – weather, natural disasters, pollution (effect regardless of density).

Natural Resources Classification

  • Renewable – can be replaced (sunlight, water, wind, trees, solar, biomass, hydropower).
  • Non‑renewable – do not replenish at consumption rate (fossil fuels, minerals, coal, natural gas).

Conservation in Ethiopia

  • Wildlife: 24 national parks, 2 sanctuaries, 6 reserves, 5 community conservation areas, controlled hunting areas.
  • Plants: indigenous practices (sacred groves, taboos, traditional farming) and modern (gene banks, botanical gardens, in‑situ/ex‑situ).
  • Soil and water: mechanical (terraces, bunds, check dams) and biological (enclosure, afforestation, agro‑forestry, Green Legacy). GERD for hydropower.

Human Environmental Impacts

  • Air pollution: CO, NO₂, SO₂, lead, smog – respiratory disease, ozone depletion, acid rain.
  • Water pollution: industrial/agricultural wastes, sewage, fertilisers, water hyacinth – biodiversity loss, waterborne disease.
  • Climate change/global warming: increased greenhouse gases (CO₂, methane, nitrous oxide) → rising temperatures, melting ice, sea‑level rise, drought, flooding.
  • Ozone depletion reactions: Cl + O₃ → ClO + O₂; ClO + O → Cl + O₂ (catalytic cycle).
  • Acid rain reactions: SO₂ + O₂ + H₂O → H₂SO₄; NO₂ + O₂ + H₂O → HNO₃.
  • Biodiversity loss: habitat destruction, overexploitation, pollution, climate change, invasive species.
  • Bioaccumulation: toxins (DDT, lead, mercury) magnify through food chains; DDT thins eggshells.
  • Resource depletion: overpopulation, poor farming, overconsumption, industrialisation.

Indigenous Conservation Practices (Konso People)

  • Terracing (stone structures to reduce erosion)
  • Contour ploughing
  • Crop rotation
  • Mixed cropping (intercropping)
  • Surface mulching
  • Agro‑forestry (Moringa trees)
  • Guassa (Menz) Qero system: rotational closure of grassland for 3–5 years; governed by elected headmen.

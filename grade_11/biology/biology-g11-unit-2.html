2.1 Characteristics of Animals

Common characteristics of animals

  • Eukaryotic – cells have a true nucleus.
  • Multicellular – bodies are made of many cells.
  • Heterotrophic – obtain food by consuming other organisms; cannot make their own food.
  • Sensitive to stimuli – detect and respond to changes in the environment.
  • Reproduce – produce offspring to ensure species continuity.
  • Protect themselves – have defence mechanisms.
  • Move (motility) – most animals can move at some life stage.
  • Respire – exchange gases (take in oxygen, release carbon dioxide).
  • Excrete – remove metabolic wastes.
  • Grow – increase in size and develop.
  • Body symmetry – body plan can be asymmetrical, radial, or bilateral.

Major groups based on backbone

  • Vertebrates – animals with a backbone (vertebral column).
  • Invertebrates – animals without a backbone.

Vertebrates are further divided by temperature regulation:

  • Homeothermic (warm‑blooded): mammals, birds.
  • Poikilothermic (cold‑blooded): fish, reptiles, amphibians.

Invertebrates include: porifera, platyhelminthes, cnidaria, arthropoda (crustacea, spiders, insects, many‑legs), annelida, echinodermata, mollusca.

2.2 Invertebrates and Vertebrates

2.2.1 Invertebrate Animals

Definition

  • Invertebrates are animals that do not have a backbone or vertebral column.

Key features

  • Most diverse group of animals; found almost everywhere.
  • Lack a rigid internal skeletal system.
  • Many are soft‑bodied; some have an external skeleton called an exoskeleton (often made of chitin) that protects the soft inner body.
  • Cold‑blooded – do not regulate internal body temperature; body temperature changes with the environment.
  • Examples: earthworms, insects, spiders, snails, sponges, jellyfish, lobsters, crabs, sea stars, squid.

2.2.2 Vertebrate Animals

Definition

  • Vertebrates are animals that possess a well‑defined internal skeleton with cartilage and a backbone (vertebral column).

Key features

  • Highly advanced group with complex, specialized organ systems.
  • Skeleton divided into:
    • Axial skeleton: skull, vertebrae, ribs, sternum.
    • Appendicular skeleton: girdles and appendages.
  • Skull protects the highly developed brain; vertebrae protect the nerve cord.
  • Circulatory system: closed system; ventral heart with 2–4 chambers; median dorsal artery.
  • Respiratory system: gills or lungs.
  • Centralized nervous system: brain and sensory organs (eyes, ears, nostrils).
  • Excretory system: paired kidneys.
  • Body symmetry: bilaterally symmetrical.
  • Examples: mammals, birds, fish, reptiles, amphibians.
  • Temperature regulation:
    • Fish, reptiles, amphibians – cold‑blooded.
    • Birds and mammals – warm‑blooded.
  • All vertebrates are chordates, but not all chordates are vertebrates.

Difference between invertebrates and vertebrates (summary table)

Feature Invertebrates Vertebrates
Backbone Absent Present
Skeleton No rigid internal skeleton; some have exoskeleton Well‑defined internal skeleton (bone/cartilage)
Body symmetry Varies (asymmetrical, radial, bilateral) Bilateral

Nervous system Less centralized (nerve net, ganglia) Centralized with brain and sensory organs

2.3 Reproduction in Animals

Reproduction is the process by which living organisms duplicate themselves to ensure continuity of their species.

Two main types:

  • Asexual reproduction
  • Sexual reproduction

2.3.1 Asexual Reproduction

  • Involves a single individual; no fusion of gametes.
  • More common in invertebrates than vertebrates.
  • Forms:
    • Budding – a new individual develops as an outgrowth from the parent.
    • Fragmentation – the parent breaks into pieces, each piece grows into a new individual.
    • Parthenogenesis – an unfertilized egg develops into a new offspring (occurs in some insects and vertebrates).

2.3.2 Sexual Reproduction

  • Involves two parents (male and female).
  • Requires the fusion of gametes (fertilization) to form a zygote.
  • Offspring inherit genetic material from both parents.
  • Parents are diploid (2n); gametes are haploid (n).
  • Males produce sperm in testes; sperm stored in epididymis until ejaculation.
  • Females produce ovum (egg) in ovary; egg matures in ovary.
  • Fertilization – fusion of sperm and egg to produce a diploid zygote (2n).

Types of fertilization

  • External fertilization: eggs and sperm are released into the environment (usually water); common in aquatic animals.
  • Internal fertilization: sperm is deposited inside the female’s body; common in land animals.

Embryonic development after fertilization

  1. Cleavage – series of mitotic cell divisions of the zygote, resulting in an eight‑celled structure.
  2. Blastula – a hollow ball of cells formed after further division and rearrangement.
  3. Gastrulation – the blastula undergoes cell division and rearrangement to form a gastrula with distinct germ layers.
  4. Organogenesis – germ layers develop into tissues, organs, and organ systems.
  5. Embryo → adult organism.

2.3.3 Reproduction in Insects (Complete and Incomplete Metamorphosis)

Insects are the largest class of phylum Arthropoda. They have segmented bodies, jointed legs, and an exoskeleton.

  • Undergo sexual reproduction; eggs are usually fertilized internally.
  • Some insects undergo parthenogenesis.
  • After fertilization, the female lays eggs; after hatching, insects undergo major body changes called metamorphosis.

Two types of metamorphosis

Feature Complete Metamorphosis Incomplete Metamorphosis
Number of stages Four Three
Stages Egg → Larva → Pupa → Adult Egg → Nymph → Adult
Example Honeybees, butterflies, beetles Grasshoppers, cockroaches
Appearance Larva looks completely different from adult; pupa is a resting stage. Nymph resembles a smaller adult and lacks wings (wings develop gradually).

Metamorphosis is controlled by chemical substances (hormones) in the insect.

2.3.4 Reproduction in Frogs

  • Frogs undergo sexual reproduction.
  • External fertilization: female releases eggs into water; male releases sperm over them.
  • A few frog species have internal fertilization.
  • Eggs are not amniotic; they are covered by a jelly‑like substance that protects them in water.
  • Frogs lay a large number of eggs at one time to increase the chances of fertilization and survival.
  • After fertilization, eggs develop into a larval stage called a tadpole.
  • The tadpole undergoes metamorphosis to become an adult frog (grows legs, loses tail, develops lungs).

Life cycle of a frog: Egg (in water) → Tadpole → Adult frog.

Disadvantage of external fertilization: eggs are vulnerable to environmental changes and predators; laying many eggs and jelly coating help overcome this.

2.3.5 Reproduction in Crocodiles

  • Crocodilians are semi‑aquatic reptiles.
  • Sexual reproduction with internal fertilization.
  • Mating takes place in water; eggs are fertilized inside the female.
  • Eggs are laid and buried in sand or deposited in mound vegetation.
  • Egg number: 10–100 depending on species.
  • Eggs have a hard, leathery shell, which is an advancement over frog eggs, providing better protection.
  • Development is direct (no larval stage); hatchlings resemble adults.

Life cycle of a crocodile: Egg (buried) → Hatchling → Adult.

2.3.6 Reproduction in Birds

  • Sexual reproduction; internal fertilization.
  • Most birds are monogamous (one male, one female for a breeding season); some are polygamous (multiple partners).
  • Male birds lack external genital organs; sperm transfer occurs by bringing the male cloaca into contact with the female cloaca.
  • Female birds usually have a single ovary.
  • The ovum (egg yolk) is released into the oviduct.

Parts of the oviduct and their functions

  • Infundibulum – funnel‑shaped; engulfs the yolk.
  • Magnum – longest part; secretes albumen (egg white).
  • Isthmus – forms inner and outer shell membranes; produces sulfur‑containing amino acids.
  • Uterus – egg stays here longest; shell forms over membranes.
  • Vagina – final section; egg passes through cloaca to be laid.

Egg structure: yolk, chalaza, albumen, shell membranes, air sac, shell.

  • After fertilization, the egg develops a hard shell; the embryo is surrounded by the amnion (fluid‑filled sac).
  • Incubation – parent(s) keep eggs warm with body heat.
    • A brood patch (area of skin with dense blood vessels) develops in the female to transfer heat efficiently.
    • Birds rotate eggs to distribute warmth evenly.
  • Hatching – the chick uses an egg tooth (tooth‑like structure on the beak) to break the shell.
    • The egg tooth is lost after hatching.
    • Chicks communicate with parents before hatching by vocal sounds.
  • Parental care: birds build nests, often hidden or with camouflaged eggs to avoid predators. Some birds provide extensive care after hatching; others leave chicks to fend for themselves.

Life cycle of a bird: Egg (incubated) → Hatchling → Adult.

2.3.7 Reproduction in Rats (Mammals)

  • Rats represent typical mammalian sexual reproduction.
  • Internal fertilization; development occurs inside the female.

Male reproductive structures: testes, scrotum, seminiferous tubules, epididymis, vas deferens, penis with bacula.
Female reproductive structures: two ovaries, oviducts, uterine horns, vagina with vulva.

Pregnancy and development

  • Fertilized zygote divides and forms a blastocyst (blastula).
  • Blastocysts implant in the uterine horns.
  • Umbilical cord nourishes the embryo; placenta transports oxygen and removes waste.
  • Amniotic sac protects the embryo.
  • Embryo develops: neural plate → brain and spinal cord; limb buds; nervous system pathways.
  • Gestation period:
    • Brown rat: 22–24 days
    • Black rat: ~22 days
    • Giant mole rat: 37–49 days
  • Birth: offspring are hairless, deaf, with sealed eyelids.
  • Litter size: 7–12 pups on average; fewer for giant mole rats.
  • Mother feeds milk; young are fully weaned at ~45 days.
  • Sexual maturity:
    • Brown/black rats: 3–4 months
    • Giant mole rats: 4–6 months

Parental care in rats

  • Females build nests and care for pups; if a mother dies, other females may take over nursing.
  • Males do not participate in parental care.

2.4 The Economic Importance of Insects

Insects have both beneficial and harmful effects on the economy, lives, and ecosystems.

2.4.1 Beneficial Aspects

A. Agriculture

  • Pollinators: insects visit flowers for nectar/pollen; transfer pollen, enabling pollination.
    • About 35% of world crop production results from insect pollination.
    • Essential for reproduction of many flowering plants and fruit/seed production.
    • High economic value.
  • Pest regulation (natural biological control):
    • Insect predators and parasitoids feed on pest insects.
    • Examples:
      • Stagmomantis (mantis) – feeds on grasshoppers and caterpillars.
      • Cheilomenes (ladybird beetle) – feeds on aphids and scale worms.
      • Epicauta (blister beetle) – eats locust egg masses.
  • Other roles:
    • Feed on weeds.
    • Improve soil aeration, nutrient cycling, and soil structure as decomposers.

B. Food

  • Edible insects are a sustainable source of animal protein.
  • Over 1,462 recorded species are consumed worldwide, especially in Asia and Central America.
  • Commonly eaten: crickets, grasshoppers, beetle/moth larvae, termites.
  • Nutritious: high protein, fat, minerals; can be farmed easily with low environmental footprint.
  • Also serve as food for many vertebrates.

C. Industry

  • Honey and beeswax – produced by honeybees (Apis mellifera); millions of tons produced yearly.
  • Silk – silk fibers from silkworms (Bombyx mori and others); used for luxurious textiles.
  • Shellac – resin secreted by lac insects (Laccifer lacca); used in dyes, inks, polishes, sealing wax, felt‑hat stiffening.
  • Cochineal pigment – from scale insect Dactylopius coccus; used as a colorant in foods, beverages, cosmetics, art.
  • Tannic acid – from gall tissues produced by trees in response to cynipid wasp secretions; used in leather tanning, dyeing, and inks.

D. Health and Medicine

  • Honey – used to treat burns, chronic wounds, post‑surgical wounds; promotes healthy immune systems, improves circulation, decreases inflammation.
  • Bee and ant venom – used to treat joint pain.
  • Cantharidan (from blister beetles) – a powerful protein blocker; prevents reproduction of some viral cells; may be useful in treating cancerous tumours resistant to radiation/chemotherapy.
  • Ground grasshopper poultices – used in some African cultures as pain relievers, especially for migraines.

2.4.2 Harmful Aspects

  • Plant pests: insects feed on leaves, stems, buds, flowers, fruits, seeds in fields and storage, reducing crop yield.
    • Examples: locusts, caterpillars, bugs, hoppers, aphids.
    • Locusts are highly destructive; they form huge swarms, especially in hot, dry regions, devouring all green plants.
  • Stored grain pests: Rice Weevil, Lesser Grain Borer, Rust Red Flour Beetle, Sawtooth Grain Beetle, Flat Grain Beetle.
  • Disease vectors:
    • Anopheles mosquito – transmits malaria (Plasmodium).
    • Culex mosquito – spreads filariasis (filarial worms).
    • Tsetse fly (Trypanosoma gambiense) – spreads African sleeping sickness.
    • Housefly (Musca domestica) – spreads food‑ and water‑borne diseases.

2.5 Animal Behavior

Animal behavior is defined as all the ways animals interact with other organisms and the physical environment. It includes movement, interactions within the environment, and learning.

2.5.1 Types of Animal Behavior

Two main types:

  • Innate (inherent) behavior
  • Learned (acquired) behavior

A. Innate (Inherent) Behavior

  • Inborn; determined by genes; independent of experience; species‑specific.
  • Three subtypes:

  1. Instinctive behavior

    • Examples: web making in spiders, nest‑building in birds, swimming in dolphins, chick mouth opening when mother returns, honeybee dance for food source location.

  2. Reflex behavior

    • A sudden, involuntary response to a stimulus.
    • The pain message travels only to the spinal cord and back, not the brain, allowing faster response.
    • Examples: pulling hand away from hot/sharp object, blinking when an object nears the eye, closing eyes against dust.

  3. Orientation behavior

    • Taxis – directed movement toward or away from a stimulus source.
      • Positive taxis: movement toward the stimulus.
      • Negative taxis: movement away from the stimulus.
      • Example: cockroaches moving away from light (negative phototaxis).
      • Types: phototaxis (light), chemotaxis (chemicals), thigmotaxis (touch), geotaxis (gravity).
    • Kinesis – undirected, random movement; response depends on stimulus intensity, not direction.
      • Example: woodlice movement in response to temperature changes.

B. Learned (Acquired) Behavior

  • Not inherited; not genetically determined.
  • Acquired during an individual’s lifetime; allows adaptation to environmental changes.
  • Modified by previous experiences.
  • Types:

  1. Habituation

    • A simple form of non‑associative learning; an animal stops responding to a repeated, harmless stimulus.
    • Example: getting used to the sound of a television while reading.

  2. Classical conditioning

    • Associative learning where a response to one stimulus becomes associated with a second, previously neutral stimulus.
    • Discovered by Ivan P. Pavlov.
    • Three stages:
      • Before conditioning: Unconditioned stimulus (UCS) → Unconditioned response (UCR) (natural, unlearned).
      • During conditioning: Neutral stimulus (conditioned stimulus, CS) is repeatedly paired with UCS.
      • After conditioning: CS alone elicits the conditioned response (CR).

  3. Operant conditioning

    • Associative learning in which behavior is modified by consequences (reward or punishment).
    • Discovered by B.F. Skinner.
    • Three types of operant responses:
      • Neutral operants – neither increase nor decrease probability of behavior.
      • Reinforcers – increase probability of behavior (positive or negative reinforcement).
      • Punishers – decrease likelihood of behavior (punishment weakens behavior).

  4. Insight learning

    • Learning based on past experience and reasoning; a hallmark of human behavior.
    • Example: problem‑solving from starting a fire to space travel.

  5. Sensitization

    • Non‑associative learning; repeated administration of a stimulus amplifies the response (reverse tolerance).
    • Occurs when a stimulus is presented above the tolerance threshold.
    • Example: a painful stimulus may make one more sensitive to a loud noise.

Difference between innate and learned behavior

Innate Behavior Learned Behavior
Genetically determined, inborn Acquired through experience
Uniform within a species Varies among individuals
Does not require practice Requires practice or exposure
Inflexible, fixed Flexible, modified by environment
Examples: reflexes, instincts Examples: habituation, conditioning

2.5.2 Patterns of Behavior

Common behavioral patterns found across many species:

  1. Behavioral cycles

    • Responses to periodic environmental changes.
    • Seasonal migration – movement of animals (birds, insects, mammals) from one habitat to another due to changes in food, sunlight, temperature, breeding conditions.
    • Circadian rhythms (biological clocks) – 24‑hour cycles regulating sleep‑wake cycles and other essential functions.

  2. Reproductive behavior

    • Coordinates timing and patterning of reproductive activity.
    • Includes locating/selecting mates, courtship displays (sounds, visual displays, chemicals), producing offspring, and rearing young.
    • Example: courtship dance in pigeons.

  3. Social behavior

    • Observed in group‑living animals; includes division of labor.
    • Examples:
      • Social insects (ants, termites, bees, wasps) – workers, soldiers.
      • Elephants – pay homage to dead relatives.
      • Penguins, primates, humans.

  4. Competition

    • Occurs when organisms compete for limited resources (space, territory, water, mates, food).
    • Two types:
      • Intraspecific competition – between members of the same species.
      • Interspecific competition – between members of different species.

  5. Territoriality

    • Protecting a space that contains necessary resources.
    • Many animals use display behavior (chest pounding, red breast display, pheromone marking) rather than fighting to warn others.
    • Examples:
      • Dogs and lions mark territory with urine (pheromones).
      • Male gorillas pound chest, thump ground.
      • Robin displays red breast.

  6. Communication

    • Essential for interaction; uses sight, sound, touch (tactile), and chemicals (pheromones).
    • Examples:
      • Birds sing, frogs croak.
      • Ants use pheromones to mark trails to food.

2.6 Homeostasis in Animals

Homeostasis is the self‑regulatory process by which animals maintain stable internal body conditions despite external changes. It maintains equilibrium at a set point (normal conditions).

Components of homeostatic control

  • Stimulus – a change in the environment or body condition (e.g., temperature change, glucose level change).
  • Receptor – detects the change and sends a signal (e.g., thermoreceptors).
  • Control center – receives the signal and sends commands; often the hypothalamus in the brain.
  • Effector – organ or tissue (e.g., kidney, liver, muscles, glands) that carries out the response to counteract the change and restore normal condition.

2.6.1 Thermoregulation

Thermoregulation is the process of maintaining constant internal body temperature.

Two groups of animals based on temperature regulation:

  1. Poikilothermic (ectothermic) animals

    • Body temperature matches the environment; varies with it.
    • Lack internal physiological control.
    • Behavioral methods to adjust temperature: burrowing, basking in sun, swimming, using group activity (e.g., bees in hive).
    • Examples: fish, reptiles, amphibians, most invertebrates.

  2. Homeothermic (endothermic) animals

    • Maintain constant internal body temperature regardless of the environment.
    • Generate internal heat; use morphological, physiological, and behavioral methods.

Temperature regulation in homeotherms

A. Cold environment – heat conservation

  • Insulation: fur, fat, feathers (e.g., arctic fox uses fluffy tail; birds puff up feathers).
  • Vasoconstriction: narrowing of blood vessels in the skin to reduce blood flow and heat loss.
  • Shivering: involuntary muscle contractions that generate heat from respiration.
  • Behavioral: huddling, seeking shelter.

B. Hot environment – heat loss

  • Vasodilation: widening of blood vessels in the skin, increasing blood flow to the surface, allowing heat loss by radiation and evaporation.
  • Sweating: sweat glands produce sweat; evaporation of sweat cools the body.
  • Behavioral: seeking shade, swimming.

Control center: Hypothalamus detects temperature changes and triggers vasodilation/constriction, sweating, shivering.

Mechanisms of heat exchange with environment

  • Radiation
  • Evaporation
  • Convection
  • Conduction

Morphological adaptations

  • Large ears in hot climates → increase heat loss.
  • Small ears and thick fur in cold climates → reduce heat loss.

Surface‑area‑to‑volume ratio

  • Smaller animals have a higher surface area to volume ratio → lose heat more rapidly → higher metabolic rate.
  • Larger animals have a lower surface area to volume ratio → lose heat more slowly.
  • Example: a rabbit (small) loses heat faster than an elephant (large).

2.6.2 Osmoregulation

Osmoregulation is the process that regulates osmotic pressure of body fluids and electrolyte balance to maintain homeostasis.

  • About 60% of the human body is fluid; ~2/3 is intracellular fluid, 1/3 is extracellular fluid (interstitial fluid + blood plasma).
  • Disruption in osmotic pressure causes imbalance in water/electrolyte movement.

Two strategies:

  • Osmoconformers: maintain internal osmotic pressure equal to the external environment. Examples: starfish, jellyfish, lobsters.
  • Osmoregulators: actively regulate osmotic pressure independent of the environment. Examples: most vertebrates, including humans.

Role of the kidney

  • Main osmoregulatory organ in humans.
  • When water level is high → produces large amount of hypotonic urine.
  • When water level is low → retains water, produces small amount of hypertonic urine.

Control: Osmoreceptors in the hypothalamus detect osmotic changes; antidiuretic hormone (ADH) from the pituitary gland regulates kidney function.

2.6.3 Blood Sugar (Glucose) Regulation

  • Glucose is the main energy source for body systems, including the brain.
  • Blood glucose concentration must be kept constant.

Hormones from the pancreas:

  • Insulin: released when blood glucose is high. Promotes conversion of glucose → glycogen (stored in liver).
  • Glucagon: released when blood glucose is low. Promotes conversion of glycogen → glucose (released into blood).

This negative feedback loop maintains sugar balance.

2.6.4 Control of Homeostasis – Feedback Mechanisms

Feedback mechanism is a physiological regulation system that returns the body to its normal internal state.

Two types:

Feature Negative Feedback Positive Feedback
Direction Reverses the change; returns to set point Amplifies the change; moves further from set point
Purpose Maintains homeostasis Drives processes to completion (e.g., childbirth, blood clotting)
Example Body temperature regulation: when too hot → sweating (cools down); when too cold → shivering (warms up) Not emphasized for maintenance; examples include oxytocin release during labor

Components of negative feedback loop (as described for temperature):

  • Stimulus: increased body temperature.
  • Receptor: thermoreceptors sense heat.
  • Control center: hypothalamus.
  • Effector: blood vessels dilate (vasodilation), sweat glands activated.
  • Response: heat loss; temperature drops to normal.

When body temperature drops too low:

  • Vasoconstriction, shivering, sweat glands inactive → conserve/generate heat.

  • Blood glucose regulation and osmoregulation also operate by negative feedback.

EXAM REVISION SUMMARY

  • Animal characteristics: eukaryotic, multicellular, heterotrophic, sensitive, reproduce, protect, move, respire, excrete, grow, body symmetry.
  • Classification: vertebrates (backbone) vs invertebrates (no backbone); vertebrates further split into homeothermic (birds, mammals) and poikilothermic (fish, amphibians, reptiles).
  • Invertebrates: lack rigid skeleton, often have exoskeleton, cold‑blooded, diverse.
  • Vertebrates: internal skeleton, axial/appendicular, closed circulation, centralized nervous system, kidneys.
  • Asexual reproduction: budding, fragmentation, parthenogenesis; single parent, no gamete fusion.
  • Sexual reproduction: involves gametes (sperm, egg), fertilization, diploid zygote; embryonic stages – cleavage, blastula, gastrula, organogenesis.
  • External vs internal fertilization: external in water (frogs); internal (crocodiles, birds, rats). Internal fertilization advantageous for land animals.
  • Insect metamorphosis: complete (4 stages: egg, larva, pupa, adult) e.g., honeybee; incomplete (3 stages: egg, nymph, adult) e.g., grasshopper. Controlled by hormones.
  • Frog reproduction: external fertilization, jelly‑coated eggs, tadpole stage, metamorphosis to adult.
  • Crocodile reproduction: internal fertilization, leathery hard eggs, direct development, nest guarding.
  • Bird reproduction: internal fertilization via cloaca, single ovary, oviduct parts (infundibulum, magnum, isthmus, uterus, vagina), hard‑shelled amniotic egg, incubation with brood patch, egg tooth for hatching, nest building and parental care.
  • Rat reproduction: internal fertilization, internal development, placenta and umbilical cord nourish embryo, gestation ~22–24 days (brown/black rat), altricial young (hairless, deaf), maternal care.
  • Insect economic importance – beneficial: pollination (35% crop production), pest control (predators like ladybirds, mantises), food source (high protein), industrial products (honey, beeswax, silk, shellac, cochineal, tannic acid), medicine (honey for wounds, bee/ant venom for pain, cantharidin for viral infections/cancer, grasshopper poultices for migraines).
  • Harmful insects: plant pests (locust swarms, caterpillars, aphids), stored grain pests, disease vectors (Anopheles – malaria, Culex – filariasis, tsetse fly – sleeping sickness, housefly – food/water diseases).
  • Animal behavior: innate (instinct, reflex, orientation – taxis/kinesis) vs learned (habituation, classical conditioning, operant conditioning, insight learning, sensitization).
  • Behavioral patterns: circadian rhythms, seasonal migration, reproductive behavior, social behavior, competition (intra‑/interspecific), territoriality (displays, pheromones), communication (sight, sound, touch, chemicals).
  • Homeostasis: maintenance of stable internal conditions via receptors, control center (hypothalamus), effectors.
  • Thermoregulation: poikilotherms (body temperature varies with environment) use behavioral methods; homeotherms use insulation, vasoconstriction/dilation, sweating, shivering. Surface area to volume ratio affects heat loss (small animals lose heat faster).
  • Osmoregulation: water/electrolyte balance; osmoconformers vs osmoregulators; kidney and ADH control in humans.
  • Blood sugar regulation: insulin lowers blood glucose (stores glycogen); glucagon raises blood glucose (breaks down glycogen); pancreatic hormones maintain balance.
  • Feedback mechanisms: negative feedback reverses changes (temperature, glucose regulation); positive feedback amplifies changes. Negative feedback maintains homeostasis.

3.1 Introduction – What are Enzymes?

Enzymes are biological catalysts that speed up chemical reactions in living organisms. They are protein in nature. An enzyme accelerates a reaction without being consumed or permanently changed; it remains unchanged after the reaction.

3.2 Properties and Functions of Enzymes

Enzyme properties are the physical and chemical characteristics that govern how enzymes work.

3.2.1 General Properties of Enzymes

A. Physical Properties

Property Description
Denaturation Breaking of non‑covalent bonds that distort the enzyme’s shape and active site. Caused by high heat (above 40 °C), extreme pH, heavy metals, high salt, solvents. Once denatured, enzymes lose function; usually irreversible.
Solubility Enzymes dissolve in water, dilute salt solutions, glycerol, and alcohol. (Alcohol can cause denaturation.)
Colloidal nature Enzymes are large molecules that do not easily pass through semi‑permeable membranes (little or no dialysis).
Biocatalyst A very small amount of enzyme converts a large quantity of substrate without being used up.
Precipitation Enzymes can be separated for analysis by using aqueous or ethanol solvents.
Molecular weight Enzymes are large protein biomolecules with high molecular weights, made of polypeptide chains of many amino acids.
Enzymatic activity The catalytic performance of an enzyme, which depends on temperature, pH, enzyme concentration, and substrate concentration. Highest activity occurs at optimum temperature and pH.

B. Chemical Properties

Property Description
Heat and pH sensitivity Enzymes are activated at optimum temperature and pH; extremes cause loss of activity.
Regulation Enzyme activity is controlled by activator and inhibitor molecules.
Catalysis Enzymes are biological catalysts with high catalytic efficiency. One enzyme molecule can convert about 100–10 000 substrate molecules per second. Enzyme‑catalysed reactions are 10³–10⁸ times faster than non‑catalysed reactions.
Reversibility Enzymes can catalyse reactions in both forward and reverse directions (both synthesis/anabolic and breakdown/catabolic).
Specificity The enzyme is restrictive in its choice of substrate.

Types of enzyme specificity:

  • Bond specificity – the enzyme is specific for a particular type of bond.
  • Group specificity – the enzyme acts on a specific chemical group.
  • Substrate specificity – the enzyme acts only on a particular substrate.
  • Optical specificity – the enzyme recognises the optical configuration (stereochemistry) of the substrate.
  • Co‑factor specificity – the enzyme requires specific co‑factors to function.

3.2.2 Functions of Enzymes

  • Enzymes speed up chemical reactions in the body; they are essential for respiration, digestion, liver, muscle, and nerve function.
  • Every cell contains thousands of enzymes that facilitate chemical reactions.
  • Turnover number is the number of substrate molecules converted to product by one enzyme molecule per second when the active site is saturated.
  • Enzymes act as markers of disease states (e.g., myocardial infarction, jaundice, pancreatitis, cancer, neurodegenerative disorders).
  • Each enzyme has an active site with a unique shape that binds the substrate and speeds up metabolism.

Examples of enzymes and their substrates:

  • Sucrase – breaks down sucrose
  • Lactase – breaks down lactose (milk sugar)
  • Carbohydrase – breaks down carbohydrates into sugars
  • Lipase – breaks down fats into fatty acids
  • Protease – breaks down proteins into amino acids

How enzymes work:
Enzymes lower the activation energy – the minimum energy required to start a reaction. Lower activation energy means a faster reaction.

Some enzymes in the body and their functions (Table):

Enzyme Function
Lipases Split fats; found in blood, gastric juice, pancreatic secretions, intestinal juices, adipose tissue; participate in digestion.
Amylase In saliva; changes starches into sugars.
Maltase In saliva and foods like potatoes; breaks maltose into glucose.
Trypsin In small intestine; breaks proteins into amino acids.
Lactase In small intestine; breaks lactose into glucose and galactose.

3.3 Protein Structures

Protein structure is the three‑dimensional arrangement of atoms in a chain of amino acids linked by peptide bonds. Since enzymes are proteins, their structure determines their function.

Proteins have four structural levels:

  1. Primary structure

    • The linear sequence of amino acids in a polypeptide chain, linked by peptide bonds.
    • Determined by the side‑chain substituents (which vary in chemical, physical, and structural properties).
    • Chains with fewer than 50 amino acids are called peptides; longer chains are polypeptides.
    • Humans need 20 amino acids; 10 are made in the body, the other 10 come from the diet.
    • Cells use 20 standard L‑α‑amino acids (with basic amino and acidic carboxyl groups).

  2. Secondary structure

    • Local folded structures formed by hydrogen bonding between backbone atoms.
    • Two main types:
      • α‑Helix – a right‑handed coiled strand. The C=O of one amino acid forms a hydrogen bond with the N–H group of an amino acid four residues away. Side chains point outward.
      • β‑Pleated sheet – strands lie side‑by‑side; hydrogen bonds form between adjacent strands (inter‑strand) or within a strand (intra‑strand). The chain is nearly fully extended, forming pleats.

  3. Tertiary structure

    • The overall three‑dimensional shape of a single polypeptide chain.
    • Formed by interactions between side‑chain groups (e.g., hydrophobic interactions, ionic bonds, disulfide bridges, hydrogen bonds).
    • The protein bends and twists to reach maximum stability (lowest energy state).

  4. Quaternary structure

    • The arrangement of two or more folded polypeptide subunits into a multi‑subunit complex.
    • Held together by hydrogen bonds and other interactions.
    • Example: Haemoglobin consists of four polypeptide chains.

Summary of protein structure levels:

Level Description
Primary Linear sequence of amino acids in a polypeptide chain.
Secondary Folding into α‑helices and β‑pleated sheets within one chain, due to backbone hydrogen bonding.
Tertiary 3‑D arrangement of all amino acids in a single chain, stabilised by side‑chain interactions.
Quaternary Assembly of multiple polypeptide chains into one functional protein.

3.4 Enzyme–Substrate Models

Enzyme–substrate models describe how the active site and substrate interact.

Two main models:

  1. Lock and key model

    • The active site has a rigid shape that is exactly complementary to the substrate.
    • The substrate fits into the active site like a key into a lock.
    • After the reaction forms products, the active site returns to its original shape and can accommodate more substrate.

  2. Induced fit model

    • The active site is not a perfect rigid fit initially.
    • When the substrate binds, the enzyme changes shape, moulding around the substrate to form the enzyme–substrate complex.
    • This conformational change can strain bonds in the substrate, lowering activation energy and facilitating the reaction.
    • The shape change may either enhance or suppress enzyme activity.

3.4.1 Enzymatic Transition State

The transition state is the high‑energy, unstable state during the conversion of substrate to product.

The enzyme‑catalysed reaction follows this sequence:

E + S ⇄ ES ⇄ ES ⇄ EP ⇄ E + P*

Where:

  • E = Enzyme
  • S = Substrate
  • ES = Enzyme‑substrate complex
  • ES* = Activated enzyme‑substrate complex (transition state)
  • EP = Enzyme‑product complex
  • P = Product

The enzyme stabilises the transition state, lowering the activation energy and speeding up the reaction.

3.5 Enzyme Regulation

Enzyme regulation is the control system that turns enzymes “on” or “off” according to the organism’s needs. Regulatory enzymes respond to the presence of certain molecules and adjust the pathway’s activity.

Regulatory enzymes need extra activation/modification. They are of two main types: allosteric enzymes and covalently modulated enzymes.

3.5.1 Allosteric Enzymes

  • Allosteric enzymes have two binding sites:
    • Active site (catalytic site) – binds substrate.
    • Allosteric site (regulatory site) – binds effector molecules (activators or inhibitors).
  • Effectors are small molecules that modulate enzyme activity by reversible non‑covalent binding to the allosteric site.
  • Effector binding causes a conformational change in the enzyme that alters the active site, modifying catalytic activity (increase or decrease).

3.5.2 Genetic and Covalent Modification

  • Genetic modification – changes to the enzyme’s gene improve enzyme properties; can produce active and inactive forms.
  • Covalent modulation – enzymes are switched between active and inactive forms by the addition or removal of chemical groups catalysed by other enzymes.
    • Most common example: Phosphorylation – addition of a phosphate group to the enzyme protein. This is the most frequent regulatory modification in cells.

3.5.3 Enzyme Inhibition

Enzyme inhibition is a decrease in enzyme activity caused by inhibitor molecules that bind to the enzyme.

Types of inhibitors:

Type Mechanism Reversibility
Irreversible inhibitor Permanently blocks enzyme action, often by forming covalent bonds. Irreversible
Reversible inhibitor Inactivates enzyme through non‑covalent interactions; can be removed to restore activity. Reversible

Reversible inhibition is further divided into:

  1. Competitive inhibitor

    • Competes with the substrate for the active site.
    • Resembles the substrate and physically blocks substrate binding.
    • Effect can be overcome by increasing substrate concentration.

  2. Non‑competitive inhibitor

    • Binds to a site other than the active site (can bind to enzyme or enzyme‑substrate complex).
    • Changes enzyme shape so the active site no longer binds substrate effectively; decreases enzyme efficacy.
    • Cannot be overcome by adding more substrate.

  3. Uncompetitive inhibitor

    • Binds only to the enzyme‑substrate complex, not to the free enzyme.
    • Slows the reaction by locking the substrate in the complex.
    • Occurs in multi‑substrate reactions.

3.6 Types of Enzymes

Enzymes can be classified structurally or functionally.

3.6.1 Structural Classification

Enzymes are either:

  • Simple proteins – active enzyme consists only of protein.
  • Conjugated proteins (holoenzymes) – consist of:
    • Protein part – called apoenzyme (inactive by itself).
    • Non‑protein part – called cofactor.
      • Prosthetic group – cofactor firmly attached, usually a metal ion.
      • Coenzyme – cofactor loosely attached, often derived from vitamin B complex.

Apoenzyme + Cofactor → Holoenzyme (active enzyme).

3.6.2 Basic (Functional) Classification

Enzymes are grouped into six classes based on the type of reaction they catalyse. All enzyme names end in “‑ase”.

Class Reaction catalysed Example pattern / description
Oxidoreductases Oxidation–reduction reactions; transfer of electrons. A⁻ + B → A + B⁻ (oxidant and reductant)
Transferases Transfer of functional groups (e.g., methyl) from a donor to an acceptor molecule.
Hydrolases Hydrolysis (cleavage of bonds by adding water).
Lyases Cleavage of bonds by means other than hydrolysis or oxidation; often form double bonds.
Isomerases Conversion of a molecule from one isomer to another.
Ligases Joining of two molecules, coupled with ATP hydrolysis (diphosphate bond breakage).

3.7 Factors Affecting Enzyme Action

Factors that influence enzyme activity:

  • Temperature
  • pH
  • Substrate concentration
  • Enzyme concentration
  • Inhibitors
  • Activators
  • Radiation
  • Water
  • End‑product (feedback) inhibition

Description of each:

Temperature:

  • Each enzyme has an optimum temperature (around 37 °C for human enzymes).
  • Low temperature reduces molecular movement, lowering activity.
  • High temperature denatures the enzyme (breaks non‑covalent bonds, distorts active site). Denaturation is usually irreversible; enzymes cannot be renatured.

pH:

  • Enzymes have an optimum pH.
  • Too low (acidic) or too high (alkaline) pH disrupts ionic bonds and hydrogen bonds, altering the active site and causing loss of activity.

Substrate concentration:

  • At low substrate concentration, increasing substrate increases reaction rate.
  • At high substrate concentration, the enzyme becomes saturated (all active sites occupied), so reaction rate reaches a maximum (Vmax). Further substrate addition does not increase rate.

Radiation:

  • Damages enzyme macromolecules, reducing efficiency.

Water:

  • Affects enzyme performance beyond optimum levels; extreme hydration or dehydration can alter enzyme structure.

End‑product (feedback) inhibition:

  • A cellular control mechanism where the end product of a metabolic pathway binds to the allosteric site of an enzyme, changing the active site shape and inhibiting the enzyme.
  • This prevents over‑production and conserves resources. Example: the HIV drug Tipranivir acts as a reversible inhibitor blocking the viral enzyme.

Inhibitors – molecules that decrease enzyme activity (see 3.5.3).

Activators – molecules that bind to an enzyme and increase its activity.

3.8 Enzyme Kinetics

Enzyme kinetics is the study of the rates of enzyme‑catalysed reactions, including binding affinities of substrates and inhibitors and the maximum catalytic rates.

Enzymes speed up reactions by lowering activation energy. The concentration of enzyme and substrate determines the reaction rate.

The Michaelis‑Menten model describes the relationship using the equation:

V1=Vmax[S]Km+[S]V_1 = \frac{V_{\text{max}} [S]}{K_m + [S]}V1​=Km​+[S]Vmax​[S]​

Where:

  • V₁ (or V₀) = initial velocity / rate of reaction
  • Vmax = maximum reaction velocity (when enzyme is saturated)
  • [S] = substrate concentration
  • Km (Michaelis constant) = substrate concentration at which the reaction rate is half of Vmax; indicates enzyme affinity for substrate (low Km = high affinity).

The model shows how reaction rate increases with substrate concentration until saturation is reached.

3.9 Applications of Enzymes in Industries and Their Benefits

Enzyme applications are the use of enzyme catalytic power for industrial processes. Enzymes are far more efficient than inorganic catalysts (hundreds to thousands of times more active).

Areas of use:

  • Food industry
  • Feed industry
  • Textile industry
  • Papermaking
  • Leather processing
  • Detergents
  • Pharmaceutical industry
  • Biofuel production
  • Molecular biology

Examples of enzyme applications:

Enzyme(s) Industry / Use Function
Cellulases Biofuel Break down cellulose into sugars for fermentation into cellulosic ethanol
Ligninase Biofuel Pre‑treatment of biomass
Protease, Amylase, Lipase Biological detergents Remove protein, starch, and fat/oil stains from laundry and dishes
Mannanase Biological detergents Remove food stains from guar gum
β‑Glucanase Brewing Improve beer filtration
Papain Culinary Tenderise meat
Rennin Dairy Hydrolyse protein in cheese manufacture
Trypsin Food processing Manufacture hypo‑allergenic baby foods
Cellulases, Pectinase Food processing Clarify fruit juices
Nuclease, DNA Ligase, Polymerase Molecular biology Restriction digestion and PCR to create recombinant DNA

General benefits:

  • Enzymes break large complex molecules into simpler ones for digestion and cellular respiration.
  • Most industrial enzymes were once extracted from animal/plant organs but are now mainly obtained by microbial fermentation.
  • Enzymes power billions of chemical reactions inside cells at lightning speed.
  • In animal feed, enzymes improve digestibility, break down anti‑nutritional factors, and enhance feed utilisation.
  • In pharmaceuticals, enzymes are used in drugs, antibiotics, and household products.
  • Enzymes contribute to environmental cleaning (e.g., in washing powders).

3.10 Malting in Ethiopian Tradition

Malting (sprouting) is a traditional and industrial process of steeping, germinating, and drying grain to convert it into malt. It is the limited, controlled germination of grain in moist air, which mobilises amylases, proteases, and other enzymes to hydrolyse and modify grain components.

3.10.1 Steps of Modern Malting

  1. Steeping

    • Grain kernels are cleaned and immersed in water with aeration for 24–48 hours (depending on grain type and size).
    • Water activates naturally existing enzymes and stimulates new enzyme production.
    • Adequate moisture allows uniform breakdown of starch and protein; starch‑ and protein‑hydrolysing enzymes are activated.

  2. Germinating

    • The grain continues growth and modification. Rootlets emerge; a shoot (acrospire) grows.
    • Modification is the breakdown of protein and carbohydrates, opening starch reserves, over 4–6 days. Temperature, moisture, and airflow are carefully controlled to produce high‑quality green malt.

  3. Kilning (Heating)

    • The germinated grain is dried to stop further germination. If germination continued, the growing plant would consume the starch reserves needed for brewing.
    • Initial drying withers the grain; further drying reduces moisture and develops colour and flavour.
    • Kilning sets enzyme activity, friability, and produces a range of malt colours and flavours for different ales and lagers.

3.10.2 Purpose of Malting

  • To modify the grain’s physical structure and to synthesise/activate enzymes (especially α‑amylase and β‑amylase, plus beta‑glucanase and protease).
  • These enzymes convert grain starches into simple sugars (monosaccharides, disaccharides like maltose) and maltodextrins.
  • Yeast then ferments the sugars to produce alcohol (alcoholic fermentation).
  • Barley is the most common grain because of its high starch‑to‑protein ratio and husk that aids processing.

3.10.3 Traditional Malting in Ethiopia

  • Traditional malting is the sprouting of barley grains to produce enzymes for local fermented drinks such as Tella.
  • Steps are the same: soaking (steeping), germinating (sprouting), and kilning (heating).
  • Other grains used include maize, millet, and sorghum, but barley is preferred.
  • Locally produced drinks include: Booka, Cheka, Keribo, Korefe, Shameta, Borde, Teji – made for holidays, weddings, and celebrations.
  • Malt production provides alcoholic beverages for consumption and income.

3.11 Renowned Biochemists in Ethiopia

  • A biochemist is a scientist who studies chemical processes and transformations in living organisms, including DNA, proteins, and cell parts.
  • Biochemists research how chemical reactions occur in cells, tissues, and organisms; record the effects of food additives and medicines; study immune systems, gene expression, isolation, analysis, and synthesis of products; mutations leading to cancer; and manage laboratory procedures.
  • They also work in agriculture, examining herbicide–plant interactions and evaluating toxicological effects.
  • No specific Ethiopian biochemists are named in the text; the unit emphasises that many Ethiopian scientists have contributed to biochemistry and related fields.

EXAM REVISION SUMMARY

  • Enzymes – biological catalysts made of protein; speed up reactions without being consumed.
  • Physical properties: denaturation (irreversible above 40 °C, extreme pH), solubility, colloidal nature, biocatalyst (small amount converts large substrate quantity), precipitation, high molecular weight, enzymatic activity depends on conditions.
  • Chemical properties: heat/pH sensitivity, regulation (activators/inhibitors), high catalytic efficiency (10³–10⁸ times faster), reversibility (both directions), specificity (bond, group, substrate, optical, co‑factor).
  • Function: lower activation energy; turnover number (substrates per second per enzyme molecule); disease markers. Examples: sucrase, lactase, lipase, amylase, trypsin, DNA polymerase.
  • Protein structure: primary (linear amino acid sequence, peptide bonds), secondary (α‑helix, β‑pleated sheet, H‑bonds), tertiary (3‑D folding, side‑chain interactions), quaternary (multi‑subunit assembly, e.g., haemoglobin).
  • Enzyme–substrate models: lock and key (rigid fit), induced fit (enzyme changes shape on binding). Transition state: E + S → ES → ES* → EP → E + P.
  • Enzyme regulation:
    • Allosteric enzymes: active site + allosteric site; effectors (activators/inhibitors) bind, cause conformational change.
    • Covalent modification: addition/removal of chemical groups (e.g., phosphorylation) to switch on/off.
    • Inhibition: irreversible (permanent), reversible (competitive – blocks active site; non‑competitive – binds elsewhere, changes shape; uncompetitive – binds only ES complex).
  • Structural enzyme classification: simple protein vs conjugated (holoenzyme = apoenzyme + cofactor; cofactor = prosthetic group or coenzyme).
  • Functional classification: oxidoreductases, transferases, hydrolases, lyases, isomerases, ligases.
  • Factors affecting enzyme activity: temperature (optimum; high = denaturation), pH (optimum; extremes denature), substrate concentration (saturation at Vmax), enzyme concentration, inhibitors, activators, radiation, water, feedback inhibition (end‑product binds allosteric site).
  • Enzyme kinetics: Michaelis‑Menten equation V₁ = Vmax[S]/(Km + [S]); Km = substrate concentration at half‑Vmax; Vmax = maximum rate.
  • Industrial applications: detergents (protease, amylase, lipase), food (papain, rennin, pectinase), biofuel (cellulase, ligninase), brewing (β‑glucanase), molecular biology (polymerase, ligase), pharmaceuticals. Enzymes obtained mainly by microbial fermentation.
  • Malting: steeping (soaking grain), germinating (sprouting, enzyme activation), kilning (drying, flavour/colour). Purpose: activate α‑ and β‑amylase to convert starch to fermentable sugars for brewing (barley preferred). Traditional Ethiopian malting produces Tella, Booka, Cheka, etc.
  • Biochemists: study chemical processes in living organisms; work in agriculture, medicine, genetics; respected Ethiopian scientists contribute to the field.

Concept

  • Many modern technologies are designed by imitating natural working systems.
  • Nature is a reliable source of knowledge, ideas and concepts for scientists and engineers.

2. BIOLOGY AND TECHNOLOGY

2.1 Definitions

  • Biology is called the study of life – structures, functions, growth, origins, evolution and distribution of living organisms.
  • Technology is called the application of scientific knowledge, skills, methods and processes to produce devices and tools for scientific investigations.
  • Biotechnology is called the blend (coupling) of biology and technology. It is a technology that uses biological systems to develop useful products. It is the integration of natural and engineering sciences to apply organisms, cells, parts and molecular analogues for products and services.
    • Example: using yeasts to produce bread and beer.

2.2 Benefits of biology to technology

Biology provides materials and information that allow us to imitate, design, adapt and develop technologies. The diversity of nature stimulates new technology.

  1. Medicinal plants

    • Biochemical substances in plants are used to develop medicines.
    • Scientists imitate nature to produce artificial versions of these substances.

  2. Gene engineering and biosynthetic materials

    • Imitating human, animal and plant genes allows gene engineering to cure genetic conditions.
    • Biosynthetic materials are created to repair damaged body tissues.

  3. Building design

    • Engineers imitate termite mound architecture to design buildings with constant temperature and humidity (chimneys).

  4. Bullet train design

    • The nose of bullet trains is designed by imitating the beak of kingfishers when they dive into water.

2.3 Uses of technology in biology

Technology is used in biology to solve human problems, provide services, and investigate new biological questions. This is the practical application of biotechnological instruments.

Biological study areas that rely on technological tools:

  • Biochemical studies – investigate carbohydrates, proteins, lipids, nucleic acids.
  • Biomedical studies – provide detailed information on chemical components of medicinal plants.
  • Biophysical studies – use physical devices to gather biological information at all scales (molecular, organismic, population).
  • Environmental studies – a multidisciplinary system dealing with human‑environment interactions.
  • Bioinformatics – computer technology to collect, store, analyze, and disseminate biological data (DNA, amino acid sequences).
  • Biogeographical information – study of species distribution and geographic ecosystems over geological periods.

Specific technological devices and their functions

  • Digital thermometer – measures body temperature.
  • Pregnancy urine test – detects human Chorionic Gonadotropin (HCG) hormone to confirm pregnancy. One line = negative; two lines = positive.
  • Diabetic blood test – measures blood sugar level using a finger‑tip blood sample.
  • HIV test – identifies infection with the virus.
  • Microscope – magnifies objects for detailed study.
  • Computer Information Technology Scanning (CITS) – investigates diseases or cancerous areas in the body.
  • Computed Tomography scan (CT scan) – a sophisticated X‑ray device that takes many X‑ray pictures to detect and screen for diseases and conditions.
  • Positron Emission Tomography (PET scan) – imaging device that uses radioactive tracers to check for cancer; visualizes metabolic processes, blood flow, chemical composition and absorption.
  • Geographical Position System (GPS) – collects biogeographical data on landscape, plants, animals, and human movements.
  • Handheld Body fat calculator – measures body fat.

3. IMPACTS OF BIOLOGY AND TECHNOLOGY ON SOCIETY AND THE NATURAL WORLD

Impacts are factors that pose positive or negative effects on society and the natural world.

3.1 Impacts of biology

Positive impacts (examples)

  • Ensuring food security through increased productivity.
  • Improved health and longevity via medicine and disease treatments.
  • Better supply of energy and clean water.
  • Increased industrial production due to microbial action.
  • Creating antibiotics to treat bacterial infections.
  • Technological devices made from biological materials provide services that influence, treat and control society and nature.

Negative impacts (examples)

  • Biological weapon production and use destroy the natural world.
  • Predetermined selection of child sex chromosomes (e.g. allowing only XY males, avoiding XX females) may lead to loss of females, affecting society and nature.

3.2 Impacts of technology

  • Technology solves societal problems but also causes environmental damage.

Two main ways technology damages the natural world:

  1. Pollution – industrial emissions and effluents pollute air and drinking water.
  2. Depletion of natural resources

Examples of negative impacts:

  • Industrial emissions and effluents.
  • Biochemical agents, pesticides and fertilizers cause toxicity and biohazards.

4. ETHICAL ISSUES IN BIOLOGY

Definition

  • Ethical issues in biology are called concerns about the rightness or wrongness of using biological discoveries for human health and wellbeing.
  • They deal with biotechnology, medicine and the environment, including the rightness or wrongness of using biotechnological information, products or devices on human health, lifestyles and livelihoods.

4.1 Ethical treatment of plants and animals

Ethical treatment is the process of subjecting animals and plants to various experiments and rigorous tests, while considering their moral status.

A. Ethical treatment of plants

  • Plants are primary producers and feeders for all living organisms.
  • Trees function in a decentralized way without a brain.
  • Unethical treatment can cause disappearance of plant species and harm dependent communities.

Unethical treatments of plants:

  • Testing seeds or berries through destructive rays.
  • Cutting tree terminals for experiments.
  • Consumption of all seeds and fruits without conservation.
  • Unplanned cutting of trees (deforestation).
  • Burning forests and seedlings for farmland.
  • Restructuring tree shapes for aesthetic appeal.

Ethical care:

  • Planting trees is a national responsibility; it saves jeopardized species and conserves for the future.

B. Ethical treatment of animals

  • Animals are sentient creatures with a nervous system; they feel pain, emotional stress, and have interests.
  • It is unethical to harm animals during experiments or rigorous testing.

Unethical treatments:

  • Approaches that expose animals to experiments causing injury or pain.

The Three Rs principle to reduce unethical treatment:

  1. Reduction – minimize the number of animals used by improving techniques and sharing information.
  2. Refinement – reduce animal suffering by using less invasive techniques, better medical care, and better living conditions.
  3. Replacement – substitute animals with alternatives such as cell cultures, biosynthesis, and computer‑simulated models.

4.2 Wider ethical issues in biology (from unit summary)

  • Unethicality, immorality, illegality, inequality, injustice, and disrespectful acts towards the values and rights of someone.
  • Commercialization and commodification of human organs.
  • Assisted reproduction techniques.
  • Manipulation of basic biological organ systems.

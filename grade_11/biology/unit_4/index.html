4.1 Genetic Materials

  • Genetics is called the branch of biology that studies genetic materials and how traits are passed from one generation to the next through genes.
  • The genetic material of an organism carries genetic information and passes it to the next generation.
  • In almost all organisms, the genetic material is DNA. In some viruses (e.g. HIV, COVID‑19) the genetic material is RNA.
  • DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid) is the hereditary material; it exists as a double helix.
  • RNA (ribonucleic acid) serves as the genetic code in some viruses and is involved in protein synthesis.

4.2 Structure and Function of DNA and RNA

4.2.1 Structure and Function of DNA

Structure of DNA

  • DNA is a ladder‑like double helix twisted into a spiral.
  • Sugar (deoxyribose) and phosphate groups form the two backbones; nitrogenous bases form the rungs.
  • DNA consists of two long chains called polynucleotides that twist around each other.
  • Nucleotides are the basic building blocks of DNA.

Composition of a nucleotide

  • One sugar (deoxyribose)
  • One phosphate group
  • One nitrogenous base

Four nitrogenous bases

  • Adenine (A) and Guanine (G) – belong to the purine family (double‑ring structure).
  • Thymine (T) and Cytosine (C) – belong to the pyrimidine family (single‑ring structure).

Base pairing

  • A pairs with T (by two hydrogen bonds); C pairs with G (by three hydrogen bonds).
  • The two strands run in opposite directions: one strand runs 5′ to 3′, the other 3′ to 5′ – this is called polarity.
  • The double helix was discovered by James Watson and Francis Crick.

Genes and chromosomes

  • Gene is a section of DNA that contains the instructions for a characteristic; it is the basic structural and functional unit of inheritance.
  • Genes are located on chromosomes.
  • A chromosome is a thread‑like structure made of histone proteins and a DNA molecule.
  • Each chromosome contains hundreds to thousands of genes arranged linearly.
  • The specific position of a gene on a chromosome is called a locus (plural: loci).
  • In diploid organisms, chromosomes exist in pairs; one chromosome is inherited from each parent. Humans have 46 chromosomes (23 pairs).

Function of DNA

  • To store all genetic information needed for growth, development, reproduction, cell control and survival.
  • To transmit hereditary instructions from parents to offspring.
  • Transmission occurs through DNA replication during cell division.

4.2.2 DNA Replication

DNA replication is the process by which DNA makes a copy of itself. It is semi‑conservative: each new DNA double helix contains one old (parental) strand and one newly synthesised strand.

Key enzymes in DNA replication

Enzyme Function
DNA helicase Binds to double‑stranded DNA and separates the two strands by breaking hydrogen bonds.
DNA polymerase Adds new nucleotides to the growing strand in the correct sequence using the template strand; works in 5′ → 3′ direction.
RNA primase Synthesises short RNA primers complementary to the DNA strand to provide a starting point for DNA polymerase.
DNA ligase Links two DNA fragments by forming phosphodiester bonds.
Topoisomerase Prevents supercoiling ahead of the replication fork.

Key structures

  • Replication fork – the Y‑shaped region where the two DNA strands are separated and new strands are synthesised.
  • Leading strand – the new strand synthesised continuously in the same direction as the replication fork (5′ → 3′).
  • Lagging strand – the new strand synthesised discontinuously in short segments (Okazaki fragments), opposite to the fork movement.

Three stages of DNA replication

  1. Unwinding and unzipping – DNA helicase unwinds the double helix and breaks hydrogen bonds between bases; the two strands separate.
  2. New strand synthesis – each original strand acts as a template. DNA polymerase adds complementary nucleotides (A‑T, C‑G). The leading strand is synthesised continuously; the lagging strand is made in fragments.
  3. Formation of two double helices – the newly synthesised strands wind with the template strands; two identical DNA molecules result.

4.2.3 Structure and Function of RNA

Structure of RNA

  • RNA is single‑stranded.
  • Contains the sugar ribose (instead of deoxyribose), phosphate groups, and the nitrogenous bases Adenine (A), Guanine (G), Cytosine (C), and Uracil (U). Uracil replaces Thymine.
  • Three main types of RNA:
    • Messenger RNA (mRNA) – carries genetic code from DNA to ribosomes.
    • Transfer RNA (tRNA) – brings specific amino acids to ribosomes; contains an anticodon.
    • Ribosomal RNA (rRNA) – forms part of the ribosome structure and helps translate mRNA into protein.

Function of RNA

  • RNA functions in protein synthesis.
  • In some viruses, RNA serves as the storage of genetic information.
  • mRNA serves as a messenger conveying instructions between DNA and ribosomes.
  • tRNA translates the mRNA codon sequence into amino acid sequence.
  • rRNA helps catalyse the assembly of amino acids into a polypeptide chain.

Differences between DNA and RNA

Feature DNA RNA
Full name Deoxyribonucleic acid Ribonucleic acid
Strands Double‑stranded (double helix) Single‑stranded
Sugar Deoxyribose Ribose
Bases A, T, C, G A, U, C, G
Base pairing A‑T, C‑G A‑U, C‑G (in transcription)

4.3 The Process of Cell Division

Cell division enables growth, repair, and reproduction. The information in DNA is transferred from cell to cell and generation to generation through cell division.

4.3.1 The Cell Cycle

The cell cycle is an ordered series of events involving cell growth and division to produce two daughter cells. It has two major phases:

  • Interphase (preparation)
  • Mitotic phase (M phase) – nuclear division and cytokinesis.

Interphase – the cell grows, replicates its DNA, and prepares for division. Three stages:

  • G1 phase (First Gap) – the cell is biochemically active; it accumulates building blocks of DNA, associated proteins, and energy reserves. Cells that are not dividing may enter a resting state called G0 phase.
  • S phase (Synthesis) – DNA replication occurs; each chromosome is duplicated to form two sister chromatids held together at the centromere. The centrosome is also duplicated.
  • G2 phase (Second Gap) – the cell replenishes energy, synthesises proteins needed for chromosome movement, and makes final preparations for mitosis.

4.3.2 Mitosis

Mitosis is the division of somatic cells (body cells). It produces two genetically identical daughter cells with the same number of chromosomes as the parent cell. The mitotic phase consists of karyokinesis (nuclear division) and cytokinesis (cytoplasmic division).

Stages of mitosis

  1. Prophase

    • Nuclear envelope begins to break down.
    • Chromosomes condense and become visible.
    • Centrosomes move to opposite poles; mitotic spindle fibres extend between them.
    • Sister chromatids develop a kinetochore at the centromere, which attaches to spindle microtubules.

  2. Metaphase

    • Chromosomes align at the metaphase plate (equator) of the cell.
    • Sister chromatids remain attached by cohesin proteins; spindle fibres are attached to each kinetochore from opposite poles.

  3. Anaphase

    • Cohesion between sister chromatids breaks down.
    • The chromatids (now individual chromosomes) are pulled to opposite poles by shortening spindle microtubules.

  4. Telophase

    • Chromosomes reach poles and decondense.
    • Mitotic spindle disassembles; nuclear envelopes re‑form around each set of chromosomes; nucleoli reappear.

Cytokinesis – the cytoplasm divides, resulting in two separate, genetically identical daughter cells.

4.3.3 Meiosis

Meiosis is the division that produces sex cells (gametes). It consists of two successive divisions: Meiosis I and Meiosis II, producing four non‑identical haploid daughter cells.

Meiosis I – reduction division (separates homologous chromosomes)

  • Prophase I
    • Chromosomes condense; nuclear envelope breaks down; spindle fibres form.
    • Homologous chromosomes pair up (synapsis) and form chiasmata, where crossing over occurs – non‑sister chromatids exchange genetic material, increasing genetic variation.
  • Metaphase I – homologous pairs align at the equator; spindle fibres attach to one chromosome of each pair.
  • Anaphase I – homologous chromosomes separate and move to opposite poles; sister chromatids remain together.
  • Telophase I – chromosomes reach poles; nuclear envelopes may re‑form; cytokinesis produces two haploid daughter cells (each with one set of duplicated chromosomes).

Meiosis II – equational division (separates sister chromatids; similar to mitosis)

  • Prophase II – chromosomes condense again; nuclear envelope dissolves; new spindle forms.
  • Metaphase II – chromosomes (sister chromatids) line up single‑file at the equator; spindle fibres attach to kinetochores.
  • Anaphase II – sister chromatids separate and move to opposite poles.
  • Telophase II – chromosomes decondense; nuclear envelopes re‑form; cytokinesis yields a total of four genetically different haploid cells.

Comparison: Mitosis vs Meiosis

Feature Mitosis Meiosis

Daughter cells Two Four
Chromosome number Same as parent (diploid → diploid) Halved (diploid → haploid)

4.4 Protein Synthesis

Protein synthesis is the stepwise process of producing proteins from amino acids. It involves DNA, mRNA, tRNA, rRNA, ribosomes, and various enzymes.

The flow of genetic information is called the central dogma:
DNA → RNA → Protein
(Also includes reverse transcription: RNA → DNA, in some viruses.)

Two main steps: Transcription and Translation.

4.4.1 Transcription

  • Occurs in the nucleus.
  • A small portion of DNA unwinds; one strand serves as the template.
  • RNA polymerase synthesises a complementary mRNA strand using base‑pairing rules (A‑U, T‑A, C‑G, G‑C).
  • The mRNA carries the genetic code in the form of codons – sequences of three nucleotides that specify a particular amino acid.
  • Example: the codon AUG is the start codon (initiator); UAG, UAA, and UGA are stop codons that terminate protein synthesis.
  • After synthesis, mRNA detaches from DNA and leaves the nucleus through nuclear pores to the cytoplasm.

4.4.2 The Genetic Code

  • There are 64 possible codons (4³ combinations) that code for the 20 amino acids.
  • The code is degenerate (more than one codon can specify the same amino acid).
  • Start codon: AUG (also codes for methionine).
  • Stop codons: UAG, UAA, UGA – signal the end of translation.

4.4.3 Translation

  • Occurs in the cytoplasm on ribosomes.
  • Ribosomes (made of rRNA and proteins) bind to mRNA.
  • tRNA molecules carry specific amino acids. Each tRNA has an anticodon (three nucleotides) complementary to an mRNA codon.
  • The anticodon pairs with the codon by hydrogen bonding, ensuring the correct amino acid is added to the growing polypeptide chain.
  • The ribosome moves along the mRNA, and successive amino acids are joined by peptide bonds to form a protein.
  • Translation ends when a stop codon is reached; the polypeptide is released.

Role of the three RNAs

  • mRNA – carries genetic information from DNA to ribosome.
  • tRNA – adaptor molecule; brings amino acids and matches them to codons via anticodon.
  • rRNA – structural and catalytic component of ribosomes; helps link amino acids.

4.5 Mendelian Inheritance

Gregor Mendel, an Austrian monk, conducted experiments on garden pea plants (Pisum sativum) and discovered the fundamental principles of heredity.

Key terms from Mendel’s work

  • Gene (factor) – unit of inheritance.
  • Allele – alternative form of a gene.
  • Dominant allele – expressed when present; masks the recessive allele. Represented by a capital letter (e.g., T for tall).
  • Recessive allele – expressed only when two copies are present. Represented by a lower‑case letter (e.g., t for short).
  • Genotype – the genetic makeup of an organism (e.g., TT, Tt, tt).
  • Phenotype – the physical expression of the genotype (e.g., tall, short).
  • Homozygous – having two identical alleles for a trait (TT or tt).
  • Heterozygous – having two different alleles for a trait (Tt).
  • P (parental) generation – the original true‑breeding individuals crossed.
  • F1 generation (first filial) – offspring of the P cross.
  • F2 generation (second filial) – offspring of crossing two F1 individuals.
  • Punnett square – a chart used to predict the possible genotypes and phenotypes of offspring from a cross.

4.5.1 Monohybrid Cross

A monohybrid cross studies the inheritance of one characteristic.

Example: cross between homozygous tall (TT) and homozygous short (tt) pea plants.

  • F1 generation: all plants are Tt (tall phenotype).
  • F1 self‑cross (Tt × Tt):
    • Genotypic ratio: 1 TT : 2 Tt : 1 tt
    • Phenotypic ratio: 3 tall : 1 short

Mendel observed the 3:1 ratio in all seven traits he studied (seed shape, seed colour, flower colour, pod shape, pod colour, flower position, stem length).

From monohybrid crosses Mendel deduced the Law of Segregation.

4.5.2 Law of Segregation

  • Organisms have two alleles for each trait (one from each parent).
  • During gamete formation (meiosis), the two alleles segregate (separate) so that each gamete receives only one allele.
  • At fertilisation, offspring receive one allele from each parent, restoring the pair.

Physical basis: separation of homologous chromosomes during Anaphase I of meiosis.

4.5.3 Dihybrid Cross

A dihybrid cross studies the inheritance of two characteristics simultaneously.

Example: cross between plants with round yellow seeds (RRYY) and wrinkled green seeds (rryy).

  • F1 generation: all plants are RrYy (round, yellow).
  • F1 self‑cross (RrYy × RrYy):
    • The four possible gametes are RY, Ry, rY, ry in equal proportions.
    • F2 phenotypic ratio: 9 round yellow : 3 round green : 3 wrinkled yellow : 1 wrinkled green (9:3:3:1).

From dihybrid crosses Mendel deduced the Law of Independent Assortment.

4.5.4 Law of Independent Assortment

  • Genes for different traits are inherited independently of each other.
  • The allele a gamete receives for one gene does not influence the allele received for another gene.
  • This occurs because non‑homologous chromosomes align independently during Metaphase I of meiosis.

4.5.5 Test Cross

  • A test cross is used to determine the unknown genotype of an individual showing a dominant phenotype.
  • The individual is crossed with a homozygous recessive individual.
  • If all offspring show the dominant phenotype, the unknown parent is homozygous dominant.
  • If any offspring show the recessive phenotype (approx. 1:1 ratio), the unknown parent is heterozygous.

Example: crossing a purple‑flowered pea plant (PP or Pp) with a white‑flowered plant (pp).

  • All purple offspring → PP.
  • 1 purple : 1 white → Pp.

A backcross is a cross between an F1 individual and one of its parents (either dominant or recessive).

4.6 Sex Determination

  • Sex chromosomes determine the sex of an individual. In humans and many mammals, females are XX and males are XY.
  • The other 22 pairs of chromosomes are called autosomes.
  • During meiosis, the male produces sperm carrying either an X or a Y chromosome (50% each). Females produce eggs carrying only X chromosomes.
  • At fertilisation:
    • X‑bearing sperm + X‑bearing egg → XX (female).
    • Y‑bearing sperm + X‑bearing egg → XY (male).
  • Therefore, the father determines the sex of the offspring.

Other sex determination systems exist (e.g., haplodiploidy in bees, environmental sex determination in some reptiles).

4.7 Non‑Mendelian Inheritance

Non‑Mendelian inheritance includes patterns that do not follow Mendel’s laws strictly, such as co‑dominance, incomplete dominance, multiple alleles, sex‑linked traits, and environmental effects.

4.7.1 Co‑dominance

  • Both alleles are equally expressed in the heterozygote; neither is dominant or recessive.
  • Example: coat colour in shorthorn cattle – red (RR) × white (WW) → all offspring are roan (RW), with both red and white hairs present.

4.7.2 Incomplete Dominance

  • The heterozygous phenotype is an intermediate blend between the two homozygous phenotypes.
  • Example: red flower (RR) × white flower (WW) → pink flower (RW). F2 ratio: 1 red : 2 pink : 1 white.

4.7.3 Multiple Alleles – ABO Blood Group System

  • Some genes exist in more than two allelic forms. The ABO blood group is controlled by three alleles: Iᴬ, Iᴮ, i.
  • Iᴬ and Iᴮ are co‑dominant; i is recessive.
  • Possible genotypes and phenotypes (blood types):
Genotype Blood type (Phenotype)
Iᴬ Iᴬ or Iᴬ i A
Iᴮ Iᴮ or Iᴮ i B
i i O
Iᴬ Iᴮ AB
  • Antigens on red blood cells: Type A has A antigen; type B has B antigen; type AB has both; type O has neither.
  • Antibodies in plasma: Type A has anti‑B; type B has anti‑A; type AB has none; type O has both anti‑A and anti‑B.
  • Blood transfusion compatibility: a recipient must not receive blood with antigens against which they have antibodies (e.g., type A cannot receive B or AB blood).

4.7.4 Rh Factor Inheritance

  • Another blood group system independent of ABO, based on the Rh (D) antigen.
  • Rh⁺ (positive) – possess the Rh antigen; genotype Rh⁺/Rh⁺ or Rh⁺/Rh⁻.
  • Rh⁻ (negative) – lack the antigen; genotype Rh⁻/Rh⁻.
  • If an Rh⁻ mother carries an Rh⁺ foetus, she may produce anti‑Rh antibodies (Rh sensitisation), which can cause complications in subsequent Rh⁺ pregnancies. This can be prevented by anti‑D vaccine.

4.7.5 Sex‑Linked Inheritance

  • Traits controlled by genes located on the sex chromosomes, especially the X chromosome, are called sex‑linked traits.
  • Males (XY) are more commonly affected by X‑linked recessive disorders because they have only one X chromosome. A single recessive allele on the X will be expressed.
  • Example: Haemophilia – a blood‑clotting disorder. Mother (carrier Xᴴ Xʰ) and normal father (Xᴴ Y) can have:
    • Carrier daughter, normal daughter, affected son, normal son.
  • Other examples: colour blindness.

4.7.6 Environmental Effects on Phenotype

  • The environment can influence the phenotype.
  • A phenocopy is an environmentally induced phenotype that mimics a genetically determined trait.
  • Example: Himalayan rabbits – when raised in moderate temperatures they have a white body with black extremities; in cold temperatures they can develop black body colouration, resembling genetically black rabbits.

4.8 Human Pedigree Analysis

A pedigree is a family tree diagram that shows the inheritance pattern of a trait over several generations.

Symbols:

  • Square = male; Circle = female.
  • Shaded (or coloured) = affected individual; unshaded = unaffected.
  • Horizontal line between a male and female = mating (marriage line).
  • Vertical line connects parents to their offspring.

Pedigrees can reveal:

  • Whether a trait is dominant or recessive.
  • Whether it is autosomal or sex‑linked.
  • Genotypes of family members.
  • Probabilities for future generations.

Examples:

  • Autosomal dominant trait appears in every generation; affected individuals have at least one affected parent.
  • Autosomal recessive trait can skip generations; affected individuals may have unaffected (carrier) parents.

4.9 Genetic Disorders

A genetic disorder is a disease caused by a change or mutation in an individual’s DNA. Mutations can occur due to errors in DNA replication or environmental factors.

Three main categories:

4.9.1 Single‑Gene (Monogenetic) Disorders

Caused by defects in one particular gene.

A. Autosomal dominant disorders

  • Expressed in heterozygous condition; only one copy of the defective allele is needed.
  • Example: Huntington’s disease – progressive neurodegenerative disorder appearing later in life.

B. Autosomal recessive disorders

  • Expressed only in homozygous condition; both parents must carry the trait.
  • Examples: Cystic fibrosis, albinism.

C. X‑linked disorders

  • Caused by genes on the X chromosome. Males are more frequently affected.
  • Example: Haemophilia (blood clotting disorder), red‑green colour blindness.

4.9.2 Chromosome Disorders

Result from changes in chromosome number or structure due to errors in cell division.

  • Aneuploidy – abnormal number of chromosomes (e.g., monosomy, trisomy).
    • Example: Down syndrome (trisomy 21) – an extra copy of chromosome 21 (2n+1).
  • Structural changes:
    • Deletion – a segment of chromosome is missing.
    • Inversion – a segment breaks, flips and reinserts.
    • Translocation – a segment moves to another chromosome.

4.9.3 Multifactorial Disorders

Caused by a combination of multiple genes and environmental/lifestyle factors (e.g., smoking, diet, pollution). Examples: diabetes, cancer.

4.10 Genetic Testing and Counselling

  • Genetic testing examines DNA to detect changes associated with genetic disorders. It can determine the likelihood of passing a disorder to offspring or diagnose conditions in a foetus (e.g., amniocentesis, chorionic villus sampling).
  • Genetic counselling is the process of evaluating family medical history, ordering and interpreting genetic tests, and helping individuals understand the risks, inheritance patterns, and options. It supports informed decision‑making about genetic health.

4.11 Gene Therapy

Gene therapy is a technique that introduces genes into a patient’s cells to treat or prevent disease.

Two types:

  • Somatic gene therapy – transfers DNA to somatic (body) cells; the effect is not passed to offspring.
  • Germline gene therapy – transfers DNA to sex cells (gametes or embryos); the change is inherited by future generations.

Mechanisms of gene therapy:

  • Replacing a disease‑causing gene with a healthy copy.
  • Inactivating a malfunctioning gene.
  • Introducing a new or modified gene to help fight disease.

Gene therapy vectors/tools:

  • Plasmid DNA – circular DNA used to carry therapeutic genes into cells.
  • Viral vectors – modified viruses that deliver genes (e.g., adenoviruses).
  • Bacterial vectors – modified bacteria used as delivery vehicles.
  • Human gene editing technology (e.g., CRISPR) – disrupts harmful genes or repairs mutations.
  • Patient‑derived cellular gene therapy – cells removed, genetically modified, and returned to the patient.

Challenges: delivering gene to the correct cells, controlling gene activation, avoiding immune responses, preventing disruption of other genes, and high cost.

4.12 Breeding

Breeding is the application of genetic principles in animal husbandry and agriculture to improve desirable traits.

  • Selective breeding (artificial selection) – choosing parents with preferred traits to mate and produce the next generation.
  • Natural selection – nature selects individuals better adapted to the environment.

Animal breeding methods:

  • Inbreeding – mating genetically similar individuals (e.g., purebred). Increases homozygosity; may expose deleterious recessive traits.
  • Crossbreeding – mating individuals from different breeds. Produces hybrid vigour (heterosis) and increases genetic diversity.

Plant breeding methods:

  • Mass selection – large numbers of plants with similar desirable phenotypes are selected; their seeds are mixed to form a new variety. Simplest form.
  • Pure line selection – individual plants from self‑pollinated crops are selected; their progeny evaluated; the best pure line is released as a variety.
  • Bulk method – F2 and later generations are harvested in bulk; individual plants selected at the end of the process.

Indigenous knowledge in Ethiopian breeding

  • Ethiopian farmers have long practiced selective breeding of livestock (cattle, sheep, goats, camels, chickens) and crops (teff, wheat, barley, sorghum, maize, coffee, enset, etc.).
  • They select for desirable traits such as growth rate, body size, disease resistance, and adaptation to local conditions.
  • Traditional knowledge complements modern breeding practices.

4.13 Bioinformatics Introduction

Bioinformatics is an interdisciplinary field combining biology, computer science, and information technology to store, organise, retrieve, and analyse biological data.

  • It provides software tools for analysing large datasets (DNA sequences, protein structures) that would be impossible to handle manually.
  • Applications:
    • Improved disease diagnosis and detection.
    • Vaccine development by screening pathogen genomes.
    • Understanding evolutionary relationships through sequence analysis.
  • Three main areas:
    • Genomics – study of genomes (DNA sequences).
    • Proteomics – study of protein function, structure, interactions and abundance.
    • Systems biology – uses computer models to study interactions between DNA, proteins, and metabolites at the cellular or organismal level.

EXAM REVISION SUMMARY

  • Genetics – study of heredity; genetic material = DNA (RNA in some viruses).
  • DNA structure: double helix of nucleotides (sugar deoxyribose, phosphate, bases A,T,C,G). A pairs with T, C with G. Two strands antiparallel (5′→3′ and 3′→5′). Discovered by Watson and Crick.
  • Gene – DNA segment coding for a trait; located on chromosomes (locus). Chromosomes = DNA + histone proteins.
  • DNA function: stores genetic information; transmits it via replication.
  • DNA replication: semi‑conservative; enzymes – helicase (unwinds), DNA polymerase (adds nucleotides), primase, ligase, topoisomerase. Three stages: unwind, synthesise (leading/lagging strands), form two double helices.
  • RNA: single‑stranded; ribose sugar; bases A,U,C,G. Types: mRNA (carries code), tRNA (brings amino acids, has anticodon), rRNA (ribosome component). Functions in protein synthesis.
  • Cell cycle: interphase (G1, S, G2) → M phase. Mitosis: division of somatic cells; 4 stages (prophase, metaphase, anaphase, telophase) + cytokinesis; produces 2 identical diploid cells.
  • Meiosis: produces gametes; two divisions (Meiosis I – separates homologous chromosomes, crossing over in Prophase I; Meiosis II – separates sister chromatids). Results in 4 genetically different haploid cells.
  • Protein synthesis: central dogma DNA→RNA→protein. Transcription (nucleus): mRNA synthesised from DNA template; codons (3‑base units). Translation (cytoplasm): ribosomes read mRNA; tRNA anticodons match codons; amino acids linked → protein. Start codon AUG; stop codons UAG, UAA, UGA.
  • Mendel: monohybrid cross (phenotypic ratio 3:1, genotypic 1:2:1). Dihybrid cross yields 9:3:3:1. Laws: Segregation (alleles separate in gamete formation), Independent Assortment (genes for different traits assort independently). Test cross: cross unknown dominant with homozygous recessive to determine genotype.
  • Sex determination: XX female, XY male; father determines sex.
  • Non‑Mendelian patterns: Co‑dominance (both alleles expressed, e.g., roan cattle); Incomplete dominance (blended phenotype, e.g., pink flowers); Multiple alleles (ABO blood: Iᴬ,Iᴮ,i); Rh factor (Rh⁺/Rh⁻, sensitisation risk); Sex‑linked traits (carried on X chromosome, males more affected, e.g., haemophilia); Environmental effects (phenocopy – temperature in Himalayan rabbits).
  • Pedigree: chart tracking trait inheritance; can identify autosomal dominant, autosomal recessive, or X‑linked patterns.
  • Genetic disorders: Single‑gene – autosomal dominant (Huntington’s), autosomal recessive (cystic fibrosis, albinism), X‑linked (haemophilia); Chromosomal – aneuploidy (Down syndrome trisomy 21), deletions, inversions, translocations; Multifactorial – diabetes, cancer.
  • Genetic testing: examines DNA for disease risk; counselling interprets results and guides families.
  • Gene therapy: introduces functional genes to treat disease; somatic (non‑heritable) or germline (heritable). Delivery methods: plasmid DNA, viral/bacterial vectors, gene editing, patient‑derived cells.
  • Breeding: selective breeding = artificial selection; inbreeding (homozygosity, may expose harmful recessives) vs crossbreeding (hybrid vigour). Plant breeding: mass selection, pure line, bulk method. Ethiopian indigenous knowledge: selection for growth, disease resistance, adapted traits.
  • Bioinformatics: uses computer tools to store and analyse biological data; genomics, proteomics, systems biology; aids disease detection, vaccine development, evolutionary studies.

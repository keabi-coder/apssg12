5.1 Human Musculoskeletal System

The musculoskeletal system is an organ system that gives the human body shape, support, protection, and the ability to move. It is composed of:

  • Bones of the skeleton
  • Muscles (keep bones in place and enable movement)
  • Cartilage (reduces friction at joints)
  • Tendons (connect muscle to bone)
  • Ligaments (fibrous connective tissues that attach bone to bone)
  • Joints (where two or more bones meet)
  • Connective tissues

Functions of the musculoskeletal system:

  • Supports the body and maintains posture
  • Protects vital organs (e.g., skull protects brain, rib cage protects heart and lungs)
  • Produces blood cells (bone marrow)
  • Stores minerals (calcium, phosphorus) and fat
  • Enables movement

5.1.1 Types of Muscles

There are three types of muscles in the human body:

Muscle Type Structure Control Location & Function
Cardiac muscle Striated, involuntary; cells joined by intercalated discs; forms the myocardium of the heart. Involuntary Pumps blood; found only in the heart wall.
Skeletal muscle Striated, voluntary; attached to bones via tendons; arranged in opposing groups around joints; makes up 30–40% of body mass. Voluntary Produces body movements, maintains posture, generates heat. A motor unit is a single motor axon and the muscle fibres it innervates.
Smooth muscle Non‑striated, involuntary; spindle‑shaped cells. Involuntary Found in walls of hollow organs (stomach, intestines, bladder, uterus, blood vessels, respiratory and reproductive tracts, eyes); controls flow of substances within lumens. In skin, the erector pili muscles cause hairs to stand erect.

5.1.2 Mechanism of Action of Skeletal Muscles

Skeletal muscles contract through the sliding filament mechanism.

Key proteins:

  • Actin – thin filaments; contains binding sites for myosin.
  • Myosin – thick filaments; molecular motor that converts ATP energy into mechanical force.
  • Troponin – regulatory protein complex on actin that binds calcium.
  • Tropomyosin – protein that blocks the myosin‑binding site on actin in a relaxed muscle.

Steps of muscle contraction:

  1. Muscle activation – a motor nerve impulse reaches the neuromuscular junction; the sarcoplasmic reticulum releases calcium ions into the muscle cell.
  2. Muscle contraction – calcium binds to troponin, causing tropomyosin to move and expose binding sites on actin. Myosin heads bind to actin forming cross‑bridges. Using energy from ATP, myosin pulls the actin filaments, shortening the sarcomere (the contractile unit of myofibrils).
  3. Recharging – ATP is re‑synthesised, allowing myosin to release, re‑cock, and re‑bind, continuing the cycle.
  4. Relaxation – nerve stimulation stops; calcium is pumped back into the sarcoplasmic reticulum, tropomyosin covers the binding sites, and actin and myosin detach.

Types of muscle contractions:

  • Isometric – muscle generates tension without changing length.
  • Concentric – muscle shortens while contracting.
  • Eccentric – muscle lengthens while contracting.

5.1.3 The Human Axial and Appendicular Skeleton

The adult human skeleton has 206 bones. Bones are composed of collagen and calcium phosphate, making them strong yet lightweight. Four types of bone cells: osteoblasts (build bone), osteocytes (mature bone cells), osteoclasts (break down bone for reshaping), and osteoprogenitor cells (repair fractures).

Bone shapes: long (femur), short (carpals), flat (skull, ribs, sternum), irregular (vertebrae), sesamoid (patella).

A. Axial skeleton (80 bones) – forms the central axis of the body.

Part Number of bones Function
Skull (cranium 8 + facial 14) 22 Protects the brain; forms facial structure
Ossicles (middle ear, 3 each side) 6 Hearing
Hyoid (neck) 1 Supports the tongue and larynx
Thoracic cage (ribs 24 + sternum 1) 25 Protects heart and lungs
Vertebral column 26 Supports the body; protects the spinal cord

B. Appendicular skeleton (126 bones) – includes limbs and girdles.

Region Number of bones
Shoulder girdles (clavicle, scapula) 4
Pelvis (hip bones) 2
Arms and forearms (humerus, radius, ulna) 6
Thighs and legs (femur, tibia, fibula) 8

Feet and ankles (tarsals, metatarsals, phalanges) 52

Functions of bones: support and movement, protection of organs, blood cell production (in bone marrow), fat storage, mineral storage and release (calcium, phosphorus, vitamin D).

5.1.4 Joints

A joint is where two or more bones meet, bound by tissues that allow movement.

Type of Joint Movement Allowed Examples
Ball‑and‑socket Movement in all directions; rotation Shoulder, hip
Hinge Movement in one plane (like a door) Elbow, knee
Condyloid Movement in two planes; no rotation Finger joints, jaw
Pivot Rotational movement around a single axis Joint between radius and ulna, neck (atlas‑axis)
Gliding (plane) Limited sliding movements Wrist (carpals), ankle

5.2 The Reproductive System

The human reproductive system produces gametes and enables internal fertilisation.

5.2.1 Male Reproductive System

External organs: penis, scrotum.
Internal organs: testes, epididymis, vas deferens, seminal vesicles, prostate gland, Cowper’s (bulbourethral) glands, urethra.

Structure Function
Testes Produce sperm (spermatogenesis) and the hormone testosterone
Seminiferous tubules Site of sperm cell production
Epididymis Maturation and storage of sperm
Vas deferens Carries sperm from epididymis to the urethra
Seminal vesicles Secrete fructose‑rich fluid that provides energy for sperm
Prostate gland Secretes alkaline buffer to protect sperm from acidic vagina
Cowper’s glands Secrete mucus‑rich fluid for lubrication and acid protection in the urethra
Urethra Passageway for urine and semen (during ejaculation)
Penis Copulatory organ; deposits sperm into the vagina. Erection occurs when blood engorges the erectile tissues (corpus cavernosa and corpus spongiosum); ejaculation expels semen.

External organs (vulva): labia majora, labia minora, clitoris, Bartholin’s glands.

5.2.3 Gametogenesis

Gametogenesis is the formation of haploid gametes from diploid germ cells by meiosis.

  • Spermatogenesis (in testes): Primordial germ cells → spermatogonia (diploid) → mitotic divisions → primary spermatocytes → meiosis I → secondary spermatocytes (haploid, 23 chromosomes) → meiosis II → spermatids → spermiogenesis → mature spermatozoa (sperm). Maturation occurs in the epididymis.
  • Oogenesis (in ovaries): Oogonia → primary oocytes (arrested in prophase I until puberty) → after puberty, one primary oocyte completes meiosis I → secondary oocyte (haploid) + first polar body; meiosis II occurs after fertilisation, yielding an ovum and a second polar body.

5.2.4 The Menstrual Cycle

The menstrual cycle is a monthly series of changes in the female reproductive system preparing for pregnancy. Average length is 28 days (range 20–45 days). It involves ovarian and uterine changes regulated by hormones.

Phases of the menstrual cycle:

Phase Days (approx.) Events
Menstruation 1–4 Shedding of the uterine lining (endometrium) as menstrual blood; oestrogen and progesterone levels are low.
Follicular phase 5–13 FSH from anterior pituitary stimulates follicle development; maturing follicle produces oestrogen, which thickens the endometrium.
Ovulation 14 (mid‑cycle) LH surge triggers release of a mature secondary oocyte from the ovary.
Luteal phase 15–28 Ruptured follicle becomes the corpus luteum; secretes progesterone (and some oestrogen) to maintain the thickened endometrium. If no fertilisation occurs, corpus luteum degenerates, hormone levels drop, and menstruation begins.

Hormonal regulation (positive and negative feedback):

  • FSH (Follicle Stimulating Hormone): stimulates follicle growth and oestrogen production.
  • Oestrogen: at low levels exerts negative feedback on FSH; at high levels (around day 12–14) exerts positive feedback on the hypothalamus and pituitary, causing the LH surge that triggers ovulation.
  • LH (Luteinizing Hormone): triggers ovulation and promotes formation of the corpus luteum; stimulates progesterone secretion.
  • Progesterone: maintains the endometrium; inhibits FSH and LH secretion (negative feedback). If fertilisation occurs, the embryo produces hCG to maintain the corpus luteum. If not, progesterone drops → menstruation.

Positive feedback leads to ovulation; negative feedback maintains hormonal balance and leads to menstruation when pregnancy does not occur.

5.2.5 Fertilisation and Pregnancy

  • Fertilisation: Union of sperm and egg (each with 23 chromosomes) in the ampulla of the fallopian tube, forming a diploid zygote (46 chromosomes). The zygote undergoes cleavage as it moves to the uterus.
  • Implantation: About 6 days after fertilisation, the embryo attaches to the endometrium; completed within 9–10 days.
  • Pregnancy (gestation): Period of foetal development inside the uterus; lasts about 40 weeks (280 days) from the last menstrual period. Signs: missed period, tender breasts, morning sickness, frequent urination, positive pregnancy test.

5.2.6 Mechanism of Action of Contraceptives

Contraception is the intentional prevention of pregnancy through various methods.

A. Barrier methods – physically block sperm from reaching the egg.

  • Male condom, female condom, diaphragm, cervical cap. (Condoms are the only method that also helps prevent STIs.)
  • Diaphragm: soft silicone dome placed in the vagina to cover the cervix.

B. Non‑barrier methods:

  • Chemical barriers / spermicides: foams, creams, gels, suppositories that kill sperm; often used with barrier methods.
  • Oral contraceptive pills (COC): contain oestrogen and progesterone; inhibit ovulation, thicken cervical mucus, thin the endometrium. Progesterone‑only pills (POP) mainly thicken cervical mucus.
  • Contraceptive injection (Depo‑Provera): synthetic progesterone; inhibits ovulation.
  • Contraceptive implant: a small rod placed under the skin releasing progesterone; effective for 3 years.
  • Intrauterine device (IUD): T‑shaped device (copper or hormonal) inserted into the uterus; prevents fertilisation and implantation.
  • Contraceptive ring (vaginal ring): releases oestrogen and progesterone.
  • Post‑pill (emergency contraceptive): levonorgestrel taken within 72 hours after unprotected sex; prevents or delays ovulation.
  • Sterilisation: permanent method.
    • Tubal ligation (female) – fallopian tubes are cut/blocked.
    • Vasectomy (male) – vas deferens are cut/blocked.

Summary of contraceptives (failure rates, mode of action, advantages, disadvantages):

  • Oral contraceptives (0.3–5% failure): inhibit ovulation; highly effective, regulate cycle; not for smokers over 35.
  • Injectable contraceptives (~1%): inhibit ovulation; long‑lasting; may cause irregular bleeding, delayed return of fertility.
  • IUD (1%): prevents fertilisation/implantation; continuous protection for years; may increase cramps and infection risk.
  • Spermicides (3–20%): kill sperm; no side effects; messy, must be applied before sex.
  • Diaphragm (3–14%): blocks cervix; no side effects; must be fitted and left in place.
  • Condom (2.6–14%): barrier; protects against STIs/HIV; may break.
  • Sterilisation (tubal ligation ~0.04%, vasectomy ~0.15%): most reliable; permanent; requires surgery.

5.2.7 Causes of Infertility in Humans

Infertility is the inability to conceive after a year of regular unprotected intercourse.

Female infertility causes:

  • Ovulatory disorders (manifested by absent or irregular periods)
  • Thyroid problems (overactive or underactive)
  • Pelvic surgery complications (tubal damage, scarring)
  • Cervical mucus problems (hostile to sperm)
  • Fibroids (non‑cancerous growths in uterus)
  • Endometriosis (endometrial tissue outside uterus)
  • Pelvic inflammatory disease (infection of upper genital tract)
  • Sterilisation
  • Medications and drugs (marijuana, cocaine, heavy cannabis, blood pressure meds, antidepressants, antipsychotics)
  • Alcohol misuse

Male infertility causes:

  • Low sperm count (<15 million/mL or total <39 million per ejaculate)
  • Abnormal sperm shape or function; immotile sperm
  • Blockages preventing sperm delivery
  • Hormonal imbalances
  • Varicocele (dilated veins in scrotum)
  • Undescended testicles
  • Infections, genetic disorders
  • Lifestyle factors: smoking, alcohol, drugs, obesity, exposure to toxins, overheating testicles

5.2.8 Major Sexually Transmitted Infections (STIs) in Ethiopia

STIs are communicable diseases transmitted primarily through sexual contact. Caused by bacteria, viruses, or parasites.

A. Bacterial STIs

Disease Causative Agent Key Symptoms Prevention/Treatment
Bacterial vaginosis Overgrowth of vaginal bacteria Thin grey/white/green discharge, fishy odour, itching, burning Medical treatment; protective sexual contact; prevalence 2.8–19.4% in Ethiopia
Chlamydia Chlamydia trachomatis Often asymptomatic; discharge, burning Protective sex; antibiotics
Syphilis Treponema pallidum Primary: painless chancre; Secondary: rash on palms/soles; Latent: no symptoms; Tertiary: gummatous lesions, cardiovascular/neurological damage Protective sex; penicillin (medical treatment)
Gonorrhea Neisseria gonorrhoeae Painful urination, discharge; may be asymptomatic Protective sex; antibiotics
Chancroid Haemophilus ducreyi Painful necrotizing genital ulcer, swollen lymph nodes Protective sex; medical treatment

B. Viral STIs

Disease Causative Agent Key Features
Genital herpes Herpes simplex virus (HSV‑1 mainly oral, HSV‑2 mainly genital) Painful blisters/sores, transmitted by skin‑to‑skin contact including sexual contact. Recurrent; no cure, but antiviral drugs manage symptoms.
Genital warts Human papillomavirus (HPV) Warts on genitals/anus; some high‑risk HPV types cause cervical and throat cancer. Vaccine available.
Molluscum contagiosum Poxvirus (Molluscum contagiosum virus) Small round painless bumps with a central dent; self‑limiting; spread by skin contact.
HIV/AIDS Human Immunodeficiency Virus (retrovirus) Attacks CD4 T‑cells, weakening immune system. Three stages: acute (flu‑like), chronic (asymptomatic latency), AIDS (CD4 <200 cells/mm³, opportunistic infections). Transmitted through blood, semen, vaginal fluids, breast milk. No cure; antiretroviral therapy (ART) manages infection.
Hepatitis B Hepatitis B virus Attacks liver; can cause cirrhosis and liver cancer; transmitted via blood, sexual contact, from mother to child. Vaccine is safe and effective.

C. Parasitic STIs

  • Pubic lice (crabs): parasites that attach to pubic hair; transmitted by close physical/sexual contact or shared bedding.
  • Trichomoniasis: caused by Trichomonas vaginalis; foul‑smelling, frothy discharge, itching, burning; treatable with antimicrobials.
  • Scabies: caused by a mite (Sarcoptes scabiei); intense itching and skin burrows; spread by prolonged skin contact, including sexual contact.

5.2.9 Epidemiology of STIs and Risk Factors in Ethiopia

Epidemiology is the study of disease occurrence, transmission, and control. In Ethiopia, STIs are a major public health problem, particularly among 15–24‑year‑olds. About 1.4 million new STIs occur globally every day.

Risk factors for STIs:

  • Unprotected sex (inconsistent or no condom use)
  • Multiple sexual partners
  • History of previous STIs
  • Being forced into sexual activity
  • Misuse of alcohol or use of recreational drugs (impairs judgement)
  • Injecting drugs (sharing needles transmits HIV, hepatitis B and C)
  • Young age (15–24 years: half of new STIs)

5.3 Harmful Traditional Practices

Harmful traditional practices violate human rights and affect physical and mental health, especially reproductive health. In Ethiopia, about 20 such practices have been recorded; 73% involve female genital mutilation (FGM).

Examples:

  • Early marriage: forcing underage girls to marry; leads to school dropout, early pregnancy, health risks.
  • Kidnapping/abduction for marriage.
  • Gender‑based violence: unwanted sexual contact, harassment, domestic violence.
  • Female Genital Mutilation (FGM): partial or total removal of external female genitalia for non‑medical reasons.

5.4 Family Planning

Family planning is the ability of individuals and couples to anticipate and attain their desired number of children, and the spacing and timing of births. It considers health, financial, social, and environmental resources.

Risks of lack of family planning:

  • Increased maternal health risks (complications during pregnancy, childbirth, postpartum)
  • Higher risk for teenage mothers (life‑threatening complications)
  • Illness or death of a mother threatens the whole family
  • Increased unsafe abortions, disability, and death

Family planning actions include:

  • Preventing unwanted pregnancy
  • Safe sexual practices to avoid STIs
  • Waiting at least 6 months after miscarriage or abortion before next pregnancy
  • Use of contraceptives and fertility management
  • Adoption
  • Tracking the menstrual cycle (family calendar)

Family planning services:

  • Pregnancy testing and counselling
  • Preconception health services (achieving pregnancy)
  • Basic infertility counselling
  • STI counselling
  • Breast and pelvic examinations
  • Cervical and breast cancer screening

5.5 Effects of Alcohol Use, Khat, Cannabis, and Other Drugs on STI Transmission and Unwanted Pregnancy

5.5.1 Effects of Alcohol

  • Short‑term: euphoria at 0.1% blood alcohol; higher concentrations cause sleepiness, confusion, coma (0.25–0.30%).
  • Long‑term: brain damage (vitamin deficiencies), liver cirrhosis, dementia, J‑shaped legs, colorectal and colon cancer, pancreatitis, cardiovascular disease.
  • Alcohol contributes to 6.2% of male and 1.1% of female deaths globally.
  • Alcohol impairs judgement, increasing risky sexual behaviour and HIV transmission.

5.5.2 Effects of Chewing Khat

  • Khat (Catha edulis) leaves contain cathinone, a stimulant.
  • Immediate effects: alertness, euphoria, increased heart rate and blood pressure, insomnia, suppressed appetite, talkativeness, psychosis.
  • Long‑term effects: depression, hypertension, gastritis, constipation, acute coronary syndrome, myocardial infarction, metabolic disorders, diabetes, hepatitis, liver cirrhosis, impotence (loss of libido), oral cancer, hallucinations.

5.5.3 Effects of Drug Use

  • Marijuana (Cannabis sativa): harms brain, breathing, heart, child development, muscle coordination.
  • Cocaine (Erythroxylum coca): harms cardiovascular, respiratory, gastrointestinal, and nervous systems; highly addictive.
  • Heroin (from opium poppy): highly addictive; can cause collapsed veins, heart infections, liver disease, overdose.
  • Inhalants: household chemicals inhaled for mood alteration; damage brain and other organs.
  • General drug effects: increased risk of infections (including HIV, syphilis), heart conditions, seizures, stroke, mental confusion, brain damage, liver damage, memory and decision‑making problems. Drug‑fuelled unprotected sex increases STIs and unwanted pregnancy.

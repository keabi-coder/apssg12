6.1 Population Ecology

Ecology is called the study of the interaction between organisms and the physical environment. Ecological organisation levels: individual, population, community, ecosystem, biosphere, biome.

Population is called a group of interacting individuals of the same species living and interbreeding within a given area.

Population ecology is called the study of the processes of interaction and changes that affect the distribution and abundance of populations in the environment. Scientists use statistical measures (demographic parameters) to investigate how populations respond to environmental changes.

6.1.1 Population Size, Density, and Dispersal

Population size (N) – the total number of individuals present in a particular habitat.

  • It is difficult to count every individual; scientists use sampling techniques to estimate population size.

  • Two common sampling methods:

    • Quadrat method – used for immobile organisms (plants, slow‑moving small animals). A quadrat is a square frame (wood, plastic, metal) placed on the ground; individuals within the boundary are counted. Many quadrats are placed randomly throughout the habitat.
    • Mark‑recapture method – used for mobile animals. Steps:
      1. Capture a sample, mark them (tags, bands, paint), and release.
      2. Later, capture a second sample containing both marked and unmarked individuals.
      3. Estimate total population size (N) using:
        N=M×nXN = \frac{M \times n}{X}N=XM×n​
        where M = number marked initially, n = total number captured in second sample, X = number of marked individuals recaptured.

  • Population density – the number of individuals per unit area or volume.
    Density=Total number of individualsTotal land area\text{Density} = \frac{\text{Total number of individuals}}{\text{Total land area}}Density=Total land areaTotal number of individuals​
    It is a dynamic characteristic; birth and immigration increase density, death and emigration decrease it.

  • Human population study – uses a census, a systematic collection of information from all individuals in a population. A census collects data on age, sex, ethnic group, religion, marital status, household size, occupation, etc.

    • Two methods:
      • De Facto method – counting takes place on a fixed date (often a full‑moon night); everyone present at their residence is counted.
      • De Jure method – enumeration occurs over two to three weeks; only permanent residents are counted; temporary visitors are not.

  • Population dispersal – how individuals are distributed in space at a given time. Three patterns:

    • Uniform dispersion – individuals spaced evenly.
    • Random dispersion – no predictable pattern.
    • Clumped dispersion – individuals grouped in clusters.

Dispersal pattern provides information about how individuals interact with each other and the environment.

6.1.2 Exponential and Logistic Growth in Populations

Population growth rate is the percentage rate of change in the number of individuals over a specified period. It measures how fast population size changes.

Two models describe population growth:

A. Exponential growth model

  • Describes growth without environmental limits; population size (N) increases without constraints.
  • Occurs in an ideal environment with unlimited resources.
  • Graph shows a J‑shaped curve.
  • Equation:
    dNdt=rN\frac{dN}{dt} = rNdtdN​=rN
    where dN/dt = growth rate, N = population size, r = per capita rate of increase (r = birth rate − death rate).
  • r can be positive (population grows), zero (no change – zero population growth), or negative (population declines).
  • When r is constant and positive, the maximum per capita rate of increase is called rₘₐₓ, which varies among species.
  • Refined formula:
    dNdt=rmaxN\frac{dN}{dt} = r_{\text{max}} NdtdN​=rmax​N

B. Logistic growth model

  • Describes growth that levels off due to limited resources.
  • Graph shows an S‑shaped curve.
  • Carrying capacity (K) is the maximum population size that the available resources in an environment can sustain.
  • As population size approaches K, growth rate slows and eventually stops.
  • Equation:
    dNdt=rN(K−NK)\frac{dN}{dt} = rN \left(\frac{K - N}{K}\right)dtdN​=rN(KK−N​)
    When N is small, growth is nearly exponential; as N approaches K, (K–N)/K approaches zero, so growth rate approaches zero.

Comparison: Exponential vs Logistic Growth

Feature Exponential Growth Logistic Growth
Resources Unlimited Limited
Carrying capacity Not considered Included (K)
Graph shape J‑shaped curve S‑shaped curve
Growth rate Constant (r) Decreases as N approaches K

6.1.3 Demographic Structure

Demography describes the dynamics of a population over time. Demographic parameters include:

  • Crude birth rate – number of live births per 1000 people per year.
  • Crude death rate – annual number of deaths per 1000 people.
  • Infant mortality rate – annual number of deaths of children under 1 year per 1000 live births.
  • Life expectancy – number of years a person can expect to live based on current mortality rates.
  • Fertility rate – measure of the number of children born.
  • Mortality rate – measure of the number of people who die.
  • Migration – movement of people from one area to another.
    • Immigration – movement into an area for permanent residence.
    • Emigration – movement out of an area to another place of permanent residence.

Age and sex are the most commonly studied elements of population composition. Younger people are more likely to reproduce; older people have higher mortality.

Population pyramid – a graphical representation of the age and sex composition of a population. It evaluates development and helps predict needs for schools, hospitals, etc.

Three types of population pyramids:

Type Shape Characteristics Examples
Expansive Broad base, narrow top Young and growing; high fertility, lower life expectancy; large percentage in young age groups. Many developing countries (e.g., Kenya)
Constrictive Narrower base Elderly and shrinking; smaller percentage of young people; high economic development, access to health and education. Developed countries (e.g., United States, Italy)
Stationary Roughly equal proportions across age groups Not growing or shrinking; stable; low birth rates, high quality of life. Some developed countries

Steps to draw a population pyramid:

  1. Collect population data classified into males/females and 5‑year age groups.
  2. Compute the percentage each group makes up of the total population.
  3. Draw a central axis; plot percentages for males on the left, females on the right.
  4. Use shading to distinguish males and females.

Ethiopian population pyramid: Ethiopia has a young population. According to the 2007 census, 45% of the population was under 15 years; only 4.8% were aged 60 or older. Women aged 15–49 accounted for 23% of the total population. The pyramid shape is expansive.

6.1.4 Survivorship Curves

A survivorship curve is a graph of the number of individuals surviving at each age interval versus time. Three types:

Type Characteristics Examples
Type I Few offspring, high parental care; most survive to adulthood; mortality high in old age. Humans, most mammals
Type II Moderate offspring, some parental care; constant mortality rate throughout life span. Some birds
Type III Many offspring, little or no care; high early mortality; survivors live longer. Plants, invertebrates, many fish

6.1.5 Population Regulation

Two categories of factors regulate population growth:

A. Density‑dependent factors – biotic (living) factors whose effect depends on population density.

  • Predation
  • Competition (intraspecific and interspecific)
  • Accumulation of waste
  • Diseases (spread faster in dense populations)
  • Low prey density increases predator mortality.

B. Density‑independent factors – abiotic (non‑living) factors that affect population regardless of density.

  • Weather
  • Natural disasters (earthquakes, floods, droughts, tornadoes, wildfires)
  • Pollution

6.2 Natural Resources

Natural resources are any natural substances or materials available in the environment that are used by human beings. They include oil, coal, natural gas, metals, stone, air, sunlight, soil, water, animals, birds, fish, and plants. They provide food, fuel, clothing, heat, light, and power.

Classification of natural resources:

Category Definition Examples
Renewable resources Can be replaced after utilisation; not exhausted by continuous use. However, can be depleted if overused or poorly managed. Sunlight, air, trees, water, wind, tidal energy, solar energy, wind energy, biomass energy, hydropower
Non‑renewable resources Do not replenish at the speed they are consumed; take millions of years to form. Rate of consumption exceeds rate of recovery. Minerals, fossil fuels, coal, natural gas, oil

6.3 Conservation of Natural Resources in Ethiopia

Conservation is the care, protection, and wise use of natural resources so they are available for future generations. It includes preservation, management, or restoration of natural environments and ecological communities. Conservation aims for sustainable social and economic utilisation.

Ethiopia has diverse natural resources due to its topography and varied climate. However, these resources are threatened unless conservation mechanisms are implemented.

6.3.1 Wildlife Conservation

Wildlife – living things that live in natural environments and are not yet domesticated. Includes wild animals.

Importance of wildlife:

  • Maintain natural ecological processes.
  • Store genetic material for the future.
  • Support wildlife tourism, contributing to the national economy.
  • Used to manufacture goods.

Examples of Ethiopian wildlife: Lion, Stressemann’s Bushcrow, Walia Ibex, Ethiopian Mountain Chameleon.

Causes of wildlife loss and decline:

  • Natural disasters: earthquakes, floods, droughts, tornadoes, wildfires.
  • Human activities: agricultural expansion, development activities, deforestation, poaching, illegal wildlife trade, habitat change, climate change, overexploitation, pollution.

Conservation mechanisms for wildlife:

  • Ethiopia has established protected areas:
    • 24 National Parks (e.g., Abijata Shala, Alatish, Awash, Bale Mountains, Gambella, Geralle, Nechsar, Omo, Simien Mountains, Yangudi Rassa, Mago, Yabello)
    • 2 Wildlife Sanctuaries (Babile Elephant Sanctuary, Senkele Swayne’s Hartebeest Sanctuary)
    • 6 Wildlife Reserves (Alledeghi, Awash West, Bale, etc.)
    • 5 Community Conservation Areas (Abune Yosef, Guassa Menz, Tama)
    • Over 10 Controlled Hunting Areas

6.3.2 Plant Conservation

Ethiopia is a centre for crop genetic diversity with rich endemic plant species. Examples: Mekmeko (Rumex abyssinicus), African juniper (Juniperus procera), Cabbage tree (Moringa stenopetala), Kosso (Hagenia abyssinica).

Causes of plant loss and decline:

  • Natural disasters (same as for wildlife)
  • Human activities: deforestation, climate change, invasive species, overexploitation, pollution, land degradation.

Conservation mechanisms for plants:

A. Indigenous practices

  • Taboos or forbidden practices against using certain species.
  • Domestication.
  • Reserves and sacred groves.
  • Selective harvesting.
  • Protection in church/mosque compounds, graveyards, monasteries.
  • Traditional farming systems maintain rich genetic diversity of vegetables and crops through selection, transfer, exchange, and conservation.

B. Modern practices

  • Establishing botanical gardens.
  • Controlling invasive species.
  • Recovery and restoration programmes.
  • Gene banks (e.g., Ethiopian national gene bank) for seed preservation.
  • In situ conservation (in natural habitat) and ex situ conservation (outside natural habitat).

6.3.3 Soil and Water Conservation

Ethiopia has large soil and water resources. Climate, topography, parent material, and land use have produced diverse soil types and water resources. Water potential includes lakes, river basins, and groundwater. The Grand Ethiopian Renaissance Dam (GERD) on the Abay (Nile) River generates hydropower and benefits Ethiopia and neighbouring countries.

Problems: soil erosion, decline in water quantity and quality, affecting agriculture, environment, and health.

Causes: deforestation, poor land husbandry, industrial and domestic wastes.

Conservation mechanisms:

A. Mechanical measures

  • Constructing bunds
  • Terraces
  • Diversion ditches
  • Check dams
  • Micro‑basins
  • Hillside terraces

B. Biological measures

  • Enclosing degraded land from human and animal interference.
  • Tree seedling production.
  • Planting trees on farmlands (agro‑forestry).
  • Afforestation.
  • Tree plantations around homesteads.
  • Example: The Green Legacy initiative (launched June 2019) aims to build a green, climate‑resilient Ethiopia.

6.4 Impact of Traffic Accidents on Wild and Domestic Animals

  • Road traffic accidents are a global public health and development problem, killing and injuring people and destroying property.
  • Developing countries, including Ethiopia, account for a large number of global road traffic deaths.
  • Car accidents are a major cause of death for both wild and domestic animals, even those in conservation areas.
  • In Ethiopia, many animals are killed by car accidents. A 2017 study in Geralle National Park found that 29% of wildlife mortality was due to car accidents.
  • Methods to reduce animal roadkill:
    • Design structures for safe animal passage.
    • Construct underpasses to promote habitat connectivity and natural movement.
    • Consider wildlife habitats when constructing roads.
    • Drive slowly in wildlife areas.

6.5 Impact of Human Activities on the Environment

Humans depend on the environment for survival, but activities to improve living standards have negative environmental impacts that affect present and future generations.

Major human activities affecting the environment:

  • Burning coal and natural gas
  • Using refrigerants and coolants
  • Applying pesticides
  • Waste incineration and waste disposal
  • Using various volatile chemicals
  • Agricultural activities
  • Industrial and sewage discharges
  • Construction of infrastructure and transportation
  • Urban development
  • Mining activities

Resulting environmental problems:

  • Environmental pollution
  • Climate change
  • Global warming
  • Acid rain
  • Ozone layer depletion
  • Loss of biodiversity
  • Toxic bioaccumulation
  • Resource depletion

6.5.1 Environmental Pollution

Environmental pollution is the contamination of environmental components that adversely affect normal environmental processes. It is the addition of substances or energy to the environment faster than they can be dispersed, diluted, decomposed, recycled, or stored harmlessly.

Causes: mainly human activities (rapid population growth, industrialisation); also natural events (forest fires, volcanoes).

Types of pollution:

  • Air pollution
  • Water pollution
  • Land pollution
  • Noise pollution
  • Light pollution

Air pollution

  • Contamination of the atmosphere by harmful gases, dust, and smoke.
  • Causes: carbon monoxide, nitrogen dioxide, ozone, lead, sulfur dioxide, smog, particulate matter from transportation, industries, burning fossil fuels, refrigerants, volatile chemicals.
  • Effects:
    • Leading environmental health risk; major cause of environmental degradation.
    • Depletion of the ozone layer.
    • Respiratory and cardiovascular diseases in humans.
    • Changes soil pH, causes acid rain.
    • Infects and toxifies plants and the food chain.

Water pollution

  • Contamination of water bodies that interferes with beneficial water use.
  • Causes: factories, industrial agricultural wastes, sewage, oil transportation, chemical fertilisers, insecticides, herbicides, pathogenic microorganisms, water hyacinth (Emboch).
  • Effects:
    • Depletion of fish populations.
    • Destruction of biodiversity.
    • Waterborne diseases leading to death.

6.5.2 Climate Change

Climate is the usual weather of a place. Climate change is a change in the usual patterns of weather (rainfall, temperature, etc.) for a month or season.

Causes:

  • Natural variations: energy, water, carbon exchange between atmosphere, oceans, land, ice; volcanic eruptions; changes in solar energy.
  • Human activities: combustion of fossil fuels increases levels of greenhouse gases – carbon dioxide (CO₂), methane, nitrous oxide, fluorinated gases.

Effects:

  • Greenhouse gases trap the sun’s heat (greenhouse effect), preventing it from returning to space, leading to global warming.
  • Acts like a blanket around the Earth, raising atmospheric, ocean, and land surface temperatures.

6.5.3 Global Warming

Global warming is the average rapid rise in Earth’s temperature, caused by greenhouse gases from burning fossil fuels and industries.

Effects:

  • Flooding
  • Melting of ice caps and glaciers
  • Rising sea levels
  • Loss of species
  • Health problems
  • Drought
  • Extinctions
  • Heavy rainfall and flooding
  • High heat stress and health risks
  • Poverty and displacement

6.5.4 Ozone Layer Depletion

Ozone (O₃) is made of three oxygen atoms. It is toxic but plays a critical role in the stratosphere (one of four atmospheric layers: troposphere, stratosphere, mesosphere, thermosphere). The ozone layer absorbs harmful UV radiation from the sun, preventing skin cancer and other damage.

Ozone depletion (ozone hole) – erosion of the protective ozone layer.

Cause: Release of chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs) (from refrigerators and fire extinguishers), hydrofluorocarbons (HCFCs), and halons. CFCs cause chemical reactions that break down ozone.

  • Chlorine reacts with ozone: Cl + O₃ → ClO + O₂
  • Chlorine monoxide then reacts with oxygen atoms: ClO + O → Cl + O₂
  • The released chlorine atom can destroy another ozone molecule, creating a catalytic cycle.

Effects:

  • Increased UV rays → skin cancer, cataracts, weakened immune system, premature skin ageing, respiratory diseases (chest pain, difficulty breathing, throat irritation).
  • Affects terrestrial and aquatic animals and plants.
  • Increases formation of ground‑level ozone (smog).

6.5.5 Acid Rain

Acid rain is rain with more acidic content (lower pH) than natural rain.

Causes: Acid‑forming pollutants dissolve in water vapour in the atmosphere. Major sources: burning fossil fuels (coal, oil), electric power plants, industrial processes, vehicle exhausts (release sulfur dioxide, SO₂, and nitrogen oxides, NOₓ).

Chemical reactions:

  • 2SO₂ (g) + O₂ (g) + 2H₂O (l) → 2H₂SO₄ (aq) (sulfuric acid)
  • 4NO₂ (g) + O₂ (g) + 2H₂O (l) → 4HNO₃ (aq) (nitric acid)
  • CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃ (carbonic acid, making rain slightly acidic naturally)

Effects:

  • Damages plant leaves (strips waxy coating), leaches nutrients (calcium, magnesium, potassium) causing drying.
  • Prevents seed germination; degrades soil nutrients; dissolves aluminium in soil, inhibiting plant growth.
  • Affects soil microorganisms, aquatic animals (fish, amphibians).
  • Reduces water quality for drinking; leaches heavy metals (iron, lead, copper) from pipes.
  • Damages buildings and monuments made of stone and metal.

6.5.6 Loss of Biodiversity

Biodiversity is the variety of life on Earth in terms of genes, species, individual organisms, and biological communities from ecosystem to global biosphere.

Loss of biodiversity – a decrease in the number of genes, species, and biological communities.

Causes:

  • Habitat loss and degradation: fragmentation, destruction, transformation of natural habitat reduces/eliminates food and living space.
  • Overexploitation: capturing/harvesting organisms beyond their capacity to recover.
  • Pollution: contaminates environment; causes health problems, death, reproductive failure.
  • Climate change: warming endangers species adapted to cold (latitude/altitude).
  • Invasive species: non‑native species disrupt ecosystems; outcompete native species.

Effects:

  • Threatens Earth’s ability to provide food, water, fertile soils, pest/disease protection.
  • Lowers ecosystem productivity and quality of services.
  • Affects economic systems; humans rely on plants/animals for food, building materials, medicines.
  • Loss of species means lost opportunities for new pharmaceuticals from rare organisms.

6.5.7 Toxic Bioaccumulation

Bioaccumulation is an increase in the concentration of chemicals in living organisms’ body tissues over time. Persistent bioaccumulative toxic substances (PBTs) do not degrade easily; they accumulate in fatty tissues and increase in concentration through the food chain.

Sources: synthetic chemicals containing halogens (fluorine, chlorine, bromine), DDT, metals (lead, mercury).

Process of bioaccumulation:

  1. Plants absorb small amounts of toxins (pesticides, pollutants).
  2. Primary consumers eat plants → toxin accumulated in low concentrations.
  3. Secondary consumers eat primary consumers → toxin absorbed and concentrated.
  4. Process repeats up trophic levels; highest concentration in top predators.

Effects:

  • PBTs are not degraded by sunlight, environmental reactions, or bacteria; resistant to metabolism.
  • Adverse health effects: DNA damage (mutagenic), cancer, neurological toxicity, reproductive toxicity, developmental toxicity, immune system damage.
  • Lead exposure impairs brain development in foetuses, infants, children.
  • Mercury damages nervous and reproductive systems in mammals, including humans.
  • DDT thins eggshells of birds of prey, causing them to break during incubation.

6.5.8 Resource Depletion

Resource depletion occurs when consumption of natural resources exceeds their recovery rate, making them scarce. Caused primarily by human activities and increasing global population.

Major causes:

  1. Overpopulation – consistent global population increase raises demand for resources.
  2. Poor farming practices – poor irrigation, poor soil management, heavy machinery destroy soil structure; excessive pesticides/herbicides kill soil microorganisms essential for nutrient replenishment.
  3. Overconsumption – large‑scale mineral and oil exploration; technological advances allow deeper and easier exploitation of ores, leading to production decline.
  4. Industrial and technological development – release of toxins and chemical by‑products; increased demand for natural resources accelerates depletion.

6.6 Indigenous Conservation Practices in Ethiopia

Indigenous knowledge is a body of knowledge built over generations by a group of local people living in a particular environment. It includes understanding of natural phenomena, resource use, conservation, and seasonal predictions (e.g., astronomy, weather patterns, crop productivity).

Ethiopian people have indigenous knowledge in:

  • Conservation of soil, water, crop seeds (selection and preservation), forests, medicinal plants, animals.
  • Predicting rainy season, drought, weather, productivity based on observation of sun, moon, stars, animal behaviour, winds, temperature.
  • Developing their own calendar.
  • Treating diseases.
  • Sustaining agricultural production.

Example: The Konso People – their soil and water conservation practices have been practiced for over 400 years and are registered by UNESCO as a world heritage.

Konso indigenous conservation practices:

Practice Description
Terracing Stone structures built on hilly areas to intercept runoff, reduce soil erosion, conserve soil and water. Retain soil, collect water, discharge excess. Konso Cultural Landscape is characterised by extensive dry stone terraces.
Contour ploughing Farming along the contours of a hill to reduce water runoff and prevent soil erosion.
Crop rotation Planting different crops sequentially on the same land to improve soil fertility and reduce pests and weeds. Alternating high‑residue crops with low‑residue crops.
Mixed cropping (intercropping) Growing two or more species on the same field at the same time (e.g., maize and groundnuts). Maintains soil fertility, cost‑effective.
Surface mulching Applying a layer of material (crop residue, branches) to the soil surface to conserve moisture, improve fertility, reduce weed growth.
Agro‑forestry Integrating trees on farmland; very common in Konso. Example: Moringa stenopetala (locally called Moringa). Trees play a role in soil conservation and have cultural significance.

Guassa Community Conservation Area (Menz, central highlands) – practiced for over 400 years under the Qero communal management system. The area is closed for 3–5 years, then opened for harvesting thatch grass and livestock grazing based on grass recovery, community needs, crop harvest success, and drought frequency. Elected headmen decide the timing.

EXAM REVISION SUMMARY

  • Population ecology – study of how populations interact with the environment; uses size (N), density, dispersal, growth rate.
  • Population size estimation: quadrat method (plants) – count individuals in random squares and extrapolate using N = (A/a) × n. Mark‑recapture method (animals) – N = (M × n)/X. Human population studied by census (De Facto – one fixed date; De Jure – enumeration period).
  • Population density: number of individuals per unit area; changes with births, deaths, immigration, emigration.
  • Dispersal patterns: uniform, random, clumped.
  • Population growth models:
    • Exponential growth: dN/dt = rN; J‑shaped curve; r = birth rate − death rate; r > 0 grows, r = 0 zero growth, r < 0 declines; rₘₐₓ under ideal conditions.
    • Logistic growth: dN/dt = rN[(K–N)/K]; S‑shaped curve; K = carrying capacity (maximum sustainable population).
  • Demographic parameters: crude birth rate, crude death rate, infant mortality rate, life expectancy, fertility rate, mortality rate, migration (immigration/emigration). Age‑sex structure influences growth (younger populations grow faster).
  • Population pyramid: expansive (young, growing, broad base – e.g., Ethiopia, Kenya), constrictive (elderly, shrinking), stationary (stable). Ethiopia has a young population; 45% under 15 years (2007 census).
  • Survivorship curves: Type I (high parental care, most survive to old age – humans, mammals), Type II (constant mortality – some birds), Type III (many offspring, low care, high early mortality – plants, fish).
  • Population regulation: Density‑dependent factors (biotic: predation, competition, disease, waste accumulation) effect increases with density. Density‑independent factors (abiotic: weather, natural disasters, pollution) affect regardless of density.
  • Natural resources: renewable (sunlight, water, air, trees, wind, solar, biomass, hydropower) can be replaced but may deplete if overused. Non‑renewable (fossil fuels, minerals, coal, natural gas) do not replenish at consumption rate.
  • Conservation: wise use and protection of resources for future generations.
  • Wildlife in Ethiopia: national economic and ecological value. Loss due to natural disasters and human activities (deforestation, poaching, habitat change, pollution). Conservation: 24 national parks, 2 sanctuaries, 6 reserves, 5 community conservation areas, controlled hunting areas.
  • Plant conservation: indigenous practices (taboos, sacred groves, selective harvesting, church/mosque compounds) and modern practices (botanical gardens, gene banks, in‑situ/ex‑situ conservation).
  • Soil and water conservation: mechanical (terraces, bunds, check dams, diversion ditches) and biological (area enclosure, afforestation, agro‑forestry, Green Legacy initiative). GERD on Abay River generates hydropower.
  • Traffic accidents: major cause of wildlife mortality (29% in Geralle National Park, 2017 study). Mitigation: safe passages, underpasses, road planning, slower driving.
  • Human impacts on environment:
    • Pollution: air (CO, NO₂, SO₂, lead, smog – causes respiratory disease, acid rain, ozone depletion); water (industrial waste, sewage, fertilisers, water hyacinth – causes biodiversity loss, waterborne disease).
    • Climate change: changes in weather patterns due to increased greenhouse gases (CO₂, methane, nitrous oxide) from fossil fuel burning.
    • Global warming: rising Earth temperature → ice melting, sea level rise, flooding, drought, species loss, health risks.
    • Ozone depletion: caused by CFCs, HCFCs, halons. Cl + O₃ → ClO + O₂; ClO + O → Cl + O₂ (catalytic cycle). UV increase → skin cancer, cataracts, immune damage, respiratory illness.
    • Acid rain: SO₂ and NOₓ react with water → H₂SO₄ and HNO₃. Damages plants (leaches nutrients, dissolves aluminium), soil, aquatic life, buildings, drinking water quality.
    • Biodiversity loss: causes – habitat loss, overexploitation, pollution, climate change, invasive species. Effects – reduced ecosystem services, loss of food/medicine sources.
    • Toxic bioaccumulation: PBTs (DDT, lead, mercury) accumulate in fatty tissues, magnify up food chain. Cause cancer, neurological damage, reproductive/developmental toxicity; DDT thins eggshells.
    • Resource depletion: causes – overpopulation, poor farming, overconsumption, industrialisation. Resources consumed faster than they recover.
  • Indigenous conservation in Ethiopia: Konso people – terracing, contour ploughing, crop rotation, mixed cropping, mulching, agro‑forestry (Moringa). Guassa (Menz) – Qero system of rotational closure for 3–5 years. Indigenous knowledge includes seasonal predictions, calendars, seed selection, disease treatment, and sustainable resource management.

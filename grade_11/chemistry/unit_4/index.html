4.1 Introduction to Chemical Kinetics

  • Chemical kinetics is the area of chemistry that studies reaction rates, the factors that influence them, and the mechanisms by which reactions occur.
  • Reactions proceed at very different speeds.
    • Fast reactions: combustion of gasoline, explosion of gunpowder, burning of paper.
    • Moderate reactions: ripening of bananas, conversion of milk to yogurt.
    • Slow reactions: rusting of iron, weathering of stone, charring of sugar, breakdown of plastics.
  • Understanding reaction rates is important. A useful reaction must occur at a reasonable rate.

4.2 The Rate of a Reaction

Definition of Rate of Reaction

  • The rate of a chemical reaction is the change in concentration of a reactant or product per unit time.
  • It measures how fast a reactant is consumed or a product is formed.
  • Mathematically, for a simple reaction A → B
    Rate=−Δ[A]Δt=Δ[B]Δt\text{Rate} = -\frac{\Delta [A]}{\Delta t} = \frac{\Delta [B]}{\Delta t}Rate=−ΔtΔ[A]​=ΔtΔ[B]​
    • Δ[A] is the change in concentration of A, a negative quantity because [A] decreases. A minus sign makes the rate positive.
    • Δ[B] is the change in concentration of B, positive because [B] increases.

  • For the general reaction aA + bB → cC + dD, the rate expressed in terms of any species is:

    The coefficients ensure the rate is numerically the same regardless of which substance is monitored.
  • The rate of disappearance of a reactant and the rate of appearance of a product are proportional to their stoichiometric coefficients.

Average Rate and Instantaneous Rate

  • Average rate: The change in concentration over a finite time interval Δt.
    • Example: For the decomposition of N₂O₅, the average rate of formation of O₂ between 300 s and 400 s is calculated from the concentration difference divided by 100 s.
    • The average rate decreases as the reaction proceeds because reactant concentrations drop.
  • Instantaneous rate: The rate at a specific instant of time. It is obtained from the slope of the tangent line to the concentration–time curve at that time.
  • Initial rate: The instantaneous rate at the very beginning of the reaction (t = 0). It is often used to study rate laws because initial concentrations are known exactly.

Determining Reaction Rates Experimentally

  • The rate can be followed by measuring a property that changes as the reaction proceeds.
    • Colour change (intensity of colour of a product, e.g., brown NO₂).
    • Temperature change.
    • Pressure change (if number of gas molecules changes).
    • Mass change (e.g., mass loss due to CO₂ escape in CaCO₃ + HCl).
    • Volume of gas evolved.
  • The measured property is proportional to concentration, allowing Δ[ ]/Δt to be determined.
  • In the decomposition of N₂O₅, the rate can be followed by pressure increase or colour intensity of NO₂.

Conditions Needed for a Chemical Reaction

Chemical reactions are explained by the collision theory.

Collision Theory

  • Reactant particles (atoms, ions, molecules) must collide in order to react.
  • The rate of reaction is directly proportional to the frequency of collisions:
    Rate∝number of collisionssecond\text{Rate} \propto \frac{\text{number of collisions}}{\text{second}}Rate∝secondnumber of collisions​

However, not every collision leads to a reaction. For a collision to be effective, two additional conditions must be met:

1. Proper Orientation (Orientation Factor)

  • Reactant molecules must collide with the correct relative orientation for bonds to break and new bonds to form.
  • Collisions that occur with wrong orientation do not produce products and are called ineffective collisions.
  • Collisions that result in product formation are effective collisions.

2. Sufficient Energy – Activation Energy (Eₐ)

  • Even if orientation is correct, the collision must possess enough kinetic energy to break existing bonds in the reactants.
  • The activation energy (Eₐ) is the minimum amount of energy required for a collision to result in a reaction.
  • If the collision energy is less than Eₐ, the particles bounce apart without reacting.
  • When the temperature increases, the average kinetic energy of particles increases, so more collisions have energy greater than Eₐ, increasing the reaction rate.

In summary, for a reaction to occur:

  • The particles must collide.
  • The collision must have the correct orientation.
  • The collision must have energy equal to or greater than the activation energy.

4.3 Factors Affecting the Rate of a Chemical Reaction

1. Nature of the Reactants

  • Different substances have inherently different reactivities.
  • Reactions involving oppositely charged ions (e.g., acid–base neutralisation) are usually very fast because no bonds need to be broken—only ion combination.
  • Reactions that require the breaking of covalent bonds and rearrangement of atoms (e.g., decomposition of H₂O₂) tend to be slower.
  • Under the same conditions, different metals react at different rates. For example, sodium reacts violently with water, calcium reacts moderately, while iron hardly reacts with cold water.
  • In experiment, magnesium reacts rapidly with HCl, whereas copper does not react at all under the same conditions. This is due to the intrinsic chemical properties of the elements.

2. Surface Area of Reactants (For Heterogeneous Reactions)

  • In heterogeneous reactions (different phases, e.g., solid + liquid), the reaction occurs only at the interface (boundary) between the phases.
  • Increasing the surface area of a solid reactant (by grinding it into a powder) exposes more particles to the other reactant, increasing the collision frequency and the rate.
  • Example: Powdered chalk reacts faster with acid than a large lump of chalk of the same mass. Wood chips burn faster than a large log.
  • For homogeneous reactions (all reactants in the same phase), surface area is not a factor.

3. Concentration of Reactants

  • At a fixed temperature, increasing the concentration of one or more reactants generally increases the reaction rate.
  • Higher concentration means more particles per unit volume. This leads to a higher frequency of collisions, so more effective collisions occur per unit time.
  • For gaseous reactions, increasing the pressure increases the concentration of gas molecules, thus increasing the reaction rate.
  • Pressure changes have no effect on solids or liquids because their volumes do not change appreciably.

4. Temperature

  • Increasing the temperature of a reaction mixture increases the reaction rate significantly.
  • Reason: At higher temperatures, the average kinetic energy of the molecules increases. This has two effects:
    • Molecules move faster, so collision frequency increases (slight effect).
    • A much larger fraction of collisions have energy equal to or greater than the activation energy, leading to many more effective collisions (main effect).
  • As a rule of thumb, for many homogeneous reactions, a 10 °C rise approximately doubles the rate.
  • Example: Sodium thiosulfate reacts with HCl to produce a sulfur precipitate that obscures a cross on paper. At higher temperatures, the cross disappears much faster because the reaction rate is higher.
  • Food spoils faster at room temperature than in a refrigerator because lower temperature slows reaction rates.

5. Presence of a Catalyst

  • A catalyst is a substance that increases the rate of a chemical reaction without being consumed in the overall process. It is recovered unchanged at the end.
  • A catalyst works by providing an alternative reaction pathway that has a lower activation energy (Eₐ).
    • With a lower activation energy, a larger proportion of collisions are effective at a given temperature, so the rate increases.
  • A catalyst does not alter the position of equilibrium. It speeds up both the forward and reverse reactions equally.
  • Positive catalysts (or simply catalysts) increase the rate (e.g., V₂O₅ in the oxidation of SO₂ to SO₃).
  • Negative catalysts (inhibitors) decrease the rate by raising the activation energy (e.g., OH⁻ can inhibit certain reactions).
  • Catalysts can be classified as:
    • Homogeneous catalysts: present in the same phase as the reactants (e.g., all in solution).
    • Heterogeneous catalysts: present in a different phase (e.g., solid catalyst with gaseous reactants).
  • Example: Decomposition of hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂ → H₂O + ½ O₂) is slow at room temperature but becomes rapid when manganese dioxide (MnO₂) is added as a solid catalyst.
  • Enzymes are biological catalysts that are highly specific and function in living organisms (e.g., yeast in bread-making accelerates fermentation).

Comprehensive Exam Summary (Medium-Length Bulleted)

  • Chemical kinetics is the study of reaction rates. The rate of a reaction is expressed as the change in concentration of a reactant or product per unit time (mol L⁻¹ s⁻¹).
  • The rate expression includes stoichiometric coefficients to make the rate uniform: Rate = –(1/a)Δ[A]/Δt = (1/c)Δ[C]/Δt.
  • Reaction rates can be monitored by measuring any property that changes over time, such as colour, pressure, mass, or volume of gas evolved.
  • Average rate is measured over a time interval. Instantaneous rate is the slope of the tangent to the concentration–time curve. The initial rate is at t = 0.
  • According to collision theory, a reaction requires that particles collide with proper orientation and with energy at least equal to the activation energy (Eₐ).
  • Activation energy is the minimum energy needed to break bonds and start a reaction. A higher Eₐ means a slower reaction.
  • Factors affecting rate:
    • Nature of reactants: reactive metals react faster; ionic reactions are often instantaneous; molecular rearrangements can be slow.
    • Surface area: For solids, powdering increases contact area and reaction rate.
    • Concentration: Higher concentration or pressure (for gases) increases collision frequency, thus increasing rate.
    • Temperature: Higher temperature gives more molecules with E ≥ Eₐ; rates roughly double per 10°C increase.
    • Catalyst: Provides a lower-Eₐ pathway, increasing rate without being consumed. Inhibitors do the opposite. Enzymes are biological catalysts.
  • Experiments illustrate these principles: CaCO₃ + HCl (mass loss), Na₂S₂O₃ + HCl (sulfur turbidity), H₂O₂ decomposition ± MnO₂ (gas volume).

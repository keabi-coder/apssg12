Unit 6: Oxygenated Organic Compounds – Complete Exam Revision Notes

6.1 Introduction

  • Organic compounds that contain oxygen as part of their functional group are called oxygenated hydrocarbons.
  • Main classes covered: alcohols, ethers, aldehydes, ketones, carboxylic acids, esters, and fats/oils.
  • Each class has distinct physical and chemical properties due to its functional group.

6.2 Alcohols and Ethers

6.2.1 Alcohols – Definition and Classification

  • An alcohol is a derivative of a hydrocarbon in which one or more hydrogen atoms have been replaced by a hydroxyl group (–OH).
  • The functional group is the hydroxyl group (–OH), attached to a saturated carbon atom.

Classification by Number of –OH Groups

  • Monohydric alcohols: Contain one –OH group.
    General formula: CnH2n+1OHC_nH_{2n+1}OHCn​H2n+1​OH
    Examples: Methanol (CH₃OH), Ethanol (C₂H₅OH).
  • Dihydric alcohols (glycols/diols): Contain two –OH groups.
    Example: Ethane-1,2-diol (HOCH₂CH₂OH).
  • Trihydric alcohols: Contain three –OH groups.
    Example: Propane-1,2,3-triol (glycerol).
  • Polyhydric alcohols: Contain three or more –OH groups.

Classification of Monohydric Alcohols (Based on the Carbon Bearing the –OH)

  • Primary (1°) alcohol: The –OH carbon is attached to one alkyl group.
    Example: Ethanol (CH₃CH₂OH).
  • Secondary (2°) alcohol: The –OH carbon is attached to two alkyl groups.
    Example: Propan-2-ol (CH₃CH(OH)CH₃).
  • Tertiary (3°) alcohol: The –OH carbon is attached to three alkyl groups.
    Example: 2-Methylpropan-2-ol ((CH₃)₃COH).

6.2.2 Nomenclature of Alcohols

  • Common names: Alkyl group + "alcohol".
    Example: Methyl alcohol, Isopropyl alcohol.
  • IUPAC names: Select the longest carbon chain containing the –OH group. Replace the "‑e" of the parent alkane with "‑ol". Number the chain from the end nearest to the –OH.
    Examples: Methanol, Ethanol, Propan‑1‑ol, Propan‑2‑ol.
  • For polyols: Keep the "‑e" and add "‑diol", "‑triol", etc., with position numbers.
    Example: Ethane‑1,2‑diol.

6.2.3 Physical Properties of Alcohols

  • The O–H bond is polar, so alcohol molecules form intermolecular hydrogen bonds.
  • Consequently, alcohols have higher boiling points than hydrocarbons, ethers, or haloalkanes of comparable molar mass.
  • Lower alcohols (up to about four carbons) are miscible with water because they can hydrogen‑bond with water molecules.
  • Water solubility decreases as the length of the non‑polar hydrocarbon chain increases.
  • Branched‑chain alcohols have lower boiling points than their straight‑chain isomers.

6.2.4 Preparation of Alcohols

Laboratory Methods

  1. Acid‑catalysed hydration of alkenes: Water is added across the double bond.

    The H atom attaches to the carbon with more hydrogens.
  2. Hydrolysis of alkyl halides: Heating an alkyl halide with aqueous sodium hydroxide replaces the halogen with –OH.
    R–X+NaOH→heatR–OH+NaX\text{R–X} + \text{NaOH} \xrightarrow{\text{heat}} \text{R–OH} + \text{NaX}R–X+NaOHheat​R–OH+NaX

    RCOOR’+KOH→RCOOK+R’OH\text{RCOOR'} + \text{KOH} \rightarrow \text{RCOOK} + \text{R'OH}RCOOR’+KOH→RCOOK+R’OH

  • Fermentation: Carbohydrates (sugar, starch) are broken down by enzymes from yeast.

    Fermentation yields an ethanol content of only about 12–15 %. Distillation is required to obtain higher concentrations.

6.2.5 Chemical Reactions of Alcohols

Reactions may involve cleavage of the O–H bond or the C–O bond.

Reactions Involving O–H Bond Cleavage

  • Reaction with active metals (Na, K): Produces hydrogen gas and a metal alkoxide.
    2ROH+2Na→2RONa+H22\text{ROH} + 2\text{Na} \rightarrow 2\text{RONa} + \text{H}_22ROH+2Na→2RONa+H2​
  • Oxidation:
    • Primary alcohol – oxidised first to an aldehyde (mild oxidising agent, e.g., hot Cu) and then to a carboxylic acid (strong oxidising agent, e.g., acidified KMnO₄).
      RCH2OH→[O]RCHO→[O]RCOOH\text{RCH}_2\text{OH} \xrightarrow{[O]} \text{RCHO} \xrightarrow{[O]} \text{RCOOH}RCH2​OH[O]​RCHO[O]​RCOOH
    • Secondary alcohol – oxidised to a ketone.
      RCH(OH)R’→[O]RCOR’\text{RCH(OH)R'} \xrightarrow{[O]} \text{RCOR'}RCH(OH)R’[O]​RCOR’
    • Tertiary alcohol – resistant to oxidation under ordinary conditions.

Reactions Involving C–O Bond Cleavage

  • Dehydration to alkenes: Heating with concentrated H₂SO₄ removes a water molecule and forms an alkene.
    CH3CH2OH→conc. H2SO4,170∘CCH2=CH2+H2O\text{CH}_3\text{CH}_2\text{OH} \xrightarrow{\text{conc. H}_2\text{SO}_4, 170^\circ\text{C}} \text{CH}_2\text{=CH}_2 + \text{H}_2\text{O}CH3​CH2​OHconc. H2​SO4​,170∘C​CH2​=CH2​+H2​O
    Primary alcohols require higher temperatures; secondary and tertiary alcohols dehydrate more readily.
  • Reaction with hydrogen halides (HX): Forms alkyl halides.
    ROH+HBr→RBr+H2O\text{ROH} + \text{HBr} \rightarrow \text{RBr} + \text{H}_2\text{O}ROH+HBr→RBr+H2​O

6.2.6 Ethers – Structure, Nomenclature, and Properties

  • Ethers have the general structure R–O–R’\text{R–O–R'}R–O–R’.
  • If R and R′ are identical, it is a symmetrical ether. If different, it is an unsymmetrical ether.

Nomenclature

  • Common names: List the two alkyl groups in alphabetical order, followed by the word "ether".
    Examples: Dimethyl ether, Ethyl methyl ether.
  • IUPAC names: The smaller alkyl group combined with the oxygen is called an alkoxy group. The larger alkyl group is the parent alkane.
    Examples: Methoxyethane (CH₃OCH₂CH₃), 2‑Methoxybutane.

Physical Properties

  • Ethers are polar molecules but cannot form hydrogen bonds with each other (no O–H bond).
  • Their boiling points are much lower than those of isomeric alcohols and are similar to those of alkanes of comparable mass.
  • Ethers can form hydrogen bonds with water. Low‑molecular‑weight ethers have some solubility in water.

Preparation

  1. Dehydration of alcohols: Intermolecular dehydration of primary alcohols at 140 °C with H₂SO₄ produces symmetrical ethers.
    2CH3CH2OH→H2SO4,140∘CCH3CH2OCH2CH3+H2O2\text{CH}_3\text{CH}_2\text{OH} \xrightarrow{\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4, 140^\circ\text{C}} \text{CH}_3\text{CH}_2\text{OCH}_2\text{CH}_3 + \text{H}_2\text{O}2CH3​CH2​OHH2​SO4​,140∘C​CH3​CH2​OCH2​CH3​+H2​O
  2. Williamson ether synthesis: An alkoxide ion reacts with a primary alkyl halide.
    R’O−+R–X→R’–O–R+X−\text{R'O}^- + \text{R} \text{–X} \rightarrow \text{R'–O–R} + \text{X}^-R’O−+R–X→R’–O–R+X−

Chemical Reactions

  • Ethers are relatively unreactive. They are not oxidised by common oxidising agents and do not react with bases.
  • With strong acids (HI, HBr) and heat, the ether linkage is cleaved.
    R–O–R’+2HI→R–I+R’–I+H2O\text{R–O–R'} + 2\text{HI} \rightarrow \text{R–I} + \text{R'–I} + \text{H}_2\text{O}R–O–R’+2HI→R–I+R’–I+H2​O
  • Their low reactivity makes them excellent solvents for many organic reactions.

6.3 Aldehydes and Ketones

6.3.1 Functional Group and General Structures

  • Both compounds contain the carbonyl group (C=O).
  • Aldehyde: The carbonyl carbon is bonded to at least one hydrogen atom.
    General formula: RCHO\text{RCHO}RCHO (or RCOH\text{RCOH}RCOH). The –CHO group is called the formyl group.
  • Ketone: The carbonyl carbon is bonded to two carbon atoms (alkyl or aryl groups).
    General formula: RCOR’\text{RCOR'}RCOR’.

6.3.2 Nomenclature

  • Aldehydes (IUPAC): Replace the "‑e" ending of the parent alkane with "‑al". The aldehyde carbon is always C‑1, so no number is needed for the functional group.
    Examples: Methanal (HCHO), Ethanal (CH₃CHO), Propanal (CH₃CH₂CHO).
  • Ketones (IUPAC): Replace the "‑e" ending with "‑one". The chain is numbered to give the carbonyl group the lowest possible number.
    Examples: Propanone (CH₃COCH₃), Butan‑2‑one (CH₃COCH₂CH₃), Pentan‑3‑one.
  • Many aldehydes and ketones have widely used common names (e.g., formaldehyde, acetaldehyde, acetone).

6.3.3 Physical Properties

  • The C=O bond is highly polar (Cδ+=Oδ−\text{C}^{\delta+} = \text{O}^{\delta-}Cδ+=Oδ−), giving the molecules strong dipole–dipole interactions.
  • Therefore, aldehydes and ketones have higher boiling points than non‑polar compounds (alkanes, ethers) of similar molecular mass but lower boiling points than alcohols (which form hydrogen bonds).
  • Lower aldehydes and ketones are soluble in water because they can form hydrogen bonds with water via the carbonyl oxygen. Solubility decreases as the hydrocarbon portion becomes larger.

6.3.4 General Methods of Preparation (Summary)

  • Oxidation of primary alcohols → aldehydes (using mild oxidising agents) → carboxylic acids (with strong oxidants).
  • Oxidation of secondary alcohols → ketones.
  • Ozonolysis of alkenes followed by a reducing work‑up yields aldehydes and/or ketones.

6.3.5 Chemical Reactions

The polar carbonyl group makes the carbon atom electrophilic, allowing nucleophilic addition reactions.

  1. Nucleophilic Addition Reactions

    • Addition of Grignard reagents (RMgX) followed by hydrolysis produces alcohols.
    • Addition of alcohols: In the presence of an acid catalyst, aldehydes/ketones react with one molecule of alcohol to form hemiacetals and with two to form acetals.

  2. Oxidation

    • Aldehydes are easily oxidised to carboxylic acids. They give positive tests with Tollens' reagent (silver mirror) and Fehling's/Benedict's solutions (red precipitate).
    • Ketones resist mild oxidation. They do not react with Tollens' or Fehling's reagents, which provides a way to distinguish aldehydes from ketones.

  3. Reduction

    • Aldehydes are reduced to primary alcohols.
    • Ketones are reduced to secondary alcohols.
    • Reducing agents: catalytic hydrogenation (H₂/Ni or Pd) or metal hydrides (LiAlH₄, NaBH₄).
      RCHO+2[H]→RCH2OH\text{RCHO} + 2[\text{H}] \rightarrow \text{RCH}_2\text{OH}RCHO+2[H]→RCH2​OH
      RCOR’+2[H]→RCH(OH)R’\text{RCOR'} + 2[\text{H}] \rightarrow \text{RCH(OH)R'}RCOR’+2[H]→RCH(OH)R’

6.4 Carboxylic Acids

6.4.1 Functional Group and Classification

  • The carboxyl group is –COOH, consisting of a carbonyl group (C=O) and a hydroxyl group (–OH) attached to the same carbon.
  • General formula for a saturated monocarboxylic acid: RCOOH\text{RCOOH}RCOOH or CnH2n+1COOH\text{C}_n\text{H}_{2n+1}\text{COOH}Cn​H2n+1​COOH.
  • Monocarboxylic acids: One –COOH group.
  • Dicarboxylic acids: Two –COOH groups (e.g., ethanedioic acid, propanedioic acid).
  • Tricarboxylic acids: Three –COOH groups (e.g., citric acid).
  • Aromatic carboxylic acids have the –COOH directly attached to an aromatic ring. The simplest is benzoic acid.

6.4.2 Nomenclature

  • Common names: Often based on natural sources (formic acid – ants, acetic acid – vinegar, butyric acid – butter). The suffix is "‑ic acid".
    Branched chains are named using Greek letters (α, β, γ…) to denote substituent positions.
  • IUPAC names: Replace the final "‑e" of the corresponding alkane with "‑oic acid". The carboxyl carbon is always C‑1.
    Examples: Methanoic acid (HCOOH), Ethanoic acid (CH₃COOH), Propanoic acid.
  • For dicarboxylic acids: the ending is "‑dioic acid". Example: Butanedioic acid.
  • For aromatic acids: The parent name is benzenecarboxylic acid. Substituted compounds use numbers, e.g., 2‑bromobenzenecarboxylic acid.

6.4.3 Physical Properties

  • Carboxylic acids form strong intermolecular hydrogen bonds. In the pure state, they exist as cyclic dimers held together by two hydrogen bonds.
  • This results in very high boiling points – higher than those of alcohols, ketones, or ethers of similar molar mass.
  • Lower acids (up to four carbons) are miscible with water because of extensive hydrogen bonding with water. Solubility decreases with increasing length of the hydrocarbon tail.
  • The lower aliphatic acids have sharp, pungent odours. Mid‑range acids (butanoic to hexanoic) have unpleasant odours. Higher acids are odourless, waxy solids.

6.4.4 Acidity and Chemical Reactions

  • Carboxylic acids are weak acids. In water, they partially dissociate to give a carboxylate ion and a hydronium ion.
  • Reactions as acids:
    • With active metals (Na, K): Produce hydrogen gas and a metal carboxylate.
      2RCOOH+2Na→2RCOONa+H22\text{RCOOH} + 2\text{Na} \rightarrow 2\text{RCOONa} + \text{H}_22RCOOH+2Na→2RCOONa+H2​
    • With bases (NaOH, KOH): Neutralisation forming a salt and water.
      RCOOH+NaOH→RCOONa+H2O\text{RCOOH} + \text{NaOH} \rightarrow \text{RCOONa} + \text{H}_2\text{O}RCOOH+NaOH→RCOONa+H2​O
    • With carbonates and hydrogen carbonates: Evolution of CO₂ gas confirms the presence of the carboxyl group.
      RCOOH+NaHCO3→RCOONa+H2O+CO2\text{RCOOH} + \text{NaHCO}_3 \rightarrow \text{RCOONa} + \text{H}_2\text{O} + \text{CO}_2RCOOH+NaHCO3​→RCOONa+H2​O+CO2​
  • Esterification: Heating a carboxylic acid with an alcohol in the presence of a strong acid catalyst (e.g., conc. H₂SO₄) produces an ester and water.
    RCOOH+R’OH⇌RCOOR’+H2O\text{RCOOH} + \text{R'OH} \rightleftharpoons \text{RCOOR'} + \text{H}_2\text{O}RCOOH+R’OH⇌RCOOR’+H2​O

6.4.5 Preparation

  1. Oxidation of primary alcohols or aldehydes: Strong oxidising agents (acidified KMnO₄ or K₂Cr₂O₇) convert them to the corresponding carboxylic acid.
    CH3CH2OH→[O]CH3COOH\text{CH}_3\text{CH}_2\text{OH} \xrightarrow{[O]} \text{CH}_3\text{COOH}CH3​CH2​OH[O]​CH3​COOH
  2. Oxidation of alkylbenzenes: Vigorous oxidation with hot alkaline KMnO₄ converts the entire side chain (regardless of length) into a –COOH group.
    Toluene (C₆H₅CH₃) → Benzoic acid (C₆H₅COOH)

6.4.6 Fatty Acids

  • Fatty acids are long‑chain monocarboxylic acids usually containing an even number of carbon atoms and an unbranched chain.
  • Saturated fatty acids: Contain no double bonds (e.g., palmitic acid, stearic acid). They have relatively high melting points that increase with chain length.
  • Unsaturated fatty acids: Contain one or more cis double bonds (e.g., oleic acid – one double bond, linoleic acid – two, linolenic acid – three). The cis configuration introduces kinks, preventing close packing, so their melting points are much lower than those of saturated analogues.

6.4.7 Uses of Carboxylic Acids

  • Acetic acid: vinegar, food preservative, starting material for polymers.
  • Fatty acids: manufacture of soaps, detergents, cosmetics.
  • Benzoic acid: food preservative, pharmaceutical preparations.

6.5 Esters

6.5.1 Structure and Nomenclature

  • An ester is a derivative of a carboxylic acid in which the –OH group is replaced by an –OR group (alkoxy group).
  • General formula: RCOOR’\text{RCOOR'}RCOOR’ or RCO2R’\text{RCO}_2\text{R'}RCO2​R’.
  • Naming: The name has two parts. The first part comes from the alcohol (alkyl group, ending in "‑yl"). The second part comes from the acid (replace "‑ic acid" with "‑ate" in common names, or "‑oic acid" with "‑oate" in IUPAC).
    Examples: Methyl ethanoate (common: methyl acetate), Ethyl propanoate.
  • The alcohol portion is always written first.

6.5.2 Physical Properties

  • Ester molecules cannot form hydrogen bonds with each other (no O–H group). Consequently, their boiling points are lower than those of isomeric carboxylic acids and alcohols, and similar to those of ketones and ethers of comparable mass.
  • Small esters have some solubility in water because the carbonyl oxygen can hydrogen‑bond with water. Solubility decreases as the hydrocarbon chains become longer.
  • Most volatile esters have pleasant, fruity odours and are responsible for the fragrances of many fruits and flowers.

6.5.3 Chemical Reactions

  1. Hydrolysis: The ester bond is cleaved by reaction with water.
    • Acid‑catalysed hydrolysis: Reversible. Produces the parent carboxylic acid and alcohol.
      RCOOR’+H2O→H+RCOOH+R’OH\text{RCOOR'} + \text{H}_2\text{O} \xrightarrow{\text{H}^+} \text{RCOOH} + \text{R'OH}RCOOR’+H2​OH+​RCOOH+R’OH
    • Base‑catalysed hydrolysis (saponification): Irreversible. Uses NaOH or KOH. Produces a carboxylate salt and an alcohol.
      RCOOR’+NaOH→RCOONa+R’OH\text{RCOOR'} + \text{NaOH} \rightarrow \text{RCOONa} + \text{R'OH}RCOOR’+NaOH→RCOONa+R’OH
  2. Reduction: Esters are reduced to two alcohols by strong reducing agents such as lithium aluminium hydride (LiAlH₄).
    RCOOR’→LiAlH4RCH2OH+R’OH\text{RCOOR'} \xrightarrow{\text{LiAlH}_4} \text{RCH}_2\text{OH} + \text{R'OH}RCOOR’LiAlH4​​RCH2​OH+R’OH

6.5.4 Preparation (Esterification)

  • The most common method is the Fischer esterification: a carboxylic acid is heated with an alcohol in the presence of a catalytic amount of concentrated H₂SO₄. Water is eliminated.
    CH3COOH+CH3OH⇌CH3COOCH3+H2O\text{CH}_3\text{COOH} + \text{CH}_3\text{OH} \rightleftharpoons \text{CH}_3\text{COOCH}_3 + \text{H}_2\text{O}CH3​COOH+CH3​OH⇌CH3​COOCH3​+H2​O

6.5.5 Uses of Esters

  • Solvents in paints, varnishes, and nail‑polish removers (e.g., ethyl ethanoate).
  • Flavourings and fragrances in foods, perfumes, and cosmetics.
  • Plasticizers (e.g., phthalates) added to plastics for flexibility.
  • Polyesters (e.g., Dacron) used in fabrics.
  • Pharmaceuticals (e.g., aspirin is an ester of salicylic acid).

6.6 Fats and Oils (Triglycerides)

6.6.1 Definition and Structure

  • Fats and oils are naturally occurring triesters of glycerol (propane‑1,2,3‑triol) with fatty acids. They are called triglycerides or triacylglycerols.
  • General structure:
    CH2–O–CO–R1∣CH–O–CO–R2∣CH2–O–CO–R3\begin{matrix} \text{CH}_2\text{–O–CO–R}_1 \\ | \\ \text{CH–O–CO–R}_2 \\ | \\ \text{CH}_2\text{–O–CO–R}_3 \end{matrix}CH2​–O–CO–R1​∣CH–O–CO–R2​∣CH2​–O–CO–R3​​
  • R₁, R₂, and R₃ are long hydrocarbon chains (may be identical or different).
  • Fats: Solid or semi‑solid at room temperature. Contain a higher proportion of saturated fatty acids. Mostly from animal sources (e.g., butter, lard, tallow).
  • Oils: Liquid at room temperature. Contain a higher proportion of unsaturated fatty acids (one or more cis double bonds). Mostly from plant sources (e.g., olive oil, sunflower oil).

6.6.2 Physical Properties

  • Fats and oils are greasy, hydrophobic substances. They are insoluble in water but soluble in non‑polar organic solvents.
  • The melting point of a triglyceride depends on the degree of saturation of the fatty acid chains. Unsaturated chains have kinks that prevent close packing, resulting in lower melting points (oils). Saturated chains pack tightly, giving higher melting points (fats).

6.6.3 Chemical Reactions

  • Hydrogenation (Hardening of oils): Liquid vegetable oils are converted into semi‑solid fats by catalytic addition of hydrogen across the C=C double bonds. Nickel or palladium is used as a catalyst under pressure. This process is used to manufacture margarine.
  • Saponification: Triglycerides undergo base‑catalysed hydrolysis with NaOH or KOH to produce glycerol and the sodium or potassium salts of the fatty acids – these salts are soaps.
  • Rancidity: When fats and oils are exposed to air, moisture, and light for extended periods, they develop an unpleasant odour and taste. This is caused by:
    • Hydrolysis of the ester linkages, releasing free fatty acids.
    • Oxidation at the double bonds of unsaturated fatty acids.
      Proper storage in airtight containers away from heat and light helps prevent rancidity.

Comprehensive Exam Summary (Medium‑Length Bulleted)

  • Alcohols contain the –OH functional group. They are classified by the number of –OH groups (mono‑, di‑, trihydric) and by the substitution at the carbon bearing the –OH (1°, 2°, 3°). IUPAC names use the suffix "‑ol".
  • Hydrogen bonding gives alcohols high boiling points and water solubility for small alcohols. Primary alcohols oxidise to aldehydes then carboxylic acids; secondary alcohols oxidise to ketones; tertiary alcohols resist oxidation. Alcohols react with sodium to liberate hydrogen, dehydrate to alkenes, and undergo esterification.
  • Ethers (R–O–R′) have no hydrogen bonding between molecules, so they boil lower than isomeric alcohols. They are prepared by Williamson synthesis or dehydration of alcohols. Chemically, they are relatively inert and are widely used as solvents.
  • Aldehydes (RCHO) and ketones (RCOR′) contain the polar carbonyl group. Aldehydes end in "‑al", ketones in "‑one". Aldehydes are easily oxidised to carboxylic acids (positive Tollens and Fehling tests), whereas ketones resist mild oxidation. Both undergo nucleophilic addition and reduction to alcohols.
  • Carboxylic acids (RCOOH) contain the carboxyl group. They form hydrogen‑bonded dimers, resulting in very high boiling points. They are weak acids, reacting with metals, bases, and carbonates. Esters are formed by Fischer esterification. Naming: "‑oic acid". Long‑chain members are fatty acids; saturation determines melting point.
  • Esters (RCOOR′) are known for their pleasant fruity odours. They are named with the alkyl group from the alcohol followed by the acid part ending in "‑ate" or "‑oate". They are prepared by esterification and hydrolysed by acid or base. Widely used as flavourings, fragrances, and solvents.
  • Fats and oils are triglycerides – triesters of glycerol with fatty acids. Fats are solid and saturated; oils are liquid and unsaturated. Hydrogenation of oils produces solid fats. Base‑catalysed hydrolysis (saponification) yields soap. Rancidity is caused by hydrolysis and oxidation.

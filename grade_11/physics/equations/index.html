UNIT 1 – PHYSICS AND HUMAN SOCIETY

Definitions

  • Physics community: an organised group of persons associated together for scientific purposes.
  • Scientific method: an ordered series of steps to acquire knowledge based on experimental evidence.
  • Experiential knowledge: knowledge gained through direct experience – sensory perception, introspection, memory, testimony.

UNIT 2 – VECTORS

Definitions

  • Scalar quantity: a physical quantity described by a single number (magnitude) and a unit.
  • Vector quantity: a quantity described by both magnitude and direction.
  • Resultant vector: a single vector that gives the combined effect of two or more vectors.
  • Collinear vectors: vectors lying along the same line.
  • Coplanar vectors: vectors lying in the same plane.
  • Parallel vectors: vectors having the same direction.
  • Antiparallel vectors: vectors having opposite directions.
  • Equal vectors: vectors with equal magnitude and equal direction.
  • Negative of a vector: vector with the same magnitude but opposite direction.
  • Null (zero) vector: vector of zero magnitude and no specific direction.
  • Unit vector: a vector of magnitude 1 used to specify direction.
  • Component of a vector: the projection of a vector on a coordinate axis.
  • Resolution of a vector: breaking a vector into its components.
  • Scalar (dot) product: product of two vectors that yields a scalar.
  • Angle between vectors: the angle formed when two vectors are placed tail to tail.

Laws / Principles (Graphical Addition)

  • Triangle law of vector addition: Vectors placed head‑to‑tail; resultant drawn from tail of first to head of last.
  • Parallelogram law of vector addition: Vectors placed tail‑to‑tail; resultant is the diagonal of the parallelogram.
  • Polygon law of vector addition: For more than two vectors, placed head‑to‑tail in any order; resultant from tail of first to head of last.
  • Vector subtraction: A⃗−B⃗=A⃗+(−B⃗)\vec{A} - \vec{B} = \vec{A} + (-\vec{B})A−B=A+(−B)

UNIT 4 – DYNAMICS

Definitions

  • Dynamics: the subdivision of mechanics dealing with motion in relation to force.
  • Force: an external agent that changes or tends to change the state of rest or uniform motion of a body.
  • Inertia: the tendency of a body to oppose any change in its state of rest or uniform motion.
  • Weight: the gravitational pull on an object; W⃗=mg⃗\vec{W} = m \vec{g}W=mg​.
  • Normal force: a force with which two objects push one another, acting perpendicularly at the contact surface.
  • Friction: a force opposing the motion of one surface over another.
  • Static friction: friction between two contacting surfaces at rest.
  • Kinetic friction: friction between two surfaces sliding over each other.
  • Equilibrium: a state of rest or uniform motion along a fixed direction; net force = 0.
  • Free‑body diagram: a drawing showing only the forces acting directly on the object of interest.
  • Work: product of force and displacement in the direction of the force; W=F⃗⋅S⃗=FScos⁡θW = \vec{F}\cdot\vec{S} = FS\cos\thetaW=F⋅S=FScosθ.
  • Kinetic energy: energy due to motion; K=12mv2K = \frac{1}{2} m v^2K=21​mv2.
  • Potential energy: energy due to position or shape; gravitational PE = mghmghmgh, elastic PE = 12kx2\frac{1}{2} k x^221​kx2.
  • Conservative force: a force for which work done is independent of the path (e.g., gravity, spring).
  • Non‑conservative force: a force for which work done depends on the path (e.g., friction).
  • Power: rate of doing work or transferring energy.
  • Linear momentum: product of mass and velocity; p⃗=mv⃗\vec{p} = m \vec{v}p​=mv.
  • Impulse: change of momentum; product of net force and time interval.
  • Center of mass: a point where the total mass of a body or system is assumed to be concentrated.

Laws

  • Newton’s first law (law of inertia): An object at rest remains at rest, and an object in motion continues in motion with constant velocity along a straight line, unless acted upon by an external force.
  • Newton’s second law: F⃗net=ma⃗\vec{F}_{net} = m \vec{a}Fnet​=ma (also expressed as F⃗net=Δp⃗Δt\vec{F}_{net} = \dfrac{\Delta \vec{p}}{\Delta t}Fnet​=ΔtΔp​​).
  • Newton’s third law: For every action there is always an equal and opposite reaction; forces come in pairs acting on different bodies.
  • First condition of equilibrium: ∑F⃗=0\sum \vec{F} = 0∑F=0 (∑Fx=0,  ∑Fy=0\sum F_x = 0,\; \sum F_y = 0∑Fx​=0,∑Fy​=0).

  • Law of conservation of mechanical energy: If only conservative forces act, KEi+PEi=KEf+PEfKE_i + PE_i = KE_f + PE_fKEi​+PEi​=KEf​+PEf​, i.e., ΔME=0\Delta ME = 0ΔME=0.

  • Elastic collision: both momentum and kinetic energy conserved.
  • Inelastic collision: momentum conserved, kinetic energy not conserved.
  • Perfectly inelastic collision: objects stick together; maximum loss of KE.

UNIT 5 – HEAT CONDUCTION AND CALORIMETRY

Definitions

  • Heat: energy in transit from one body to another due to temperature difference.
  • Temperature: measure of the average kinetic energy of particles.
  • Internal energy: sum of internal kinetic energy (molecular motion) and internal potential energy (molecular forces).
  • Thermodynamic work: quantity of energy transferred from one system to another by mechanical means.
  • Conduction: heat transfer through a material by atomic/molecular collisions; no bulk motion.
  • Convection: heat transfer by fluid movement due to density differences.
  • Radiation: heat transfer by electromagnetic waves; no medium required.
  • Heat capacity (C): heat required to raise an object’s temperature by 1 °C or 1 K; C=QΔTC = \dfrac{Q}{\Delta T}C=ΔTQ​. Unit: J/K or J/°C.
  • Specific heat capacity (c): heat required to raise 1 kg of a substance by 1 °C or 1 K; c=QmΔTc = \dfrac{Q}{m\Delta T}c=mΔTQ​ or c=Cmc = \dfrac{C}{m}c=mC​. Unit: J/kg·°C or J/kg·K.
  • Thermal expansion: change in size of matter in response to a temperature change.
  • Linear expansion: ΔL=αL0ΔT\Delta L = \alpha L_0 \Delta TΔL=αL0​ΔT; α\alphaα = coefficient of linear expansion.
  • Area expansion: ΔA=βA0ΔT\Delta A = \beta A_0 \Delta TΔA=βA0​ΔT; β≈2α\beta \approx 2\alphaβ≈2α.
  • Volume expansion: ΔV=γV0ΔT\Delta V = \gamma V_0 \Delta TΔV=γV0​ΔT; γ≈3α\gamma \approx 3\alphaγ≈3α.
  • Phase change: transition of matter between solid, liquid, gas at constant temperature.
  • Latent heat (L): heat absorbed or released per unit mass during a phase change without temperature change; Q=mLQ = m LQ=mL.
  • Latent heat of fusion (LfL_fLf​): solid ↔ liquid.
  • Latent heat of vaporization (LvL_vLv​): liquid ↔ gas.
  • Triple point: the unique temperature and pressure where solid, liquid, and gas phases coexist in equilibrium.
  • Critical point: the temperature and pressure above which liquid and gas phases become indistinguishable.
  • Sublimation: direct transition from solid to gas without passing through liquid.
  • Calorimetry: measurement of the quantity of heat exchanged between substances.
  • Calorimeter: a device used to measure specific heat capacity and specific latent heat.

UNIT 6 – ELECTROSTATICS AND ELECTRIC CIRCUIT

Definitions

  • Electric charge: fundamental property; two types – positive and negative; quantized (q=±neq = \pm n eq=±ne).
  • Coulomb’s law: F=k∣q1q2∣r2F = k \frac{|q_1 q_2|}{r^2}F=kr2∣q1​q2​∣​ (force between two point charges).
  • Electric field: a region where a charge experiences a force; E⃗=F⃗/q\vec{E} = \vec{F}/qE=F/q.
  • Electric field lines: lines that begin on + charges, end on – charges, never cross, perpendicular to surfaces, density indicates strength.
  • Electric flux (Φ\PhiΦ): measure of the number of field lines through a surface; Φ=E⃗⋅A⃗=EAcos⁡θ\Phi = \vec{E}\cdot\vec{A} = E A \cos\thetaΦ=E⋅A=EAcosθ.
  • Electric potential (V): potential energy per unit charge; V=U/qV = U/qV=U/q. Unit: volt (V).
  • Potential difference (ΔV\Delta VΔV): work per unit charge to move a test charge between two points.
  • Equipotential surfaces: surfaces of constant potential; perpendicular to field lines; no work to move charge along them.
  • Electric current (I): rate of flow of charge; I=Δq/ΔtI = \Delta q / \Delta tI=Δq/Δt. Unit: ampere (A).
  • Current density (J): current per unit area; J=I/AJ = I/AJ=I/A. Unit: A/m².
  • Drift velocity (vdv_dvd​): average velocity of free electrons in a conductor under an electric field.
  • Resistance (R): opposition to current; R=V/IR = V/IR=V/I. Unit: ohm (Ω\OmegaΩ).
  • Resistivity (ρ\rhoρ): material property; R=ρL/AR = \rho L/AR=ρL/A. Unit: Ω⋅m\Omega\cdot mΩ⋅m.
  • Conductivity (σ\sigmaσ): reciprocal of resistivity; σ=1/ρ\sigma = 1/\rhoσ=1/ρ.
  • Ohm’s law: for ohmic materials, V=IRV = IRV=IR.
  • Ammeter: measures current; connected in series; low resistance.
  • Voltmeter: measures voltage; connected in parallel; high resistance.
  • Galvanometer: sensitive instrument for small currents; can be converted to ammeter or voltmeter.
  • Potentiometer: device for measuring emf by balancing against a known potential difference without drawing current.
  • Wheatstone bridge: circuit for measuring unknown resistance by balancing two legs.
  • Capacitor: device for storing electric charge and energy; capacitance C=Q/VC = Q/VC=Q/V. Unit: farad (F).
  • Dielectric: insulating material inserted between capacitor plates; increases capacitance by factor kkk.
  • Dielectric strength: maximum electric field a dielectric can withstand without breakdown.

UNIT 7 – NUCLEAR PHYSICS

Definitions

  • Atomic nucleus: consists of protons and neutrons (nucleons) held together by the strong nuclear force.
  • Isotopes: members of a family of an element with the same number of protons (Z) but different numbers of neutrons (N).
  • Strong nuclear force: a very short‑range attractive force between nucleons; ~100 times stronger than electromagnetic force at 10−1510^{-15}10−15 m.
  • Weak nuclear force: responsible for beta decay; even shorter range than the strong force.
  • Nuclear binding energy: the energy required to separate an atomic nucleus completely into its constituent protons and neutrons, or the energy released when nucleons combine to form a nucleus.
  • Mass defect: the difference between the sum of the masses of separate nucleons and the mass of the nucleus.
  • Radioactivity: the spontaneous disintegration of unstable atomic nuclei to form more stable nuclei, emitting radiation.
  • Radioactive decay: a highly exoergic, statistically random process where a small amount of mass is converted to energy.
  • Alpha particle: a helium nucleus, 24He^4_2\text{He}24​He, emitted in alpha decay.
  • Beta particle: an electron (β−\beta^-β−) or positron (β+\beta^+β+) emitted in beta decay.
  • Gamma ray: high‑energy electromagnetic radiation emitted from an excited nucleus.
  • Half‑life (t1/2t_{1/2}t1/2​): the time taken for half of the radioactive nuclei in a sample to decay.
  • Decay constant (λ\lambdaλ): the probability of decay per unit time; SI unit s−1s^{-1}s−1.
  • Activity (A): the number of decays per second; A=λNA = \lambda NA=λN. Unit: becquerel (Bq); 1 Bq=11 \, \text{Bq} = 11Bq=1 decay/s. Also curie (Ci): 1 Ci=3.7×1010 Bq1 \, \text{Ci} = 3.7 \times 10^{10} \, \text{Bq}1Ci=3.7×1010Bq.
  • Nuclear fission: the splitting of a heavy nucleus into two or more smaller nuclei, releasing energy and neutrons.
  • Nuclear fusion: a reaction in which two or more light atomic nuclei combine to form a heavier nucleus, releasing energy.
  • Chain reaction: neutrons released in fission trigger further fissions; can be controlled (reactor) or uncontrolled (bomb).
  • Critical mass: the minimum mass of fissile material needed to sustain a chain reaction.

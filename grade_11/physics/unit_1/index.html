PHYSICS AND HUMAN SOCIETY – Complete Exam Revision Notes

1.1 Importance of Physics to Society

Key Contributions of Physics

  • Medical applications: Physics provides the basic understanding for instruments and techniques such as:
    • Computer tomography
    • Magnetic resonance imaging
    • Positron emission tomography
    • Ultrasonic imaging
    • Laser surgery
  • Transportation: Automobiles, airplanes, and space shuttles cannot be built without physics experts.
  • Military purposes: Physics informs the design of weapons; physicists were responsible for inventing the atomic bomb and are still involved in creating nuclear weapons.
  • Everyday technologies: Many devices and systems that benefit society are directly based on physics principles, including electronics, communication tools, and energy systems.

In summary, physics improves our quality of life by enabling new instrumentation, transport, medical techniques, and energy solutions.

1.2 Physics Communities and Their Roles

Definition of a Physics Community

A physics community is an organized group of persons associated together for scientific purposes.
These communities exist at national, continental, and worldwide levels.

Example: Ethiopian Physical Society (EPS) – Objectives

  • Promote physics education and research in the country.
  • Organize and coordinate conferences on physics education and exchange of scientific information.
  • Popularize physics so students develop interest in the subject.
  • Encourage active participation of Ethiopian physicists and the general public in designing and implementing the physics curriculum.
  • Create means for disseminating scientific information.

Roles of Physics Communities

  • Foster collaboration among physicists.
  • Support research through equipment, computing facilities, and scholarships.
  • Advance physics knowledge and its applications for the benefit of society.

1.3 Making of Physics Knowledge

Two Main Types of Knowledge

Physics knowledge is built through experiential knowledge and non-experiential knowledge, structured by the scientific method.

A. Experiential Knowledge

Knowledge gained through direct experience. Four sources:

  1. Sensory perception

    • The dominant source; we gather knowledge by seeing, touching, hearing, etc.
    • Raises a critical question: how reliable are our senses?

  2. Introspection

    • Like a “sixth sense” that looks into our own minds.
    • Allows us to inspect our feelings and thoughts (e.g., describing the nature of a pain to a doctor).
    • Others rely on our introspective description because they cannot experience it directly.

  3. Memory

    • Acts like a recording device, capturing events in roughly the order they occur.
    • Enables us to recall past experiences.

  4. Testimony

    • Knowledge gained from the reports of others.
    • Written sources are usually more reliable than oral sources, but depend on the integrity of the author, publisher, and fact‑gathering methods.

B. Non-Experiential Knowledge

  • Knowledge not gained through direct personal experience.
  • Examples: Information accepted from a trusted textbook, a teacher’s explanation, or a documentary.
  • Important note: Non‑experiential knowledge ultimately traces back to someone else’s experiential knowledge (e.g., the author performed experiments or gathered observations).

The Scientific Method

The scientific method is an ordered series of steps used to acquire knowledge based on experimental evidence.

Steps of the Scientific Method (with an example on boiling point of water)

  1. Observation / Question
    Notice that water boils at different temperatures in different locations.
  2. Hypothesis
    The boiling temperature depends on altitude.
  3. Experiment
    Heat water at various altitudes and record the boiling temperature.
    Altitude (m) Boiling point of water (°C)
    0 100
    150 99.5
    305 99
    610 98
    1524 95
  4. Analysis
    Examine the data – as altitude increases, the boiling point decreases.
  5. Conclusion
    The experiment supports the hypothesis: atmospheric pressure (which decreases with altitude) affects boiling point.

1.4 Mission of Physics and Career Awareness

The Mission of Physics

  • To advance science, engineering, and innovation throughout the world for the benefit of all, serving society.

Typical Careers in Physics

A physics degree opens a wide range of job opportunities. Below are the main career areas mentioned in the unit.

Field Examples of Careers / Roles
Space & Astronomy Astronomer, astrophysicist, space mission specialist
Healthcare Medical physicist, radiology specialist, CT scan technician
Engineering Design engineer, materials scientist, product development
Energy Renewable energy specialist, nuclear energy scientist
Technology Semiconductor researcher, IT hardware developer, robotics engineer
Geophysics & Meteorology Weather forecaster, seismologist, climate modeller
Research Scientist Data scientist, nanotechnology researcher, particle physicist

Detailed Career Descriptions

Physics careers in space and astronomy

  • Study the universe beyond Earth.
  • Use telescopes and space probes to observe planets, stars, galaxies.
  • Research areas include the origin of the universe, dark matter, and exoplanets.

Physics careers in healthcare

  • Apply physics to diagnose and treat illness.
  • Work with imaging technologies such as CT scans, MRI, and ultrasound.
  • Radiation therapy for cancer treatment.
  • Ensure the safety and accuracy of medical equipment.

Physics careers in engineering

  • Use physics principles to design, build, and improve structures, machines, and systems.
  • Areas: mechanical, electrical, civil, aerospace, and materials engineering.
  • Innovate in fields like robotics, telecommunications, and transport.

Physics careers in energy

  • Develop sustainable energy sources: solar, wind, nuclear.
  • Improve energy efficiency and storage (batteries, fuel cells).
  • Address the challenge of global energy demand while reducing environmental impact.

Physics careers in technology

  • Drive innovation in computing, communication, and consumer electronics.
  • Work on semiconductor devices, lasers, fibre optics, and quantum computing.
  • Bridge pure research and practical products.

Geophysics and meteorology careers

  • Study the Earth’s physical processes: earthquakes, volcanoes, ocean currents.
  • Weather prediction and climate modelling.
  • Help mitigate natural disasters and understand climate change.

Research scientist careers

  • Conduct fundamental or applied research in universities, government labs, or industry.
  • Specialise in fields such as particle physics, condensed matter, astrophysics, etc.
  • Publish findings, present at conferences, and contribute to scientific progress.

1.5 Current Status of Physics

Major Recent Discoveries in Physics

1. Discovery of Exoplanets

  • What is an exoplanet? A planet orbiting a star outside our solar system.
  • First confirmed detection around a sun‑like star in 1995.
  • Today, thousands of exoplanets have been identified.
  • Nobel Prize in Physics 2019 awarded for this discovery.
  • This opened the field of exoplanet atmosphere study and the search for life elsewhere.

2. Black Hole

  • A black hole is a region of space where gravity is so strong that nothing, not even light, can escape.
  • The Event Horizon Telescope captured the first image of a black hole in 2019.
  • Nobel Prize in Physics 2020 awarded to Roger Penrose (theoretical work on black hole formation), and Reinhard Genzel and Andrea Ghez (discovery of a supermassive compact object at the centre of our galaxy).

3. Quantum Cryptography

  • Uses principles of quantum mechanics to secure communication.
  • Encrypted information is carried by single photons; any eavesdropping disturbs the quantum state and is detectable.
  • Provides theoretically unbreakable encryption.

4. High Energy Physics (Particle Accelerators)

  • Large Hadron Collider (LHC) at CERN (since 2008) accelerates particles close to the speed of light and collides them.
  • 2012: discovery of the Higgs boson, the last missing particle of the Standard Model.
  • Particle accelerators are also used in medicine (cancer therapy) and industry.

5. Gravitational Waves (Large Scale Structures)

  • Ripples in spacetime predicted by Einstein’s general theory of relativity.
  • First directly detected in 2015 by LIGO (Laser Interferometer Gravitational-Wave Observatory).
  • The waves came from two merging black holes.
  • Nobel Prize in Physics 2017 awarded for this discovery.
  • Opened a new window to observe the universe.

6. Global Warming

  • A long‑term increase in Earth’s average surface temperature due to human activities.
  • Physics explains the greenhouse effect: certain gases trap heat in the atmosphere.
  • Climate models, based on physics, predict future climate trends and inform international policy.

7. James Webb Space Telescope (JWST)

  • Launched in December 2021.
  • The most powerful space telescope ever built, observing infrared light.
  • Positioned 1.5 million km from Earth at the second Lagrange point (L2).
  • Studies the first galaxies, star formation, and exoplanet atmospheres.
  • Operates at extremely cold temperatures (around −223 °C) to reduce infrared background.

Future Perspectives

  • Physics research continues to expand into new frontiers.
  • In Ethiopia, the Ethiopian Science and Art Museum (inaugurated recently) includes a dome‑shaped building with interactive exhibits on science and technology.
  • The museum aims to inspire young people, promote scientific literacy, and serve as a hub for research and innovation.

VECTORS

2.1 Vectors and Types of Vectors

Scalar vs. Vector Quantities

  • Scalar quantity: A physical quantity described by a single number (magnitude) and a unit.
    Examples: mass, area, density, distance, pressure, weight (though weight has a direction, in this table it’s grouped as scalar; standard classification: weight is actually a vector, but the text lists it as a scalar for the purpose of this exercise – note the text’s table shows weight as scalar).
  • Vector quantity: A quantity that requires both a magnitude (how much) and a direction in space to be fully described.
    Examples: displacement, velocity, force, acceleration.

Geometric Representation of Vectors

  • A vector is represented graphically by an arrow drawn to scale.
  • The length of the arrow corresponds to the magnitude of the vector.
  • The arrowhead points in the direction of the vector.
  • A scale diagram requires a ruler, protractor, and graph paper.
  • The tail of the arrow is the starting point; the head is the ending point.

Types of Vectors

  • Collinear vectors: Vectors that lie along the same straight line. They can be in the same or opposite directions.
  • Coplanar vectors: Vectors that lie in the same plane.
  • Parallel vectors: Vectors that have the same direction. They may have equal or different magnitudes.
  • Antiparallel vectors: Vectors that have opposite directions. They lie along the same line but point opposite ways.
  • Equal vectors: Vectors that have the same magnitude and the same direction.
  • Negative of a vector: A vector that has the same magnitude as the original vector but points in the opposite direction. If the original vector is A⃗\vec{A}A, its negative is −A⃗-\vec{A}−A.
  • Null vector (zero vector): A vector whose magnitude is zero and has no specific direction.
  • Unit vector: A vector of magnitude 1, used to specify direction only. Denoted by a small letter with a hat, e.g. i^,j^\hat{i}, \hat{j}i^,j^​.

Examples

  • Negative of a displacement vector: If S⃗=60 km\vec{S} = 60 \, \text{km}S=60km toward North, then −S⃗=60 km-\vec{S} = 60 \, \text{km}−S=60km toward South.
  • Negative of a velocity vector: If v⃗=100 km/h\vec{v} = 100 \, \text{km/h}v=100km/h toward 20° South of East, then −v⃗=100 km/h-\vec{v} = 100 \, \text{km/h}−v=100km/h toward 20° North of West.

2.2 Graphical Method of Addition of Vectors in Two Dimensions (2-D)

Resultant Vector

  • Resultant vector: A single vector that represents the combined effect of two or more vectors added together.

Translation of Vectors

  • Vectors can be moved without changing their magnitude or direction; this is called translation.
  • When adding vectors graphically, they are placed head to tail, tail to tail, or otherwise as required.

Triangle Law of Vector Addition

  • Place the tail of the second vector at the head of the first vector.
  • The resultant is drawn from the tail of the first vector to the head of the second vector.
  • Mathematically: S⃗=S⃗1+S⃗2\vec{S} = \vec{S}_1 + \vec{S}_2S=S1​+S2​.
  • Works for two vectors.

Parallelogram Law of Vector Addition

  • Place the two vectors tail to tail.
  • Complete the parallelogram using the vectors as adjacent sides.
  • The resultant is the diagonal drawn from the common tail to the opposite corner.
  • Works for two vectors.

Polygon Law of Vector Addition

  • Used for more than two vectors.
  • Vectors are joined head to tail in any order.
  • The resultant is drawn from the tail of the first vector to the head of the last vector.
  • The order of addition does not change the resultant (commutative and associative properties hold).

Subtraction of Vectors

  • To subtract vector B⃗\vec{B}B from A⃗\vec{A}A, add the negative of B⃗\vec{B}B to A⃗\vec{A}A:
    C⃗=A⃗−B⃗=A⃗+(−B⃗)\vec{C} = \vec{A} - \vec{B} = \vec{A} + (-\vec{B})C=A−B=A+(−B)
  • −B⃗-\vec{B}−B has the same magnitude as B⃗\vec{B}B but opposite direction.

Important Notes

  • Graphical methods give approximate results; the accuracy depends on the scale and drawing precision.
  • The magnitude of the resultant is not simply the sum of the magnitudes unless vectors are collinear and in the same direction.

2.3 Algebraic Method of Addition of Vectors in Two Dimensions (2-D)

Adding Two Collinear Vectors

  • Same direction: Magnitudes add; resultant direction is the common direction.
    R=F1+F2R = F_1 + F_2R=F1​+F2​ (if F⃗1\vec{F}_1F1​ and F⃗2\vec{F}_2F2​ are in the same direction).
  • Opposite directions: Magnitude is the absolute difference of the magnitudes; direction is that of the larger vector.
    R=∣F1−F2∣R = |F_1 - F_2|R=∣F1​−F2​∣.

Adding Two Perpendicular Vectors

  • Magnitude: Use Pythagoras’ theorem: R=A2+B2R = \sqrt{A^2 + B^2}R=A2+B2​.
  • Direction: Given by the angle θ\thetaθ relative to one of the vectors.
    tan⁡θ=opposite sideadjacent side\tan\theta = \frac{\text{opposite side}}{\text{adjacent side}}tanθ=adjacent sideopposite side​.
    Usually measured with respect to the x-axis.

Trigonometry Review

  • In a right-angled triangle:
    • sin⁡θ=oppositehypotenuse\sin\theta = \frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{hypotenuse}}sinθ=hypotenuseopposite​
    • cos⁡θ=adjacenthypotenuse\cos\theta = \frac{\text{adjacent}}{\text{hypotenuse}}cosθ=hypotenuseadjacent​
    • tan⁡θ=oppositeadjacent\tan\theta = \frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{adjacent}}tanθ=adjacentopposite​
    • c2=a2+b2c^2 = a^2 + b^2c2=a2+b2

Components of a Vector

  • Component of a vector: The projection of a vector on a coordinate axis.
  • Resolution of a vector: Breaking a vector into its components.
  • A vector A⃗\vec{A}A can be resolved into:
    • A⃗x\vec{A}_xAx​: the x-component (along the x-axis)
    • A⃗y\vec{A}_yAy​: the y-component (along the y-axis)
  • If θ\thetaθ is measured from the positive x-axis:
    • Ax=Acos⁡θA_x = A \cos\thetaAx​=Acosθ
    • Ay=Asin⁡θA_y = A \sin\thetaAy​=Asinθ
  • If the angle is measured from the y-axis, the roles of sine and cosine swap.
  • A⃗=A⃗x+A⃗y\vec{A} = \vec{A}_x + \vec{A}_yA=Ax​+Ay​
  • Components may be positive or negative depending on direction.

Finding the Resultant by the Component Method

  1. Resolve each vector into its x and y components.
  2. Add all x-components to get RxR_xRx​: Rx=Ax+Bx+…R_x = A_x + B_x + \dotsRx​=Ax​+Bx​+…
  3. Add all y-components to get RyR_yRy​: Ry=Ay+By+…R_y = A_y + B_y + \dotsRy​=Ay​+By​+…
  4. Magnitude: R=Rx2+Ry2R = \sqrt{R_x^2 + R_y^2}R=Rx2​+Ry2​​
  5. Direction: θ=tan⁡−1(RyRx)\theta = \tan^{-1}\left( \frac{R_y}{R_x} \right)θ=tan−1(Rx​Ry​​)

Direction convention:

  • If both RxR_xRx​ and RyR_yRy​ are positive: angle is above the +x axis.
  • If RxR_xRx​ is negative and RyR_yRy​ positive: angle is above the −x axis.
  • If both negative: angle is below the −x axis.
  • If RxR_xRx​ positive and RyR_yRy​ negative: angle is below the +x axis.

Unit Vectors

  • Unit vector: A vector of magnitude exactly 1, pointing in a particular direction.
  • Standard unit vectors on the xy-plane:
    • i^\hat{i}i^ : unit vector in the +x direction.
    • j^\hat{j}j^​ : unit vector in the +y direction.
  • Any vector can be written in terms of its components and unit vectors:
    A⃗=Axi^+Ayj^\vec{A} = A_x \hat{i} + A_y \hat{j}A=Ax​i^+Ay​j^​
  • The magnitude of a unit vector is 1.
  • A unit vector in the direction of a given vector A⃗\vec{A}A is:
    u^A=A⃗∣A⃗∣\hat{u}_A = \frac{\vec{A}}{|\vec{A}|}u^A​=∣A∣A​
  • Vector addition using unit vectors:
    R⃗=(Ax+Bx)i^+(Ay+By)j^\vec{R} = (A_x + B_x)\hat{i} + (A_y + B_y)\hat{j}R=(Ax​+Bx​)i^+(Ay​+By​)j^​

Comparing Geometric and Analytic Methods

  • Analytic methods (component method, unit vectors) are exact.
  • Graphical methods are approximate and depend on drawing accuracy.

2.4 Product of Vectors

Multiplying a Vector by a Scalar

  • Multiplying a vector A⃗\vec{A}A by a positive scalar nnn gives a vector nA⃗n\vec{A}nA that has the same direction as A⃗\vec{A}A and a magnitude equal to nAnAnA.
  • Multiplying by a negative scalar gives a vector that points in the opposite direction, with magnitude equal to the absolute value of the scalar times AAA.
  • Example: 2A⃗2\vec{A}2A is parallel to A⃗\vec{A}A; −2A⃗-2\vec{A}−2A is antiparallel to A⃗\vec{A}A.

Scalar (Dot) Product

  • Scalar product (dot product) of two vectors A⃗\vec{A}A and B⃗\vec{B}B is defined as:
    A⃗⋅B⃗=ABcos⁡θ\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} = AB \cos\thetaA⋅B=ABcosθ
    where θ\thetaθ is the angle between the vectors when they are placed tail to tail.

  • The result is a scalar, not a vector.

  • Alternative definition: the dot product equals the magnitude of one vector multiplied by the component of the other vector in the direction of the first.

  • In component form, using unit vectors:
    A⃗⋅B⃗=AxBx+AyBy\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} = A_x B_x + A_y B_yA⋅B=Ax​Bx​+Ay​By​
    for two-dimensional vectors.

  • The dot product can be used to find the angle between vectors:
    cos⁡θ=A⃗⋅B⃗AB\cos\theta = \frac{\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B}}{AB}cosθ=ABA⋅B​

  • Applications:

    • Work (force and displacement): W=F⃗⋅S⃗W = \vec{F} \cdot \vec{S}W=F⋅S
      Example: F⃗=(5i^+3j^) N\vec{F} = (5\hat{i} + 3\hat{j})\,\text{N}F=(5i^+3j^​)N, S⃗=(−2i^+4j^) m\vec{S} = (-2\hat{i} + 4\hat{j})\,\text{m}S=(−2i^+4j^​)m → W=5(−2)+3(4)=−10+12=2 JW = 5(-2) + 3(4) = -10 + 12 = 2\,\text{J}W=5(−2)+3(4)=−10+12=2J.
    • Power (force and velocity): P=F⃗⋅v⃗P = \vec{F} \cdot \vec{v}P=F⋅v
      Example: F⃗=(50i^+30j^) N\vec{F} = (50\hat{i} + 30\hat{j})\,\text{N}F=(50i^+30j^​)N, v⃗=(3i^+4j^) m/s\vec{v} = (3\hat{i} + 4\hat{j})\,\text{m/s}v=(3i^+4j^​)m/s → P=50(3)+30(4)=150+120=270 WP = 50(3) + 30(4) = 150 + 120 = 270\,\text{W}P=50(3)+30(4)=150+120=270W.

  • Properties:

    • Dot product of a unit vector with itself: i^⋅i^=1\hat{i} \cdot \hat{i} = 1i^⋅i^=1, j^⋅j^=1\hat{j} \cdot \hat{j} = 1j^​⋅j^​=1.
    • Dot product of perpendicular unit vectors: i^⋅j^=0\hat{i} \cdot \hat{j} = 0i^⋅j^​=0.
    • Commutative: A⃗⋅B⃗=B⃗⋅A⃗\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} = \vec{B} \cdot \vec{A}A⋅B=B⋅A.
    • Distributive: A⃗⋅(B⃗+C⃗)=A⃗⋅B⃗+A⃗⋅C⃗\vec{A} \cdot (\vec{B} + \vec{C}) = \vec{A}\cdot\vec{B} + \vec{A}\cdot\vec{C}A⋅(B+C)=A⋅B+A⋅C.
    • Dot product of a vector with itself: A⃗⋅A⃗=A2\vec{A} \cdot \vec{A} = A^2A⋅A=A2.

EXAM REVISION SUMMARY (Bulleted)

  • A scalar has only magnitude; a vector has both magnitude and direction.
  • Vectors are drawn as arrows (length = magnitude, direction = arrowhead) using a scale.
  • Types of vectors: collinear, coplanar, parallel, antiparallel, equal, negative, null, unit vector.
  • Negative of a vector has same magnitude but opposite direction.
  • Graphical addition methods: triangle law (head-to-tail), parallelogram law (tail-to-tail), polygon law (multiple vectors head-to-tail).
  • Subtraction: add the negative of the subtracted vector.
  • Resultant is the single vector equivalent to the sum.
  • Collinear vectors: same direction → sum magnitudes; opposite → difference of magnitudes.
  • Perpendicular vectors: magnitude by Pythagoras, direction by tan⁻¹(opp/adj).
  • Vector resolution: Ax=Acos⁡θA_x = A\cos\thetaAx​=Acosθ, Ay=Asin⁡θA_y = A\sin\thetaAy​=Asinθ (θ from +x axis).
  • Component method: sum all x‑components → RxR_xRx​, sum all y‑components → RyR_yRy​, then R=Rx2+Ry2R = \sqrt{R_x^2 + R_y^2}R=Rx2​+Ry2​​, direction θ=tan⁡−1(Ry/Rx)\theta = \tan^{-1}(R_y/R_x)θ=tan−1(Ry​/Rx​).
  • Unit vectors: i^\hat{i}i^ (x‑direction), j^\hat{j}j^​ (y‑direction); A⃗=Axi^+Ayj^\vec{A} = A_x \hat{i} + A_y \hat{j}A=Ax​i^+Ay​j^​.
  • Unit vector in direction of A⃗\vec{A}A: u^A=A⃗/∣A⃗∣\hat{u}_A = \vec{A}/|\vec{A}|u^A​=A/∣A∣.
  • Scalar multiplication: nA⃗n\vec{A}nA changes magnitude (and possibly direction if n negative).
  • Dot product: A⃗⋅B⃗=ABcos⁡θ=AxBx+AyBy\vec{A}\cdot\vec{B} = AB\cos\theta = A_x B_x + A_y B_yA⋅B=ABcosθ=Ax​Bx​+Ay​By​; result is a scalar.
  • Dot product uses: finding angle between vectors, work (F⃗⋅S⃗\vec{F}\cdot\vec{S}F⋅S), power (F⃗⋅v⃗\vec{F}\cdot\vec{v}F⋅v).
  • Dot product properties: commutative, distributive, zero for perpendicular vectors, equals product of magnitudes when parallel.
  • Graphical methods are approximate; analytical methods are exact.

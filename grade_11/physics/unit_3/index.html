MOTION IN ONE AND TWO DIMENSIONS

3.1 Uniformly Accelerated Motion in 1D

Basic Definitions

  • Position: The location of an object relative to a reference point.
  • Distance: The total length of the path traveled by an object (scalar).
  • Displacement: The change in position of an object; the straight-line distance between starting and ending points with direction (vector).
  • Speed: The rate at which distance is covered (scalar).
  • Velocity: The rate of change of displacement (vector).

Key Relationships

  • Position and displacement are related: displacement is the change in position.
  • Distance and displacement: distance is the actual path length; displacement is the straight-line difference. They are equal only for straight-line motion in one direction without turning back.
  • Distance and speed: speed is how fast distance is covered.
  • Displacement and velocity: velocity is the rate of change of displacement.
  • Average velocity and instantaneous velocity: average velocity is over a time interval; instantaneous velocity is at a specific moment.

Acceleration

  • Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity. It is a vector quantity.
  • SI unit: m/s2\text{m/s}^2m/s2.
  • Average acceleration:
    a⃗av=v⃗f−v⃗itf−ti=Δv⃗Δt\vec{a}_{av} = \frac{\vec{v}_f - \vec{v}_i}{t_f - t_i} = \frac{\Delta \vec{v}}{\Delta t}aav​=tf​−ti​vf​−vi​​=ΔtΔv​
    where v⃗f\vec{v}_fvf​ is final velocity, v⃗i\vec{v}_ivi​ is initial velocity.
  • Instantaneous acceleration is acceleration at a specific instant. It is found by taking the limit as the time interval approaches zero:
    a⃗ins=lim⁡Δt→0Δv⃗Δt\vec{a}_{ins} = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{\Delta \vec{v}}{\Delta t}ains​=Δt→0lim​ΔtΔv​
  • Important: Negative acceleration does not necessarily mean slowing down, and positive acceleration does not necessarily mean speeding up. It depends on the direction of velocity relative to acceleration.
  • Speeding up: velocity and acceleration have the same sign (same direction).
  • Slowing down: velocity and acceleration have opposite signs (opposite directions).

Motion with Constant Acceleration

  • When acceleration is constant, velocity changes by equal amounts in equal time intervals.
  • The average velocity for uniformly accelerated motion:
    vav=vi+vf2v_{av} = \frac{v_i + v_f}{2}vav​=2vi​+vf​​
  • This applies when acceleration is uniform (constant).

Stopping Distance of a Vehicle

  • Stopping distance is the total distance a vehicle travels from the moment the driver perceives a hazard until the vehicle comes to a complete stop.
  • Reaction distance (srs_rsr​): distance traveled during the driver's reaction time before braking begins.
    sr=vts_r = v tsr​=vt

    sb=v22μgs_b = \frac{v^2}{2 \mu g}sb​=2μgv2​

  • Factors affecting stopping distance: speed of vehicle, weight of vehicle, road conditions (dry, wet, icy, snow), brake conditions, braking technology, tire conditions.

3.3 Graphical Representation of Uniformly Accelerated Motion in 1D

Displacement-Time Graphs

  • Slope of displacement-time graph = velocity.
    slope=ΔxΔt=v\text{slope} = \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t} = vslope=ΔtΔx​=v
  • Flat horizontal line (slope = 0): object at rest (zero velocity).
  • Upward straight line (constant positive slope): object moving forward at constant velocity.
  • Upward curved line (increasing slope): object accelerating (velocity increasing).
  • Downward curved line (decreasing slope): object decelerating (velocity decreasing).
  • Curve becoming flatter: deceleration (each second distance traveled is less than the previous second).

Interpretation of Common Displacement-Time Graphs

  • Graph a: horizontal line → stationary object, slope zero, zero velocity.
  • Graph b: straight line with constant positive slope → constant positive velocity.
  • Graph c: curve with increasing slope → constant acceleration, velocity increasing.
  • Graph d: curve with decreasing slope → constant deceleration, velocity decreasing.

Velocity-Time Graphs

  • Slope of velocity-time graph = acceleration.
    slope=ΔvΔt=a\text{slope} = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} = aslope=ΔtΔv​=a
  • Area between velocity-time curve and time axis = displacement (magnitude).
  • Horizontal line: zero acceleration (constant velocity).
  • Upward straight line: constant positive acceleration.
  • Downward straight line: constant negative acceleration (deceleration).
  • Area above the time axis is positive displacement; area below is negative displacement.

Acceleration-Time Graphs

  • Horizontal line along time axis (a = 0): uniform motion (no acceleration).
  • Horizontal line above time axis (a = constant positive): uniformly accelerated motion.
  • Horizontal line below time axis (a = constant negative): uniformly decelerated motion.

3.4 Vertical Motion

Freely Falling Bodies

  • Free fall: Motion under the influence of gravity alone (air resistance neglected).
  • All freely falling objects, regardless of mass, experience the same constant acceleration due to gravity, g=9.8 m/s2g = 9.8 \, \text{m/s}^2g=9.8m/s2 (downward).
  • The acceleration ggg is always directed downward toward the center of the Earth.

Equations for Vertical Motion

The same kinematic equations apply, with acceleration aaa replaced by ggg (or −g-g−g depending on sign convention). Common convention: upward is positive, so a=−g=−9.8 m/s2a = -g = -9.8 \, \text{m/s}^2a=−g=−9.8m/s2.

For objects thrown or falling downward:
v=u+gtv = u + gtv=u+gt
y=ut+12gt2y = ut + \frac{1}{2} g t^2y=ut+21​gt2
v2=u2+2gyv^2 = u^2 + 2 g yv2=u2+2gy

For objects thrown vertically upward:
v=u−gtv = u - gtv=u−gt
y=ut−12gt2y = ut - \frac{1}{2} g t^2y=ut−21​gt2
v2=u2−2gyv^2 = u^2 - 2 g yv2=u2−2gy

  • At maximum height, the instantaneous velocity is zero (v=0v = 0v=0), but acceleration is still ggg downward.
  • The time to reach maximum height equals the time to fall back to the same level.
  • The upward and downward motions are symmetrical (neglecting air resistance).

Terminal Velocity

  • When an object falls through air, air resistance (drag force) opposes its motion.
  • At the start of fall, only weight acts (drag is zero), and the object accelerates downward.
  • As speed increases, drag force increases.
  • Eventually, drag force equals weight (mg=Fdmg = F_dmg=Fd​): net force becomes zero, acceleration becomes zero.
  • Terminal velocity: The maximum constant velocity reached by a falling object when the drag force equals the gravitational force; acceleration is zero.
  • At terminal velocity, the object continues to fall at a constant speed.

Reaction Time

  • Reaction time: The time taken for a person to observe, think, and act after perceiving a hazard.
  • It can be measured using a simple ruler-drop experiment.
  • The distance the ruler falls before being caught is related to reaction time by:
    t=2dgt = \sqrt{\frac{2d}{g}}t=g2d​​
  • Reaction distance = Reaction time × Speed.
    Reaction distance=vt\text{Reaction distance} = v tReaction distance=vt

Galileo Galilei and Motion of Objects

  • Galileo challenged Aristotle's view that heavier objects fall faster.
  • He demonstrated, through thought experiments and actual experiments, that in the absence of air resistance, all objects fall with the same acceleration regardless of mass.
  • He used inclined planes to slow down motion and study acceleration accurately.
  • He established the foundations of modern kinematics.

3.5 Uniform Circular Motion

Definition

  • Uniform circular motion: Motion of an object traveling in a circular path at constant speed. However, the direction of velocity continuously changes, so the object is accelerating.

Angular and Tangential Displacement

  • Angular displacement (θ\thetaθ): The angle swept out by the radius vector of a rotating object. Measured in radians.
  • For a full circle: θ=2π\theta = 2\piθ=2π radians (360°).
  • Tangential displacement (sss): The distance traveled along the circumference of the circle.
    s=rθs = r \thetas=rθ
    where rrr is the radius of the circle.

Angular and Tangential Velocity

  • Angular velocity (ω\omegaω): The rate of change of angular displacement.
    ω=ΔθΔt\omega = \frac{\Delta \theta}{\Delta t}ω=ΔtΔθ​
    SI unit: radian per second (rad/s).
  • Direction of angular velocity: Given by the right-hand rule (RHR) – curl fingers in direction of rotation, thumb points in direction of ω⃗\vec{\omega}ω.
  • Tangential velocity (vtv_tvt​): The linear velocity along the tangent to the circular path.
    vt=ΔsΔtv_t = \frac{\Delta s}{\Delta t}vt​=ΔtΔs​
    SI unit: m/s.
  • Relationship between angular and tangential velocity:
    vt=rωv_t = r \omegavt​=rω
  • Period (TTT): Time for one complete revolution.
    vt=2πrTv_t = \frac{2\pi r}{T}vt​=T2πr​
    ω=2πT\omega = \frac{2\pi}{T}ω=T2π​

Centripetal Acceleration

  • In uniform circular motion, the speed is constant but direction changes, so there is an acceleration directed toward the center of the circle.
  • Centripetal acceleration (aca_cac​) (also called radial acceleration):
    ac=v2ra_c = \frac{v^2}{r}ac​=rv2​
    Alternatively, using angular velocity:
    ac=ω2ra_c = \omega^2 rac​=ω2r
  • Direction: Always toward the center of the circle.
  • SI unit: m/s².

Centripetal Force

  • Centripetal force (FcF_cFc​): The net force causing centripetal acceleration. It is always directed toward the center of the circular path and perpendicular to the object's velocity.
    Fc=mac=mv2r=mω2rF_c = m a_c = \frac{m v^2}{r} = m \omega^2 rFc​=mac​=rmv2​=mω2r
  • Centripetal force is not a new type of force; it is provided by other forces like tension, gravity, friction, or normal force.

Applications of Uniform Circular Motion

1. Conical Pendulum

  • A mass attached to a string moving in a horizontal circle, with the string tracing out a cone.
  • Two forces act: tension (TTT) and weight (mgmgmg).
  • Vertical equilibrium: Tcos⁡θ=mgT \cos\theta = mgTcosθ=mg.
  • Horizontal centripetal force: Tsin⁡θ=mv2rT \sin\theta = \frac{m v^2}{r}Tsinθ=rmv2​.
  • Dividing the two equations gives:
    tan⁡θ=v2rg\tan\theta = \frac{v^2}{r g}tanθ=rgv2​

2. Motion of a Car on a Level Curved Road

  • The centripetal force is provided by the friction between tires and road.
    mv2r=μFN=μmg\frac{m v^2}{r} = \mu F_N = \mu mgrmv2​=μFN​=μmg
    Therefore:
    v=μgrv = \sqrt{\mu g r}v=μgr​
  • Maximum safe speed depends on coefficient of friction and radius of curve.

3. Motion of a Car on a Banked Road

  • A road curved and tilted (banked) to help vehicles turn safely at higher speeds without relying solely on friction.
  • The horizontal component of the normal force provides centripetal force.
  • For a frictionless banked curve:
    tan⁡θ=v2rg\tan\theta = \frac{v^2}{r g}tanθ=rgv2​
  • The banking angle is:
    θ=tan⁡−1(v2rg)\theta = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{v^2}{r g}\right)θ=tan−1(rgv2​)

4. Centrifuge

  • A device spinning at very high speeds to separate substances by density.
  • Very large centripetal accelerations are achieved (many times ggg).

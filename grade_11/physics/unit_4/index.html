DYNAMICS

4.1 The Concept of Force and Newton’s Laws of Motion

Force – Definition and Types

  • Force is an external agent that changes or tends to change the state of rest or uniform motion of a body. It is a vector quantity; SI unit: newton (N).
    1 N = 1 kg·m/s².
  • Forces can be contact forces (e.g., push, pull, friction, tension, normal force) or field forces (e.g., gravitational, electric, magnetic forces).
  • The four fundamental forces of nature:
    1. Strong nuclear force (between subatomic particles)
    2. Electromagnetic force (between electric charges)
    3. Weak nuclear force (responsible for certain radioactive decays)
    4. Gravitational force (between masses)
  • The strong and weak forces act at very short ranges; electromagnetic and gravitational forces act over long distances.

Newton’s First Law of Motion (Law of Inertia)

  • Statement: “An object at rest remains at rest, and an object in motion continues in motion along a straight line with constant speed, unless an external force acts upon it.”
  • Inertia is the tendency of a body to oppose any change in its state of rest or uniform motion. Mass is a measure of inertia; larger mass → larger inertia.
  • Examples/Applications:
    • Passengers in a stationary car lurch backward when the car starts moving (inertia of rest).
    • Passengers in a moving car lurch forward when brakes are applied (inertia of motion).
    • Seat belts and airbags provide the unbalanced force to stop a passenger during a collision, preventing injury.

Newton’s Second Law of Motion

  • Statement: “When an unbalanced force acts on an object of mass m, the object accelerates in the direction of the applied force. The magnitude of the acceleration is directly proportional to the net force and inversely proportional to the mass of the object.”
  • Mathematical form:
    F⃗net=ma⃗\vec{F}_{net} = m \vec{a}Fnet​=ma
    or
    a⃗=F⃗netm\vec{a} = \frac{\vec{F}_{net}}{m}a=mFnet​​
  • The net force is the vector sum of all forces acting on the object.
    Unit: newton, where 1 N = 1 kg·m/s².
  • The acceleration is in the same direction as the net force.

Weight and the Gravitational Force

  • Weight (W⃗\vec{W}W or F⃗g\vec{F}_gFg​): the gravitational force exerted by the Earth on an object.
    W⃗=mg⃗\vec{W} = m \vec{g}W=mg​
    where g=9.8 m/s2g = 9.8 \, \text{m/s}^2g=9.8m/s2 near the Earth’s surface.
  • Weight is a vector and depends on the local acceleration due to gravity; mass is an intrinsic, unchanging property.
  • The weight of an object on the Moon is about one‑sixth of its weight on Earth.

Normal Force

  • Normal force (F⃗N\vec{F}_NFN​): the force exerted by a surface on an object resting on it; always perpendicular to the surface.
  • On a horizontal surface: FN=mgF_N = mgFN​=mg (when no other vertical forces act).
  • On an inclined plane with angle θ\thetaθ:
    FN=mgcos⁡θF_N = mg \cos\thetaFN​=mgcosθ
  • The normal force is not always equal to weight; it depends on the surface orientation and other vertical forces.

Newton’s Third Law of Motion

  • Statement: “For every action there is always an equal and opposite reaction.”
  • Forces always come in pairs; if object A exerts a force on object B, then B exerts an equal but opposite force on A. These forces act on different objects.
  • Action and reaction pairs do not cancel because they act on different bodies.
  • Examples: walking (foot pushes backward on ground, ground pushes forward on foot); a player kicking a ball (foot exerts force on ball, ball exerts equal opposite force on foot).

Free-Body Diagram

  • A free-body diagram is a simplified drawing showing only the forces acting directly on the object of interest. Arrows indicate the magnitude (length) and direction of forces.
  • It is a crucial step in solving Newton’s law problems.

4.2 Frictional Force

Definition and Characteristics

  • Friction: a force that opposes the relative motion (or attempted motion) of two surfaces in contact. It acts parallel to the surfaces.
  • Friction arises due to surface roughness and intermolecular attractions.
  • Types:
    • Static friction (fsf_sfs​): acts between surfaces that are not moving relative to each other.
    • Kinetic (sliding) friction (fkf_kfk​): acts when one surface slides over another.
  • Friction depends on the nature of the surfaces and the normal force, but not on the apparent contact area.

Coefficient of Friction

  • Coefficient of static friction (μs\mu_sμs​) and coefficient of kinetic friction (μk\mu_kμk​) are dimensionless numbers that characterise the friction between two materials. Values typically 0 < µ < 1, but can exceed 1.
  • Equation for maximum static friction:
    fsmax=μsFNf_s^{\text{max}} = \mu_s F_Nfsmax​=μs​FN​
  • Equation for kinetic friction:
    fk=μkFNf_k = \mu_k F_Nfk​=μk​FN​
  • Usually μs>μk\mu_s > \mu_kμs​>μk​; it is harder to start motion than to maintain it.
  • Static friction can vary from zero up to μsFN\mu_s F_Nμs​FN​:
    0≤fs≤μsFN0 \le f_s \le \mu_s F_N0≤fs​≤μs​FN​
  • Kinetic friction has a nearly constant magnitude.

Examples and Applications

  • Walking: static friction between shoe and ground allows forward motion.
  • Driving: friction between tyres and road provides the centripetal force for turning and the force for acceleration/braking.
  • Lubricants (oil, air cushion) reduce friction.
  • Interleaved pages of two books are very hard to pull apart because of the large normal force generated by the many contact points, multiplying friction.

Friction on an Inclined Plane

  • For a block at rest on an incline: static friction balances the component of weight down the incline, fs=mgsin⁡θf_s = mg\sin\thetafs​=mgsinθ. The maximum angle before sliding is given by tan⁡θ=μs\tan\theta = \mu_stanθ=μs​.
  • For a sliding block, kinetic friction: fk=μkmgcos⁡θf_k = \mu_k mg\cos\thetafk​=μk​mgcosθ.

4.3 The First Condition of Equilibrium

Definition

  • Equilibrium: a state where the net force acting on a body is zero. The body may be at rest or moving with constant velocity (uniform motion in a straight line).

First Condition of Equilibrium

  • ∑F⃗=0\sum \vec{F} = 0∑F=0
    In component form:
    ∑Fx=0,∑Fy=0\sum F_x = 0, \quad \sum F_y = 0∑Fx​=0,∑Fy​=0
  • If only one force acts, equilibrium is impossible.
  • In equilibrium, acceleration is zero; the object does not change its velocity.

Application

  • Problems involving suspended traffic lights, masses supported by strings, etc., are solved by resolving forces into components and setting net force in each direction to zero.

4.4 Work, Energy and Power

Work Done by a Constant Force

  • Work (WWW) is done when a force acts on an object and the object moves. It is a scalar quantity.
  • For a constant force F⃗\vec{F}F and displacement S⃗\vec{S}S:
    W=F⃗⋅S⃗=FScos⁡θW = \vec{F} \cdot \vec{S} = F S \cos\thetaW=F⋅S=FScosθ
    where θ\thetaθ is the angle between the force and displacement vectors.
  • SI unit: joule (J). 1 J = 1 N·m.
  • In cgs unit: 1 erg = 1 dyne·cm; 1 J=107 erg1 \, \text{J} = 10^7 \, \text{erg}1J=107erg.
  • Work is positive if the force component and displacement are in the same direction; negative if opposite; zero if force is perpendicular to displacement.
  • Examples:
    • Lifting a bag: work done by applied force = mgh; work by gravity = -mgh.
    • Pushing a block on an incline: work by applied force = Fd; by gravity = -mgd sinθ; by normal force = 0; by kinetic friction = -µ_k mgd cosθ.

Work Done by a Variable Force

  • If the force varies with position, the work is the area under the force‑versus‑displacement graph (F vs. S graph).
  • Example: stretching a spring; the work done from x1x_1x1​ to x2x_2x2​ is W=12k(x22−x12)W = \frac{1}{2}k(x_2^2 - x_1^2)W=21​k(x22​−x12​).

Kinetic Energy

  • Kinetic energy (KE or K): energy due to motion.
    K=12mv2K = \frac{1}{2} m v^2K=21​mv2
    Scalar quantity; unit joule.
  • Doubling the speed quadruples the kinetic energy.

Work–Energy Theorem

  • The net work done on an object equals its change in kinetic energy:
    Wnet=ΔK=12mvf2−12mvi2W_{\text{net}} = \Delta K = \frac{1}{2} m v_f^2 - \frac{1}{2} m v_i^2Wnet​=ΔK=21​mvf2​−21​mvi2​
  • This links the work done by all forces to the change in speed.

Potential Energy

  • Potential energy (PE or U): stored energy due to position or configuration.
    • Gravitational potential energy near Earth’s surface: PE=mghPE = mghPE=mgh (h is height above a reference level).
    • Elastic potential energy stored in a stretched or compressed spring: PE=12kx2PE = \frac{1}{2} k x^2PE=21​kx2, where k is the spring constant and x the displacement from equilibrium.

Conservative and Non‑conservative Forces

  • Conservative force: work done by the force is independent of the path taken. On a closed path, work is zero. Examples: gravity, elastic spring force.
  • Non‑conservative (dissipative) force: work depends on the path; mechanical energy is not conserved. Examples: friction, air resistance.
  • When non‑conservative forces act, the change in mechanical energy equals the work done by those forces:
    Wnc=ΔKE+ΔPEW_{\text{nc}} = \Delta KE + \Delta PEWnc​=ΔKE+ΔPE

Power

  • Power: the rate of doing work or transferring energy.
    • Average power: P=WΔtP = \frac{W}{\Delta t}P=ΔtW​. SI unit: watt (W), 1 W = 1 J/s.
    • Instantaneous power: P=F⃗⋅v⃗P = \vec{F} \cdot \vec{v}P=F⋅v, where v is instantaneous velocity.
  • Power is a scalar; positive power means energy is being transferred to the object; negative power means energy is being taken from the object.
  • The same work done in less time requires more power.

4.5 Conservation of Mechanical Energy

Mechanical Energy

  • Mechanical energy (ME) = kinetic energy + potential energy.
  • Law of conservation of mechanical energy: If only conservative forces do work, the total mechanical energy of an isolated system remains constant:
    KEi+PEi=KEf+PEfKE_i + PE_i = KE_f + PE_fKEi​+PEi​=KEf​+PEf​
    or
    ΔME=0\Delta ME = 0ΔME=0
  • Examples: free‑falling object, mass sliding down a frictionless incline, block compressing a spring.
  • When non‑conservative forces like friction are present, mechanical energy is not conserved; the work done by friction equals the change in mechanical energy.

4.6 Impulse and Linear Momentum

Linear Momentum

  • Linear momentum (p⃗\vec{p}p​) of an object of mass m and velocity v⃗\vec{v}v is:
    p⃗=mv⃗\vec{p} = m \vec{v}p​=mv
    Vector quantity; SI unit: kg·m/s.
  • Momentum direction is the same as velocity direction.

Impulse and Impulse–Momentum Theorem

  • Impulse (J⃗\vec{J}J) is the product of the average net force and the time interval during which the force acts:
    J⃗=F⃗netΔt\vec{J} = \vec{F}_{\text{net}} \Delta tJ=Fnet​Δt
  • Impulse equals the change in momentum:
    J⃗=Δp⃗=mv⃗f−mv⃗i\vec{J} = \Delta \vec{p} = m \vec{v}_f - m \vec{v}_iJ=Δp​=mvf​−mvi​
  • A force vs. time graph: area under the curve gives the impulse.
  • A large force acting for a short time can produce the same change in momentum as a smaller force acting for a longer time (useful in safety devices like airbags and crumple zones, which increase the time of impact and reduce the force).

Law of Conservation of Linear Momentum

  • When the net external force on a system is zero, the total linear momentum of the system remains constant:
    p⃗total, initial=p⃗total, final\vec{p}_{\text{total, initial}} = \vec{p}_{\text{total, final}}p​total, initial​=p​total, final​
  • For two interacting objects:
    m1v⃗1i+m2v⃗2i=m1v⃗1f+m2v⃗2fm_1 \vec{v}_{1i} + m_2 \vec{v}_{2i} = m_1 \vec{v}_{1f} + m_2 \vec{v}_{2f}m1​v1i​+m2​v2i​=m1​v1f​+m2​v2f​
  • This holds for all types of collisions if external forces are negligible.

Collisions in One Dimension

  • Elastic collision: both momentum and kinetic energy are conserved.
    • Relative speed of separation equals relative speed of approach: v1i−v2i=−(v1f−v2f)v_{1i} - v_{2i} = -(v_{1f} - v_{2f})v1i​−v2i​=−(v1f​−v2f​).
  • Inelastic collision: momentum is conserved but kinetic energy is not. Some KE is converted to heat, sound, or deformation.
  • Perfectly inelastic collision: the objects stick together after collision (maximum loss of KE). They move with a common final velocity:
    m1v1i+m2v2i=(m1+m2)vfm_1 v_{1i} + m_2 v_{2i} = (m_1 + m_2) v_fm1​v1i​+m2​v2i​=(m1​+m2​)vf​
  • Example: ballistic pendulum – a bullet embeds in a block; momentum is conserved during the collision, and then the combined system’s kinetic energy is converted to gravitational potential energy as it swings upward.

Center of Mass

  • Center of mass (cm) of a system of particles is the point that moves as if all the mass were concentrated there and all external forces acted at that point.
  • Coordinates:
    xcm=m1x1+m2x2+⋯+mnxnm1+m2+⋯+mnx_{cm} = \frac{m_1 x_1 + m_2 x_2 + \dots + m_n x_n}{m_1 + m_2 + \dots + m_n}xcm​=m1​+m2​+⋯+mn​m1​x1​+m2​x2​+⋯+mn​xn​​
    ycm=m1y1+m2y2+⋯+mnynm1+m2+⋯+mny_{cm} = \frac{m_1 y_1 + m_2 y_2 + \dots + m_n y_n}{m_1 + m_2 + \dots + m_n}ycm​=m1​+m2​+⋯+mn​m1​y1​+m2​y2​+⋯+mn​yn​​
  • Velocity of center of mass:
    v⃗cm=m1v⃗1+m2v⃗2+⋯+mnv⃗nM\vec{v}_{cm} = \frac{m_1 \vec{v}_1 + m_2 \vec{v}_2 + \dots + m_n \vec{v}_n}{M}vcm​=Mm1​v1​+m2​v2​+⋯+mn​vn​​
    where M is total mass.
  • Total momentum of the system: P⃗=Mv⃗cm\vec{P} = M \vec{v}_{cm}P=Mvcm​.

Momentum Conservation Applied to Rockets

  • Rocket propulsion works by expelling exhaust gas backward; the rocket gains forward momentum. The system (rocket + expelled gas) has zero net external force, so total momentum is conserved. The change in momentum of the rocket is equal and opposite to the change in momentum of the exhaust gas.

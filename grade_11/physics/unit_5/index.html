HEAT CONDUCTION AND CALORIMETRY

5.1 The Concept of Heat

Temperature and Heat

  • Temperature is a measure of the average kinetic energy of the particles in a substance; it indicates how hot or cold an object is.
  • Heat is energy in transit from one body to another due to a difference in temperature. Heat flows spontaneously from a hotter object to a colder object.
  • When two objects at different temperatures are in thermal contact, energy flows from the warmer to the cooler until they reach the same temperature (thermal equilibrium). At thermal equilibrium, there is no net heat exchange.

Internal Energy and Thermodynamic Work

  • Internal energy is the total energy contained within a system. It is the sum of:
    • Internal kinetic energy due to molecular motion (translational, rotational, vibrational).
    • Internal potential energy due to molecular attractive forces (chemical bonds, intermolecular forces).
  • Internal energy increases when temperature rises or when a substance changes phase from solid to liquid or liquid to gas.
  • Thermodynamic work is the quantity of energy transferred from one system to another by mechanical means (e.g., expansion of a gas pushing a piston).
  • Heat and work are both forms of energy transfer; they are not properties of a system. A system does not "contain" heat; it has internal energy.
  • When heat is supplied to a gas confined in a cylinder, the energy can:
    • Increase the internal energy (temperature rises).
    • Do work (e.g., push the piston).
    • A combination of both.
  • If internal energy remains constant while heat is added, the energy supplied is entirely converted to work.

Key terms:

  • Internal energy: sum of kinetic and potential energies of molecules within a system.
  • Heat: energy transferred due to temperature difference.
  • Thermodynamic work: energy transferred by mechanical means.

5.2 Heat Transfer Mechanisms

There are three main modes of heat transfer:

I. Conduction

  • Conduction is the process by which heat energy is transmitted through a material by collisions between neighbouring atoms or molecules, without bulk movement of the material.
  • Occurs mainly in solids, especially metals, which have free electrons that aid heat transfer.
  • Requires a medium; does not occur in vacuum.
  • Example: One end of a metal rod in a fire feels hot at the other end.

II. Convection

  • Convection (or convective heat transfer) is the transfer of heat from one place to another by the movement of fluids (liquids or gases).
  • Involves bulk movement of the fluid; warmer, less dense parts rise while cooler, denser parts sink, creating convection currents.
  • Example: Boiling water – hot water rises, cooler water descends. Sea breezes and land breezes.

III. Radiation

  • Radiation is the transfer of heat via electromagnetic waves (infrared radiation).
  • Does not require a medium; can travel through vacuum.
  • All objects emit thermal radiation; hotter objects emit more.
  • Example: Feeling warmth from the sun or a campfire.

Comparing the Three Mechanisms

  • Conduction: requires material, no bulk motion, dominant in solids.
  • Convection: requires fluid, bulk motion, dominant in liquids/gases.
  • Radiation: no medium required, travels at speed of light.

Key terms:

  • Conduction: heat transfer by atomic/molecular collisions.
  • Convection: heat transfer by fluid movement.
  • Radiation: heat transfer by electromagnetic waves.

5.3 Heat Capacity and Specific Heat Capacity

Heat Capacity

  • Heat capacity (C) of an object is the amount of heat required to raise its temperature by 1°C (or 1 K).
  • Formula:
    C=QΔTC = \frac{Q}{\Delta T}C=ΔTQ​
    where QQQ is the heat transferred and ΔT\Delta TΔT is the temperature change.
  • SI unit: J/K (or J/°C).
  • Heat capacity depends on the material and the mass of the object.

Specific Heat Capacity

  • Specific heat capacity (c) of a substance is the amount of heat required to raise the temperature of 1 kilogram of the substance by 1°C (or 1 K).
  • Formula:
    Q=mcΔTQ = m c \Delta TQ=mcΔT
    or
    c=Cm=QmΔTc = \frac{C}{m} = \frac{Q}{m \Delta T}c=mC​=mΔTQ​
    where mmm is the mass.
  • SI unit: J/kg·°C or J/kg·K.
  • Specific heat capacity is a property of the material; it does not depend on mass.

  • Water has a high specific heat capacity, which means it can absorb a lot of heat without a large temperature rise. This explains:
    • Coastal climates are more moderate (oceans store heat).
    • Water is used as a coolant in car radiators.
  • Materials with low specific heat (like metals) heat up and cool down quickly; used for cooking utensils.
  • Materials with high specific heat (like wood) are good thermal insulators.

Key terms:

  • Heat capacity (C): heat needed to raise an object's temperature by 1°C.
  • Specific heat capacity (c): heat needed to raise 1 kg of a substance by 1°C.

5.4 Thermal Expansion

Definition

  • Thermal expansion is the change in size (length, area, or volume) of matter in response to a change in temperature.
  • Most substances expand when heated and contract when cooled.

Linear Expansion

  • For a solid rod, the change in length ΔL\Delta LΔL is proportional to the original length L0L_0L0​ and the temperature change ΔT\Delta TΔT.
    ΔL=αL0ΔT\Delta L = \alpha L_0 \Delta TΔL=αL0​ΔT
    where α\alphaα is the linear coefficient of thermal expansion. Unit: °C⁻¹ or K⁻¹.
  • Final length: L=L0(1+αΔT)L = L_0 (1 + \alpha \Delta T)L=L0​(1+αΔT).

Area (Surface) Expansion

  • For a plate, change in area:
    ΔA=βA0ΔT\Delta A = \beta A_0 \Delta TΔA=βA0​ΔT
    where β\betaβ is the coefficient of surface expansion. For isotropic solids, β=2α\beta = 2\alphaβ=2α.
  • Final area: A=A0(1+2αΔT)A = A_0 (1 + 2\alpha \Delta T)A=A0​(1+2αΔT).

Volume Expansion

  • Change in volume:
    ΔV=γV0ΔT\Delta V = \gamma V_0 \Delta TΔV=γV0​ΔT
    where γ\gammaγ is the coefficient of volume expansion. For isotropic solids, γ=3α\gamma = 3\alphaγ=3α.
  • Final volume: V=V0(1+γΔT)V = V_0 (1 + \gamma \Delta T)V=V0​(1+γΔT).
  • Liquids and gases only have volume expansion (no fixed shape).

Peculiarity of Water Expansion

  • Water behaves differently between 0 °C and 4 °C: its volume decreases as temperature increases.
  • Water reaches its maximum density at 4 °C.
  • This is why ice floats (ice is less dense than water) and why lakes freeze from the top down, allowing aquatic life to survive in winter.

Real and Apparent Coefficients of Cubic Expansion of a Liquid

  • When a liquid is heated in a container, both expand. The observed (apparent) expansion is less than the real expansion of the liquid alone.
  • Relationship:
    γreal=γapparent+γcontainer\gamma_{\text{real}} = \gamma_{\text{apparent}} + \gamma_{\text{container}}γreal​=γapparent​+γcontainer​
    where γreal\gamma_{\text{real}}γreal​ is the coefficient for the liquid, γapparent\gamma_{\text{apparent}}γapparent​ is the measured expansion, and γcontainer\gamma_{\text{container}}γcontainer​ is the coefficient for the container material.

Thermal Expansion of Gases

  • Gases expand significantly with temperature; their expansion depends on whether pressure is kept constant. The ideal gas law describes this behaviour.

Applications of Thermal Expansion

  • Bimetallic strip: two different metals bonded together; when heated, they expand by different amounts, causing the strip to bend. Used in thermostats.
  • Riveting: hot rivets are inserted and then contract on cooling, pulling metal sheets tightly together.
  • Removing a stopper: heating the neck of a glass bottle expands the glass, making it easier to remove a tight stopper.
  • Liquid-in-glass thermometers: rely on expansion of mercury or alcohol.
  • Expansion gaps in bridges, railway tracks, and pipelines to prevent buckling.
  • Sealed bottles: leave space at the top to allow for expansion.

Key terms:

  • Thermal expansion: change in size due to temperature change.
  • Linear expansion: ΔL=αL0ΔT\Delta L = \alpha L_0 \Delta TΔL=αL0​ΔT.

5.5 Change of Phase

What is Phase Change?

  • Phase change is the transition of matter from one state (solid, liquid, gas) to another.
  • During a phase change, the temperature remains constant even though heat is being added or removed. The energy goes into breaking or forming intermolecular bonds.

Latent Heat

  • Latent heat (L) is the amount of heat absorbed or released per kilogram of a substance during a phase change at constant temperature.

  • Formula:
    Q=mLQ = m LQ=mL
    where mmm is mass.

  • SI unit: J/kg.

  • Latent heat of fusion (L_f): heat required to change 1 kg of solid to liquid at melting point (or released when liquid freezes).

  • Latent heat of vaporization (L_v): heat required to change 1 kg of liquid to gas at boiling point (or released when gas condenses).

Values for water:

  • Lf=3.33×105 J/kgL_f = 3.33 \times 10^5 \, \text{J/kg}Lf​=3.33×105J/kg
  • Lv=2.26×106 J/kgL_v = 2.26 \times 10^6 \, \text{J/kg}Lv​=2.26×106J/kg

Heating Curve

  • A graph of temperature vs. heat added shows:
    • Sloping sections: temperature increases (solid, liquid, or gas warming). Q=mcΔTQ = m c \Delta TQ=mcΔT.
    • Flat plateaus: phase change occurs; temperature constant. Q=mLQ = m LQ=mL.
  • Example: heating ice from -50°C to steam at 100°C involves five stages:
    1. Warming ice: Q=mciceΔTQ = m c_{\text{ice}} \Delta TQ=mcice​ΔT
    2. Melting ice at 0°C: Q=mLfQ = m L_fQ=mLf​
    3. Warming water: Q=mcwaterΔTQ = m c_{\text{water}} \Delta TQ=mcwater​ΔT
    4. Vaporizing water at 100°C: Q=mLvQ = m L_vQ=mLv​
    5. Warming steam (if continued): Q=mcsteamΔTQ = m c_{\text{steam}} \Delta TQ=mcsteam​ΔT

Phase Diagram

  • A phase diagram is a graph of pressure vs. temperature showing the regions where solid, liquid, and gas phases exist.
  • Triple point: the unique temperature and pressure where all three phases coexist in equilibrium.
  • Critical point: the temperature and pressure above which liquid and gas phases become indistinguishable (supercritical fluid). The substance cannot be liquefied by pressure alone above the critical temperature.
  • Sublimation: direct transition from solid to gas without passing through liquid phase (e.g., dry ice).

Key terms:

  • Phase change: transition between states of matter.
  • Latent heat: heat for phase change per unit mass, no temperature change.
  • Latent heat of fusion (L_f): solid ↔ liquid.
  • Latent heat of vaporization (L_v): liquid ↔ gas.
  • Triple point: all three phases coexist.
  • Critical point: liquid and gas phases merge.

5.6 Calorimetry

Definition

  • Calorimetry is the measurement of the quantity of heat exchanged between substances.
  • Based on the principle of conservation of energy: in an isolated system, heat lost by hot bodies = heat gained by cold bodies.
  • A calorimeter is a device used to measure specific heat capacity and latent heat.

Measuring Specific Heat Capacity

To determine specific heat capacity, one must know:

  • mass of the substance,
  • amount of heat supplied,
  • initial temperature,
  • final temperature.

Methods:

  1. Mixture method: A hot solid of known mass and temperature is placed in a calorimeter containing cold water (or liquid) of known mass and temperature. The final equilibrium temperature is measured. Using heat balance:

  2. Electrical method: An electrical heater of known power PPP supplies energy for time ttt. Assuming no heat losses:
    Pt=mcΔTP t = m c \Delta TPt=mcΔT
    This can be used for solids or liquids.

Everyday Applications of Specific Heat and Latent Heat

  • Cooking utensils: made of materials with low specific heat (copper, aluminum) for quick heating. Handles made of high specific heat materials (wood, plastic) for insulation.
  • Ocean as heat reservoir: water’s high specific heat stabilizes climate; coastal areas have milder temperatures.
  • Insulation: high specific heat materials (wood) keep houses cooler in summer.
  • Steaming food: uses latent heat of vaporization.
  • Cooling drinks with ice: ice absorbs large amounts of heat (latent heat of fusion) while melting, cooling the drink effectively.
  • Melting ice on roads with salt: salt lowers the freezing point, causing ice to melt.
  • Sweating: evaporation of sweat absorbs latent heat from the body, cooling it.

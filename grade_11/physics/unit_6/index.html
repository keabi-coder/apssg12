ELECTROSTATICS AND ELECTRIC CIRCUIT

6.1 Coulomb’s Law

Electric Charge

  • Charge is a fundamental property of matter; two types: positive and negative.
  • Protons carry positive charge, electrons carry negative charge.
  • Quantization of charge: charge always occurs as integer multiples of the elementary charge eee.
    q=±ne(n=1,2,3,… )q = \pm n e \quad (n = 1, 2, 3, \dots)q=±ne(n=1,2,3,…)
    • Charge of electron: e=−1.6×10−19 Ce = -1.6 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{C}e=−1.6×10−19C
    • Charge of proton: +1.6×10−19 C+1.6 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{C}+1.6×10−19C
  • Conservation of charge: the net charge of an isolated system remains constant; charge cannot be created or destroyed.

Coulomb’s Law

  • The magnitude of the electrostatic force between two point charges q1q_1q1​ and q2q_2q2​ separated by distance rrr is:
    F=k∣q1q2∣r2F = k \frac{|q_1 q_2|}{r^2}F=kr2∣q1​q2​∣​
    where k=9.0×109 N\cdotpm2/C2k = 9.0 \times 10^9 \, \text{N·m}^2/\text{C}^2k=9.0×109N\cdotpm2/C2 (Coulomb constant). In terms of permittivity of free space ε0\varepsilon_0ε0​:
    k=14πε0,ε0=8.85×10−12 C2/N\cdotpm2k = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}, \quad \varepsilon_0 = 8.85 \times 10^{-12} \, \text{C}^2/\text{N·m}^2k=4πε0​1​,ε0​=8.85×10−12C2/N\cdotpm2
  • The force is attractive for unlike charges, repulsive for like charges. It acts along the line joining the centers of the two charges.

Vector Form of Coulomb’s Law

  • The force on charge q1q_1q1​ due to q2q_2q2​ is:
    F⃗12=kq1q2r2r^12\vec{F}_{12} = k \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2} \hat{r}_{12}F12​=kr2q1​q2​​r^12​
    where r^12\hat{r}_{12}r^12​ is a unit vector pointing from q2q_2q2​ to q1q_1q1​.

Force due to Multiple Charges (Superposition Principle)

  • The net electrostatic force on a charge q1q_1q1​ due to several point charges q2,q3,…,qnq_2, q_3, \dots, q_nq2​,q3​,…,qn​ is the vector sum of the individual forces:
    F⃗net=F⃗12+F⃗13+⋯+F⃗1n\vec{F}_{\text{net}} = \vec{F}_{12} + \vec{F}_{13} + \dots + \vec{F}_{1n}Fnet​=F12​+F13​+⋯+F1n​

6.2 Electric Fields

Electric Field Definition

  • An electric field is a region around a charged object where a force would be experienced by another charge.
  • The electric field strength E⃗\vec{E}E at a point is the force per unit positive test charge:
    E⃗=F⃗q\vec{E} = \frac{\vec{F}}{q}E=qF​
    Unit: N/C.

Electric Field due to a Point Charge

  • The magnitude of the electric field a distance rrr from a point charge QQQ is:
    E=k∣Q∣r2E = k \frac{|Q|}{r^2}E=kr2∣Q∣​
  • Direction: radially outward from a positive charge, radially inward toward a negative charge.

Electric Field Lines

  • Properties:
    • Begin on positive charges and end on negative charges.
    • Never cross each other.
    • Are always perpendicular to the surface of the charged conductor.
    • Density of lines indicates field strength: closer lines → stronger field.
    • Equally spaced parallel lines represent a uniform electric field (e.g., between two parallel oppositely charged plates).

Electric Field due to Multiple Point Charges

  • The net electric field at a point is the vector sum of the fields due to individual charges:
    E⃗net=E⃗1+E⃗2+⋯+E⃗n\vec{E}_{\text{net}} = \vec{E}_1 + \vec{E}_2 + \dots + \vec{E}_nEnet​=E1​+E2​+⋯+En​

Electric Flux

  • Electric flux Φ\PhiΦ through a surface of area AAA in a uniform electric field E⃗\vec{E}E is:
    Φ=E⃗⋅A⃗=EAcos⁡θ\Phi = \vec{E} \cdot \vec{A} = E A \cos \thetaΦ=E⋅A=EAcosθ
    where θ\thetaθ is the angle between E⃗\vec{E}E and the normal to the surface.
  • For a surface perpendicular to the field (θ=0\theta = 0θ=0), Φ=EA\Phi = E AΦ=EA.
  • Unit: N·m²/C.

6.3 Electric Potential

Electric Potential Energy

  • The electric potential energy UUU of a charge qqq at a distance rrr from a point charge QQQ is:
    U=kqQrU = k \frac{q Q}{r}U=krqQ​
  • Work done by an external force to bring a charge from infinity to that point equals the change in potential energy.

Electric Potential (Voltage)

  • Electric potential VVV at a point is the electric potential energy per unit charge:
    V=UqV = \frac{U}{q}V=qU​
    Unit: volt (V); 1 V=1 J/C1 \, \text{V} = 1 \, \text{J/C}1V=1J/C.
  • Potential due to a point charge QQQ at distance rrr:
    V=kQrV = k \frac{Q}{r}V=krQ​
  • The potential is a scalar; sign depends on the sign of QQQ.

Potential Difference

  • The potential difference ΔV\Delta VΔV between two points A and B is the work done per unit charge to move a test charge from A to B:
    ΔV=Wq\Delta V = \frac{W}{q}ΔV=qW​
  • In a uniform electric field:
    ΔV=Ed\Delta V = E dΔV=Ed
    where ddd is the distance along the field direction. This gives 1 N/C=1 V/m1 \, \text{N/C} = 1 \, \text{V/m}1N/C=1V/m.

Potential due to a System of Point Charges

  • The total potential at a point due to several charges is the algebraic (scalar) sum:
    V=k∑i=1nqiriV = k \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{q_i}{r_i}V=ki=1∑n​ri​qi​​
    (Signs of charges must be included.)

Equipotential Lines/Surfaces

  • Equipotential surfaces are surfaces on which the electric potential is constant.
  • Characteristics:
    • No work is needed to move a charge along an equipotential surface.
    • Equipotential lines are always perpendicular to electric field lines.
    • For a point charge, equipotential surfaces are concentric spheres.
    • In a uniform field, they are parallel planes.
    • They never intersect.
    • They are closer together where the field is stronger.

6.4 Electric Current, Resistance and Ohm’s Law

Electric Current

  • Electric current III is the rate of flow of charge:
    I=ΔqΔtI = \frac{\Delta q}{\Delta t}I=ΔtΔq​
    Unit: ampere (A); 1 A=1 C/s1 \, \text{A} = 1 \, \text{C/s}1A=1C/s.
  • Conventional current direction is opposite to the direction of electron flow.

Current Density

  • Current density JJJ is current per unit cross-sectional area:
    J=IAJ = \frac{I}{A}J=AI​
    Unit: A/m². It is a vector quantity pointing in the direction of current flow for positive charge carriers.

Drift Velocity

  • Free electrons in a conductor move with an average drift velocity vdv_dvd​ under an electric field.
  • The current is related to drift velocity by:
    I=nAevdI = n A e v_dI=nAevd​
    where nnn is the number of free electrons per unit volume, eee the elementary charge, and AAA the cross‑sectional area.
  • Current density can also be written as:
    J=nevdJ = n e v_dJ=nevd​

Resistance and Ohm’s Law

  • Resistance RRR is the opposition to current flow. For many conductors (ohmic materials), the voltage across them is proportional to the current through them:
    V=IRV = I RV=IR
    (Ohm’s law). Unit of resistance: ohm (Ω\OmegaΩ); 1 Ω=1 V/A1 \, \Omega = 1 \, \text{V/A}1Ω=1V/A.
  • Non‑ohmic devices do not follow this linear relationship.
  • Resistance depends on the material and geometry:
    R=ρLAR = \rho \frac{L}{A}R=ρAL​
    where ρ\rhoρ is the resistivity (unit: Ω⋅m\Omega \cdot \text{m}Ω⋅m).
  • Conductivity σ\sigmaσ is the reciprocal of resistivity: σ=1/ρ\sigma = 1/\rhoσ=1/ρ. Then J=σEJ = \sigma EJ=σE.

Resistors in Series

  • The same current flows through each resistor.
  • Equivalent resistance:
    Req=R1+R2+R3+…R_{\text{eq}} = R_1 + R_2 + R_3 + \dotsReq​=R1​+R2​+R3​+…
  • Total voltage is the sum of individual voltages: V=V1+V2+V3+…V = V_1 + V_2 + V_3 + \dotsV=V1​+V2​+V3​+…

Resistors in Parallel

  • The same voltage appears across each resistor.
  • The total current is the sum of the currents through each branch.
  • Equivalent resistance:
    1Req=1R1+1R2+1R3+…\frac{1}{R_{\text{eq}}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \frac{1}{R_3} + \dotsReq​1​=R1​1​+R2​1​+R3​1​+…
    For two resistors in parallel: Req=R1R2R1+R2R_{\text{eq}} = \frac{R_1 R_2}{R_1 + R_2}Req​=R1​+R2​R1​R2​​.

Measuring Instruments

  • Ammeter: measures current; connected in series with the circuit; must have very low resistance to avoid altering the current.
  • Voltmeter: measures potential difference; connected in parallel with the component; must have very high resistance to draw negligible current.

Conversion of a Galvanometer

  • A galvanometer is a sensitive instrument that gives full‑scale deflection for a small current IgI_gIg​ and has coil resistance RgR_gRg​.
  • To ammeter: connect a low resistance (shunt) RshR_{sh}Rsh​ in parallel with the galvanometer.
    Rsh=IgI−IgRgR_{sh} = \frac{I_g}{I - I_g} R_gRsh​=I−Ig​Ig​​Rg​
    where III is the desired full‑scale current.
  • To voltmeter: connect a high resistance (multiplier) RMR_MRM​ in series with the galvanometer.
    RM=VIg−RgR_M = \frac{V}{I_g} - R_gRM​=Ig​V​−Rg​
    where VVV is the desired full‑scale voltage.

Potential Divider (Voltage Divider)

  • Two resistors R1R_1R1​ and R2R_2R2​ in series across a source VVV:
    V1=VR1R1+R2,V2=VR2R1+R2V_1 = V \frac{R_1}{R_1 + R_2}, \quad V_2 = V \frac{R_2}{R_1 + R_2}V1​=VR1​+R2​R1​​,V2​=VR1​+R2​R2​​
  • Used to obtain a desired fraction of the input voltage.

Potentiometer

  • A device for measuring unknown emf by balancing it against a known potential difference without drawing current.
  • Principle: a uniform resistance wire with a steady current has a uniform potential drop along its length. The emf of a cell is proportional to the balance length:
    EX=E0AXABE_X = E_0 \frac{AX}{AB}EX​=E0​ABAX​
    (where E0E_0E0​ is the driver cell’s emf, AXAXAX the balance length for the unknown cell, and ABABAB the total length of the wire).
  • Can also compare two emfs: EXEY=ACAD\frac{E_X}{E_Y} = \frac{AC}{AD}EY​EX​​=ADAC​.

Wheatstone Bridge

  • A circuit used to measure an unknown resistance RxR_xRx​ by balancing two legs of a bridge.
  • The bridge is balanced (no current through the galvanometer) when:
    R2R1=R4R3\frac{R_2}{R_1} = \frac{R_4}{R_3}R1​R2​​=R3​R4​​
  • At balance, the products of opposite arms are equal: R1R4=R2R3R_1 R_4 = R_2 R_3R1​R4​=R2​R3​.

Kirchhoff’s Rules

  • Junction rule (first rule): the sum of currents entering a junction equals the sum of currents leaving it (conservation of charge).
    ∑Iin=∑Iout\sum I_{\text{in}} = \sum I_{\text{out}}∑Iin​=∑Iout​
  • Loop rule (second rule): the algebraic sum of potential changes around any closed loop is zero (conservation of energy).
    ∑ΔV=0\sum \Delta V = 0∑ΔV=0
    Sign conventions:
    • Emf is positive when going from – to + terminal, negative when + to –.
    • IR drop is positive when moving opposite to current direction, negative when moving with the current.

Brightness of Bulbs

  • Brightness depends on the power dissipated: P=I2R=V2/RP = I^2 R = V^2 / RP=I2R=V2/R.
  • In series, bulbs share current; lower resistance bulbs are dimmer.
  • In parallel, each bulb receives full source voltage; lower resistance bulbs are brighter.

6.6 Capacitors and Capacitance

Capacitor Definition

  • A capacitor is a device that stores electric charge and energy. It consists of two conductors (plates) separated by an insulator.
  • Capacitance CCC is defined as:
    C=QVC = \frac{Q}{V}C=VQ​
    Unit: farad (F); 1 F=1 C/V1 \, \text{F} = 1 \, \text{C/V}1F=1C/V. Common submultiples: μF,nF,pF\mu\text{F}, \text{nF}, \text{pF}μF,nF,pF.

Parallel‑Plate Capacitor

  • For two plates of area AAA and separation ddd in vacuum:
    C0=ε0AdC_0 = \varepsilon_0 \frac{A}{d}C0​=ε0​dA​
    where ε0=8.85×10−12 F/m\varepsilon_0 = 8.85 \times 10^{-12} \, \text{F/m}ε0​=8.85×10−12F/m.
  • With a dielectric (insulator) of dielectric constant kkk, the capacitance increases:
    C=k ε0Ad=kC0C = k \, \varepsilon_0 \frac{A}{d} = k C_0C=kε0​dA​=kC0​

Dielectrics

  • A dielectric is an insulating material placed between capacitor plates.
  • Effects of inserting a dielectric (with the capacitor isolated from the source):
    • Capacitance increases by factor kkk.
    • Potential difference between plates decreases.
    • Charge on plates remains unchanged.
    • Electric field between plates decreases.
  • Dielectric strength: the maximum electric field a material can withstand without breaking down (becoming conductive). Exceeding it causes dielectric breakdown.

Capacitors in Parallel

  • Each capacitor has the same voltage VVV.
  • Total charge is sum of individual charges: Q=Q1+Q2+…Q = Q_1 + Q_2 + \dotsQ=Q1​+Q2​+….
  • Equivalent capacitance:
    Ceq=C1+C2+C3+…C_{\text{eq}} = C_1 + C_2 + C_3 + \dotsCeq​=C1​+C2​+C3​+…

Capacitors in Series

  • Each capacitor stores the same charge QQQ.
  • Total voltage is sum of individual voltages: V=V1+V2+…V = V_1 + V_2 + \dotsV=V1​+V2​+….
  • Equivalent capacitance:
    1Ceq=1C1+1C2+1C3+…\frac{1}{C_{\text{eq}}} = \frac{1}{C_1} + \frac{1}{C_2} + \frac{1}{C_3} + \dotsCeq​1​=C1​1​+C2​1​+C3​1​+…
    For two capacitors in series: Ceq=C1C2C1+C2C_{\text{eq}} = \frac{C_1 C_2}{C_1 + C_2}Ceq​=C1​+C2​C1​C2​​.

6.7 Electric Circuits in Our Surroundings

Household Electrical Installations

  • Household circuits are connected in parallel; each appliance receives the full supply voltage (typically 220 V).
  • This allows independent operation and switching of devices.
  • Fuses and circuit breakers provide overcurrent protection.

Color Code of Resistors

  • Resistors often have four or five colored bands that indicate their resistance value and tolerance.
  • 4‑band code:
    • 1st band: first significant digit.
    • 2nd band: second significant digit.
    • 3rd band: multiplier (power of ten).
    • 4th band: tolerance (gold = ±5%, silver = ±10%, no band = ±20%).
  • Example: Brown‑Black‑Red‑Gold → 1, 0, ×10² = 1000 Ω = 1 kΩ, ±5%.
  • The tolerance indicates the range within which the actual resistance may vary from the marked value.

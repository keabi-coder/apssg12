NUCLEAR PHYSICS

7.1 The Nucleus

Structure of the Atom

  • An atom consists of a small, dense nucleus at its centre, surrounded by electrons.
  • The nucleus contains two types of particles, called nucleons:
    • Protons: positively charged, each with charge +1.602×10−19 C+1.602 \times 10^{-19}\, \text{C}+1.602×10−19C and mass 1.6726×10−27 kg1.6726 \times 10^{-27}\, \text{kg}1.6726×10−27kg (≈ 1.0072766 u).
    • Neutrons: electrically neutral, mass 1.6749×10−27 kg1.6749 \times 10^{-27}\, \text{kg}1.6749×10−27kg (≈ 1.0086654 u).
  • Electrons orbit the nucleus; they are negatively charged with mass 9.1094×10−31 kg9.1094 \times 10^{-31}\, \text{kg}9.1094×10−31kg (≈ 0.00054858 u).

Notation for a Nuclide

  • A nuclide is represented by the symbol ZAX_{Z}^{A}\mathrm{X}ZA​X, where:
    • X is the chemical symbol of the element.
    • Z = atomic number = number of protons.
    • A = mass number = total number of nucleons (protons + neutrons).
    • Therefore, number of neutrons N=A−ZN = A - ZN=A−Z.
  • Examples: 612C_{6}^{12}\mathrm{C}612​C (carbon-12), 92235U_{92}^{235}\mathrm{U}92235​U (uranium-235).

Size of the Nucleus

  • The nucleus is approximately spherical. Its radius RRR depends on the mass number AAA as:
    R=R0A1/3,where R0=1.2×10−15 m=1.2 fm.R = R_0 A^{1/3}, \quad \text{where } R_0 = 1.2 \times 10^{-15}\,\text{m} = 1.2\,\text{fm}.R=R0​A1/3,where R0​=1.2×10−15m=1.2fm.
  • The volume of the nucleus is only a tiny fraction of the volume of the atom; for example, the nucleus occupies about 0.0565%0.0565\%0.0565% of the volume of a uranium-238 atom.

Isotopes

  • Isotopes are atoms of the same element (same ZZZ) that have different numbers of neutrons (different AAA).
  • They have identical chemical properties but different masses.
  • Examples:
    • Hydrogen isotopes: 11H_{1}^{1}\mathrm{H}11​H (protium), 12H_{1}^{2}\mathrm{H}12​H (deuterium), 13H_{1}^{3}\mathrm{H}13​H (tritium).
    • Carbon isotopes: 612C_{6}^{12}\mathrm{C}612​C, 613C_{6}^{13}\mathrm{C}613​C, 614C_{6}^{14}\mathrm{C}614​C.

Historical Discovery of the Nucleus (Rutherford’s Gold Foil Experiment)

  • Rutherford bombarded a thin gold foil with alpha particles.
  • Observations and conclusions:
    • Most alpha particles passed straight through → most of the atom is empty space.
    • A few were deflected at small angles → positive charge is concentrated in a small region.
    • Very few (about 1 in 8000) bounced back → almost all the mass and all the positive charge are concentrated in a tiny central nucleus.

Discovery of the Proton and Neutron

  • Proton: identified by Rutherford; later named and recognised as a fundamental particle. It is the nucleus of hydrogen-1.
  • Neutron: discovered by James Chadwick in 1932 by bombarding beryllium with alpha particles; a penetrating neutral particle was emitted – the neutron.

Nuclear Forces

  • Strong nuclear force:
    • Acts between all nucleons (protons and neutrons).
    • Very short range (≈ 10−15 m10^{-15}\,\text{m}10−15m).
    • Attractive and strong enough to overcome the electrostatic repulsion between protons.
    • At the range of 10−15 m10^{-15}\,\text{m}10−15m, it is about 100 times stronger than the electromagnetic force, 10610^{6}106 times stronger than the weak force, and 103810^{38}1038 times stronger than gravity.
  • Weak nuclear force:
    • Responsible for certain types of radioactive decay, particularly beta decay.
    • Even shorter range than the strong force; acts inside individual nucleons.

Nuclear Binding Energy

  • Mass defect (Δm\Delta mΔm): The mass of a nucleus is always less than the sum of the masses of its separate nucleons. This difference is the mass defect.
    Δm=Zmp+(A−Z)mn−M\Delta m = Z m_p + (A - Z) m_n - MΔm=Zmp​+(A−Z)mn​−M
    where mpm_pmp​, mnm_nmn​ are the masses of a proton and neutron, and MMM is the mass of the nucleus.
  • Binding energy (BE) is the energy equivalent of the mass defect, given by Einstein’s equation:
    BE=(Δm)c2BE = (\Delta m) c^2BE=(Δm)c2
    In convenient units:
    1 u×c2=931.5 MeV(approx. 931.1 MeV in the text)1\,\text{u} \times c^2 = 931.5\,\text{MeV} \quad (\text{approx. }931.1\,\text{MeV in the text})1u×c2=931.5MeV(approx. 931.1MeV in the text)
    So, BE(MeV)=Δm(u)×931.1 MeVBE (\text{MeV}) = \Delta m (\text{u}) \times 931.1\,\text{MeV}BE(MeV)=Δm(u)×931.1MeV.
  • Binding energy per nucleon (BEN):
    BEN=BEABEN = \frac{BE}{A}BEN=ABE​
    It measures the stability of a nucleus; the higher the BEN, the more stable the nucleus.
  • Example: For oxygen-16, BEN ≈ 7.4 MeV.

Nuclear Stability

  • A graph of binding energy per nucleon versus mass number shows a peak around A=56A = 56A=56 (iron). Nuclei with BEN near this peak are most stable.
  • Very light and very heavy nuclei have lower BEN; they can release energy by fusion and fission, respectively.
  • A plot of neutron number vs. proton number for stable nuclei shows that heavier stable nuclei have more neutrons than protons to reduce electrostatic repulsion.

7.2 Radioactivity

Definition

  • Radioactivity is the spontaneous disintegration of unstable atomic nuclei, emitting particles or electromagnetic radiation, to form more stable nuclei.
  • Radioactive decay is a random, statistically governed process in which a small amount of mass is converted into energy.

Dangers of Ionizing Radiation

  • Radiation can damage living tissue by ionising atoms and molecules, breaking chemical bonds, and altering DNA.
  • The damage depends on:
    • Type of radiation and its energy.
    • Sensitivity of the exposed tissues/organs.
    • Duration and manner of exposure.
    • The specific isotopes involved.
    • Characteristics of the person (age, gender, health).

Absorbed Dose and Effective Dose

  • Absorbed dose: energy deposited per unit mass of tissue.
    • Unit: gray (Gy), 1 Gy=1 J/kg1\,\text{Gy} = 1\,\text{J/kg}1Gy=1J/kg.
    • Also rad: 1 rad=0.01 Gy1\,\text{rad} = 0.01\,\text{Gy}1rad=0.01Gy.
  • Effective dose (sievert, Sv): takes into account the biological effect of different types of radiation.
    • 1 Sv=1 J/kg1\,\text{Sv} = 1\,\text{J/kg}1Sv=1J/kg (equivalent biological effect).
    • Doses above 3 Gy3\,\text{Gy}3Gy (300 rad) can be fatal; above 6 Gy6\,\text{Gy}6Gy (600 rad) almost certainly fatal.

Radiation Detectors

  • Scintillator: a material that emits flashes of light when struck by radiation; the light pulses are converted to electrical signals.
  • Geiger‑Müller (GM) counter: a gas‑filled tube that produces an electrical pulse when radiation ionises the gas inside. It can detect alpha, beta, and gamma radiation (alphas require a thin window).
  • Semiconductor detectors, ionisation chambers, etc., are also used.

Safety Precautions with Radioactive Sources

  • Handle sources with tongs/forceps, never with bare hands.
  • Keep at arm’s length and point away from the body.
  • Minimise exposure time; increase distance; use appropriate shielding (lead, concrete, water).
  • Store sources in sealed, labelled containers; never eat, drink or smoke near them.
  • Wash hands after handling.

  • Because radioactive decay is a random process, the “lifetime” of a single nucleus is unpredictable; however, the half‑life of a large number of identical nuclei is a well‑defined constant.

7.3 Use of Nuclear Radiation

Medical Applications

  • Radiotherapy: using radiation to kill cancerous cells.
    • External beam (teletherapy): e.g., gamma rays from cobalt‑60 are directed at the tumour from outside the body.
    • Internal radiation therapy (brachytherapy): radioactive sources (e.g., iodine‑131 in wire or seeds) are placed inside or near the tumour.
    • Systemic therapy: the patient swallows or is injected with a radioactive substance (e.g., iodine‑131 for thyroid cancer) that selectively targets cancer cells.
  • Diagnostic imaging (nuclear medicine): radioactive tracers (e.g., technetium‑99m) are introduced into the body; their distribution is detected by gamma cameras to produce images of organs and detect abnormalities.
  • In Ethiopia, the Black Lion Hospital (Addis Ababa) uses radioisotopes for cancer treatment.

Radioactive Dating

  • Carbon‑14 dating: used to determine the age of organic materials (up to ~50 000 years).
    • Living organisms maintain a constant ratio of 14C^{14}\mathrm{C}14C to 12C^{12}\mathrm{C}12C while alive. After death, 14C^{14}\mathrm{C}14C decays with a half‑life of about 5730 years.
    • By measuring the remaining 14C^{14}\mathrm{C}14C activity, the time since death can be calculated using N=N0e−λtN = N_0 e^{-\lambda t}N=N0​e−λt.
    • Example: dating of fossils like “Lucy” (Dinknesh).
  • Potassium‑40 dating: used for older rocks (half‑life ~1.25 billion years).
  • Uranium‑lead dating: for even older geological samples.

7.4 Nuclear Reaction and Energy Production

Nuclear Fission

  • Nuclear fission is the splitting of a heavy nucleus into two (or more) smaller nuclei, accompanied by the release of a large amount of energy and several neutrons.

  • The mass of the products is less than the mass of the reactants; the mass defect is converted into kinetic energy of the products (~200 MeV per fission).

Chain Reaction

  • The neutrons released in fission can trigger further fissions, leading to a chain reaction.
  • Critical mass: the minimum amount of fissile material required to sustain a chain reaction.
  • Controlled chain reaction → nuclear power plants. Uncontrolled → atomic bomb.

Nuclear Fusion

  • Nuclear fusion is the combining of two light nuclei to form a heavier nucleus, releasing energy because the products have a greater binding energy per nucleon.
  • Fusion requires very high temperatures (millions of degrees) to overcome the electrostatic repulsion between the positively charged nuclei.

Nuclear Reactor Components

  • Fuel: usually uranium oxide (UO₂) pellets arranged in fuel rods.
  • Moderator: material (water, heavy water, graphite) that slows down neutrons so they can cause further fissions.
  • Control rods: neutron‑absorbing materials (cadmium, boron, hafnium) inserted/withdrawn to control the reaction rate.
  • Coolant: fluid (water, gas, liquid metal) that removes heat from the core.
  • Steam generator: produces steam from the heat to drive a turbine.
  • Pressure vessel/containment: robust steel and concrete structure that encloses the reactor and prevents release of radiation.

Nuclear Waste and Safety Issues

  • Used fuel rods are highly radioactive and must be stored safely for thousands of years.
  • High‑level waste must be cooled and shielded; low‑level waste includes contaminated tools, clothing, etc.
  • Concerns: potential for accidents, leakage, proliferation of nuclear materials, and long‑term storage.

Differences between Fission and Fusion

7.5 Safety Rules Against Hazards of Nuclear Radiation

Basic Principles of Radiation Protection

  • Time: minimise the time spent near a radiation source.
  • Distance: increase the distance from the source; intensity follows the inverse‑square law.
  • Shielding: use dense materials (lead, concrete, water) to absorb radiation.
    • Alpha particles – stopped by paper or skin.
    • Beta particles – stopped by a few mm of aluminium.
    • Gamma rays – reduced by thick lead or concrete.

Safety Rules in Schools/Laboratories

  • Radioactive sources must be handled with tongs or forceps; never with bare hands.
  • Keep sources at arm’s length and pointed away from the body, especially the eyes.
  • Wash hands after experiments and before eating.
  • Store sources in labelled, shielded containers.
  • Follow disposal regulations; never throw radioactive waste into ordinary bins.

Nuclear Safety (General)

  • Strict regulations cover mining, transport, use, storage, and disposal of radioactive materials.
  • Nuclear power plants have multiple barriers (fuel cladding, reactor vessel, containment building) to prevent release of radioactivity.
  • Monitoring of radiation levels, regular safety drills, and emergency plans are essential.
  • In the event of a nuclear accident: shelter, evacuate, decontaminate as per official instructions.

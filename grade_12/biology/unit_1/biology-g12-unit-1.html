Unit 1

1. Appreciation of Nature

  • Nature is the phenomena of the physical world including plants, animals, the landscape, and other features and products of the earth.
  • Relationship with biology: Biology helps us understand the living world and how species (including humans) function, evolve, and interact.
  • Benefits of nature: Provides food, air, water; supports health, happiness, prosperity; viewing nature reduces anger, fear, stress and increases pleasant feelings.
  • Ways to appreciate the beauty of nature:
    • Planting a tree
    • Hugging a tree
    • Making a garden
    • Going for hiking trails
    • Watching sunrises and sunsets
    • Protecting nature
    • Reducing pollution

2. Conservation of Natural Resources

  • Conservation: The careful maintenance and wise use of natural resources to prevent them from disappearing. It is the protection and preservation of the natural environment so that non-renewable resources are used sparingly and renewable resources are managed properly to last for a long time.
  • Natural resources: Physical supplies that exist in nature, including soil, water, air, plants, animals, and energy.
  • Ethiopia’s natural resources: Gold, platinum, potash, limestone, natural gas, coal, timber, crop plants, coffee, diverse animal/plant species, rich ecosystems, domestic animal breeds.
  • Renewable resources: Mainly living things and their products. They can be used, reused, and replaced when managed carefully.
    • Examples: Crop plants, trees, cattle, chickens, coffee, Walia Ibex, sunlight, Ethiopian Bush crow.
  • Non-renewable resources: Not living things; once used they cannot be replaced.
    • Examples: Gold, iron, fossil fuels (gas, coal, oil).
  • Risk of losing renewable resources: Cutting all trees at once prevents forest renewal and loses all species within it; overhunting leads to extinction; habitat destruction eliminates feeding/breeding grounds, causing permanent loss of species.
  • Extinct: No longer in existence.
  • Role of biology in conservation: Creating awareness on natural resource conservation, development, and genetic resources conservation.

3. Food Security

  • Food security (UN definition): A state in which all people have physical, social, and economic access to sufficient, safe, and nutritious food that meets their food preferences and dietary needs for an active and healthy life at all times.
  • Consequences of food insecurity: Rooted in poverty; prolonged undernourishment stunts growth, slows cognitive development, increases susceptibility to illness.
  • Conditions that ensure food security:
    • Enough food is available for all in a country.
    • All individuals have the capacity to buy food of acceptable quality.
    • There is no barrier to access food.
  • Role of biology:
    • Producing high-nutrient staple crops.
    • Developing new products that combat malnutrition.
    • Improving food utilization.
    • Biotechnologists design manufacturing processes for consistent flavor, color, and texture in mass-produced food and drink.

4. Creating Conscious Citizens and Sustainable Development

  • Conscious citizen: One who places value on being fully human while connecting with a higher purpose; values human life and the relationship with all living things; takes responsibility for transforming skills into action through ethical decision making to ultimately improve life and living on the planet.
  • Role of biology in creating conscious citizens:
    • Expanding awareness of social, global, and environmental conditions.
    • Empowering people to assume personal responsibility.
    • Engaging in, committing to, and initiating positive impact.
  • Sustainable development: Economic development conducted without depletion of natural resources; meeting human development goals while sustaining the ability of natural systems to provide resources and ecosystem services.
  • Three dimensions of sustainability: Economic, social, environmental (represented as pillars, embedded circles, or overlapping Venn diagram).
  • Contribution of biotechnology to sustainable development: Developing innovative and cost-effective bio-based technologies that consume fewer resources, incorporate recycling and reuse, reduce waste, and sustain a greener earth.

5. Development of Careers

  • Career development: The process of setting goals and acquiring the skills to achieve those goals, involving personal improvement to fulfill a career plan. It enables learning better aspects of work and improving capabilities.
  • Types of development programs in organizations: Organization development, employee development, management development, career development.
  • Career paths for biology graduates: Academic research and teaching, medical and public health, biotechnology, industrial research, environmental sciences, agricultural research.

6. Medicine

  • Health (WHO definition): A state of complete physical, mental and social wellbeing, not merely the absence of disease or infirmity.
  • Applications of biology in medicine: Fast diagnosis tools, drug and vaccine production, gene therapy, immuno-diagnosis, immunotherapy, transplantation, medicinal plants.
  • Biotechnology contributions to health:
    • Effective diagnostics, prevention, and treatment.
    • Production of novel drugs and recombinant vaccines.
    • Effective drug delivery approaches.
    • New methods for therapeutics.
    • Nutritionally enriched and genetically modified crops.
    • Environmental cleanup methods.
    • Increased life quality and expectancy worldwide.
  • PCR (Polymerase Chain Reaction): A broadly applied laboratory test for diagnosis of central nervous system (CNS) diseases, including genetic and autoimmune diseases, malignant neoplasms, and infections.
  • Rapid diagnosis of infectious diseases: AIDS and tuberculosis diagnosed rapidly at low cost using molecular diagnostic tools – PCR, recombinant antigens, monoclonal antibodies.
  • Modern diagnostic and therapeutic tools: Diagnostic test kits, rickettsial/bacterial/viral vaccines, radiolabeled biological therapeutics for imaging and analysis.
  • Recombinant vaccines: Have potential to eradicate non-communicable diseases like cancer. Types include naked DNA vaccines, viral vector vaccines, and plant-derived vaccines – effective against many bacterial and viral diseases.
  • COVID-19 detection: Biosensor devices are used to detect the presence or absence of the coronavirus.

7. Waste Treatment

  • Biotechnological processes are used for wastewater treatment and reuse, involving engineers, biologists, and chemists.
  • Properly designed waste management systems can:
    • Remove hazardous wastes from the environment.
    • Produce renewable energy (biofuels, hydrogen).
  • Waste treatment technologies used extensively: Activated sludge process, trickling filters, bio-trickling filters, oxidation ponds, anaerobic treatment, composting units, biogas reactors.
  • Bio-mining: Use of microorganisms to extract minerals from natural ores, replacing chemical mining that generates many pollutants.

8. Biotechnology

8.1 Definition and Types

  • Biotechnology: The science and technology of harnessing the natural and biological capabilities of plants, animals, and microbes for the benefit of people. It involves applying biological knowledge, techniques, and living organisms to make products or run processes for human use.
  • Traditional biotechnology: Domestication and traditional fermentations – brewing beers, making wines, bread, cheese, yoghurt.
  • Modern biotechnology: Genetic engineering to change crops and animals, producing new medicines, providing new energy sources.
  • Significance: Helps people improve and control their lives.
  • Microbial biotechnology: Enabled by genome studies, leads to improved vaccines, disease-diagnostic tools, microbial agents for biological control of pests, modified pathogens with reduced virulence, new industrial catalysts and fermentation organisms, and microbial agents for bioremediation of contaminated soil and water.

8.2 Biological Engineering (Technology)

  • Biological engineering: Application of principles of biology and tools of engineering to create usable, tangible, economically viable products.
  • Fields involved: Organ transfer, biocatalysts, bioinformatics, separation and purification processes, bioreactor design, polymer science.
  • Bioengineers work with doctors, clinicians, and researchers to apply traditional engineering principles to real-world biological and medical problems. They also apply expertise to genetic modification, bioprocess engineering, and biocatalysis.
  • Key definitions:
    • Bio-process engineering: Planning, construction, execution, and revision of biological and mechanical processes required to create new products within the life sciences.
    • Bioinformatics: Sub-discipline of biology and computer science concerned with acquisition, storage, analysis, and dissemination of biological data, most often DNA and amino acid sequences.
    • Bio-catalysis: Use of enzymes that have been isolated or enzymes still residing inside living cells to perform chemical conversions of organic compounds.
    • Bioreactor: Any manufactured device or system that supports a biologically active environment.

8.3 Soil Fertility, Tissue Culture, Animal Breeding and Transgenics

  • Soil fertility management using biological knowledge:
    • Use of organic fertilizers (bio-fertilizers).
    • Vermicomposting (e.g., using water hyacinth, earthworm rearing to produce compost).
    • Crop rotation with legumes.
    • Use of improved germplasm.
    • Adapting practices to local conditions.
  • Tissue culture:
    • Plants can be propagated quickly and in large quantity.
    • Examples of plants produced: palm trees, orchids, bananas, carrots.
    • Allows large quantity of food with desired quality in a small area.
    • Genetic engineering has produced seedless fruits (watermelon, papaya, orange, grape), reducing the crop cycle.
    • Enables more efficient cultivation methods.
  • Animal breeding:
    • Addresses genetic value of livestock.
    • Selecting animals with superior traits in growth rate, egg/meat/milk/wool production, or other desirable traits has revolutionized livestock production.
  • Transgenic animals: Genetically modified animals for valuable traits.
    • Applications:
      • Enhancing prolificacy and reproductive performance.
      • Increasing feed utilization and growth rate.
      • Improving carcass composition.
      • Improving milk production and/or composition.
      • Modification of hair or fiber.
      • Increasing disease resistance.
    • Gene transfer allows rapid alteration of the genome of domestic livestock, improving efficiency and cost-effectiveness.
  • Model organisms in research: Organisms suitable for studying a specific trait, disease, or phenomenon due to short generation time, characterized genome, or similarity to humans. Examples: fly, fish, rodent, pig. Invertebrates can replace vertebrates in many research areas due to ethical concerns.

8.4 Industrial Biotechnology (Food, Brewery, Pharmaceuticals, Textile, etc.)

  • Industrial biotechnology (white biotechnology): Modern use and application of biotechnology for sustainable production and processes of bio-products from renewable sources using living cells and/or their enzymes.
  • Products made using microorganisms and enzymes:
    • Food supplements (vitamin B2)
    • Pharmaceuticals (drugs, vaccines)
    • Bioplastics (polylactic acid)
    • Energy carriers (biogas, bioethanol)
    • Detergents, pulp and paper, quality textile fibers, agricultural products.
  • Single-cell proteins (SCP): Edible unicellular microorganisms; biomass or protein extract from pure or mixed cultures of algae, yeasts, fungi, or bacteria used as ingredient/substitute for protein-rich foods, suitable for human consumption or animal feed.
    • Examples: Mushroom, spirulina, yeast, green algae.
    • SCP technology is promising for alleviating food security issues.
  • Ethanol production steps from raw plant materials (lignocellulose, sugar cane, corn):
    1. Extraction → Sugars
    2. Pre-saccharification
    3. Fermentation → Must
    4. Distillation → Ethanol 90%
    5. Drying → Ethanol 99%
      (Hemicellulose can also be used in the process.)
  • Food preservation (biological methods): Adding cultures of innocuous microorganisms of high purity that inhibit undesirable decomposition microorganisms.

8.5 Forensic Science

  • Forensic biologists inspect crime scenes for evidence (blood, saliva, hair) and analyze specimens in the laboratory focusing on DNA analysis.
  • Fingerprints: Unique to each individual, do not change throughout life; used to investigate crime and determine paternity.
  • Bioinformatics helps gather evidence with computational tools.
  • PCR in forensic analysis:
    • Examines variability at 5–10 different Variable Number of Tandem Repeat (VNTR) loci.
    • The odds of two random individuals sharing the same genetic pattern by chance is approximately 1 in 10 billion.
    • Example: In a crime scene, individuals A and C can be eliminated while individual B remains a suspect.
    • Paternity testing uses a similar approach; gel electrophoresis results can confirm fatherhood (e.g., suspect 2 is the father).

8.6 Biological Warfare

  • Biological warfare (germ warfare): The use of biological toxins or infectious agents such as bacteria, viruses, and fungi with the intent to kill or incapacitate humans, animals, or plants as an act of war.
  • Biological weapons: Microorganisms (bacteria, viruses, fungi) or toxins found in nature that can be used to kill or injure people.
  • Bioterrorism: Ranges from a hoax to actual use of biological agents.
  • Concerns: Nations or terrorist groups may acquire technologies and expertise; agents can be used for assassination, mass incapacitation/death, and long-term environmental contamination.

9. Promises of Biology to Society

  • Biology addresses issues of overpopulation, food security, environmental wellbeing, health care, and biodiversity.
  • Relevance to Ethiopia:
    • Ethiopia has rich biological wealth (diverse flora, fauna, microbial resources) but limited knowledge. Understanding this diversity is essential for development and sustained use.
    • Indigenous biological knowledge can be systematically introduced into modern science through training biologists and biological literacy campaigns.
    • Effective control of land degradation, biodiversity loss, and diseases, as well as development of biological and agricultural potentials, is needed.
    • Biologists work in teaching, research, agriculture, medical fields, fisheries, conservation, industry, natural resource development, biodiversity/genetic resources conservation, and pest/disease control.
    • Ethiopia possesses important biological materials for studying human evolutionary history, crop domestication, endemic plant and animal species, and crop genetics – all helpful for advancing biological sciences.

8. Biotechnology

  • Traditional: Domestication, brewing, wine making, bread, cheese, yoghurt.
  • Modern: Genetic engineering of crops/animals, new medicines, new energy sources.
  • Microbial biotechnology: Genome-based breakthroughs – better vaccines, diagnostics, biocontrol agents, reduced pathogen virulence, industrial catalysts, fermentation organisms, bioremediation microbes.

8.2 Biological Engineering

  • Biological engineering: Merges biology with engineering to create viable products.
  • Involves: Organ transfer, biocatalysts, bioinformatics, separation/purification, bioreactor design, polymer science.
  • Key subfields defined:
    • Bio-process engineering: Planning and executing biological/mechanical processes for life-science products.
    • Bioinformatics: Biology + computer science for DNA/amino acid sequence data handling.
    • Bio-catalysis: Using isolated or cell-contained enzymes for organic chemical conversions.
    • Bioreactor: A device/system supporting a biologically active environment.

8.3 Agriculture & Breeding Applications

  • Soil fertility: Organic fertilizers, vermicomposting, crop rotation with legumes, improved germplasm.
  • Tissue culture: Rapid mass propagation of plants (palms, orchids, bananas, carrots); genetic engineering yields seedless fruits (watermelon, papaya, orange, grape) and shorter crop cycles.
  • Animal breeding: Selection for superior growth, egg/meat/milk/wool traits.
  • Transgenic animals: Gene transfer to enhance prolificacy, feed efficiency, growth, carcass/milk quality, disease resistance.
  • Model organisms: Flies, fish, rodents, pigs – used because of short generation times and known genomes; invertebrates replace vertebrates in many studies.

8.4 Industrial Biotechnology

  • White biotechnology: Sustainable production of bio-products (chemicals, materials, fuels) using cells/enzymes.
  • Products: Vitamin B2, drugs, vaccines, bioplastics, biogas, bioethanol, detergents, paper, textiles, agricultural goods.
  • SCP (Single-cell proteins): Edible microbes (mushroom, spirulina, yeast, green algae) used as protein-rich food/feed; promising for food security.
  • Ethanol production steps:
    • Extraction → Sugars → Pre-saccharification → Fermentation → Must → Distillation → 90% ethanol → Drying → 99% ethanol.
  • Food preservation: Adding pure harmless microbial cultures that inhibit spoilage organisms.

8.5 Forensic Science

  • Forensic biologists examine blood, saliva, hair; DNA analysis is central.
  • Fingerprints: Uniquely identify individuals; used in crime and paternity investigations.
  • PCR in forensics: Compares VNTR loci (5–10 sites); chance of random match ~1 in 10 billion. Suspect identification and paternity confirmation done via DNA pattern comparisons.

8.6 Biological Warfare

  • Definition: Use of microbial/toxin agents (bacteria, viruses, fungi) to harm/kill humans, animals, or plants during war.
  • Biological weapons: Natural microorganisms or toxins.
  • Bioterrorism: From hoaxes to actual agent release; potential for mass casualties and long-term environmental threats.

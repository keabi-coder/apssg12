Unit 2

2.1 What are Microorganisms?

  • Microorganisms (microbes): Life forms including acellular viruses that are too small to be seen with the naked eye. They are microscopic organisms, diverse in form and function, inhabiting every environment on Earth that supports life.
  • Nature: Many are simple single-celled organisms; some form complex structures; a few are multicellular.
  • Microbial communities: Microbes typically live in complex communities, with activities regulated by interactions with one another, their environments, and other organisms.
  • Microbiology: The scientific study of microorganisms – what they are, how they work, and what they do.
  • Impact: Microorganisms have tremendous impacts on the entire biosphere. Most are harmless or beneficial.
    • Essential for recycling nutrients that form the bodies of all organisms and sustain metabolic cycles of life.
    • Produce about 50% of the oxygen gas we breathe.
    • Provide nutrients to many other organisms.
    • Found in/on many foods, in air, water, and on our bodies (skin, mouth, ears, nose, throat, digestive tract).
    • Natural microbiota: The ~100 billion microorganisms living in/on us; beneficial, help resist disease, regulate development and nutrition.
  • Pathogens: Disease-causing agents; they are rare among microorganisms.

2.2 Types of Microorganisms

  • Three main evolutionary lines: Bacteria, Archaea, and Eucarya (based on small subunit rRNA gene sequences).
  • Five main groups (general): Bacteria, Fungi, Viruses, Protozoa, Some algae.

2.2.1 Eubacteria (True Bacteria)

  • Definition: All bacteria except archaea. Bacteria are relatively simple, single-celled (unicellular) organisms.
  • Prokaryotes: Cells lacking a nuclear membrane-enclosed nucleus; include bacteria and archaea.
  • Eukaryotes: Organisms whose cells have a nucleus enclosed within a nuclear envelope.
  • Characteristics:
    • Unicellular.
    • Prokaryotic – no nuclear membrane.
    • Include cyanobacteria (bluish pigments plus chlorophyll, can photosynthesize).
    • Most are useful; some harmful.
    • Thought to be first organisms on Earth (~4 billion years ago). Oldest known fossils are bacteria-like.
  • Classification by shape:
    • Cocci (singular coccus): spherical
    • Bacilli (singular bacillus): rod-shaped
    • Spirochaetes: spiral or corkscrew-shaped
  • Classification by cell wall (Gram staining):
    • Gram-positive: Retain purple crystal violet stain due to thick peptidoglycan layer. Examples: all staphylococci, all streptococci, some listeria species.
    • Gram-negative: Lose crystal violet stain and take red counterstain; thin peptidoglycan layer.
    • Peptidoglycan: Rigid layer of bacterial cell walls composed of N-acetylglucosamine, N-acetylmuramic acid, and a few amino acids.
    • Gram’s staining: A differential staining test to distinguish bacteria based on cell wall properties.

2.2.2 Archaea

  • Definition: Prokaryotes that often inhabit extreme environments.
  • Methanogens: Diversity within Archaea; among oldest microbes, promote anaerobic decomposition, consume fermentation products, release methane as waste (part of carbon cycle).
  • Extremophiles: Known for existing in conditions preventing most life.
    • Thermophiles: high temperatures
    • Hyperthermophiles: really high temperatures (record 121°C)
    • Psychrophiles (cryophiles): cold (e.g., Antarctic species grows best at 4°C)
    • Halophiles: very saline (like Dead Sea)
    • Acidophiles: low pH (as low as pH 1, die at pH 7)
    • Alkaliphiles: high pH
  • Ethiopian habitats: Extreme thermophiles expected in Danakil Depression (Dallol), Lake Basaka, Gargdi, Filwoha, Sodere, Wondo Genet, Woliso, Lake Abijata. Hot spring temperatures vary 42–98°C seasonally.
  • Importance of Archaea:
    • Source of enzymes for detergents (active at high temperature and pH).
    • Enzymes convert cornstarch into dextrin fiber.
    • Bioremediation potential (cleaning contaminated sites).
    • Thermus aquaticus (thermophilic archaeon) provides DNA polymerase used in Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR), essential for molecular biology.

2.2.3 Fungi

  • Definition: Eukaryotic organisms with a distinct nucleus containing DNA, surrounded by a nuclear membrane. May be unicellular or multicellular.
  • Cell wall: Composed primarily of chitin.
  • Photosynthesis: Cannot carry out photosynthesis (unlike plants).
  • Forms:
    • Yeasts: Unicellular, oval, larger than bacteria.
    • Molds: Form visible masses called mycelia, composed of long filaments (hyphae) that branch and intertwine. Cottony growths on bread/fruit are mold mycelia.
    • Slime molds: Amoeba-like fungi growing on decayed plants and wet areas.
  • Nutrition: Absorb organic materials from environment (soil, water, or host).
  • Reproduction: Sexually or asexually.
  • Ecological role: Crucial for ecosystem balance. Decomposers and recyclers that supply nutrients to other kingdoms. Food webs would be incomplete without them.
  • Harmful activities:
    • Saprotrophs damage timber, fuel, food, manufactured goods.
    • Parasites cause heavy crop losses, diseases in humans and domestic animals.
  • Beneficial uses:
    • Used as food, in food processing, alcohol production, and traditional fermentation.
    • Roles in Ethiopian fermented products: Enjera (Buddeenaa), Tej (Daadhii), Tella (Farsoo), Kocho.
    • Fermentation industry produces antibiotics, drugs, agricultural fungicides, plant growth regulators, vitamins, enzymes.
    • With genetic manipulation, fungi produce hormones and proteins previously only from mammals (part of biotechnology).

2.2.4 Protozoa

  • Definition: Unicellular eukaryotic microscopic organisms, free-living or parasitic; obtain energy from organic carbon.
  • Cellular features: True membrane-bound nucleus, membrane-bounded organelles in cytoplasm.
  • Locomotion:
    • Pseudopods (false feet) – amoebae.
    • Flagella – long appendages.
    • Cilia – numerous shorter appendages.
  • Nutrition: Some are photosynthetic (e.g., Euglena use light and CO₂). Others absorb or ingest organic compounds.
  • Reproduction: Sexually or asexually.
  • Ecological importance: Many beneficial – improve water quality by eating bacteria and particles. Some are disease-causing.
  • Major protozoal parasites and diseases:
    • Malaria: Plasmodium spp. (P. falciparum, P. vivax, P. ovale, P. malariae). Transmitted by female Anopheles mosquito. Life cycle: sporozoites inoculated into human, infect liver cells, mature into schizonts, rupture releasing merozoites.
    • African trypanosomiasis (sleeping sickness): Trypanosoma brucei.
    • Chagas disease: Trypanosoma cruzi.
    • Leishmaniasis: Leishmania spp. (e.g., L. donovani). Spread by sand flies. Types: cutaneous, mucocutaneous, visceral.
    • Toxoplasmosis: Toxoplasma gondii.
    • Trichomoniasis: Trichomonas vaginalis.
    • Intestinal protozoan infections: Giardia lamblia (giardiasis, diarrheal disease), Entamoeba histolytica (amoebiasis), Cryptosporidium parvum, Cyclospora cayetanensis.
  • Giardiasis: Caused by Giardia duodenalis, lives in intestines, passed in stool, survives outside body for weeks/months.
  • Amoebiasis: Caused by Entamoeba histolytica, infects bowel, causes diarrhea. Handwashing prevents spread.
  • Trypanosomiasis in livestock: Nagana (Gandii/Gendi).
  • Leishmaniasis local names: Cutaneous – Afaan Oromo: Sinbira halkanii (lamxii), Amharic: Kunchir.

2.3 Viruses

  • Definition: Small parasite particle that cannot reproduce by itself; obligate intracellular parasite.
  • Structure:
    • Virion: Entire infectious virus particle.
    • Genome: Either DNA or RNA, single-stranded or double-stranded.
    • Capsid: Outer protein shell; composed of capsomeres (morphological subunits), which are made of protomers.
    • Nucleocapsid: Complex of nucleic acid and capsid.
    • Envelope: Some viruses have a lipoprotein/glycoprotein bilayer outside the capsid (acquired from host cell during budding). Viruses without envelope are naked viruses.
    • Spikes (peplomers): Glycoprotein projections (e.g., hemagglutinin, neuraminidase) involved in host cell binding.
    • Enzymes: Some viruses carry enzymes, e.g., lysozyme in bacteriophages (makes holes in bacterial cell wall), reverse transcriptase in retroviruses (RNA-dependent DNA polymerase).
  • Characteristics of viruses:
    • Inner core of nucleic acid surrounded by protein capsid.
    • Size range 20–250 nm.
    • Inert outside host cell.
    • Obligate intracellular parasites, filterable.
    • Occupy between living and non-living (crystallizable outside host).
    • Depend fully on host cell machinery; metabolically inefficient.
    • Cause dreadful diseases in humans and plants.
  • Viral symmetry:
    • Helical: Identical protein subunits assembled in helix around genome; e.g., tobacco mosaic virus, Sendai virus.
    • Icosahedral: 20 equilateral triangular faces, 12 vertices; rigid; e.g., papovavirus, picornavirus, adenovirus, toga virus.
    • Complex: Not fitting above; e.g., bacteriophages.
  • DNA vs. RNA viruses:
    • DNA viruses: mostly double-stranded; stable; replication in nucleus.
    • RNA viruses: mostly single-stranded; higher mutation rate; unstable; replication in cytoplasm.
  • Host range: Infect all cellular life: eukaryotes (animals, plants, fungi) and prokaryotes (bacteria, archaea). Bacteriophages (phages) infect bacteria.
  • HIV: Human immunodeficiency virus, attacks immune system; if untreated, leads to AIDS.
  • Common viral diseases in Ethiopia: High prevalence of hepatitis B virus; also hepatitis C, E, HIV. Population exposed to poliomyelitis, hepatitis A, measles, rubella, mumps early in life. Rotaviral diarrhoea is important cause of infant morbidity/mortality. Yellow fever and rabies are endemic in vast areas.

2.4 Reproduction in Microorganisms

  • Asexual reproduction: Single microbe produces two identical offspring (clones).
  • Sexual reproduction: Two microbes mix genetic information, offspring are genetically different.

Prokaryotic replication

  • Binary fission: Bacteria and archaea split one cell into two equal halves.
    • Steps:
      1. Parent cell has large circular chromosome and smaller plasmid.
      2. Chromosome and plasmid replicate.
      3. Copies move to opposite ends (poles) of cell.
      4. Cell wall grows inward at middle (septation).
      5. Growing walls meet to form a septum.
      6. Cells separate (cytokinesis) into two identical daughter cells.
    • Example: Escherichia coli divides every 20 minutes under optimal conditions.
  • Conjugation: Direct DNA transfer from one prokaryote to another via a conjugation pilus.
    • F plasmid (fertility factor) encodes ability to conjugate and F pilus proteins.
    • F⁺ cells (donor) contain F plasmid; F⁻ cells (recipient) lack it.
    • DNA transfer occurs through rolling circle replication.

Eukaryotic microbial replication

  • Binary fission-like process in yeast (e.g., Saccharomyces pombe):
    • Replicate chromosomes (two copies of each).
    • Separate sister chromatids via mitosis.
    • Cytokinesis yields two daughter cells.
  • Budding (e.g., Saccharomyces cerevisiae, bakery yeast):
    • Daughter cell grows as bud from mother cell.
    • Mitosis occurs; one nucleus moves into bud.
    • Bud separates, leaving scar on mother cell.
  • Mitosis stages (eukaryotic cell division):
    1. Prophase: Chromosomes condense, mitotic spindle forms.
    2. Prometaphase: Nuclear envelope disintegrates, chromosomes attach to spindle microtubules.
    3. Metaphase: Chromosomes align at cell middle.
    4. Anaphase: Sister chromatids separate to opposite poles.
    5. Telophase: New nuclear envelopes form.
    6. Cytokinesis: Cell divides into two identical daughter cells.

Viral replication

  • Six main stages:
    1. Attachment to host cell.
    2. Penetration.
    3. Uncoating of viral genome.
    4. Replication of viral genome and production of viral proteins.
    5. Assembly of new virus particles.
    6. Release of new virus particles.

2.5 Economic Importance of Microorganisms

  • Microorganisms greatly advance human health and welfare; exploited for products, energy, environmental cleanup.

A. Agriculture

  • Roles: organic matter decomposition, humus formation, nitrogen fixation, phosphate solubilization, potassium mobilization, antagonism toward pathogens/pests.
  • Convert essential elements (C, N, O, S, P) into forms usable by plants/animals.
  • Decomposers (bacteria, fungi) return CO₂ to atmosphere; algae, cyanobacteria, plants use CO₂ in photosynthesis to produce carbohydrates.
  • Nitrogen fixation: only bacteria naturally convert atmospheric nitrogen to nitrate usable by plants/animals.

B. Sewage Treatment

  • Anaerobic bacteria reduce sludge volume and produce methane gas (alternative energy source).
  • No need to add oxygen; they obtain oxygen from their food source.
  • Also aid phosphorus removal.

C. Bioremediation

  • Uses microorganisms and/or their derivatives (enzymes, spent biomass) to degrade or alter environmental contaminants.
  • Microbes break down pollutants (e.g., hydrocarbons, PCBs, PAHs, radionuclides, metals) via inherent metabolic processes.
  • The large diversity of microbial species expands the range of degradable chemical pollutants.
  • Bioremediation is natural: microbes increase when contaminant present, decline when it’s degraded. Residues are usually harmless (water, CO₂, cell biomass).

D. Food

  • Foods like bread, beer, cheese produced with yeasts.
  • Bacteria involved in butter, yogurt, and many traditional fermented staple foods, beverages, condiments.

E. Medicine

  • Microorganisms help produce medicines that target defective genes.
  • Human insulin is now synthesized in bacteria and yeast; these microbes are used as vectors for drug delivery.

F. Health

  • Human body contains ~10 times more microbial cells than body cells.
  • Beneficial functions: E. coli in intestine releases compounds aiding digestion; gut bacteria synthesize vitamins like biotin, vitamin K, folic acid.
  • Help fight against harmful disease-causing bacteria.

G. Biotechnology

  • Microorganisms used to produce human insulin, growth hormones, etc.
  • Biotechnological processes use microbes as vectors and plasmids for drug delivery.
  • Increase soil fertility, enhance plant production.
  • Functions: suppress soil-borne pathogens, recycle/increase availability of plant nutrients, degrade toxicants (pesticides), produce antibiotics and bioactive compounds, produce simple organic molecules for plant uptake, solubilize insoluble nutrient sources, produce polysaccharides for soil aggregation.

2.6 Role of Bacteria in Recycling Minerals through Ecosystems

  • Many bacteria are decomposers; break down complex molecules from dead organisms into simpler molecules, releasing minerals into the environment.
  • Nearly 90% of all living organisms are made of C, O, N, H; these are limited, so recycling by decomposers is essential.

Carbon Cycle

  • Bacteria and fungi decompose organic wastes/dead organisms, return CO₂ to atmosphere.
  • Algae, cyanobacteria, and plants use CO₂ for photosynthesis, producing carbohydrates.

Nitrogen Cycle

  • Atmospheric nitrogen is not directly usable by plants/animals; only bacteria convert it to usable forms (nitrate).
  • Root nodules: Found on roots of legumes, form symbiosis with nitrogen-fixing bacteria (rhizobia). Under nitrogen-limiting conditions, plant forms symbiotic relationship with a host-specific rhizobia strain. Nitrogen fixation in nodules is very oxygen sensitive.

Sulfur Cycle

  • Sulfur is found in many proteins.
  • Bacteria involved play various roles in sulfur transformations (details referenced in table, but general principle is bacterial oxidation/reduction of sulfur compounds).

Phosphorus Cycle

  • Phosphorus occurs in soils as organic and inorganic forms.
  • Organic P: Mostly of microbial origin; tightly held, not available to plants until mineralization by microbes; rate affected by soil moisture, organic material composition, oxygen concentration, pH.
  • Inorganic P: Very small concentration in soil solution; occurs as aluminum, iron, or calcium compounds.
  • Microbes mineralize organic P to release phosphate.

2.7 Common Disease-Causing Microorganisms

  • Normal microbiota/flora: Microorganisms living on/in the human body that do no harm; some protect against disease by preventing overgrowth of harmful microbes, and produce vitamin K and B vitamins. Under certain conditions (e.g., leaving their habitat), normal microbiota can cause disease.
  • Germ theory of disease: Microorganisms cause infectious diseases.
  • Pathogen: Any organism that causes disease.
  • Infectious disease: Disease caused by a microorganism infecting the body; also called communicable disease because it can be transmitted from person to person.

Modes of Disease Transmission (four main routes)

  1. Contact (direct contact with infected person or surface; e.g., athlete’s foot, ringworm).
  2. Droplet infection: Respiratory diseases spread via tiny droplets from coughs/sneezes; inhaled. Examples: common cold, influenza, pneumonia.
  3. Airborne (though not detailed separately in table, included with droplet).
  4. Common vehicle:
    • Drinking contaminated water: Gut infections; organisms excreted in faeces, re-enter water. Examples: cholera, typhoid fever.
    • Eating contaminated food: Food poisoning, mostly bacterial; e.g., salmonellosis, typhoid, listeriosis, botulism.
    • Sexual intercourse: Sexually transmitted infections: candidiasis, syphilis, AIDS, gonorrhoea. Some transmitted in semen/vaginal secretions, saliva.
    • Blood-to-blood contact: Many sexually transmitted diseases; needle sharing transmits AIDS, hepatitis B.
    • Animal vectors: Insect bites spread diseases: mosquitoes (malaria), tsetse flies (sleeping sickness). Flies carry microbes from faeces to food.

2.8 Unit Summary (Key Points)

  • Microbiology is the study of bacteria, viruses, fungi, protozoa, and algae.
  • Microorganisms are microscopic, require magnification to observe.
  • Microbes are indispensable for balanced life on earth; play many roles in ecosystems.
  • Algae and certain bacteria photosynthesize; other microbes decompose and recycle nutrients.
  • Microbes are essential for maintenance of air, soil, water.
  • Biotechnology applies microbial power for industrial products, foods, drugs.
  • Microbes are the basis of genetic engineering and recombinant DNA technology.
  • Bioremediation uses microbes to clean up pollutants.
  • Microbial simplicity, growth rate, adaptability led to diverse sub-sciences (immunology, epidemiology, food/dairy/aquatic/industrial microbiology).
  • History: Leeuwenhoek discovered “animalcules”; spontaneous generation abandoned for biogenesis; germ theory of disease developed.
  • Eukaryotic cells have a nucleus; prokaryotic cells do not.

2.1 Understanding Microorganisms

  • Microorganisms/microbes: life forms too small to see with naked eye, including acellular viruses. They inhabit every life-supporting environment on Earth.
  • Diversity: single-celled, complex structures, some multicellular. Live in complex communities, activities regulated by interactions.
  • Microbiology: scientific study of microorganisms.
  • Beneficial roles:
    • Recycle nutrients that form bodies of all organisms, sustain metabolic cycles.
    • Produce ~50% of Earth’s oxygen.
    • Provide nutrients to many other organisms.
    • Inhabit foods, air, water, and our body surfaces; ~100 billion microbes form natural microbiota, help resist disease, regulate development and nutrition.
  • Pathogens: disease-causing microbes; they are rare.

2.2 Classifying Microorganisms

  • Evolutionary lines: Bacteria, Archaea, Eukarya (based on rRNA gene sequences).
  • Five practical groups: Bacteria, Fungi, Viruses, Protozoa, some Algae.

2.2.1 Bacteria (Eubacteria)

  • True bacteria, single-celled prokaryotes (no nuclear membrane).
  • Include cyanobacteria (photosynthetic, bluish pigments).
  • First organisms on Earth (~4 billion years ago).
  • Shape classification: Cocci (spherical), Bacilli (rod), Spirochaetes (spiral/corkscrew).
  • Gram stain classification:
    • Gram-positive: thick peptidoglycan wall retains purple crystal violet stain.
    • Gram-negative: thin peptidoglycan, loses crystal violet, takes red counterstain.
  • Key terms: Peptidoglycan, prokaryote, eukaryote.

2.2.2 Archaea

  • Prokaryotes, many extremophiles.
  • Methanogens: anaerobically decompose organic matter, produce methane, part of carbon cycle.
  • Extremophile types: Thermophiles, hyperthermophiles, psychrophiles, halophiles, acidophiles, alkaliphiles.
  • Ethiopian extreme thermophile habitats: Danakil, Lake Basaka, hot springs (42–98°C).
  • Uses: Enzymes for detergents, starch conversion, bioremediation, Thermus aquaticus DNA polymerase for PCR.

2.2.3 Fungi

  • Eukaryotic, uni- or multicellular; cell walls of chitin; no photosynthesis.
  • Forms: Yeasts (unicellular, oval), molds (mycelia of hyphae), slime molds.
  • Nutrition: absorb organics from environment.
  • Reproduction: sexual/asexual.
  • Ecological role: Decomposers/recyclers crucial for nutrient cycling.
  • Harmful: Saprotrophic damage, parasitic crop/human/animal diseases.
  • Beneficial: Food, fermentation (Enjera, Tej, Tella, Kocho), antibiotics, drugs, enzymes, hormones via genetic manipulation.

2.2.4 Protozoa

  • Unicellular eukaryotes, free-living or parasitic.
  • Motility: pseudopods, flagella, cilia.
  • Some photosynthetic (Euglena).
  • Diseases:
    • Malaria (Plasmodium spp., mosquito vector, liver/blood cycle).
    • African trypanosomiasis, Chagas disease, Leishmaniasis (sand fly vector), Toxoplasmosis, Trichomoniasis.
    • Intestinal: Giardiasis, Amoebiasis, Cryptosporidiosis.
  • Local names: Nagana (Gandii/Gendi); cutaneous leishmaniasis = Sinbira halkanii/Kunchir.

2.2.5 Viruses

  • Acellular, obligate intracellular parasites.
  • Virion structure: Nucleic acid core (DNA or RNA, ss or ds), capsid (protein), sometimes envelope (lipid bilayer) and spikes. Enzymes like lysozyme or reverse transcriptase may be present.
  • Characteristics: Inert outside host, size 20–250 nm, require host machinery.
  • Symmetry: Helical (tobacco mosaic virus), icosahedral (adenovirus), complex (bacteriophages).
  • DNA vs RNA viruses: DNA viruses generally double-stranded, stable, nuclear replication; RNA viruses mostly single-stranded, higher mutation, cytoplasmic replication.
  • HIV: attacks immune system, leads to AIDS.
  • Ethiopia viral diseases: Hepatitis B (high prevalence), C, E, HIV, polio, measles, rubella, mumps, rotaviral diarrhea, yellow fever, rabies.

2.3 Reproduction Methods

  • Asexual reproduction (clones): binary fission in prokaryotes; mitosis/budding in eukaryotes.
  • Sexual reproduction: genetic mixing.

Prokaryotic: Binary Fission

  1. Chromosome and plasmid replicate.
  2. Copies move to poles.
  3. Cell wall grows inward, septum forms.
  4. Two identical daughter cells separate.
    E. coli divides every 20 minutes.

Prokaryotic: Conjugation

  • F plasmid (fertility factor) enables donor (F⁺) to form F pilus and transfer DNA to recipient (F⁻).

Eukaryotic Microbes: Mitosis

  • Chromosomes condense, spindle forms, nuclear envelope breaks, chromosomes align, chromatids separate, new envelopes form, cytokinesis.

Eukaryotic Microbes: Budding

  • Baker’s yeast forms a bud, mitosis occurs, nucleus moves to bud, bud separates.

Viral Replication

  1. Attachment → 2. Penetration → 3. Uncoating → 4. Genome replication & protein synthesis → 5. Assembly → 6. Release.

2.4 Economic Importance

  • Agriculture: Decomposition, humus formation, nitrogen fixation, phosphate solubilization, pathogen antagonism. Only bacteria fix atmospheric nitrogen.
  • Sewage treatment: Anaerobic bacteria reduce sludge, produce methane, remove phosphorus.
  • Bioremediation: Microbes degrade contaminants (hydrocarbons, metals); residues are harmless.
  • Food: Yeasts for bread, beer, cheese; bacteria for butter, yogurt; traditional fermentations.
  • Medicine: Produce human insulin, drug delivery via vectors, gene therapy.
  • Health: Normal microbiota aid digestion, produce vitamins (biotin, K, folic acid), fight pathogens.
  • Biotechnology: Microbes used for hormones, bioactive compounds, soil fertility improvement, pollutant degradation.

2.5 Nutrient Cycling by Bacteria

  • Carbon cycle: Decomposers return CO₂; photosynthesis fixes CO₂ into carbohydrates.
  • Nitrogen cycle: Only bacteria fix N₂ gas into nitrate; legumes form symbiosis with rhizobia in root nodules (oxygen-sensitive).
  • Sulfur cycle: Bacteria mediate transformations of sulfur in proteins.
  • Phosphorus cycle: Microbes mineralize organic P to phosphate; inorganic P forms include Al, Fe, Ca compounds.

2.6 Infectious Diseases and Transmission

  • Normal microbiota: beneficial unless displaced; produce vitamins, block pathogens.
  • Germ theory: microorganisms cause infectious diseases.
  • Pathogen: disease-causing organism.
  • Transmission routes:
    • Droplet (colds, flu, pneumonia)
    • Contaminated water (cholera, typhoid)
    • Contaminated food (salmonellosis, botulism)
    • Direct contact (athlete’s foot, ringworm)
    • Sexual intercourse (AIDS, syphilis, gonorrhoea)
    • Blood-to-blood (HIV, hepatitis B)
    • Animal vectors (malaria – mosquito; sleeping sickness – tsetse fly)

..... Summary .....

Microorganisms are tiny life forms, including viruses, that exist everywhere and are essential for life. Most are beneficial: they recycle nutrients, produce oxygen, aid digestion, and are used in food, medicine, and industry. Some, however, cause infectious diseases. The main types are Bacteria, Archaea, Fungi, Protozoa, and Viruses. They reproduce through binary fission, mitosis, budding, or by hijacking host cells. Microbes drive nutrient cycles (carbon, nitrogen, sulfur, phosphorus) and are applied in agriculture, bioremediation, sewage treatment, and biotechnology. Understanding their structure, classification, transmission, and roles is critical.

Key Points

  • Microorganisms (microbes): microscopic organisms including viruses; most are harmless or beneficial; a rare few are pathogens.
  • Normal microbiota: beneficial microbes on/in the body; produce vitamins, prevent disease.
  • Three evolutionary lines: Bacteria, Archaea, Eucarya; five practical groups: bacteria, fungi, viruses, protozoa, algae.
  • Bacteria: unicellular prokaryotes; shapes (cocci, bacilli, spirochaetes); Gram-positive (thick peptidoglycan, retain purple) vs Gram-negative (thin, red).
  • Archaea: extremophile prokaryotes (thermophiles, halophiles, etc.); source of heat-stable enzymes and PCR polymerase.
  • Fungi: eukaryotic, chitin cell walls; yeasts (unicellular), molds (multicellular hyphae); decomposers; vital in fermentation and drug production.
  • Protozoa: unicellular eukaryotes; motile (pseudopods, flagella, cilia); cause malaria, leishmaniasis, sleeping sickness, giardiasis, amoebiasis.
  • Viruses: acellular obligate parasites; nucleic acid core + capsid; may have envelope; helical, icosahedral, or complex symmetry; cause hepatitis, HIV, measles, rabies.
  • Reproduction: asexual (binary fission, budding, mitosis) and sexual; viruses replicate via host cell hijacking.
  • Economic importance: nutrient cycling, food fermentation, medicine (insulin, antibiotics), agriculture (N-fixation), bioremediation, sewage treatment, biotechnology.
  • Disease transmission: droplet, contaminated water/food, direct contact, sexual, blood-to-blood, animal vectors.
  • Nutrient cycles: only bacteria fix nitrogen; rhizobia in legume root nodules; decomposers recycle carbon, sulfur, phosphorus.

Definitions

  • Microorganisms / microbes: Life forms too small to be seen with the naked eye, including acellular viruses.
  • Microbiology: The scientific study of microorganisms.
  • Biosphere: The regions of the Earth's surface and atmosphere occupied by living organisms.
  • Prokaryotes: Unicellular organisms lacking a membrane-enclosed nucleus (Bacteria, Archaea).
  • Eukaryotes: Organisms whose cells have a nucleus enclosed within a nuclear envelope.
  • Unicellular: Consisting of a single cell.
  • Multicellular: Consisting of many cells.
  • Peptidoglycan: Rigid layer of bacterial cell walls composed of N-acetylglucosamine, N-acetylmuramic acid, and amino acids.
  • Gram staining: A differential staining procedure distinguishing bacteria based on cell wall properties (Gram-positive vs Gram-negative).
  • Extremophiles: Microorganisms living in extreme environments.
    • Thermophiles: High temperatures.
    • Hyperthermophiles: Extremely high temperatures (record 121°C).
    • Psychrophiles / Cryophiles: Cold environments.
    • Halophiles: Saline environments.
    • Acidophiles: Low pH (as low as pH 1).
    • Alkaliphiles: High pH.
  • Hyphae: Long, branching filaments of molds.
  • Mycelia: Visible masses of hyphae.
  • Chitin: Substance composing fungal cell walls.
  • Pathogen: Any organism that causes disease.
  • Germ theory: Microorganisms cause infectious diseases.
  • Infectious disease: Disease caused by a microorganism infecting the body; often communicable.
  • Normal microbiota / flora: Microorganisms living on or in the body without causing harm; often beneficial.
  • Virion: The entire infectious virus particle (nucleic acid + capsid).
  • Capsid: Protein coat surrounding viral nucleic acid.
  • Nucleocapsid: The complex of nucleic acid and capsid.
  • Envelope: A lipid bilayer (lipoprotein/glycoprotein) outside the capsid of some viruses.
  • Capsomere: Morphological subunit of a capsid; composed of protomers.
  • Reverse transcriptase: RNA-dependent DNA polymerase found in retroviruses.
  • Bacteriophage (phage): A virus that infects bacteria.
  • Retrovirus: An RNA virus that converts its RNA genome into DNA inside a host (e.g., HIV).
  • Single-cell proteins (SCP): Edible unicellular microorganisms (mushroom, spirulina, yeast, algae) used as protein-rich food/feed.
  • Biofertilizer: Organic fertilizer using living microorganisms to improve soil fertility.
  • Bioremediation: Use of microorganisms to degrade or detoxify environmental pollutants.

Processes

Bacterial Binary Fission

  1. Parent cell contains large circular chromosome and smaller plasmid.
  2. Chromosome and plasmid replicate.
  3. Copies move to opposite poles.
  4. Cell wall grows inward at middle (septation).
  5. Growing walls meet to form a septum.
  6. Cells separate into two identical daughter cells (cytokinesis).

Conjugation (Bacterial DNA Transfer)

  • F⁺ (donor) cell contains F plasmid, forms F pilus.
  • Pilus brings F⁺ and F⁻ cells into contact.
  • DNA transferred via rolling circle replication through the pilus.

Eukaryotic Mitosis (Cell Division)

  1. Prophase: Chromosomes condense, mitotic spindle forms.
  2. Prometaphase: Nuclear envelope disintegrates; chromosomes attach to spindle.
  3. Metaphase: Chromosomes align at cell equator.
  4. Anaphase: Sister chromatids separate, move to opposite poles.
  5. Telophase: New nuclear envelopes form.
  6. Cytokinesis: Cell divides into two identical daughter cells.

Budding (Yeast Asexual Reproduction)

  • A small bud emerges from mother cell.
  • Mitosis occurs; one nucleus migrates into bud.
  • Bud detaches, leaving scar on mother cell.

Viral Replication Cycle

  1. Attachment to host cell.
  2. Penetration into host.
  3. Uncoating of viral genome.
  4. Replication of viral genome and synthesis of viral proteins.
  5. Assembly of new virus particles (virions).
  6. Release from host cell.

Malaria Parasite Life Cycle

  1. Infected female Anopheles mosquito injects sporozoites into human during blood meal.
  2. Sporozoites infect liver cells, mature into schizonts.
  3. Schizonts rupture, releasing merozoites into blood.

Ethanol Production Steps

Extraction → Sugars → Pre-saccharification → Fermentation (must) → Distillation (90% ethanol) → Drying (99% ethanol).

Other Points (Classifications, Examples, Transmission, Lists)

Five Main Groups of Microorganisms:

  • Bacteria
  • Fungi
  • Viruses
  • Protozoa
  • Some algae

Bacterial Shapes:

  • Cocci (spherical)
  • Bacilli (rod-shaped)
  • Spirochaetes (spiral/corkscrew)

Gram Stain Classification:

  • Gram-positive: thick peptidoglycan, retain purple stain. Examples: Staphylococcus, Streptococcus.
  • Gram-negative: thin peptidoglycan, take red counterstain.

Archaea Habitats in Ethiopia:
Danakil Depression (Dallol), Lake Basaka, Filwoha, Sodere, Wondo Genet, Woliso, Lake Abijata; hot springs 42–98°C.

Fungal Groups & Uses:

  • Yeasts (e.g., Saccharomyces): baking, brewing, alcohol.
  • Molds: antibiotic production, food processing.
  • Ethiopian fermented products: Enjera, Tej, Tella, Kocho.

Protozoan Diseases & Vectors:

  • Malaria – Plasmodium spp., Anopheles mosquito
  • African trypanosomiasis – Trypanosoma brucei, tsetse fly
  • Chagas disease – T. cruzi, triatomine bug
  • Leishmaniasis – Leishmania spp., sand fly (cutaneous: “Sinbira halkanii”/”Kunchir”)
  • Giardiasis – Giardia lamblia, fecal-oral
  • Amoebiasis – Entamoeba histolytica, fecal-oral

Viral Symmetry Examples:

  • Helical: tobacco mosaic virus
  • Icosahedral: adenovirus, papovavirus, picornavirus
  • Complex: bacteriophages

Difference Between DNA and RNA Viruses:

  • DNA viruses: mostly dsDNA, stable, replicate in nucleus.
  • RNA viruses: mostly ssRNA, higher mutation rate, unstable, replicate in cytoplasm.

Modes of Disease Transmission:

  • Droplet: common cold, flu, pneumonia
  • Contaminated water: cholera, typhoid fever
  • Contaminated food: salmonellosis, botulism
  • Direct contact: athlete’s foot, ringworm
  • Sexual intercourse: AIDS, syphilis, gonorrhea
  • Blood-to-blood: HIV, hepatitis B
  • Animal vectors: malaria (mosquito), sleeping sickness (tsetse fly)

Economic Roles of Microorganisms:

  • Agriculture: nitrogen fixation, decomposition, biofertilizers.
  • Sewage treatment: anaerobic digestion, methane production.
  • Bioremediation: degrade oil, heavy metals, pesticides.
  • Food: bread, yoghurt, cheese, traditional fermented foods.
  • Medicine: antibiotics, human insulin, vaccines, drug delivery.
  • Health: vitamin K/B synthesis, protection against pathogens.
  • Biotechnology: enzyme production, genetic engineering, PCR enzyme (Taq polymerase from Thermus aquaticus).

Nutrient Cycles:

  • Carbon cycle: microbes decompose organics → CO₂; photosynthesis fixes CO₂.
  • Nitrogen cycle: bacteria fix N₂ → nitrate; rhizobia in legume root nodules.
  • Sulfur cycle: bacteria transform sulfur compounds in proteins.
  • Phosphorus cycle: microbes mineralize organic phosphorus into phosphate.

Unit 3

3.1 Energy and Metabolism

  • Energy: All living things require energy to carry out life processes. Cells need energy to grow, reproduce, and simply maintain themselves. Cells obtain energy in many forms, but that energy is seldom used directly; cells have mechanisms to convert energy from one form to another.
  • Metabolism: From the Greek word for “change,” it consists of all chemical reactions that change or transform energy in a cell.
  • Metabolic pathways: Step-by-step sequences of reactions where the product of one reaction becomes the substrate of the next. Each step is catalyzed by a specific enzyme.
  • Enzymes: Macromolecules that speed up chemical reactions, enabling metabolic reactions to proceed fast enough to sustain life.
  • Feedback inhibition: A regulatory mechanism where the end product of a pathway inhibits an upstream process.
  • Two subcategories of metabolism:
    • Anabolism (synthesis/biosynthesis): The constructive process where nutrients are used to build complex molecules from simpler ones. This requires energy (ATP). It builds macromolecules such as proteins, nucleic acids, and polysaccharides. Anabolism = growth.
    • Catabolism (degradation): The destructive process that breaks down large or complex molecules (proteins, polysaccharides, fats) into small molecules (amino acids, monosaccharides, fatty acids). This releases energy. It includes glycolysis and the citric acid cycle.
  • Main goal of nutrition: To keep anabolism always greater than catabolism.
  • Key metabolic processes:
    • Glycolysis: The metabolic process that converts glucose to pyruvic acid and hydrogen ions. It is a chain of ten enzyme-catalyzed reactions. Energy released is used to form NADH and ATP.
    • Citric acid cycle: A sequence of chemical reactions used by aerobic organisms to release stored energy from the oxidation of carbohydrates, fats, and proteins.

Difference Between Catabolism and Anabolism:

CATABOLISM ANABOLISM
All catabolic reactions in a cell All anabolic reactions in a cell
Releases energy Requires energy
Involves breaking of bonds; energy is released when bonds break Involves creation of bonds; energy is required to create bonds
Larger molecules broken into smaller molecules (degradative reactions) Smaller molecules bonded to create larger molecules (biosynthetic reactions)

3.2 Chemical Structure of Carbohydrates and Lipids

3.2.1 Carbohydrates

  • General formula: Monosaccharides have the formula [C(H₂O)]ₙ, where n = 3–7. They are called simple sugars.
  • Classification by size:
    • Monosaccharides: Simplest carbohydrates; cannot be broken down into smaller sugars under mild conditions. Subclassified by carbon number:
      • Triose sugars: 3-carbon skeleton
      • Tetrose sugars: 4-carbon skeleton
      • Pentose sugars: 5-carbon skeleton
      • Hexose sugars: 6-carbon skeleton (most prevalent)
    • Oligosaccharides: Composed of 4 to 10 simple sugar units. Disaccharides (two sugars) are common; trisaccharides occur frequently.
    • Polysaccharides: Polymers of simple sugars and their derivatives; may be linear or branched; contain hundreds or thousands of monosaccharide units.
  • Functional groups: All carbohydrates have hydroxyl (–OH) groups and a carbonyl (C=O) group.
    • Aldoses (aldehyde sugars): Carbonyl group at the end of the carbon chain. Example: glucose.
    • Ketoses (ketone sugars): Carbonyl group at any other position. Example: fructose.
  • Disaccharides: Two monosaccharide rings joined by a glycosidic linkage (a central oxygen covalently bonded to two carbons, one in each ring). The linkage generally forms between carbon 1 of one molecule and carbon 4 of the other.
    • Maltose: Two covalently linked α-glucose units.
    • Sucrose, lactose, maltose have the general formula C₁₂H₂₂O₁₁.
  • Formation (dehydration synthesis/condensation): Disaccharides form when two monosaccharides release a water molecule.
  • Hydrolysis: The opposite reaction; a disaccharide and water react to form two monosaccharides.
  • Oligosaccharides: Carbohydrates containing 3 to 10 single sugar residues. Examples: raffinose, stachyose, verbascose.
  • Polysaccharides: Long polymers of monosaccharides linked by dehydration synthesis; they are complex carbohydrates.
    • Glycogen: A readily mobilized storage form of glucose in animals; very large, branched polymer of glucose. Most glucose residues are linked by α-1,4-glycosidic bonds; branches at about every tenth residue are created by α-1,6-glycosidic bonds.
    • Starch: A polysaccharide carbohydrate (C₆H₁₀O₅)ₙ consisting of many glucose molecules joined by α-1,4-glycosidic bonds. Found especially in seeds, bulbs, and tubers. Principal carbohydrate composed of amylose (10–30%) and amylopectin (70–90%) in plants.
    • Cellulose: Long polymer chains of glucose units connected by beta acetal linkage (β-1,4-glycosidic bonds). All monomer units are beta-D-glucose. Forms wood and plant cell walls.
    • Chitin: Found in exoskeletons of insects, cell walls of fungi, and certain hard structures in invertebrates and fish. Large, structural polysaccharide made from chains of modified glucose.
    • Pectin: A polysaccharide found in the cell walls of fruits and vegetables.
  • Alpha vs. Beta bonds:
    • Alpha-D-glucose: –OH group on carbon 1 is below the ring plane. When α-glucose molecules join, they form starch and glycogen.
    • Beta-D-glucose: –OH group on carbon 1 is above the ring plane. When β-glucose molecules join, they form cellulose and chitin.
    • 1,4 alpha glycosidic bonds: –OH on carbon-1 is below the glucose ring.
    • 1,4 beta glycosidic bonds: –OH on carbon-1 is above the plane.
  • Reducing sugars: Sugars that can donate electrons; Benedict’s test is used to detect them.

3.2.2 Lipids

  • Elements: Contain the same elements as carbohydrates but proportionately less oxygen.
  • Solubility: Dissolve in organic solvents, not in water.
  • Biological roles: Cell membrane components, energy storage molecules, insulation, hormones, cushioning of vital organs, transmission of nerve impulses, transport of fat-soluble nutrients.
  • Energy content: Provide more than double the amount of energy compared to equal weight of carbohydrates or proteins.

Classification of Lipids:

  1. Simple lipids

    • (a) Fats and oils (triacylglycerols/triglycerides): Esters composed of three fatty acids joined to glycerol (a trihydroxy alcohol).
      • Fat if solid at 25°C; oil if liquid at 25°C.
      • The difference in melting points reflects the degree of unsaturation of fatty acids.
      • Saturated fatty acid: No double bonds.
      • Unsaturated fatty acid: At least one double bond.
      • Polyunsaturated fatty acid: More than one double bond.
      • Monounsaturated fatty acid: Exactly one double bond.
      • Plants lipids are less saturated than those in animals.
    • (b) Waxes: Esters of a long-chain alcohol (usually monohydroxy) and a fatty acid. Chain lengths are typically 12–34 carbon atoms.

  2. Compound lipids

    • (a) Phospholipids: Made of fatty acids, glycerol (or other alcohol), and phosphoric acid. Major constituents of cell membranes. They are amphipathic: a hydrophilic (polar) phosphate head and two hydrophobic fatty acid tails.
    • (b) Glycolipids: Lipids with a carbohydrate attached by a glycosidic bond. Found on the surface of all eukaryotic cell membranes; maintain membrane stability and facilitate cellular recognition (important for immune response and tissue formation).

  3. Derived lipids: Hydrolysis products of simple and compound lipids. Include fatty acids, glycerol, sphingosine, and steroid derivatives.

    • Sterols: Lipids with four carbon rings (e.g., vitamin D, cortisone, cholesterol). Cholesterol is part of cell membranes; it is the starting material for synthesizing sex hormones (testosterone, estrogen). The liver manufactures cholesterol when it breaks down saturated fats. Excess cholesterol can build up on inner linings of blood vessels and impede blood flow.

3.3 Photosynthesis

  • Autotroph (“self-feeder”): An organism that uses inorganic substances (water, CO₂) to produce organic compounds.
  • Heterotroph: An organism that obtains carbon by consuming preexisting organic molecules. (Humans, all other animals, fungi, many microbes.)
  • Photosynthesis: The process by which plants, algae, and some microbes harness solar energy and convert it into chemical energy. It is a series of chemical reactions that uses light energy to assemble CO₂ into glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆) and other carbohydrates. Water is used, and oxygen gas (O₂) is released as a by-product.
  • Overall chemical equation:
    6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light energy → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂
  • Photoautotrophs: Plants, multicellular algae, unicellular protists, cyanobacteria, purple sulfur bacteria.
  • Importance of photosynthesis:
    • Provides food for the plant and energy, raw materials, and O₂ for most heterotrophs.
    • The number one source of oxygen in the atmosphere.
    • Contributes to the carbon cycle among earth, oceans, plants, and animals.
    • Contributes to symbiotic relationships between plants, humans, and animals.
    • Directly or indirectly affects most living things.
    • Serves as the primary energy process for most trees and plants.

3.3.1 Leaf Structure and Site of Photosynthesis

  • External leaf parts: Leaf base, leaf lamina, petiole.
  • Epidermis: Outer leaf layer; secretes a waxy cuticle to retain water. Contains guard cells that regulate gas exchange through stomata.
  • Internal leaf structure: Epidermis with stomata, mesophyll cells, vascular system (xylem and phloem).
  • Mesophyll: The tissue in the interior of the leaf; contains the majority of chloroplasts (~30–40 per cell).
  • Chloroplast structure:
    • Two surrounding membranes.
    • Dense fluid inside called the stroma.
    • Suspended within stroma is a third membrane system of sacs called thylakoids. The thylakoid interior is the thylakoid lumen.
    • Grana (singular: granum): Stacks of thylakoid sacs.
    • Chlorophyll: Green pigment residing in thylakoid membranes; absorbs light energy that drives synthesis of organic molecules.
  • Stomata (singular: stoma): Microscopic pores through which CO₂ enters and O₂ exits the leaf.
  • Veins: Deliver water from roots to leaves and export sugar to non-photosynthetic parts of the plant.

3.3.2 Photosynthetic Pigments

  • Pigments are chemical compounds that absorb certain wavelengths of visible light and reflect others; they appear colorful.

  • Three basic classes:

    1. Chlorophylls: Greenish pigments containing a porphyrin ring that can gain or lose electrons easily, providing energized electrons.

    • Chlorophyll a: The most important; found in all plants, algae, and cyanobacteria.
    • Chlorophyll b: Occurs only in green algae and plants.
    • Chlorophyll c: Found only in photosynthetic Chromista and dinoflagellates.

    2. Carotenoids: Usually red, orange, or yellow pigments (e.g., carotene). They are accessory pigments: they cannot transfer sunlight energy directly to the photosynthetic pathway; they must pass absorbed energy to chlorophyll. Example: fucoxanthin (brown pigment in brown algae and diatoms).

    3. Phycobilins: Water-soluble pigments found in the cytoplasm or stroma of the chloroplast; occur only in Cyanobacteria and Rhodophyta.

  • Absorption spectra:

    • Chlorophyll a absorbs violet-blue and reddish orange-red wavelengths.
    • Chlorophyll b absorbs mostly blue and yellow light.
    • Neither absorbs green strongly; leaves appear green because green light is reflected.
    • Carotenoids absorb in the blue-green region and transfer energy to chlorophylls, expanding the wavelength range for photosynthesis.
    • Only absorbed light (largely blue and red) is useful in photosynthesis.

3.3.3 Light-Dependent and Light-Independent Reactions

Photosynthesis occurs in two stages:

A. Light-Dependent Reactions (Light Reactions)

  • Occur in the thylakoid membranes.
  • Convert solar energy to chemical energy.
  • Water is split (photolysis), providing electrons and protons (H⁺) and releasing O₂.
  • Light absorbed by chlorophyll drives the transfer of electrons and H⁺ from water to NADP⁺, reducing it to NADPH.
  • ATP is generated via chemiosmosis (photophosphorylation).
  • Products: ATP and NADPH. No sugar is produced at this stage.

Photosystem II and Photosystem I (Non-cyclic photophosphorylation):

  1. Electrons in chlorophyll of Photosystem II are excited by light photons and escape to a primary electron acceptor.
  2. Photolysis of water: 2H₂O → O₂ + 4H⁺ + 4e⁻. Electrons replace those lost from chlorophyll.
  3. Electrons pass along an electron transport chain (plastoquinone → cytochromes → plastocyanin), losing energy.
  4. One cytochrome complex acts as a proton pump, moving H⁺ from stroma into thylakoid space. This gradient drives chemiosmotic ATP synthesis.
  5. Electrons in Photosystem I are excited by light, escape, and are replaced by electrons coming from Photosystem II.
  6. Electrons pass through a second chain (ferredoxin, NADP reductase) and combine with H⁺ and NADP⁺ to form NADPH.

Summary: Light energy excites electrons → oxygen gas produced, ADP phosphorylated to ATP, NADP⁺ reduced to NADPH. ATP and NADPH provide energy for the Calvin cycle.

B. Light-Independent Reactions (Calvin Cycle)

  • Occur in the stroma of the chloroplast.
  • Do not use light as a reactant (can occur day or night) but require ATP and NADPH from the light reactions.
  • Enzymes of the Calvin cycle are activated by light.
  • The cycle captures CO₂ and uses ATP and NADPH to produce sugar.

Three main stages:

  1. Carbon fixation: CO₂ combines with a five-carbon acceptor, ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate (RuBP). This reaction is catalyzed by the enzyme RuBP carboxylase/oxygenase (rubisco). The resulting six-carbon compound splits into two molecules of 3-phosphoglyceric acid (3-PGA), a three-carbon compound.
  2. Reduction: ATP and NADPH convert 3-PGA into glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate (G3P), a three-carbon sugar. NADPH donates electrons (reduction).
  3. Regeneration: Some G3P molecules are used to make glucose, while the rest are recycled to regenerate RuBP. This requires ATP.

Net yield per three turns of the Calvin cycle (one net G3P):

  • Carbon: 3 CO₂ combine with 3 RuBP → 6 G3P. 1 G3P exits to make glucose; 5 G3P recycled to regenerate 3 RuBP.
  • ATP: 9 ATP → 9 ADP (6 during fixation step, 3 during regeneration step).
  • NADPH: 6 NADPH → 6 NADP⁺ (during reduction step).

To make one glucose molecule (6 carbons), two G3P are needed, requiring 6 turns of the cycle: 6 CO₂, 18 ATP, 12 NADPH.

3.3.4 C3, C4, and CAM Pathways

  • C3 pathway: The Calvin cycle; the first stable compound is the three-carbon 3-PGA. About 95% of plant species are C3 (e.g., cereals, peanuts, tobacco, spinach, soybeans, most trees).

  • Photorespiration: A wasteful process where rubisco adds O₂ instead of CO₂ to RuBP, leading to loss of fixed CO₂ and wasting ATP and NADPH. Contributes to low efficiency in C3 plants under hot, dry conditions.

  • C4 plants: Minimize photorespiration by physically separating the Calvin cycle from O₂-rich air spaces.

    • Light reactions occur in mesophyll cells.
    • CO₂ is first fixed into a four-carbon compound (the C4 pathway) in mesophyll cells using a three-carbon “ferry” molecule.
    • The four-carbon compound moves into bundle-sheath cells, where CO₂ is released. The Calvin cycle then fixes carbon again.
    • The ferry molecule returns to mesophyll at the cost of 2 ATP.
    • Examples: crabgrass, sugarcane, corn. About 1% of plant species.

  • CAM plants (Crassulacean Acid Metabolism):

    • Open stomata only at night to fix CO₂ into a four-carbon compound stored in vacuoles.
    • Stomata close during the day; the stored compound releases CO₂ inside chloroplasts for the Calvin cycle.
    • Both fixation reactions occur in the same cell.
    • Reduces water loss and photorespiration by generating high CO₂ concentrations inside chloroplasts.
    • Examples: pineapple, cacti. About 3–4% of plant species. Adapted to dry habitats; cannot compete with C3 plants in cool environments due to reduced carbon availability.

3.4 Cellular Respiration

  • Cellular respiration: The series of metabolic pathways that extract energy from the bonds in glucose and convert it into a usable form (ATP). Both plants and consumers undergo cellular respiration.
  • Overall equation: C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + energy (ATP)

3.4.1 ATP – The Energy Currency

  • ATP (Adenosine Triphosphate): The principal molecule for storing and transferring energy in cells; often called the energy currency of the cell.
  • Structure: A nucleotide consisting of an adenine base, a ribose sugar, and three phosphate groups. The phosphate groups are linked by two high-energy phosphoanhydride bonds.
  • ATP hydrolysis: When one phosphate group is removed by breaking a phosphoanhydride bond, energy is released, and ATP → ADP (adenosine diphosphate) + Pᵢ.
  • ADP hydrolysis: ADP → AMP (adenosine monophosphate) + Pᵢ also releases energy.
  • ATP synthesis: AMP and ADP can be recycled back to ATP by forming new phosphoanhydride bonds, storing energy.
  • Coupled reactions: Exothermic (energy-releasing) reactions are coupled with endothermic (energy-requiring) reactions via ATP. Example: ATP hydrolysis (exothermic) provides energy for muscle contraction (endothermic).
    • ATP + H₂O → ADP + Pᵢ + energy (energy released)
    • Relaxed muscle + energy → contracted muscle (energy used)
    • Creatine phosphate can also donate energy: Creatine phosphate + H₂O → creatine + Pᵢ + energy, which is used to resynthesize ATP: ADP + Pᵢ + energy → ATP + H₂O.

3.4.2 Stages of Aerobic Respiration

Aerobic respiration consists of four main stages:

  1. Glycolysis (cytosol)
  2. Pyruvate oxidation (link reaction) (mitochondrial matrix)
  3. Citric acid cycle (Krebs cycle) (mitochondrial matrix)
  4. Oxidative phosphorylation (electron transport chain & chemiosmosis) (inner mitochondrial membrane)

Stage 1: Glycolysis

  • Location: cytosol. Does not require oxygen.
  • Breaks one glucose (6C) into two molecules of pyruvate (3C).
  • Divided into:
    • Preparatory phase: glucose converted into three-carbon sugar phosphates; uses 2 ATP.
    • Pay-off phase: generates 4 ATP and 2 NADH.
  • Net reaction:
    Glucose + 2NAD⁺ + 2Pi + 2ADP → 2 Pyruvate + 2H⁺ + 2NADH + 2ATP + 2H₂O
    Net gain: 2 ATP, 2 NADH per glucose.

Stage 2: Pyruvate Oxidation (Link Reaction)

  • Location: mitochondrial matrix (eukaryotes).
  • Pyruvate is converted to acetyl coenzyme A (acetyl CoA).
  • Three-step process:
    1. Decarboxylation: a carboxyl group is removed from pyruvate, releasing CO₂.
    2. Oxidation: the remaining hydroxyethyl group is oxidized to an acetyl group; electrons are picked up by NAD⁺ → NADH.
    3. Transfer: the acetyl group is transferred to coenzyme A, producing acetyl CoA.
  • Overall reaction per pyruvate:
    Pyruvate + NAD⁺ + CoA → Acetyl-CoA + NADH + H⁺ + CO₂
    For one glucose (two pyruvates): 2 acetyl-CoA, 2 NADH, 2 CO₂ are produced.

Stage 3: Citric Acid Cycle (Krebs Cycle)

  • Location: mitochondrial matrix.
  • Acetyl-CoA (2C) combines with oxaloacetate (OAA, 4C) to form citrate (6C).
  • Through a series of reactions, citrate is rearranged and decarboxylated, releasing two CO₂ molecules and regenerating OAA.
  • Products per turn (per acetyl-CoA):
    • 3 NADH
    • 1 FADH₂
    • 1 GTP (equivalent to 1 ATP)
    • 2 CO₂
  • For one glucose (two turns): 6 NADH, 2 FADH₂, 2 GTP, 4 CO₂.

Stage 4: Oxidative Phosphorylation

  • Location: inner mitochondrial membrane.
  • Consists of the electron transport chain (ETC) and chemiosmosis.
  • Electron Transport Chain (ETC): A series of protein electron carriers (complexes I, II, III, IV) embedded in the inner mitochondrial membrane.
    • NADH and FADH₂ donate electrons to the chain.
    • Electrons move through carriers, releasing energy. The final electron acceptor is O₂, which combines with electrons and H⁺ to form H₂O.
    • As electrons flow, some complexes (I, III, IV) pump protons (H⁺) from the mitochondrial matrix to the intermembrane space, creating a proton gradient.
  • Chemiosmosis: The proton gradient drives H⁺ back into the matrix through ATP synthase (a protein complex). The energy released by this flow is used to phosphorylate ADP → ATP.
  • Three sites of ATP production:
    • Site I: between FMN and Coenzyme Q (Complex I)
    • Site II: between cyt b and cyt c1 (Complex III)
    • Site III: between cyt a and cyt a3 (Complex IV)
  • ATP yield per reduced cofactor:
    • 1 NADH ≈ 3 ATP
    • 1 FADH₂ ≈ 2 ATP
  • Oxidative phosphorylation produces about 90% of total ATP in the cell.

Total ATP yield from one glucose molecule (theoretical):

  • Glycolysis: 2 ATP + 2 NADH → 2 ATP + (2 × 3 ATP) = 8 ATP
  • Pyruvate oxidation: 2 NADH → 2 × 3 ATP = 6 ATP
  • Citric acid cycle: 6 NADH + 2 FADH₂ + 2 GTP → (6×3) + (2×2) + (2×1) = 18 + 4 + 2 = 24 ATP
  • Total: 8 + 6 + 24 = 38 ATP (some texts note 36 due to mitochondrial NADH shuttle variation).

Key terms:

  • Coenzyme A: derived from pantothenic acid, needed for respiration.
  • Acetyl coenzyme A: produced by reaction of coenzyme A with pyruvate.
  • Dehydrogenation: removing hydrogen from a molecule.
  • Decarboxylation: removing carbon (as CO₂) from a molecule.
  • Terminal electron acceptor: the final molecule at the end of the ETC that accepts electrons (O₂ in aerobic respiration).

Respirometers:

  • Work on the principle that O₂ is used in aerobic respiration and CO₂ is produced.
  • The overall equation (C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O) predicts equal volumes of O₂ consumed and CO₂ produced.
  • CO₂ is absorbed by a substance such as potassium hydroxide (KOH). Therefore, the reduction in gas volume inside the respirometer corresponds to O₂ consumption.

3.5 Fermentation (Anaerobic Pathway)

  • When oxygen is not present or an organism cannot undergo aerobic respiration, pyruvate undergoes fermentation.
  • Fermentation replenishes NAD⁺ from NADH so that glycolysis can continue. It does not require oxygen (anaerobic).
  • Facultative anaerobes: Organisms that can undergo fermentation when deprived of oxygen (e.g., yeast).

A. Alcohol Fermentation

  • Occurs in yeast and some other microorganisms.
  • Process:
    1. Pyruvate is decarboxylated (CO₂ removed) to form acetaldehyde.
    2. Acetaldehyde is reduced by NADH + H⁺ to ethanol, regenerating NAD⁺.
  • Overall: Pyruvate → ethanol + CO₂ with regeneration of NAD⁺.

B. Lactic Acid Fermentation

  • Occurs in some bacteria and in animal muscle cells when oxygen is limited (oxygen debt).
  • Process: Pyruvate is directly reduced by NADH + H⁺ to lactic acid (lactate), regenerating NAD⁺.
  • Overall: Pyruvate + NADH → lactate + NAD⁺
  • Used in muscle cells during strenuous exercise when O₂ supply is insufficient.

3.6 Importance of Photosynthesis and Respiration for Life

  • Photosynthesis produces oxygen and organic molecules that sustain almost all life on Earth.
  • It removes CO₂ from the atmosphere, helping to regulate the carbon cycle and inhibit global warming.
  • Cellular respiration releases the energy stored in photosynthetic products for use by all living organisms.
  • Together, these processes maintain the balance of O₂ and CO₂ in the atmosphere.

Energy and Metabolism

  • Energy: essential for all life processes. Cells convert energy from one form to another via metabolic pathways.
  • Metabolism: all chemical reactions in a cell; organized into pathways catalyzed by enzymes.
  • Anabolism (biosynthesis): builds complex molecules from simple ones; requires energy (ATP); e.g., synthesis of proteins, polysaccharides.
  • Catabolism (degradation): breaks down large molecules into small ones; releases energy; e.g., glycolysis, citric acid cycle.
  • Glycolysis: glucose → pyruvate + energy (ATP, NADH).
  • Citric acid cycle: oxidizes acetyl-CoA to CO₂, producing energy carriers (NADH, FADH₂, GTP).

Carbohydrates

  • Monosaccharides: simple sugars, formula [C(H₂O)]ₙ (n=3–7). Classified by carbon number: triose, tetrose, pentose, hexose.
  • Aldose vs. Ketose: carbonyl at end (aldose, e.g., glucose) vs. internal carbonyl (ketose, e.g., fructose).
  • Disaccharides: two monosaccharides linked by glycosidic bond (maltose, sucrose, lactose). Formed via dehydration synthesis; broken by hydrolysis.
  • Polysaccharides: polymers of monosaccharides.
    • Starch: α-glucose polymer (amylose + amylopectin); plant storage.
    • Glycogen: highly branched α-glucose polymer; animal storage.
    • Cellulose: β-glucose polymer; plant cell walls.
    • Chitin: β-glucose derivative; fungal cell walls, insect exoskeletons.
  • α-1,4 bond: OH below ring → starch/glycogen. β-1,4 bond: OH above ring → cellulose/chitin.

Lipids

  • Fats and oils: triglycerides = glycerol + 3 fatty acids. Solid (fat) or liquid (oil) at 25°C depending on saturation.
  • Saturated: no double bonds; unsaturated: ≥1 double bond; polyunsaturated: multiple double bonds.
  • Phospholipids: glycerol + 2 fatty acids + phosphate; amphipathic; key membrane component.
  • Glycolipids: lipid + carbohydrate; cell recognition.
  • Sterols: four-ring structure; cholesterol (membrane fluidity, precursor to sex hormones). Excess cholesterol can block blood vessels.

Photosynthesis

  • Equation: 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂.
  • Site: chloroplasts, mainly in leaf mesophyll. Thylakoids (light reactions), stroma (Calvin cycle).
  • Pigments:
    • Chlorophyll a (primary), chlorophyll b (accessory), carotenoids (accessory, photoprotection).
    • Absorb mainly blue and red light; reflect green.
  • Light reactions: In thylakoid membranes. Photolysis of water releases O₂, electrons, H⁺. Products: ATP, NADPH.
  • Calvin cycle (light-independent): In stroma. Uses ATP and NADPH to fix CO₂ into G3P (→ glucose). Requires enzyme rubisco. Three phases: carbon fixation, reduction, regeneration.
  • C3 plants: Only Calvin cycle; photorespiration reduces efficiency.
  • C4 plants: Spatially separate initial CO₂ fixation (mesophyll) and Calvin cycle (bundle-sheath cells) to reduce photorespiration; e.g., corn, sugarcane.
  • CAM plants: Temporally separate CO₂ fixation (night) and Calvin cycle (day) to conserve water; e.g., cacti, pineapple.

Cellular Respiration

  • Equation: C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + energy (ATP).
  • ATP: energy currency; adenine + ribose + 3 phosphates. Hydrolysis of terminal phosphate releases energy.
  • Coupled reactions: energy released from exothermic reactions (ATP hydrolysis) drives endothermic reactions (muscle contraction).

Stages:

  1. Glycolysis (cytosol): Glucose → 2 pyruvate; net 2 ATP, 2 NADH.
  2. Pyruvate oxidation (matrix): Pyruvate → acetyl-CoA; produces 2 NADH, 2 CO₂ per glucose.
  3. Krebs cycle (matrix): Acetyl-CoA → 2 CO₂; produces 3 NADH, 1 FADH₂, 1 GTP per turn; per glucose: 6 NADH, 2 FADH₂, 2 GTP.
  4. Oxidative phosphorylation (inner membrane): ETC transfers electrons from NADH/FADH₂ to O₂, pumping H⁺; ATP synthase produces ATP. Yields ~34 ATP per glucose. Total ~38 ATP.

Fermentation

  • Anaerobic; regenerates NAD⁺ to sustain glycolysis.
  • Alcohol fermentation: pyruvate → acetaldehyde → ethanol + CO₂; by yeast.
  • Lactic acid fermentation: pyruvate → lactic acid; by bacteria and muscle cells in oxygen debt.

COMPREHENSIVE UNIT SUMMARY, KEY POINTS, DEFINITIONS, PROCESSES

Overall Summary

Unit 3 covers energy transformation in living organisms. It begins with the chemical structures of carbohydrates and lipids, the primary energy-storing molecules. Metabolism is the sum of all anabolic (building) and catabolic (breaking) reactions. Photosynthesis captures light energy to produce glucose and oxygen in chloroplasts, using light-dependent reactions (ATP, NADPH) and the Calvin cycle. Plants have C3, C4, and CAM pathways to adapt to different environments. Cellular respiration breaks down glucose in the presence of oxygen through glycolysis, pyruvate oxidation, the Krebs cycle, and oxidative phosphorylation to produce ~38 ATP per glucose. Without oxygen, fermentation (alcoholic or lactic acid) regenerates NAD⁺ to continue glycolysis. These processes sustain life by cycling energy and matter.

Key Points

  • Energy is required for all life processes; cells transform energy via metabolic pathways.
  • Metabolism = anabolism (builds, uses energy) + catabolism (breaks down, releases energy).
  • Carbohydrates are classified into monosaccharides, disaccharides, and polysaccharides; alpha (α-1,4) bonds form starch/glycogen, beta (β-1,4) bonds form cellulose/chitin.
  • Lipids include triglycerides, phospholipids, glycolipids, sterols; they store energy, form membranes, and act as hormones.
  • Photosynthesis converts light energy to chemical energy: 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂.
    • Light reactions (thylakoid membrane): produce ATP, NADPH, O₂.
    • Calvin cycle (stroma): fixes CO₂ using ATP and NADPH to make sugar.
    • C3 pathway (most plants), C4 (separate fixation spatially), CAM (separate fixation temporally).
  • Cellular respiration releases energy from glucose: C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + ~38 ATP.
    • Glycolysis (cytosol) → pyruvate.
    • Pyruvate oxidation (matrix) → acetyl-CoA.
    • Krebs cycle (matrix) → CO₂, NADH, FADH₂, GTP.
    • ETC/oxidative phosphorylation (inner membrane) → bulk ATP via proton gradient.
  • ATP is the energy currency; hydrolysis → ADP + Pᵢ releases energy; coupled reactions drive cellular work.
  • Fermentation (anaerobic): alcohol fermentation (yeast: pyruvate → ethanol + CO₂) and lactic acid fermentation (muscles: pyruvate → lactate). Both regenerate NAD⁺.
  • Photosynthesis and respiration are complementary processes that balance O₂ and CO₂, sustaining life on Earth.

Definitions

  • Energy: The capacity to do work or produce change.
  • Metabolism: All chemical reactions in a cell; organized into pathways.
  • Enzyme: A macromolecule (usually protein) that speeds up a chemical reaction.
  • Anabolism: Constructive metabolism; building of complex molecules from simpler ones; requires energy (e.g., protein synthesis).
  • Catabolism: Destructive metabolism; breakdown of complex molecules into simpler ones; releases energy (e.g., glycolysis).
  • Monosaccharide: Simplest carbohydrate; cannot be hydrolyzed to a smaller sugar (e.g., glucose, fructose).
  • Disaccharide: Two monosaccharides linked by a glycosidic bond (e.g., maltose, sucrose).
  • Polysaccharide: Polymer of many monosaccharides (e.g., starch, glycogen, cellulose).
  • Aldose: Monosaccharide with an aldehyde group (carbonyl at end).
  • Ketose: Monosaccharide with a ketone group (carbonyl internal).
  • Glycosidic bond: Covalent bond linking two monosaccharides.
  • Dehydration synthesis (condensation): Reaction that removes water to link molecules.
  • Hydrolysis: Reaction that adds water to break a bond.
  • Lipid: Hydrophobic organic molecule; includes fats, phospholipids, sterols.
  • Triglyceride: Glycerol + three fatty acids; fat (solid) or oil (liquid).
  • Saturated fatty acid: No double bonds between carbons.
  • Unsaturated fatty acid: At least one double bond.
  • Phospholipid: Amphipathic lipid with phosphate head and two fatty acid tails; forms cell membranes.
  • Sterol: Lipid with four fused carbon rings (e.g., cholesterol).
  • Photoautotroph: Organism that uses light energy to synthesize organic compounds from inorganic substances.
  • Heterotroph: Organism that obtains carbon by consuming organic compounds.
  • Photosynthesis: Process using light to convert CO₂ and H₂O into glucose and O₂.
  • Chloroplast: Organelle where photosynthesis occurs; contains thylakoids and stroma.
  • Thylakoid: Flattened membrane sac within chloroplast; site of light reactions.
  • Granum (grana): Stack of thylakoids.
  • Stroma: Fluid-filled space surrounding thylakoids; site of Calvin cycle.
  • Chlorophyll: Green pigment; primary receptor of light energy in photosynthesis.
  • Accessory pigment: Pigment that absorbs light and transfers energy to chlorophyll (e.g., carotenoids).
  • Photolysis: Light-driven splitting of water (2H₂O → O₂ + 4H⁺ + 4e⁻).
  • NADP⁺/NADPH: Electron carrier; NADPH is reduced form, carries high-energy electrons.
  • Calvin cycle: Light-independent reactions of photosynthesis; fixes CO₂ into G3P using ATP and NADPH.
  • Rubisco: Enzyme that catalyzes CO₂ fixation in Calvin cycle.
  • Photorespiration: Wasteful reaction where rubisco fixes O₂ instead of CO₂.
  • C4 pathway: Carbon fixation pathway that spatially separates initial CO₂ capture (mesophyll) from Calvin cycle (bundle-sheath).
  • CAM pathway: Carbon fixation that temporally separates CO₂ capture (night) and Calvin cycle (day).
  • Cellular respiration: Metabolic process that releases energy from glucose; uses O₂ and produces CO₂.
  • ATP (adenosine triphosphate): Energy currency of the cell; hydrolysis releases energy.
  • Glycolysis: First stage of cellular respiration; breaks glucose into pyruvate in cytosol; yields 2 ATP, 2 NADH.
  • Pyruvate oxidation (link reaction): Conversion of pyruvate to acetyl-CoA; produces NADH and CO₂.
  • Krebs cycle (citric acid cycle): Cyclic series of reactions that oxidizes acetyl-CoA to CO₂; produces NADH, FADH₂, GTP.
  • Electron transport chain (ETC): Series of protein complexes that transfer electrons from NADH/FADH₂ to O₂, pumping H⁺.
  • Chemiosmosis: Process by which H⁺ gradient drives ATP synthesis via ATP synthase.
  • Oxidative phosphorylation: Combined ETC and chemiosmosis; produces most ATP in aerobic respiration.
  • Fermentation: Anaerobic pathway that regenerates NAD⁺ from NADH; allows glycolysis to continue.
  • Alcohol fermentation: Pyruvate → ethanol + CO₂ (yeast).
  • Lactic acid fermentation: Pyruvate → lactate (muscle cells, some bacteria).

Processes

Glycolysis (Cytosol)

  1. Glucose (6C) is phosphorylated (uses 2 ATP) and split into two 3-carbon compounds.
  2. Series of reactions oxidizes each 3-carbon compound, producing NADH and ATP.
  3. End: 2 pyruvate + 2 ATP (net) + 2 NADH.

Pyruvate Oxidation (Link Reaction)

Per pyruvate:

  1. Decarboxylation: CO₂ removed.
  2. Oxidation: remaining group oxidized; NAD⁺ → NADH.
  3. CoA attachment: acetyl-CoA formed.
    For glucose: 2 acetyl-CoA, 2 CO₂, 2 NADH.

Krebs Cycle (Matrix)

Per acetyl-CoA (one turn):

  1. Acetyl-CoA (2C) + oxaloacetate (4C) → citrate (6C).
  2. Series of reactions: decarboxylations (2 CO₂ released), redox reactions produce 3 NADH, 1 FADH₂, 1 GTP.
  3. Oxaloacetate regenerated.
    Two turns per glucose.

Electron Transport Chain and Chemiosmosis

  1. NADH and FADH₂ donate electrons to protein complexes in inner membrane.
  2. Electrons flow through complexes, pumping H⁺ into intermembrane space.
  3. Final electron acceptor: O₂ → H₂O.
  4. H⁺ gradient drives ATP synthase to produce ATP.

Light-Dependent Reactions (Photosynthesis)

  1. Light excites electrons in Photosystem II; water is split, O₂ released.
  2. Electrons travel via ETC to Photosystem I; H⁺ pumped into thylakoid lumen.
  3. Photosystem I re-energizes electrons; they reduce NADP⁺ to NADPH.
  4. H⁺ gradient drives ATP synthase → ATP.

Calvin Cycle (Stroma)

  1. Carbon fixation: CO₂ + RuBP → 2 × 3-PGA (rubisco).
  2. Reduction: 3-PGA → G3P using ATP and NADPH.
  3. Regeneration: most G3P used to regenerate RuBP (uses ATP); one G3P exits for glucose synthesis.

Alcohol Fermentation

  1. Pyruvate → acetaldehyde (CO₂ released).
  2. Acetaldehyde + NADH → ethanol + NAD⁺.

Lactic Acid Fermentation

  1. Pyruvate + NADH → lactate + NAD⁺.

Other Points

Classification of Carbohydrates:

  • Monosaccharides: glucose, fructose, galactose.
  • Disaccharides: maltose, sucrose, lactose.
  • Oligosaccharides: raffinose, stachyose.
  • Polysaccharides: starch (amylose, amylopectin), glycogen, cellulose, chitin, pectin.

Classification of Lipids:

  1. Simple lipids: fats/oils (triglycerides), waxes.
  2. Compound lipids: phospholipids, glycolipids.
  3. Derived lipids: fatty acids, glycerol, sterols (cholesterol, vitamin D, sex hormones).

ATP Yield Summary (per glucose):

  • Glycolysis: 2 ATP, 2 NADH → 2 + (2×3) = 8 ATP
  • Link reaction: 2 NADH → 6 ATP
  • Krebs cycle: 6 NADH (18 ATP) + 2 FADH₂ (4 ATP) + 2 GTP (2 ATP) = 24 ATP
  • Total = 38 ATP (theoretical maximum; actual often ~30-32 due to membrane leak and shuttle costs).

Key Enzymes Mentioned:

  • Rubisco (C fixation in Calvin cycle)
  • ATP synthase (ATP production in both respiration and photosynthesis)

Key Carriers/Electron Acceptors:

  • NAD⁺/NADH, NADP⁺/NADPH, FAD/FADH₂.
  • Cytochromes in ETC.

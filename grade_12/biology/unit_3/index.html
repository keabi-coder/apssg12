3.1 Introduction to the Physical States of Matter

Definition of Matter

  • Matter is anything that occupies space and has mass.
  • It is the material of the universe.

The Three Common States of Matter

  • Matter commonly exists as a solid, liquid, or gas.
  • The same substance can exist in all three states.
    Example: Water exists as ice (solid), liquid water, and steam (water vapour/gas).

General Properties of Each State

Solid

  • A solid has a definite shape and a definite volume.
  • Solids cannot be compressed.
  • The particles are tightly packed and highly organized. They vibrate about fixed positions.
  • Solids have higher densities than liquids and gases.
  • Examples: metals, chalk, sand, ice.

Liquid

  • A liquid has a definite volume but no definite shape. It takes the shape of its container.
  • Liquids are very slightly compressible.
  • Particles are close together but can move past each other, allowing liquids to flow.
  • Examples: water, ethanol, mercury, oil.

Gas

  • A gas has neither a definite shape nor a definite volume. It fills the entire container.
  • Gases are highly compressible because most of the volume is empty space.
  • Particles are far apart and move rapidly and randomly.
  • Examples: air, hydrogen, oxygen, carbon dioxide, nitrogen.

Plasma

  • Plasma is the fourth state of matter. It exists at extremely high temperatures (millions of degrees Celsius).
  • At such temperatures, atoms are stripped of their electrons, forming a gaseous mixture of positive ions and electrons.
  • Plasma is an ionized gas.
  • Like a gas, it has no fixed shape or volume and is less dense than solids or liquids.
  • No material can exist in the plasma state under ordinary conditions.

Fluids: Liquids and gases are called fluids because they flow.
Condensed states: Solids and liquids are referred to as condensed states because their densities are much higher than those of gases.

3.2 Kinetic Theory and Properties of Matter

Kinetic Theory of Matter

  • Every substance is made of a very large number of very small particles (atoms, ions, or molecules).
  • These particles are in constant, random motion with all possible velocities.
  • The motion of particles increases with an increase in temperature.

Assumptions of the Kinetic Theory of Matter:

  1. All matter is composed of particles that are constantly moving.
  2. All particles have kinetic energy (energy of motion) and potential energy. Molecules in solids have the least energy; gas particles have the greatest.
  3. The difference between the three states is the energy content and motion of the particles. A change in phase occurs when particle energy changes.
  4. The temperature of a substance is a measure of the average kinetic energy of its particles.
  5. There are spaces between particles. The average empty space increases from solid to liquid to gas.
  6. There are attractive forces (intermolecular forces) between particles. These become stronger as particles move closer together.

Properties of Matter Explained by Kinetic Theory

Properties of Gases

  • Gases have no fixed shape or volume. They assume the shape and volume of their container.
  • Gases are easily compressed. Pressure reduces volume because particles are far apart.
  • Gases have low densities due to the large spaces between particles.
  • Gases exert pressure in all directions. Pressure results from collisions of gas particles with container walls.
  • Gases flow and diffuse through one another easily.

Properties of Liquids

  • Liquids have a definite volume but no definite shape. They take the shape of the container.
  • Liquids have higher densities than gases because particles are closer.
  • Liquids are only slightly compressible. There is very little free space between particles.
  • Liquids are fluids. They flow, but more slowly than gases.

Properties of Solids

  • Solids have a definite shape and a definite volume. Strong attractive forces hold particles in fixed positions.
  • Solids generally have the highest densities. Particles are packed very closely.
  • Solids are extremely difficult to compress. Interparticle forces are very strong.
  • Solids are not fluids. They do not flow because particles are rigidly held in place.

Relative trends (increasing order):

  • Intermolecular force: gas < liquid < solid
  • Density: gas < liquid < solid (generally)
  • Compressibility: gas > liquid > solid (gases most compressible, solids least)
  • Kinetic energy (at same temperature): equal on average, but freedom of motion increases from solid to gas.

3.3 The Gaseous State

Kinetic Molecular Theory of Gases

This model explains the behaviour of gases under many conditions.

Assumptions:

  1. A gas consists of particles (atoms or molecules) in constant, straight-line motion.
  2. Gas particles do not attract or repel each other. They collide with each other and container walls elastically.
  3. The volume of the gas particles themselves is negligible compared to the total volume of the gas. There is a lot of empty space.
  4. The average kinetic energy of gas particles is directly proportional to the Kelvin temperature.
    KE=12mv2KE = \frac{1}{2} mv^2KE=21​mv2

General properties of gases:

  • Highly compressible
  • Exert pressure equally in all directions
  • Much lower density than solids and liquids
  • Volume and shape are not fixed
  • Mix evenly and completely (diffusion)

Measurable Properties of Gases

  • Pressure (P): Force per unit area. Caused by collisions of gas molecules with surfaces.
    Pressure=ForceArea\text{Pressure} = \frac{\text{Force}}{\text{Area}}Pressure=AreaForce​
    Units: atmosphere (atm), Pascal (Pa), torr, mmHg.
    1 atm = 760 mmHg = 760 torr = 101,325 Pa = 101.325 kPa
  • Volume (V): Space occupied by the gas.
    SI unit: cubic meter (m³). Common: liter (L), milliliter (mL), cm³, dm³.
    1 L = 1000 mL = 1000 cm³ = 1 dm³
  • Temperature (T): Must be in Kelvin (K) for gas law calculations.
    K = °C + 273
    (Conversion to Fahrenheit: °C = (°F – 32) × 5/9)
  • Amount (n): Moles of gas.
  • Molar volume at STP: At standard temperature and pressure (0 °C and 1 atm), one mole of any ideal gas occupies 22.4 L.

Avogadro’s Law (Volume–Amount Relationship, constant T and P)

  • Statement: At constant temperature and pressure, the volume of a gas is directly proportional to the number of moles.
  • V∝nV \propto nV∝n or Vn=k\frac{V}{n} = knV​=k

The Ideal Gas Equation

  • Combines Boyle’s law, Charles’ law, and Avogadro’s law.
  • An ideal gas is a hypothetical gas that obeys the gas laws exactly at all conditions. Real gases approximate ideal behaviour at high temperature and low pressure.
  • The equation:
    PV=nRTP V = n R TPV=nRT

3.4 The Liquid State

Behaviour of Liquid Particles

  • Particles in a liquid are in constant motion, closer together than in a gas.
  • Attractive forces (intermolecular forces: dipole–dipole, London dispersion, hydrogen bonding) are stronger than in gases but weaker than in solids.
  • Liquids are more ordered than gases but less ordered than solids.

Evaporation (Vaporization)

  • Evaporation: The process by which molecules at the surface of a liquid gain enough kinetic energy to overcome attractive forces and escape into the gas phase.
  • In an open container, evaporation continues until all liquid becomes vapour.
  • In a closed container, evaporation and condensation occur simultaneously.

Condensation

  • Condensation: The process by which gaseous molecules return to the liquid state.
  • In a closed container, as more vapour forms, the rate of condensation increases.
  • Eventually, the rates of evaporation and condensation become equal. This is a dynamic equilibrium.

Vapour Pressure

  • Vapour pressure: The partial pressure exerted by a vapour in dynamic equilibrium with its liquid at a given temperature.
  • At a fixed temperature, vapour pressure is constant.
  • Vapour pressure increases as temperature increases.

Factors Affecting the Rate of Evaporation

  1. Temperature: Higher temperature → higher average kinetic energy → more molecules escape → faster evaporation.
  2. Intermolecular forces: Stronger forces → fewer molecules can escape → slower evaporation → lower vapour pressure.
    Example: Water has strong hydrogen bonding, giving it a relatively low vapour pressure. Diethyl ether (weak dispersion forces) has a high vapour pressure.
  3. Surface area: Larger surface area → more molecules at the surface → higher rate of evaporation.

Volatile and Non-volatile Liquids

  • Volatile liquids: Evaporate readily at room temperature due to weak intermolecular forces.
    Examples: diethyl ether, ethyl alcohol, benzene, mercury.
  • Non-volatile liquids: Evaporate slowly because of strong intermolecular forces.
    Examples: water, glycerin, motor oil, molten ionic compounds.

Boiling and Boiling Point

  • Boiling: The change of a liquid to vapour, with bubble formation throughout the liquid. It occurs when vapour pressure inside bubbles equals the external pressure.
  • Boiling point: The temperature at which the vapour pressure of a liquid equals the external (usually atmospheric) pressure.
  • Normal boiling point: The temperature at which a liquid boils under a pressure of exactly 1 atm.
  • Boiling point changes with external pressure: lower pressure → lower boiling point.
    Example: At high altitude, water boils below 100 °C.
  • During boiling, the temperature remains constant until all liquid is vaporized.

Molar Heat of Vaporization and Condensation

  • Molar heat of vaporization (ΔH_vap): The amount of heat required to convert one mole of a liquid at its boiling point to a gas.
  • Vaporization is an endothermic process (absorbs energy).
  • Molar heat of condensation (ΔH_cond): The amount of heat released when one mole of a gas condenses to a liquid at its condensation point.
  • Condensation is an exothermic process (releases energy).
  • The two are equal in magnitude but opposite in sign:
    ΔHvap=−ΔHcond\Delta H_{\text{vap}} = - \Delta H_{\text{cond}}ΔHvap​=−ΔHcond​

3.5 The Solid State

Melting and Freezing

  • Melting (fusion): The process of converting a solid into a liquid by supplying heat.
  • Melting point: The temperature at which a crystalline solid changes to a liquid. At this temperature, solid and liquid are in dynamic equilibrium.
  • Freezing (solidification): The process of converting a liquid into a solid when cooled.
  • Freezing point: The temperature at which a liquid becomes a solid. It is identical to the melting point for a pure substance.
  • Melting points, unlike boiling points, are affected only slightly by pressure changes.
  • Example: Ice melts at 0 °C; water freezes at 0 °C.

Molar Heat of Fusion and Solidification

  • Molar heat of fusion (ΔH_fus): The quantity of heat required to convert one mole of a solid at its melting point to a liquid.
  • Melting is an endothermic process.
  • Molar heat of crystallization (solidification) (ΔH_cryst): The quantity of heat released when one mole of a liquid converts to a solid at its freezing point.
  • Solidification is an exothermic process.
  • ΔHcryst=−ΔHfus\Delta H_{\text{cryst}} = - \Delta H_{\text{fus}}ΔHcryst​=−ΔHfus​

Sublimation and Deposition

  • Sublimation: The direct change of a solid to a vapour without passing through the liquid state.
    Example: iodine crystals sublime when warmed.
  • Sublimation requires energy input; it is endothermic.
  • Enthalpy of sublimation (ΔH_sub): The energy required to convert one mole of a solid to a gas.
  • Deposition: The direct change of a vapour to a solid, bypassing the liquid state.
    Example: frost formation.
  • Deposition releases energy; it is exothermic.
  • Enthalpy of deposition (ΔH_dep): The energy released when one mole of a gas converts to a solid.
    ΔHsub=−ΔHdep\Delta H_{\text{sub}} = - \Delta H_{\text{dep}}ΔHsub​=−ΔHdep​
  • Relation: ΔHsub=ΔHfus+ΔHvap\Delta H_{\text{sub}} = \Delta H_{\text{fus}} + \Delta H_{\text{vap}}ΔHsub​=ΔHfus​+ΔHvap​

Heating Curve

  • A heating curve is a graph of temperature versus heat added for a substance.
  • Key features:
    • Sloping regions: temperature increases as heat is added. The substance remains in one phase.
    • Plateau (flat) regions: temperature remains constant while a phase change occurs (melting or boiling). Heat energy is used to overcome intermolecular forces, not to increase kinetic energy.
  • The curve shows the sequence: solid → melting (constant T) → liquid → boiling (constant T) → gas.

Comprehensive Exam Summary (Medium-Length Bulleted)

  • Matter exists as solid, liquid, gas, and plasma. Solids have fixed shape and volume; liquids have fixed volume but take shape of container; gases have neither fixed shape nor volume.
  • The kinetic theory explains states in terms of particle motion: solids – vibration about fixed points; liquids – particles move but stay close; gases – rapid, random motion with large spaces.
  • Gas laws describe relationships among pressure (P), volume (V), temperature (T), and moles (n). All temperatures must be in Kelvin.
  • Boyle’s law: P₁V₁ = P₂V₂ (constant T, n)
  • Charles’ law: V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂ (constant P, n)
  • Gay-Lussac’s law: P₁/T₁ = P₂/T₂ (constant V, n)
  • Combined gas law: P₁V₁/T₁ = P₂V₂/T₂
  • Avogadro’s law: V₁/n₁ = V₂/n₂ (constant T, P)
  • Ideal gas equation: PV = nRT. R = 0.0821 L·atm/mol·K. STP: 0°C, 1 atm, 1 mol gas = 22.4 L.
  • Graham’s law of diffusion: rate ∝ 1/√M. Lighter gases diffuse faster.
  • Liquids: evaporation (surface escape), condensation (return to liquid). In a closed container, dynamic equilibrium establishes a constant vapour pressure.
  • Vapour pressure increases with temperature. Boiling occurs when vapour pressure equals external pressure. Normal boiling point is at 1 atm.
  • Volatile liquids have high vapour pressure (weak intermolecular forces); non-volatile liquids have low vapour pressure (strong forces).
  • Heat of vaporization (ΔH_vap) is endothermic; heat of condensation (ΔH_cond) is exothermic. They are equal in magnitude.
  • Solids: melting (fusion) is endothermic; freezing (solidification) is exothermic. Melting point = freezing point for a pure substance.
  • Molar heat of fusion (ΔH_fus) is energy to melt one mole; molar heat of crystallization (ΔH_cryst) is energy released when one mole freezes; ΔH_cryst = –ΔH_fus.
  • Sublimation: solid → gas (endothermic, ΔH_sub). Deposition: gas → solid (exothermic, ΔH_dep). ΔH_sub = ΔH_fus + ΔH_vap.
  • A heating curve shows temperature plateaus during phase changes where added heat is used to overcome forces, not to raise temperature.
  • Physical properties such as boiling point and melting point can be used to identify substances.

Unit 5: Chemical Equilibrium – Complete Exam Revision Notes

5.1 Introduction

  • In Unit 4 we studied reaction rates. Some reactions go to completion, but many do not.
  • Chemical equilibrium answers the questions: Do all reactions go to completion? How much reactant remains? Why does a reaction reach equilibrium?
  • In this unit, we learn about reversible reactions, dynamic equilibrium, the equilibrium constant, factors affecting equilibrium, and industrial applications.

5.2 Chemical Equilibrium

5.2.1 Reversible and Irreversible Reactions

Definitions

  • A reaction that proceeds to completion, where almost all reactants are converted to products, is called an irreversible reaction (one‑way reaction). It is represented with a single arrow (→).
    Example: C(s) + O₂(g) → CO₂(g)
  • A reaction that does not go to completion and proceeds in both the forward and reverse directions is called a reversible reaction (two‑way reaction). It is represented with a double arrow (⇌).
    General form: aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD
    Forward reaction: aA + bB → cC + dD
    Reverse reaction: cC + dD → aA + bB
    Example: N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2NH₃(g)

5.2.2 Chemical Equilibrium and Its Characteristics

How equilibrium is established

  • In a reversible reaction, the forward reaction rate (r_f) is initially fast because reactant concentrations are high.
  • As products form, the reverse reaction rate (r_r) increases.
  • Eventually, the rate of the forward reaction becomes equal to the rate of the reverse reaction. This state is called chemical equilibrium.
  • At equilibrium: rf=rrr_f = r_rrf​=rr​

Characteristics of Chemical Equilibrium

  • The reaction is reversible.
  • There is no net change in the concentrations of reactants and products – macroscopic properties remain constant.
  • The rates of the forward and reverse reactions are equal (dynamic balance).
  • The system must be in a closed vessel; no matter enters or leaves.
  • Equilibrium is dynamic – reactants and products are continually interconverting at the molecular level.
  • The concentrations of reactants and products at equilibrium are generally not equal; only the rates are equal.

  • Product concentrations in the numerator, reactant concentrations in the denominator.

  • For homogeneous reactions, all species appear in the expression.

5.2.4 Uses of the Equilibrium Constant

1. Predicting the Extent of Reaction

  • If KCK_CKC​ (or KPK_PKP​) is very large (>>1), the equilibrium mixture contains mainly products – the reaction goes nearly to completion (lies to the right).
  • If KCK_CKC​ is very small (<<1), the equilibrium mixture contains mainly reactants – the reaction hardly proceeds (lies to the left).
  • Rule of thumb: reaction essentially complete if K>1010K > 10^{10}K>1010, and does not occur if K<10−10K < 10^{-10}K<10−10.

2. Predicting the Direction of Reaction – Reaction Quotient (Q)

  • The reaction quotient (Q_C or Q_P) has the same form as the equilibrium constant but uses concentrations (or partial pressures) at any point, not necessarily at equilibrium.
  • By comparing Q with K:
    • If Q=KQ = KQ=K, the system is at equilibrium.
    • If Q<KQ < KQ<K, the reaction will proceed in the forward direction (to the right) to form more products until equilibrium is reached.
    • If Q>KQ > KQ>K, the reaction will proceed in the reverse direction (to the left) to reduce products and increase reactants.

3. Calculating Equilibrium Concentrations

Steps to calculate equilibrium concentrations from initial amounts:

  1. Write the balanced equation and the KCK_CKC​ expression.
  2. List the initial concentrations (or amounts) of all species.
  3. If products are present initially, calculate QQQ to determine the direction of the net reaction.
  4. Define the change in concentration (x) needed to reach equilibrium. Express the equilibrium concentrations in terms of x, using the stoichiometric coefficients.
  5. Substitute the equilibrium concentrations into the KCK_CKC​ expression and solve for x. If a quadratic equation results, use the quadratic formula; discard any root that gives a negative concentration.
  6. Use x to find all equilibrium concentrations, and check by substituting back into the KCK_CKC​ expression.

5.2.5 Factors Affecting Chemical Equilibrium – Le Châtelier’s Principle

  • Le Châtelier’s principle states: If a change (stress) is imposed on a system at equilibrium, the position of equilibrium will shift in a direction that tends to reduce (partially counteract) that change.

1. Change in Concentration

  • Adding more of a reactant → equilibrium shifts to the right (forward direction) to consume the added substance.
  • Removing a product → equilibrium shifts to the right to produce more product.
  • Adding more of a product → equilibrium shifts to the left (reverse direction).
  • Removing a reactant → equilibrium shifts to the left.
  • Note: Adding or removing a reactant or product changes the value of Q but does not change the value of K (as long as temperature remains constant).

2. Change in Pressure (by changing volume)

  • Only affects systems involving gases and where the total number of gaseous moles differs between reactants and products (Δn ≠ 0).
  • An increase in pressure (decrease in volume) shifts the equilibrium towards the side with fewer moles of gas.
  • A decrease in pressure (increase in volume) shifts the equilibrium towards the side with more moles of gas.
  • If Δn = 0, pressure changes have no effect on the equilibrium position.
  • Adding an inert gas at constant volume does not change partial pressures of reactants/products, so it has no effect on equilibrium.

3. Change in Temperature

  • Temperature is the only factor that changes the value of the equilibrium constant K.
  • If a reaction is exothermic (ΔH < 0, heat is a product):
    • Increasing temperature adds heat → equilibrium shifts to the left (reverse, endothermic direction). K decreases.
    • Decreasing temperature removes heat → equilibrium shifts to the right (forward, exothermic direction). K increases.
  • If a reaction is endothermic (ΔH > 0, heat is a reactant):
    • Increasing temperature adds heat → equilibrium shifts to the right (forward direction). K increases.
    • Decreasing temperature removes heat → equilibrium shifts to the left. K decreases.

4. Effect of a Catalyst

  • A catalyst speeds up both the forward and reverse reactions equally.
  • It does not change the position of equilibrium or the value of the equilibrium constant.
  • It only helps the system reach equilibrium faster.

5.2.6 Industrial Applications of Equilibrium Principles

The Haber Process (Production of Ammonia, NH₃)

  • Reaction: N2(g)+3H2(g)⇌2NH3(g)ΔH=−92 kJ mol−1N_2(g) + 3H_2(g) \rightleftharpoons 2NH_3(g) \quad \Delta H = -92 \text{ kJ mol}^{-1}N2​(g)+3H2​(g)⇌2NH3​(g)ΔH=−92 kJ mol−1
  • According to Le Châtelier:
    • High pressure favours the forward reaction (4 moles gas → 2 moles). Typical pressure: 200–400 atm.
    • Low temperature favours higher yield (exothermic), but the rate is too slow. A compromise temperature of about 500 °C is used.
    • An iron catalyst is used to speed up the reaction.
  • Ammonia is continuously removed by liquefaction, driving the forward reaction. Unreacted N₂ and H₂ are recycled.

The Contact Process (Production of Sulfuric Acid, H₂SO₄)

  • Key equilibrium step: 2SO2(g)+O2(g)⇌2SO3(g)ΔH=−196 kJ2SO_2(g) + O_2(g) \rightleftharpoons 2SO_3(g) \quad \Delta H = -196 \text{ kJ}2SO2​(g)+O2​(g)⇌2SO3​(g)ΔH=−196 kJ
  • Conditions optimised using Le Châtelier:
    • Pressure: 3 moles of gas → 2 moles, so high pressure favours SO₃. Usually conducted near atmospheric pressure with efficient catalyst.
    • Temperature: Exothermic, low temperature favours SO₃, but rate too slow. Compromise temperature ≈ 450 °C.
    • Catalyst: Vanadium(V) oxide (V₂O₅) increases the rate.
  • Overall process steps:
    1. Burn sulphur in air to make SO₂.
    2. Catalytic oxidation of SO₂ to SO₃.
    3. Dissolve SO₃ in concentrated H₂SO₄ to form oleum (H₂S₂O₇) – not directly in water to avoid uncontrollable fog.
    4. Dilute oleum with water to obtain H₂SO₄ of desired concentration.

Unit 1 – Atomic Structure and Periodic Properties

Definitions (“… is called …”)

  • The tiny, indestructible particles proposed by Democritus are called atomos, meaning “indivisible”.
  • Atoms of the same element that have different numbers of neutrons and different mass numbers are called isotopes.
  • The weighted average mass of the naturally occurring isotopes of an element is called the atomic mass.
  • The spontaneous emission of particles and/or radiation from unstable nuclei is called radioactivity or radioactive decay.
  • A quantum (packet) of electromagnetic energy is called a photon.
  • The minimum energy required to remove an electron from a metal’s surface in the photoelectric effect is given by E0=hν0E_0 = h\nu_0E0​=hν0​, where ν0\nu_0ν0​ is called the threshold frequency.
  • The lowest energy state of an atom (n=1n=1n=1) is called the ground state.
  • A state higher in energy than the ground state is called an excited state.
  • A region of space around the nucleus where the probability of finding an electron is high is called an orbital.
  • The principle that electrons occupy the lowest available energy orbitals first is called the Aufbau principle.
  • The rule that no two electrons in an atom can have the same set of four quantum numbers is called the Pauli exclusion principle.
  • The rule that degenerate orbitals are singly occupied before pairing begins is called Hund’s rule.
  • The distribution of electrons among atomic orbitals is called the electron configuration.
  • Electrons in the outermost principal quantum level are called valence electrons.
  • Inner electrons are called core electrons.
  • The net positive charge experienced by an electron after accounting for shielding by inner electrons is called the effective nuclear charge (Zeff).
  • The energy required to remove one mole of electrons from one mole of gaseous atoms is called the ionization energy (IE).
  • The energy change that occurs when an electron is added to a gaseous atom is called the electron affinity (EA).
  • A measure of the tendency of an atom in a molecule to attract bonding electrons is called electronegativity.
  • The chemical properties associated with the ability to lose electrons are called metallic character.

Main Points

  • Democritus proposed the idea of atomos (indivisible particles).
  • Dalton’s atomic theory included five postulates and was based on the laws of conservation of mass, definite proportions and multiple proportions.
  • Modern atomic theory differs from Dalton’s: atoms are divisible, isotopes exist, and nuclear reactions can change elements.
  • J.J. Thomson discovered the electron using cathode rays.
  • Millikan measured the electron’s charge, allowing the electron’s mass to be calculated.
  • Radioactive emissions are alpha (helium nuclei), beta (electrons), and gamma (high-energy rays).
  • Rutherford’s gold foil experiment showed the atom has a tiny, dense, positively charged nucleus.
  • Chadwick discovered the neutron.
  • The nucleus contains protons (positive) and neutrons (neutral). Atomic number = number of protons. Mass number = protons + neutrons.
  • Isotopes of an element have the same Z but different A.
  • Electromagnetic radiation is characterised by wavelength λ, frequency ν, and speed c (c = λν).
  • Planck proposed that energy is quantized: E=hνE = h\nuE=hν.
  • Einstein used quantum theory to explain the photoelectric effect: KE=hν−hν0KE = h\nu - h\nu_0KE=hν−hν0​.
  • De Broglie proposed that matter has wave properties: λ=h/mv\lambda = h/mvλ=h/mv.
  • Bohr’s model of the hydrogen atom: quantized orbits, En=−RH/n2E_n = -R_H/n^2En​=−RH​/n2, explains the line spectrum.
  • Energy is emitted or absorbed when an electron moves between allowed levels.
  • Spectral series: Lyman (UV), Balmer (visible), Paschen (IR), Brackett (IR).
  • Heisenberg uncertainty principle: it is impossible to know both the exact position and momentum of an electron.
  • Schrödinger’s wave equation gives wave functions. Ψ² gives the probability density.
  • Orbitals are described by four quantum numbers: n, ℓ, mℓ, ms.
  • Orbital shapes: s (spherical), p (dumbbell), d (cloverleaf), f (complex).
  • Electron configurations follow the Aufbau order, with exceptions for Cr and Cu due to extra stability of half-filled and filled d subshells.
  • The periodic law: properties are periodic functions of atomic number.
  • Periodic trends: atomic radius decreases across a period and increases down a group. Ionization energy and electronegativity increase across a period and decrease down a group. Metallic character follows opposite trends.

Processes and Steps

Calculating Average Atomic Mass

  1. Multiply the mass of each isotope by its fractional abundance.
  2. Add the products together.

Writing the Electron Configuration of an Atom

  1. Find the atomic number (Z) to know the total number of electrons.
  2. Fill the orbitals in Aufbau order: 1s, 2s, 2p, 3s, 3p, 4s, 3d, 4p, 5s, 4d, 5p, 6s, 4f, …
  3. Place a maximum of two electrons per orbital with opposite spins (Pauli).
  4. When filling degenerate orbitals (p, d, f), put one electron in each before pairing (Hund).
  5. Note exceptions: Cr is [Ar] 4s¹ 3d⁵, Cu is [Ar] 4s¹ 3d¹⁰.

Determining Allowed Quantum Numbers for a Given n

  1. ℓ can be 0, 1, 2, …, n‑1.
  2. For each ℓ, mℓ runs from –ℓ to +ℓ in integer steps.
  3. For each orbital, ms can be +½ or –½.

Calculating Energy and Wavelength of an Electron Transition in Hydrogen

  1. Identify initial (nin_ini​) and final (nfn_fnf​) levels.
  2. Compute the energy change: ΔE=RH(1ni2−1nf2)\Delta E = R_H\left(\frac{1}{n_i^2} - \frac{1}{n_f^2}\right)ΔE=RH​(ni2​1​−nf2​1​) where RH=2.18×10−18 JR_H = 2.18 \times 10^{-18} \, \text{J}RH​=2.18×10−18J.
  3. Use λ=hcΔE\lambda = \frac{hc}{\Delta E}λ=ΔEhc​ or ν=ΔEh\nu = \frac{\Delta E}{h}ν=hΔE​ to find wavelength or frequency.

Unit 2 – Chemical Bonding

Definitions

  • The attractive force that holds atoms, ions or molecules together is called a chemical bond.
  • The electrostatic attraction between positive and negative ions is called an ionic bond (or electrovalent bond).
  • The enthalpy change that occurs when one mole of an ionic solid separates into its gaseous ions is called the lattice energy (U).
  • A bond formed when a pair of electrons is shared between two atoms is called a covalent bond.
  • A covalent bond in which one atom donates both electrons is called a coordinate covalent bond (or dative bond).
  • Structures that have the same atom positions but different arrangements of bonding and lone electron pairs are called resonance structures. The true structure is called the resonance hybrid.
  • A bond in which electrons are shared equally is called a non‑polar covalent bond.
  • A bond in which electrons are shared unequally is called a polar covalent bond.
  • The measure of the polarity of a bond or molecule is called the dipole moment.
  • Electron pairs in the valence shell that stay on one atom and are not shared are called non‑bonding pairs or lone pairs.
  • The weak attractive forces that hold particles (molecules) together are called intermolecular forces. Forces within a particle are called intramolecular forces.
  • A particularly strong dipole–dipole attraction involving H bonded to N, O or F is called a hydrogen bond.
  • Weak attractive forces arising from temporary, instantaneous dipoles are called London forces (dispersion forces).
  • The mixing of atomic orbitals on the same atom to form new orbitals of equal energy is called hybridization. The new orbitals are called hybrid orbitals.
  • A bond formed by head‑on (end‑to‑end) overlap is called a sigma (σ) bond.
  • A bond formed by sideways (lateral) overlap of p orbitals is called a pi (π) bond.
  • A crystalline solid with a well‑defined, ordered three‑dimensional structure is called a crystalline solid. An unordered solid is called an amorphous solid.

Main Points

  • Atoms bond to achieve a stable octet (or duplet for hydrogen).
  • Ionic bonds involve electron transfer and are formed between metals and non‑metals.
  • The Born‑Haber cycle uses Hess’s law to calculate lattice energy.
  • Lattice energy depends on ionic charge and size: larger charges, smaller ions → larger U.
  • Lewis symbols show valence electrons as dots. Lewis structures show bonds as shared electron pairs.
  • Covalent bonds can be single, double or triple. Double bond = one σ + one π. Triple bond = one σ + two π.
  • The VSEPR theory predicts molecular shape by minimising electron‑pair repulsions.
  • Molecular polarity depends on bond polarity and shape. Symmetrical molecules may be non‑polar even with polar bonds.
  • Intermolecular forces: London forces (all molecules), dipole–dipole (polar molecules), hydrogen bonding (H on N, O, F).
  • Metallic bonding is described by the electron‑sea model: cations immersed in delocalised electrons.
  • Valence bond theory explains covalent bonds as orbital overlap.
  • Hybridization (sp, sp², sp³, sp³d, sp³d²) explains molecular shapes and bond angles.
  • Molecular orbital theory combines atomic orbitals into bonding and antibonding molecular orbitals. Bond order = ½(bonding e⁻ – antibonding e⁻). O₂ is paramagnetic (MO theory explains this).
  • Crystalline solids are classified as ionic, molecular, covalent network or metallic.

Processes and Steps

Steps for Writing a Lewis Structure (Covalent)

  1. Determine the total number of valence electrons. For an anion, add one per negative charge. For a cation, subtract one per positive charge.
  2. Write the skeletal structure. The least electronegative atom (except hydrogen) is usually central.
  3. Connect atoms with single bonds and distribute remaining electrons as lone pairs on terminal atoms first to complete octets.
  4. If the central atom lacks an octet, move lone pair(s) from a terminal atom into the bonding region to form multiple bonds.

Steps for the Born‑Haber Cycle (Finding Lattice Energy)

  1. Write the direct formation reaction from elements: ΔH°_f is known.
  2. Break the process into steps: atomisation of the metal, atomisation of the non‑metal (e.g., ½ bond energy), ionisation of the metal, electron gain by the non‑metal, formation of the solid from gaseous ions (lattice energy, U).
  3. Apply Hess’s law: ΔH°_f = sum of all step enthalpies.
  4. Solve for U.

Steps for Applying the VSEPR Model

  1. Draw the Lewis structure of the molecule or ion.
  2. Count the electron groups (bonding pairs + lone pairs) around the central atom. Multiple bonds count as one group.
  3. Determine the electron‑pair arrangement that minimises repulsions (linear, trigonal planar, tetrahedral, etc.).
  4. Determine the molecular shape by considering the positions of atoms only (lone pairs influence shape but are not part of the name).
  5. Predict bond angle deviations: lone pairs compress bond angles.

Steps for Determining a Hybridization Scheme

  1. Draw a valid Lewis structure.
  2. Use VSEPR to find the electron‑pair arrangement around the central atom.
  3. Match the arrangement to the hybridization: 2 groups → sp, 3 → sp², 4 → sp³, 5 → sp³d, 6 → sp³d².
  4. Describe which atomic orbitals mix and the overlaps that form bonds.

Unit 3 – Physical States of Matter

Definitions

  • Anything that occupies space and has mass is called matter.
  • The four physical states of matter are solid, liquid, gas and plasma.
  • A gaseous mixture of positive ions and electrons, existing at extremely high temperatures, is called plasma.
  • The theory that explains the properties of matter in terms of particle motion and energy is called the kinetic theory of matter.
  • A gas that obeys the gas laws exactly under all conditions is called an ideal gas.
  • The constant R in the ideal gas equation is called the gas constant.
  • The volume occupied by one mole of a gas at STP (22.4 L) is called the standard molar volume.
  • The spreading of gas particles throughout a container is called diffusion.
  • The process by which molecules escape from the surface of a liquid into the gas phase is called evaporation (or vaporization).
  • The process by which a vapour returns to the liquid state is called condensation.
  • The partial pressure exerted by a vapour in dynamic equilibrium with its liquid at a given temperature is called the vapour pressure.
  • The temperature at which the vapour pressure of a liquid equals the external atmospheric pressure is called the boiling point.
  • The change of a liquid to bubbles of vapour that appear throughout the liquid is called boiling.
  • The amount of heat needed to convert one mole of a liquid at its boiling point to a gas is called the molar heat of vaporization (ΔH_vap).
  • The heat released when one mole of vapour condenses at the condensation point is called the molar heat of condensation (ΔH_cond).
  • The change of a solid to a liquid is called melting (or fusion). The temperature at which this occurs is called the melting point.
  • The change of a liquid to a solid is called freezing (or solidification). The temperature at which this occurs is called the freezing point.
  • The quantity of heat needed to convert one mole of a solid at its melting point to a liquid is called the molar heat of fusion (ΔH_fus).
  • The direct change of a solid to a vapour without passing through the liquid state is called sublimation. The reverse process is called deposition.

Main Points

  • Solid: fixed shape and volume. Liquid: fixed volume, no fixed shape. Gas: neither fixed shape nor volume.
  • Kinetic theory: particles in constant motion; temperature measures average kinetic energy.
  • Gas laws describe relationships among pressure (P), volume (V), temperature (T) and amount (n). All temperatures must be in Kelvin.
  • Boyle’s law: P1V1=P2V2P_1V_1 = P_2V_2P1​V1​=P2​V2​ (constant T, n).
  • Charles’ law: V1/T1=V2/T2V_1/T_1 = V_2/T_2V1​/T1​=V2​/T2​ (constant P, n).
  • Gay‑Lussac’s law: P1/T1=P2/T2P_1/T_1 = P_2/T_2P1​/T1​=P2​/T2​ (constant V, n).
  • Combined gas law: P1V1/T1=P2V2/T2P_1V_1/T_1 = P_2V_2/T_2P1​V1​/T1​=P2​V2​/T2​.
  • Avogadro’s law: V∝nV \propto nV∝n (constant T, P). Molar volume at STP = 22.4 L/mol.
  • Ideal gas equation: PV=nRTPV = nRTPV=nRT. R = 0.0821 L·atm/mol·K.
  • Graham’s law of diffusion: rate ∝ 1/√M. Lighter gases diffuse faster. r1/r2=M2/M1r_1/r_2 = \sqrt{M_2/M_1}r1​/r2​=M2​/M1​​.
  • Evaporation occurs at the liquid surface. In a closed container, evaporation and condensation reach dynamic equilibrium, establishing a vapour pressure.
  • Vapour pressure increases with temperature. Boiling occurs when vapour pressure equals external pressure.
  • Phase changes involve energy: melting, vaporization, sublimation are endothermic. Freezing, condensation, deposition are exothermic.
  • Heating curve: flat regions during phase changes (temperature constant) where intermolecular forces are overcome.

Processes and Steps

Solving Combined Gas Law Problems

  1. List initial and final conditions: P₁, V₁, T₁ and P₂, V₂, T₂.
  2. Convert all temperatures to Kelvin (K = °C + 273).
  3. Use the equation P1V1T1=P2V2T2\frac{P_1 V_1}{T_1} = \frac{P_2 V_2}{T_2}T1​P1​V1​​=T2​P2​V2​​.
  4. Solve for the unknown variable.

Using Graham’s Law to Compare Diffusion Rates

  1. Write the relationship: r1r2=M2M1\frac{r_1}{r_2} = \sqrt{\frac{M_2}{M_1}}r2​r1​​=M1​M2​​​.
  2. If rates are given in terms of time, use r∝1/tr \propto 1/tr∝1/t, so t1t2=M1M2\frac{t_1}{t_2} = \sqrt{\frac{M_1}{M_2}}t2​t1​​=M2​M1​​​.
  3. Substitute known molecular masses and solve.

Interpreting a Heating Curve

  1. Slanted regions: temperature increases as heat is added. One phase is present.
  2. Horizontal plateau regions: temperature remains constant while a phase change occurs. Energy is used to break intermolecular forces, not to increase kinetic energy.

Unit 4 – Chemical Kinetics

Definitions

  • The area of chemistry concerned with reaction rates and the steps by which reactions occur is called chemical kinetics.
  • The change in concentration of a reactant or product per unit time is called the rate of reaction.
  • The theory that reactions occur when particles collide with proper orientation and sufficient energy is called collision theory.
  • Collisions that result in the formation of products are called effective collisions. Collisions that do not are called ineffective collisions.
  • The minimum energy required for a collision to lead to a reaction is called the activation energy (Eₐ).
  • A substance that increases the rate of a reaction without being consumed is called a catalyst.
  • A catalyst that slows a reaction by raising Eₐ is called a negative catalyst or inhibitor.
  • A catalyst in the same phase as the reactants is called a homogeneous catalyst; one in a different phase is called a heterogeneous catalyst.
  • Biological catalysts are called enzymes.

Main Points

  • Reaction rates are expressed as −1aΔ[A]Δt=1cΔ[C]Δt-\frac{1}{a}\frac{\Delta[A]}{\Delta t} = \frac{1}{c}\frac{\Delta[C]}{\Delta t}−a1​ΔtΔ[A]​=c1​ΔtΔ[C]​.
  • Average rate is calculated over a time interval. Instantaneous rate is the slope of the tangent to the concentration–time curve. Initial rate is at t = 0.
  • For a reaction to occur, particles must collide with proper orientation and energy ≥ Eₐ.
  • Five factors affect the rate of reaction:
    1. Nature of reactants – intrinsically different reactivities.
    2. Surface area – greater area (smaller particles) increases rate.
    3. Concentration (or pressure for gases) – higher concentration increases collision frequency.
    4. Temperature – higher temperature gives more particles with E ≥ Eₐ; rates approximately double per 10 °C rise.
    5. Catalyst – provides a lower‑Eₐ pathway; not consumed.

Processes and Steps

Writing the Rate Expression for a Reaction

  1. For a general reaction aA + bB → cC + dD, write:
    Rate = −1aΔ[A]Δt=−1bΔ[B]Δt=1cΔ[C]Δt=1dΔ[D]Δt-\frac{1}{a}\frac{\Delta[A]}{\Delta t} = -\frac{1}{b}\frac{\Delta[B]}{\Delta t} = \frac{1}{c}\frac{\Delta[C]}{\Delta t} = \frac{1}{d}\frac{\Delta[D]}{\Delta t}−a1​ΔtΔ[A]​=−b1​ΔtΔ[B]​=c1​ΔtΔ[C]​=d1​ΔtΔ[D]​.
  2. Insert the appropriate stoichiometric coefficients.

Calculating the Average Rate from Concentration Data

  1. Choose a time interval Δt=t2−t1\Delta t = t_2 - t_1Δt=t2​−t1​.
  2. Determine the change in concentration Δ[X]=[X]t2−[X]t1\Delta[X] = [X]_{t_2} - [X]_{t_1}Δ[X]=[X]t2​​−[X]t1​​.
  3. Divide the change by Δt\Delta tΔt. Add a minus sign for reactants.

Determining Instantaneous Rate from a Graph

  1. Plot concentration versus time.
  2. At the time of interest, draw a tangent line to the curve.
  3. Calculate the slope of the tangent. For a reactant, the instantaneous rate = –(slope).

Unit 5

PROCESSES AND STEPS

How to Write the Equilibrium Constant Expression (K_C)

  1. Write the balanced chemical equation for the reversible reaction.
  2. Place the equilibrium concentrations of the products in the numerator, each raised to the power of its stoichiometric coefficient.
  3. Place the equilibrium concentrations of the reactants in the denominator, each raised to the power of its stoichiometric coefficient.
  4. Omit pure solids and pure liquids from the expression (only gaseous and aqueous species appear).

How to Write K_P for Gaseous Reactions

  1. Use the partial pressures of gaseous products and reactants instead of molar concentrations.
  2. The expression has the same form as K_C: product pressures in the numerator, reactant pressures in the denominator, raised to their coefficients.

How to Relate K_P and K_C

  1. Use the equation KP=KC(RT)ΔnK_P = K_C(RT)^{\Delta n}KP​=KC​(RT)Δn
  2. Δn\Delta nΔn = (total moles of gaseous products) – (total moles of gaseous reactants).
  3. If Δn=0\Delta n = 0Δn=0, then KP=KCK_P = K_CKP​=KC​.

How to Predict the Direction of a Reaction Using the Reaction Quotient (Q)

  1. Write the expression for Q in exactly the same form as K, but using the given concentrations (or pressures) at the moment of interest (not necessarily at equilibrium).
  2. Calculate the numerical value of Q.
  3. Compare Q to K:
    • If Q = K, the system is at equilibrium.
    • If Q < K, the reaction proceeds to the right (forward direction) to form more products.
    • If Q > K, the reaction proceeds to the left (reverse direction) to form more reactants.

Steps to Calculate Equilibrium Concentrations from Initial Amounts (ICE Table)

  1. Write the balanced equation and the K_C (or K_P) expression.
  2. List the initial concentrations of all species.
  3. If any product is present initially, calculate Q to decide the direction of the net reaction.
  4. Define the change in concentration as ‘x’ (multiplied by the stoichiometric coefficient). Express all equilibrium concentrations in terms of x.
  5. Substitute these expressions into the equilibrium constant expression and solve for x (if a quadratic equation results, use the quadratic formula).
  6. Use the value of x to find all equilibrium concentrations.
  7. Check by substituting back into the equilibrium expression.

Applying Le Châtelier’s Principle

  • Concentration change:
    • Adding a reactant → equilibrium shifts to the right (forward direction).
    • Adding a product → equilibrium shifts to the left (reverse direction).
    • Removing a reactant → equilibrium shifts to the left.
    • Removing a product → equilibrium shifts to the right.
  • Pressure change (by changing volume):
    • Increase in pressure (decrease in volume) → equilibrium shifts toward the side with fewer moles of gas.
    • Decrease in pressure (increase in volume) → equilibrium shifts toward the side with more moles of gas.
    • If the number of gaseous moles is equal on both sides, pressure change has no effect.
  • Temperature change:
    • Increase in temperature → equilibrium shifts in the endothermic direction.
    • Decrease in temperature → equilibrium shifts in the exothermic direction.
    • Temperature change alters the value of the equilibrium constant.
  • Catalyst:
    • Adding a catalyst does not shift the equilibrium. It only speeds up the rate at which equilibrium is reached by lowering the activation energy for both forward and reverse reactions equally.

Industrial Processes

  • Haber Process for NH₃ production:
    1. Mix nitrogen (from air) and hydrogen (from natural gas/steam) in a 1:3 ratio.
    2. Compress the gases to high pressure (200–400 atm) and heat to about 500 °C.
    3. Pass over an iron catalyst.
    4. Cool the gas mixture to liquefy ammonia, which is removed.
    5. Unreacted N₂ and H₂ are recycled to the reactor.
  • Contact Process for H₂SO₄ production:
    1. Burn sulphur in excess dry air to produce SO₂.
    2. Purify the SO₂–air mixture to remove catalyst poisons.
    3. Oxidise SO₂ to SO₃ at about 450 °C and low pressure using vanadium(V) oxide (V₂O₅) catalyst.
    4. Dissolve the SO₃ in concentrated H₂SO₄ to form oleum (H₂S₂O₇) – this avoids an uncontrollable mist.
    5. Dilute the oleum with a calculated amount of water to obtain sulphuric acid of the desired concentration.

DEFINITIONS (“... is called …”)

  • A reaction that proceeds to completion, with reactants almost entirely converted to products, is called an irreversible reaction.
  • A reaction that does not go to completion and proceeds in both forward and reverse directions is called a reversible reaction.
  • The state at which the rates of the forward and reverse reactions are equal, so there is no net change in the amounts of reactants and products, is called chemical equilibrium.
  • The equilibrium that is dynamic in nature, meaning reactions continue at the molecular level while macroscopic properties stay constant, is called dynamic equilibrium.
  • The rule stating that the rate at which A and B combine is directly proportional to the product of their concentration terms each raised to the power of its coefficient in the balanced equation is called the law of mass action.
  • The constant obtained from the ratio of rate constants kf/krk_f/k_rkf​/kr​, equal to the ratio of product concentrations to reactant concentrations raised to their stoichiometric powers at equilibrium, is called the equilibrium constant.
  • The equilibrium constant expressed in terms of molar concentrations is called K_C.
  • The equilibrium constant expressed in terms of partial pressures is called K_P.
  • The ratio of concentrations of products to reactants raised to their stoichiometric coefficients at any stage before equilibrium is called the reaction quotient (Q).
  • The principle stating that if a change is imposed on a system at equilibrium, the position of the equilibrium will shift in a direction that tends to reduce that change is called Le Châtelier’s principle.
  • The industrial process for synthesizing ammonia from nitrogen and hydrogen is called the Haber process.
  • The industrial process for producing sulphuric acid by the catalytic oxidation of SO₂ to SO₃ is called the Contact process.

MAIN POINTS

  • Reversible vs Irreversible Reactions:

    • Irreversible reactions go to completion (single arrow →). Example: C + O₂ → CO₂.
    • Reversible reactions do not go to completion; they establish a dynamic equilibrium (double arrow ⇌). Example: N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃.

  • Characteristics of Equilibrium:

    • The forward and reverse reaction rates are equal.
    • Macroscopic properties (concentration, pressure, colour) remain constant.
    • Equilibrium can only be maintained in a closed system.
    • It is dynamic – molecular processes continue.

  • Equilibrium Constant Expression:

    • For a general reaction aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD:
      KC=[C]c[D]d[A]a[B]bK_C = \frac{[C]^c [D]^d}{[A]^a [B]^b}KC​=[A]a[B]b[C]c[D]d​
    • Pure solids and pure liquids are omitted from the expression.
    • For gases, KP=PCcPDdPAaPBbK_P = \frac{P_C^c P_D^d}{P_A^a P_B^b}KP​=PAa​PBb​PCc​PDd​​.

  • Relationship between K_P and K_C:

    • KP=KC(RT)ΔnK_P = K_C (RT)^{\Delta n}KP​=KC​(RT)Δn where Δn\Delta nΔn accounts only for gaseous species.

  • What K tells us:

    • Very large K (>>1): equilibrium lies far to the right (products favoured).
    • Very small K (<<1): equilibrium lies far to the left (reactants favoured).
    • K ≈ 1: significant amounts of both reactants and products present.

  • Reaction Quotient Q:

    • Used to predict the direction a reaction must shift to reach equilibrium.
    • Q < K → proceeds forward. Q > K → proceeds in reverse. Q = K → at equilibrium.

  • Calculating Equilibrium Concentrations:

    • Use ICE (Initial, Change, Equilibrium) table method to express equilibrium concentrations in terms of a variable x, substitute into K expression, and solve.

  • Le Châtelier’s Principle – Effect of Changes:

    • Concentration: Adding a reactant shifts equilibrium right; adding a product shifts left.
    • Pressure (volume): Increased pressure (smaller volume) favours the side with fewer moles of gas.
    • Temperature: Increase favours endothermic direction (changes K). For exothermic reactions, K decreases with increasing temperature; for endothermic, K increases.
    • Catalyst: Does not change K or equilibrium position; only helps equilibrium to be reached faster.

  • Industrial Applications:

    • Haber process: High pressure (200-400 atm), moderate temperature (~500 °C), iron catalyst, product removal by liquefaction.
    • Contact process: Moderate temperature (~450 °C), V₂O₅ catalyst, SO₃ absorbed in concentrated H₂SO₄ to form oleum, then diluted to make H₂SO₄.

Unit 6 – Oxygenated Organic Compounds

Definitions

  • An organic compound containing the hydroxyl group (–OH) attached to a saturated carbon is called an alcohol.
  • Alcohols containing one –OH group are called monohydric alcohols.
  • Alcohols containing two –OH groups are called dihydric alcohols (glycols/diols).
  • Alcohols containing three –OH groups are called trihydric alcohols.
  • An alcohol in which the –OH carbon is attached to one alkyl group is called a primary (1°) alcohol.
  • An alcohol in which the –OH carbon is attached to two alkyl groups is called a secondary (2°) alcohol.
  • An alcohol in which the –OH carbon is attached to three alkyl groups is called a tertiary (3°) alcohol.
  • Compounds in which an oxygen atom is bonded to two alkyl groups (R–O–R′) are called ethers.
  • The slow decomposition of carbohydrates by enzymes to form ethanol and carbon dioxide is called fermentation.
  • The functional group consisting of a carbonyl group bonded to at least one hydrogen (R–CHO) is called an aldehyde.
  • The functional group consisting of a carbonyl group bonded to two carbon atoms (R–CO–R′) is called a ketone.
  • The functional group consisting of a carbonyl group with a hydroxyl group on the same carbon (–COOH) is called a carboxyl group.
  • Compounds containing the carboxyl group are called carboxylic acids.
  • Carboxylic acids with two –COOH groups are called dicarboxylic acids; those with three are called tricarboxylic acids.
  • Long‑chain carboxylic acids obtained from natural fats and oils are called fatty acids.
  • Derivatives of carboxylic acids in which the –OH is replaced by an –OR group (RCOOR′) are called esters.
  • The acid‑catalysed reaction between a carboxylic acid and an alcohol to form an ester and water is called esterification.
  • The base‑catalysed hydrolysis of an ester to form a carboxylate salt and an alcohol is called saponification.
  • Triesters of glycerol with fatty acids are called triglycerides (or triacylglycerols).
  • A triglyceride that is solid at room temperature is called a fat.
  • A triglyceride that is liquid at room temperature is called an oil.
  • The process of converting liquid oils into solid fats by catalytic addition of hydrogen is called hardening of oils (hydrogenation).
  • The deterioration of fats and oils producing an unpleasant odour is called rancidity.

Main Points

Alcohols

  • Functional group: –OH. General formula for monohydric: CₙH₂ₙ₊₁OH. IUPAC names end in “‑ol”.
  • Hydrogen bonding → high boiling points and water solubility for smaller alcohols.
  • Preparation: hydration of alkenes, hydrolysis of alkyl halides/esters, fermentation (for ethanol).
  • Reactions: active metals → H₂; oxidation (1° → aldehyde → acid, 2° → ketone, 3° resistant); dehydration → alkenes; esterification.

Ethers

  • R–O–R′. Named as alkoxyalkanes (IUPAC) or alkyl alkyl ether (common).
  • No hydrogen bonding between ether molecules → low boiling points.
  • Prepared by Williamson synthesis or dehydration of 1° alcohols.
  • Chemically inert; used as solvents.

Aldehydes and Ketones

  • Both contain the carbonyl group C=O. Aldehydes: at least one H on carbonyl carbon. Ketones: carbonyl bonded to two carbons.
  • IUPAC: aldehydes “‑al”, ketones “‑one”.
  • Polar C=O bond → moderate boiling points, small ones water‑soluble.
  • Aldehydes oxidise to acids (positive Tollens/Fehling tests). Ketones resist mild oxidation.
  • Reduction: aldehydes → 1° alcohols, ketones → 2° alcohols.

Carboxylic Acids

  • –COOH functional group. IUPAC: “‑oic acid”.
  • Strong hydrogen bonding (dimers) → very high boiling points. Small ones miscible with water.
  • Weak acids: react with metals, bases, carbonates (CO₂ test).
  • Esterification with alcohols.
  • Prepared by oxidation of primary alcohols/aldehydes or oxidation of alkylbenzenes.
  • Fatty acids are long‑chain carboxylic acids. Saturated: higher melting points. Unsaturated (cis double bonds): lower melting points.

Esters

  • RCOOR′. Named as alkyl alkanoate (IUPAC) or alkyl acid‑ate (common).
  • No H‑bonding between themselves → moderate boiling points. Pleasant fruity odours.
  • Hydrolysed by acid (reversible) or base (irreversible, saponification).
  • Prepared by Fischer esterification (acid + alcohol, H⁺ catalyst).

Fats and Oils

  • Triglycerides = glycerol + three fatty acid chains.
  • Fats: saturated, solid at room temperature. Oils: unsaturated (cis double bonds), liquid.
  • Hydrogenation hardens oils into solid fats.
  • Saponification yields glycerol and soaps (fatty acid salts).
  • Rancidity occurs by hydrolysis and oxidation.

Processes and Steps

Naming an Alcohol (IUPAC)

  1. Find the longest continuous carbon chain that contains the –OH group.
  2. Name it by replacing the “‑e” of the corresponding alkane with “‑ol”.
  3. Number the chain from the end nearest the –OH group.
  4. Indicate the position of the –OH by the number of the carbon it is on (if needed).
  5. Name and locate any other substituents.

Naming an Ether (IUPAC)

  1. Choose the larger alkyl group as the parent alkane.
  2. Name the smaller alkyl group together with the oxygen atom as an alkoxy substituent (e.g., methoxy, ethoxy).
  3. Number the parent chain to give the alkoxy group the lowest number.

Naming an Aldehyde (IUPAC)

  1. Find the longest chain containing the –CHO group.
  2. Replace the “‑e” of the corresponding alkane with “‑al”.
  3. The carbonyl carbon is always C‑1; no number is needed for the aldehyde group.
  4. Number the chain from the carbonyl carbon and name substituents.

Naming a Ketone (IUPAC)

  1. Find the longest chain containing the carbonyl group.
  2. Replace the “‑e” of the corresponding alkane with “‑one”.
  3. Number the chain from the end nearer the carbonyl group and give its position number before the “‑one” suffix.

Naming a Carboxylic Acid (IUPAC)

  1. Find the longest chain containing the –COOH group.
  2. Replace the “‑e” of the corresponding alkane with “‑oic acid”.
  3. The carboxyl carbon is C‑1. Number the chain and name substituents accordingly.

Naming an Ester

  1. Identify the alcohol part (R′–O–). Name the alkyl group as the first word (e.g., methyl, ethyl).
  2. Identify the acid part (R–CO–). Change the acid’s “‑oic acid” ending to “‑oate” (IUPAC) or “‑ic acid” to “‑ate” (common).
  3. Combine: alkyl + alkanoate (or acid‑ate).

Fischer Esterification Procedure

  1. Mix the carboxylic acid and the alcohol.
  2. Add a catalytic amount of concentrated sulphuric acid.
  3. Heat the mixture. The reaction forms the ester and water.
  4. The reaction is an equilibrium; removing water or using excess one reactant increases yield.

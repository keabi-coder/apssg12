UNIT 5 – CHEMICAL EQUILIBRIUM: Exam-Targeted Summary

1. Reversible Reactions and Dynamic Equilibrium

Key definitions:

  • Irreversible reaction: goes to completion (one‑way arrow →).
  • Reversible reaction: can go both forward and backward (double arrow ⇌).
  • Dynamic equilibrium: state where the rates of the forward and reverse reactions are equal, and the concentrations of reactants and products remain constant.
  • Not static – both reactions continue at the molecular level.
  • Equilibrium can only be established in a closed system.

Exam trap:
“At equilibrium, concentrations of reactants and products are equal.” → False; only rates are equal, concentrations usually are not.

2. Law of Mass Action and Equilibrium Constant Expressions

2.1 General Form

For the reaction:
aA+bB⇌cC+dDaA + bB \rightleftharpoons cC + dDaA+bB⇌cC+dD

  • Equilibrium constant in terms of concentration:
    Kc=[C]c[D]d[A]a[B]bK_c = \frac{[C]^c [D]^d}{[A]^a [B]^b}Kc​=[A]a[B]b[C]c[D]d​
  • The expression uses equilibrium concentrations only.

2.2 Rules for Writing K Expressions (Extremely Important)

  • Products in numerator, reactants in denominator.
  • Coefficients become exponents.
  • Pure solids (s) and pure liquids (l) are excluded – their concentrations are constant.
  • Gases and aqueous species are included.

Exam Q5 example:
2ICl(s)⇌I2(s)+Cl2(g)2ICl(s) \rightleftharpoons I_2(s) + Cl_2(g)2ICl(s)⇌I2​(s)+Cl2​(g)
→ Only the gas is included: Kc=[Cl2]K_c = [Cl_2]Kc​=[Cl2​].

Trap: Forgetting to omit solids/liquids leads to a wrong expression.

2.3 Kp for Gaseous Reactions

Kp=(PC)c(PD)d(PA)a(PB)bK_p = \frac{(P_C)^c (P_D)^d}{(P_A)^a (P_B)^b}Kp​=(PA​)a(PB​)b(PC​)c(PD​)d​

  • Only gaseous species appear; solids/liquids omitted.
  • Partial pressures in atm or other units.

Example: CaCO3(s)⇌CaO(s)+CO2(g)CaCO_3(s) \rightleftharpoons CaO(s) + CO_2(g)CaCO3​(s)⇌CaO(s)+CO2​(g) → Kp=PCO2K_p = P_{CO_2}Kp​=PCO2​​.

2.4 Units of Kc and Kp

  • Depend on Δn\Delta nΔn = (moles of gaseous products) – (moles of gaseous reactants) for Kp, or total moles in solution for Kc.
  • For Kc, unit = (molL−1)Δn(mol L^{-1})^{\Delta n}(molL−1)Δn.
  • For Kp, unit = (atm)Δn(atm)^{\Delta n}(atm)Δn.

Exam Q18 example:
N2(g)+3H2(g)⇌2NH3(g)N_2(g) + 3H_2(g) \rightleftharpoons 2NH_3(g)N2​(g)+3H2​(g)⇌2NH3​(g)
Δn=2−4=−2\Delta n = 2 - 4 = -2Δn=2−4=−2 → unit of Kc = (molL−1)−2=mol−2L2(mol L^{-1})^{-2} = mol^{-2} L^2(molL−1)−2=mol−2L2.

2.5 Relationship Between Kp and Kc

Kp=Kc(RT)ΔnK_p = K_c (RT)^{\Delta n}Kp​=Kc​(RT)Δn

  • R=0.0821 L atm mol−1K−1R = 0.0821 \, L \, atm \, mol^{-1} K^{-1}R=0.0821Latmmol−1K−1
  • If Δn=0\Delta n = 0Δn=0, then Kp=KcK_p = K_cKp​=Kc​.

Exam Q20 example:
Same Kp and Kc when Δn=0\Delta n = 0Δn=0: H2(g)+Cl2(g)⇌2HCl(g)H_2(g) + Cl_2(g) \rightleftharpoons 2HCl(g)H2​(g)+Cl2​(g)⇌2HCl(g) (2 moles gas each side).

2.6 Magnitude of K and Extent of Reaction

  • K >> 1 (large): equilibrium lies to the right, products favoured.
  • K << 1 (small): equilibrium lies to the left, reactants favoured.
  • K ≈ 1: significant amounts of both.

Exam Q4: If K=100 and [SO₂]=[SO₃], then K=[SO3]2/([SO2]2[O2])=1/[O2]=100K = [SO₃]^2 / ([SO₂]^2[O₂]) = 1/[O₂] = 100K=[SO3​]2/([SO2​]2[O2​])=1/[O2​]=100 → [O₂] = 0.01 M.

3. Reaction Quotient (Q) and Predicting Direction

Definition: Q has the same form as K, but uses concentrations (or pressures) at any point, not necessarily at equilibrium.

3.1 Comparison of Q and K

  • Q < K: Too much reactant; reaction proceeds forward (to the right).
  • Q > K: Too much product; reaction proceeds reverse (to the left).
  • Q = K: System is at equilibrium.

Exam Q9: “For a reaction to shift towards the reactant direction, which condition holds TRUE?”
→ Qc > Kc (too many products, shift left).

Exam Q4 type: Given initial concentrations, calculate Q, compare to K, and predict direction.

4. Le Chatelier’s Principle – Effect of Changes on Equilibrium

Statement: If a stress is applied to a system at equilibrium, the system will shift in the direction that reduces the stress.

4.1 Effect of Concentration Changes

  • Add reactant or remove product → shifts right (forward).
  • Add product or remove reactant → shifts left (reverse).
  • Kc remains unchanged (only temperature changes K).

Exam Q17: N2+O2⇌2NON_2 + O_2 \rightleftharpoons 2NON2​+O2​⇌2NO; production of NO favoured by high concentration of N₂ (add reactant).

4.2 Effect of Pressure/Volume Changes (Gaseous Systems)

  • Only affects reactions where number of gas moles changes (Δn≠0\Delta n \neq 0Δn=0).
  • Increase pressure (decrease volume): shift to side with fewer gas moles.
  • Decrease pressure (increase volume): shift to side with more gas moles.
  • If Δn=0\Delta n = 0Δn=0, pressure has no effect.

Exam Q7 example:
2SO2+O2⇌2SO32SO_2 + O_2 \rightleftharpoons 2SO_32SO2​+O2​⇌2SO3​ (Δn=−1\Delta n = -1Δn=−1). To increase SO₃, decrease volume (increase pressure) → shift right. (Only II in that question.)

Exam Q19: N2O4⇌2NO2N_2O_4 \rightleftharpoons 2NO_2N2​O4​⇌2NO2​; compress → shift to side with fewer moles → reactants favoured.

Exam Q21: Not affected by pressure → Δn=0\Delta n = 0Δn=0: N2+O2⇌2NON_2 + O_2 \rightleftharpoons 2NON2​+O2​⇌2NO.

4.3 Effect of Temperature Changes

  • Temperature changes the value of K.
  • Exothermic (ΔH<0\Delta H < 0ΔH<0, heat is product):
    • Increase T → shift left (reverse), K decreases.
    • Decrease T → shift right (forward), K increases.
  • Endothermic (ΔH>0\Delta H > 0ΔH>0, heat is reactant):
    • Increase T → shift right, K increases.
    • Decrease T → shift left, K decreases.

Exam Q22: “An increase in temperature for a reaction at equilibrium will favour…”
→ the endothermic direction.

Exam Q6 type: Simultaneous effects – need to consider both equilibrium and kinetics. (Exothermic: decreasing T favours products but slows rate; careful with wording.)

4.4 Effect of a Catalyst

  • Lowers activation energy for both directions equally.
  • Speeds up the rate at which equilibrium is reached.
  • Does NOT change the position of equilibrium or the value of K.

Exam Q7 (III): Adding a catalyst does NOT increase the amount of SO₃ → only II (decrease volume) is correct.

4.5 Adding an Inert Gas

  • At constant volume: no change in partial pressures → no effect on equilibrium.
  • At constant pressure: volume increases, partial pressures decrease → may shift depending on Δn\Delta nΔn.

5. Important Industrial Applications (Must Know Conditions)

5.1 Haber Process (Ammonia Synthesis)

N2(g)+3H2(g)⇌2NH3(g)N_2(g) + 3H_2(g) \rightleftharpoons 2NH_3(g)N2​(g)+3H2​(g)⇌2NH3​(g), ΔH=−92 kJ\Delta H = -92 \, kJΔH=−92kJ (exothermic)

Optimum conditions:

  • Pressure: high (~200–400 atm) because Δn=−2\Delta n = -2Δn=−2 (fewer product moles).
  • Temperature: compromise ~400–500 °C (low T favours yield but slow; high T gives faster rate).
  • Catalyst: finely divided iron (increases rate, does not affect yield).
  • Product removal: NH₃ is liquefied and removed, shifting equilibrium right.
  • Recycling: unreacted N₂/H₂ are fed back.

Exam Q8: Hydrogen production step: CH4+H2O⇌CO+3H2CH_4 + H_2O \rightleftharpoons CO + 3H_2CH4​+H2​O⇌CO+3H2​, ΔH=+205\Delta H = +205ΔH=+205 (endothermic). To favour H₂:

  • Endothermic → high T.
  • Δn=4−2=+2\Delta n = 4-2 = +2Δn=4−2=+2 → low pressure.
    Answer: Low pressure, high temperature.

5.2 Contact Process (Sulfuric Acid)

Key step: 2SO2(g)+O2(g)⇌2SO3(g)2SO_2(g) + O_2(g) \rightleftharpoons 2SO_3(g)2SO2​(g)+O2​(g)⇌2SO3​(g), ΔH=−196 kJ\Delta H = -196 \, kJΔH=−196kJ (exothermic)

Conditions:

  • Temperature: ~450 °C (compromise).
  • Pressure: 1–2 atm (equilibrium already far right; high pressure not economic).
  • Catalyst: V2O5V_2O_5V2​O5​ (vanadium(V) oxide).
  • SO₃ absorbed in conc. H₂SO₄ to form oleum, then diluted.

6. Equilibrium Calculations – Step by Step

6.1 Calculating K from Equilibrium Data

  • Substitute equilibrium concentrations into the K expression.

6.2 Using ICE Tables (Initial, Change, Equilibrium)

  • Write balanced equation.
  • Set up initial concentrations.
  • Define change (x) based on stoichiometry.
  • Write equilibrium concentrations in terms of x.
  • Substitute into K expression and solve for x.
  • Check for approximations if K is very small.

6.3 Using the Reaction Quotient Q

  • Calculate Q from given concentrations.
  • Compare with K to determine direction.

Exam Q4 type:
2SO2+O2⇌2SO32SO_2 + O_2 \rightleftharpoons 2SO_32SO2​+O2​⇌2SO3​, K=100. If [SO₂] = [SO₃], then K=[SO3]2[SO2]2[O2]=1[O2]K = \frac{[SO_3]^2}{[SO_2]^2[O_2]} = \frac{1}{[O_2]}K=[SO2​]2[O2​][SO3​]2​=[O2​]1​ → [O₂] = 1/100 = 0.01 M.

9. Common Traps and Distractors

  • “Catalyst increases yield” → False; only speeds up equilibrium attainment.
  • “Equilibrium concentrations are equal” → False; rates are equal, not concentrations.
  • “Adding a solid reactant shifts equilibrium” → False; solids/liquids do not appear in K.
  • “K changes when concentration changes” → False; only temperature changes K.
  • “If Q > K, reaction goes forward” → False; it goes reverse to reduce product concentration.
  • “High pressure always favours products” → Only if product side has fewer gas moles.
  • “Exothermic reactions are favoured by high temperature” → False; they are favoured by low temperature.
  • Forgetting to omit solids/liquids in K expressions.
  • Using Δn incorrectly, especially when some species are solid/liquid.
